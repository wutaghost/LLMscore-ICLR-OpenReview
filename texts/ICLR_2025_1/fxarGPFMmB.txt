009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Challenges remain in providing interpretable explanations for neural network
012            reasoning in explainable AI (xAI). Existing methods like Integrated Gradients
013            produce noisy maps, and LIME, while intuitive, may deviate from the model’s
014            reasoning. We introduce a framework that uses hierarchical segmentation tech-
015            niques for faithful and interpretable explanations of Convolutional Neural Net-
016
               works (CNNs). Our method constructs model-based hierarchical segmentations
               that maintain the model’s reasoning fidelity and allow both human-centric and
017
               model-centric segmentation. This approach can be combined with various xAI
018
               methods and provides multiscale explanations that help identify biases and im-
019            prove understanding of neural network decision-making. Experiments show
020            that our framework, xAiTrees, delivers highly interpretable and faithful model
021            explanations, not only surpassing traditional xAI methods but shedding new
022            light on a novel approach to enhancing xAI interpretability. Code at: https:
023            //anonymous.4open.science/r/reasoning_with_trees-F3E1.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      In modern deep learning applications, especially in healthcare and finance, there is a growing need
028
      for transparency and explanation. Understanding a model’s rationale is crucial before relying on
029
      its predictions. This need arises from biases present at various stages of model development and
030   deployment. While some biases help in learning data distribution (Goyal & Bengio, 2022), others
031   may indicate data imbalance, incorrect correlations, or prejudices in data collection.
032
033
      To meet the demand for explanation, Explainable Artificial Intelligence (xAI) provides methods
      that clarify models’ decision-making processes. In healthcare, tools like GradCAM (Selvaraju
034
      et al., 2017), which shows heatmaps of important image regions, and LRP (Bach et al., 2015),
035
      which attributes importance to features (pixels), help in understanding deep learning models across
036   various applications (Borys et al., 2023; Dharshini et al., 2023; Chaddad et al., 2023), including
037   ultrasound (Born et al., 2021) and X-ray (Abeyagunasekera et al., 2022) imaging. These techniques
038   were crucial during the recent Covid-19 outbreaks, aiding in the diagnosis process (Lu et al., 2022;
039   Haghanifar et al., 2022). However, these methods are approximations of model behavior. Different
040   techniques prioritize either faithfulness to the model’s behavior or human interpretability, posing a
041   challenge in balancing the two.
042   Object-structure-based visualizations enhance human interpretation by decomposing images in ways
043   that mimic human perception, grouping objects by attributes like color, texture, and edges (Hubel
044   & Wiesel, 1959). Techniques such as LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) and KernelSHAP (Lundberg &
045   Lee, 2017) have used this approach effectively, segmenting images into meaningful parts to improve
046   interpretability. However, the size of segmented regions affects the information extracted: small
047   regions can be hard to interpret, while large regions may miss fine details. Additionally, using a
048   segmentation framework introduces human bias, which aids comprehension but may reduce fidelity
049   to the model’s actual behavior.
050   In this paper, we explore the trade-off between explaining Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
051   with model faithfulness and human interpretability. We introduce a framework that combines hierar-
052   chical segmentation with region-based explanation methods, creating a human-friendly multiscale
053   visualization, inspired by the Multiscale Interpretable Visualization (Ms-IV) technique (Rodrigues
      et al., 2024). Unlike traditional region-based xAI techniques that segment images into a fixed number

      2   R ELATED WORK
105
106   Classification problems and xAI: One fundamental task in machine learning is classification. The
107   basic concept involves working with a training dataset, denoted as DS = (Ii , GTi )i∈[1,NbIm] , which
      consists of pairs of images Ii and their associated labels GTi . Each label belongs to one of a set of


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      classes represented by c ∈ [1, NbClasses]. The goal is to train a model, denoted as Ξ, to effectively
109   distinguish between different classes within the dataset.
110
111   In this configuration, we express Ξ as Ξ = Ξclassif ◦ Ξenc , the combination of two elements: an Ξenc ,
112
      responsible for converting each input image Ii into a feature vector, and a Ξclassif , which analyzes
      these features to classify the images. The outcome of this process, referred to as the “logit” for image
113
      Ii , is a vector outi ∈ RNbClasses that signifies the activation levels across various classes. Typically,
114
      we apply a Softmax layer to outi to determine the class with the highest activation, ideally aligning
115   with the ground truth label GTi for perfect classification.
116
117   Pixel-wise explanations: In neural networks, optimizing the model Ξ involves the backpropagation
118
      process. Exploiting this process, certain explainable Artificial Intelligence (xAI) methods like
      Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017), Guided-Backpropagation (Springenberg et al., 2015),
119
      and Deconvolution (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014) utilize it to identify input features that enhance the
120
      response of a specific class, aiming to maximize the value of a particular position in the output vector
121   outi . Consequently, attribution maps are generated, illustrating pixel-level explanations, as depicted
122   in Figure 1 for Integrated Gradients (IG).
123
124
      Region-based explanations: Additional techniques like Sensitivity Analysis (Zeiler & Fergus,
      2014), LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016), and SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) utilize occlusions of image
125
      regions to assess the network’s sensitivity to each region within an image. These methods provide
126
      explanations at a region level rather than a pixel level, as illustrated in Figure 1 for LIME. More
127   recently, a region-based technique, XRAI (Kapishnikov et al., 2019), proposed to combine Integrated
128   Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017) and perturbation-based approaches to generate saliency maps as
129   explanations.
130
      Concept-based explanations: However, many of these techniques focus on explaining individual
131
      samples separately, which limits our understanding of how the model behaves globally across
132
      various scenarios. That is why methods like TCAV (Kim et al., 2018), ACE (Ghorbani et al., 2019),
133   Explanatory graphs (Zhang et al., 2018), LGNN (Tan et al., 2022), and Ms-IV (Rodrigues et al.,
134   2024) aim to comprehend the overall behavior of the model. In particular, Ms-IV also considers the
135   impact of occlusions, not on individual predictions, but on the model’s output space.

      3    P RELIMINARIES
140
141
142
      To ensure a thorough understanding of the sequel, we provide in this section the general techniques
143
      and metrics employed during this work. In subsection A, we provide a brief overview of the selected
144   hierarchical segmentation techniques, highlighting their significance. In subsection B, we shortly
145   present the occlusion-based metrics used in the construction of our methodology.
146
147
      A. Segmentation techniques: As an important step for our framework, we employ image segmen-
      tation algorithms that decompose images into more interpretable structures, enabling better human
148
      understanding and interpretation. We specifically employ hierarchical segmentation techniques due to
149
      their capability to decompose images into multiple levels of detail, from fine to coarse, mirroring how
150   humans naturally perceive objects: initially observing the overall structure before delving into the
151   finer details. A hierarchical segmentation algorithm produces a merging tree, that indicates how two
152   given regions merge. In this paper, we use the tree structures available in the Higra package (Perret
153   et al., 2019; 2018) : Binary Partition Tree (BPT) and Hierarchical watershed. See details in A.1.
154
      B. Occlusion-based metrics: In this work, we use two metrics to generate our segmentation based
155
      on the model explainability: (i) Occlusion, which is the impact of occluding an image region on its
156   classification output, and (ii) CaOC which is the intra-class impact of occluding an image region.
157   For (i), we assess how the output of a model changes when an image region is occluded. For (ii),
158   we employ a sliding metric that ranks images based on the highest activations for a given class. We
159   then measure the movement in this ranking after occluding a region of the image, determining the
160   intra-class impact of the occlusion (detailed in A.2). Although the main experimentation uses these
161   methods, we present in A.7 a framework’s variation using LIME to show xAiTrees versatility with
      other types of xAI techniques. Explanations example in Figure 3 (a).

      4   M ETHODOLOGY
189
190   In this section, we outline our four-step methodology (Figure 2): (1) hierarchical segmentation, (2)
191   attribute computation, (3) tree shaping, and (4) hierarchical visualization. In step 1, we convert the
192   data into a hierarchical representation, creating various regions at different scales in the image. In
193   step 2, we evaluate some xAI-based attributes (B) on the regions. In step 3, we assess the importance
194   of the region attributes. Finally, in step 4, we explain how to generate a visualization map from the
195   importance of the attributes.
196   1. Hierarchical segmentation:
197
198
      Intuitively, any hierarchical segmentation algorithm works by iteratively merging first the pixels, then
      the regions, according to a similarity criterion. In this paper, we test two ways for measuring the
199
      similarity: human-based and model-based.
200
201   • The human-based approach relies on the Structured Edge Detection (SED) algorithm (Dollár &
202   Zitnick, 2014), which captures complex edge patterns and produces precise edge maps, in accordance
203   with human intuition.
204   • The model-based approach uses a visual representation of the image’s pixels most influential in
205
      a model’s decision. Although less intuitive for humans, this approach helps to understand how the
      model reasons. We test pixel-wise explainable AI methods: Integrated Gradients (IG) (Sundararajan
206
      et al., 2017), Guided-Backpropagation (Springenberg et al., 2015), Input x Gradient (Shrikumar et al.,
207
      2017), and Saliency (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015) (all from Captum framework). The methods
208   were chosen for their state-of-the-art, pixel-wise importance attribution.
209
210   Using such a similarity criterion, we obtain a hierarchical segmentation, which can be represented as
211   a tree T, completing the first step of our pipeline. See Figure 2, first column.
212   2. Attribute computation: The segmentation tree generated in the previous step provides many
213   segments. We assess the model’s response on each segmented region in the tree, for all regions large
214   enough. We apply a metric to evaluate the occlusion impact caused by each region. These occlusion
215   scores reveal the influence of each segmented regions on the model’s output. The metric employed to
      assess the impact of regions can be any occlusion-based metric. See Figure 2, second column.


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      3. Tree shaping: To assess the importance of the nodes’ attributes, it is not enough to simply take the
217   regions with the highest attributes: there are too many of them. Instead, we rely on a process called
218   shaping (Xu et al., 2015; 2016). The main idea is to look at the undirected, vertices-weighted graph
219   G, whose vertices are the node of T, whose edges are formed by the parent-children relationship in
220   T, and whose weights are the attributes of the nodes. We now look at the level-sets of G. A vertex
221   of G (a node of T) is important according to its persistence in the level sets of G. More precisely, a
222   connected component is born when a local maximum of the attribute appear; when two connected
223   components merge, one of the two maxima disappear, and the time of life of this maximum is its
224   persistence. We can compute such persistence by building a new tree T’ on G, T’ is the tree of all the
225
      connected component of the upper-level sets of G. The persistence of a node of T is easily computed
      on T’ by computing the length of the branch it belongs to. We refer to Xu et al. (2015; 2016) for
226
      more details. See Figure 2, third column.
227
228   4. Construction of the hierarchical visualization: With T’ from the previous step, we now produce
229   a visualization of the important regions. Using the persistence of a node directly for visualization
230   can yield conflicting results for interpretation. Consider an example where we want to generate
231
      explanations for a model that classifies images of dogs. The persistence might indicate that eyes are
      the primary features for correct classification. If the image under scrutiny shows a dog with its owner,
232
      the persistence might erroneously highlight the eyes of both the human and the dog as relevant, which
233
      is misleading since only the dog’s eyes should matter (in an ideal, unbiased model). To avoid such
234   effect, we recursively sum the persistence of each node from the root to the leaves of T’. This ensures
235   that smaller segments inherit the importance of their parent nodes. In our example, if the parent
236   segment of the eyes is the entire face, the dog’s face carries importance for the model’s decision,
237   while the human face does not. By adding the dog’s facial region information to the eye segments,
238   we ensure the dog’s eyes are prioritized over the human eyes and, therefore, become more prominent
239   in the explanation. This process aggregates the importance of various scales of the image into the
240   pixels, resulting in a hierarchical, multi-scale, visualization. We show an example in Figure 3 (b). We
241   use this aggregated persistence as the final score for each region of the hierarchical segmentation. We
242
      select a minimum importance score, and retain the regions accordingly. We superpose the retained
      region on the original image to generate the Final visualization (Figure 2, fourth column).

275   5.1   Q UANTITATIVE EVALUATIONS
276
277   We selected state-of-the-art region-based methods as baseline (B) to be compared: Occlusion, Grad-
278   CAM, LIME, Ms-IV, and XRAI (configuration of each method in A.4). Although ACE presents
279   good concept-based explanations, we only use it in the human evaluation experiments because, as
280
      a global explanation method, it is not directly comparable to the local ones in these quantitative
      experiments (more details in A.6). We compare the baseline methods with two configurations of
281
      our proposed methodology (more configurations in A.3 and A.5): C1 and C2. This is done to
282
      explore variations in the configuration of our methodology, including variations in the size of minimal
283   regions in the visualizations, pixel weights for graph construction in segmentation, and methods for
284   generating hierarchical segmentation. In C1, we present results for minimal regions of 500 pixels
285   (Cat vs. Dog and ImageNet) and 64 pixels (CIFAR-10), utilizing edges and Integrated Gradients
286   (IG) as pixel weights, and the watershed-by-area hierarchical segmentation. This configuration was
287   selected for its stability across different minimal region sizes in the datasets, and its visualizations
288   were used for human evaluation. While C2 presents results for minimal regions of 200 pixels (Cat
289   vs. Dog and ImageNet) and 4 pixels (CIFAR-10), using edges and Guided Backpropagation (BP)
290   as pixel weights, and the BPT tree. This configuration yielded the highest performance. In A.5, we
291
      provide comprehensive results for other configurations. Here, we propose four main quantitative
      evaluations, (i and ii) inspired by feature removal (Covert et al., 2021) and (iii and iv) inspired
292
      by Performance Information Curves (PICs) (Kapishnikov et al., 2019): (i) Exclusion of important
293
      regions; (ii) Inclusion of important regions; (iii) Softmax Information Curve (SIC); and (iv) Accuracy
294   Information Curve (AIC). For (i) and (ii), we used the McNemar test (McNemar, 1947) to compare
295   each method with the best configuration and determine whether there were statistically significant
296   differences between the results. The details are discussed below:
297
      Exclusion of important regions: Given that each region-based explainable AI (xAI) method
298
      identifies important regions that explain the prediction of a model, we performed occlusion of these
299
      regions, in order to measure the impact of each selection. For methods that assign scores to regions,
300   we masked the 25% highest scores (this excludes LIME, which inherently provides information to
301   directly mask each region – a detailed explanation is included in A.6).
302
303
      The first idea for the metric was to calculate the impact on the logits after occlusion. However, any
      kind of perturbation can affect the logits and not necessarily the classification. In this particular case,
304
      since we are dealing with a classification problem, we consider the class change as the main evidence
305
      that an important image region has been occluded. Therefore, the values from Table 1 Ch. is the
306   percentage of class changing images, Same is the percentage of images with same class prediction
307   but with reduced logits (reduced classification certainty), and Total is the percentage of all images
308   with the class negatively impacted by the removal of important regions (sum of Ch. and Same).
309
      In Table 1, we present results (Ch., Same, Total) for each explainable technique (B – Baseline, C1,
310
      and C2 – our proposition) applied to a network (VGG or ResNet) classifying images from a dataset
311
      (Cat vs. Dogs, Cifar10, or ImageNet). Higher Ch. values indicate that the identified regions are
312   more class-representative. High Same values complement Ch., suggesting that the best results are
313   shown by higher Ch. and Same values. Thus, while Total sums Ch. and Same, the optimal result is
314   reflected by initially higher Ch. and then higher Same values.

345   We present the results of a second experiment in Table 2. To address the issue of reduced precision

348

349
      of the image (details in A.5 Equation 2). Higher PIR values indicate that each occluded pixel has
350   a significant average impact, suggesting that concealing larger portions or the entire image leads
351   to lower PIR, indicating less precision in the concealed area. Table 2 displays for each network,

      model predictions, providing more faithful and interpretable explanations. Our approach outperforms

      A.1    A. S EGMENTATION TECHNIQUES
706
      As an important step for our framework, we employ segmentation techniques so we can decompose
707
      images, based on specific attributes, into more interpretable structures, enabling better human
708   understanding and interpretation. We specifically employ hierarchical segmentation techniques due to
709   their capability to decompose images into multiple levels of detail, mirroring how humans naturally
710   perceive objects: initially observing the overall structure before delving into the finer details.
711
      Trees: A tree is an acyclic graph, consisting of nodes that connect to zero or more other nodes. It
712
      starts with a “root” node that branches out to other nodes, ending in “leaves” with no children. In
713
      image representation, the root node represents the entire image, and each leaf represents a pixel,
714   resulting in as many leaves as pixels. The structure between the root and leaves groups pixels into
715   clusters at each level based on similarity metrics, with each level abstracting the one below. Using
716   a segmentation tree, we can make cuts at various levels to obtain different numbers and sizes of
717   segmented regions.
718
      Binary Partition Tree (BPT): A Binary Partition Tree (BPT) is a data structure in which each node
719
      represents a region of the image. Similarly, the tree starts with a root node representing the entire
720   image and branches out through a series of binary splits until reaching the leaf nodes, representing
721   the individual pixels. Different from the tree, in which a node could have multiple splits, in the BPT
722   each split, divides a region into two smaller sub-regions based on a criterion.
723
      Watershed: This algorithm (Cousty et al., 2008) constructs a hierarchical segmentation tree based on
724
      a minimum-spanning forest rooted in the local minima of an edge-weighted graph. In this context,
725
      local minima are points in the graph where the surrounding edge weights are higher, representing the
726   lowest values in their neighborhood. These minima serve as starting points for the segmentation. The
727   algorithm iteratively merges regions beginning from these local minima, guided by the edge weights
728   that indicate dissimilarity between adjacent pixels. By progressively combining these regions, the
729   algorithm builds the segmentation tree, effectively capturing the hierarchical structure of the image.
730
731   A.2    B. O CCLUSION - BASED METRICS :
732
733   Here, we discuss the metrics used to generate our segmentation based on the model explainability
734   (block B in Figure 2). We present two metrics: (i) Occlusion, which is the impact of occluding an
735   image region on its classification output, and (ii) CaOC which is the intra-class impact of occluding
736   an image region. For (i), we assess how the output of a model changes when an image region is
737   occluded. For (ii), we employ a sliding metric that ranks images based on the highest activations for
738   a given class. We then measure the movement in this ranking after occluding a region of the image,
739
      determining the intra-class impact of the occlusion.
740   Occlusion: Let us say we have a model Ξ producing an output outi for an image Ii . By concealing
741   portions of this image, creating a new image Ii■ , we obtain a different model output out■     i . The
742   significance of the occluded area concerning a particular class c is assessed by comparing the outputs:
743
744
745                                              outi,c − out■
                                                             i,c .                                           (1)
746
747   If there is a significant difference, it indicates that the model strongly relies on this region for class
748   activation, meaning that these regions have a high impact on the model’s decision.
749
      CAOC: In the Ms-IV method, introduced by Rodrigues et al. (Rodrigues et al., 2024), CaOC employs
750   rankings to assess how occlusions affect the model’s output space. A ranking is a sequence of objects
751   ordered according to a specific criterion, from the object most aligned with it to the least aligned.
752   Suppose the criterion is to maximize class c. In that case, the first index i in this sequence represents
753   the object (in our case, the image Ii ) with the highest activation for class c in the output outi . If we
754   define a function argsort to obtain the indices of an ordered sequence of objects, we can derive the
755   sequence of image indices that maximize class c: Seq c = argsort (out.,c , decreasing), with out.,c
      the vector of outputs for class c of a set of input images (Ii )i∈[1,NbIm] .

843   Exclusion of important regions: Given that each region-based explainable AI (xAI) method
844   identifies important regions in an image to explain the prediction of a model, we performed occlusion
845
      of these regions to measure the impact of each selection and evaluate the methods.
846   Except for LIME, which proposes a ranking of the most important image segments, the methods
847   we used as a baseline provide values for measuring the importance of pixels, which in visualization
848   is similar to regions or segmentation for humans. However, if we select all the positive importance
849   values provided by these methods, we are likely to cover a large part of the image. By selecting
850   regions with only the top 25% higher values, we reduce the size of the mask. In fact, different datasets
851
      and models will show different visualization behaviors, so we chose to define a high threshold that is
      fixed (not specific to a single dataset) and common to all methods in order to have a fair comparison.
852
      Therefore, for methods that assign scores to regions, we masked the 25% highest scores.

1659   A.7    P OSSIBLE ADAPTATIONS OF THE FRAMEWORK
1660
1661
       Regarding the adaptability of the framework for using other xAI methods to score regions, we present
1662   in Table 16 preliminary results of the exclusion of important regions experiment (Section 5 and A.5)
1663   by using LIME in the place of Occlusion and CaOC. The results demonstrate Tree-LIME improves
1664   the class change under important regions’ occlusion for the Cat vs. Dog dataset (compared to Table 1).
1665   We also show two image explanations generated by Tree-LIME in Figure 11. We do not include this
1666   variation in all the experiments due to the time consumption (Table 14). However, these preliminary
1667   results demonstrate Tree-LIME can increase the class change (Ch.) reaching higher percentages than
1668   the best configuration presented in the main paper.
1669   Regarding the adaptability of the framework to other tasks such as learning representations, one sug-
1670   gestion would be calculating the distance between the two learned representations (original and after
1671   occlusion) to attribute regions’ scores instead of verifying the logits different as in classification tasks.
1672   The type of distance applied should be tested. A more sophisticated evaluation of impact (scoring the
1673   hierarchy regions) would be to include a network to quantify the quality of the representation for the
       task at hand.
