009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) offer an efficient framework for processing event-
012            driven data due to their sparse, spike-based communication, making them ideal
013            for real-time tasks. However, their inability to capture long-range dependencies
014            limits their effectiveness in complex temporal modeling. To address this challenge,
015            we present a SPLR (SPiking Network for Learning Long-range Relations), a
016            novel architecture designed to overcome these limitations. The core contribution
017            of SPLR is the Spike-Aware HiPPO (SA-HiPPO) mechanism, which adapts the
018            HiPPO framework for discrete, spike-driven inputs, enabling efficient long-range
019            memory retention in event-driven systems. Additionally, SPLR includes a convo-
020
               lutional layer that integrates state-space dynamics to enhance feature extraction
               while preserving the efficiency of sparse, asynchronous processing. Together, these
021
               innovations enable SPLR to model both short- and long-term dependencies effec-
022
               tively, outperforming prior methods on various event-based datasets. Experimental
023            results demonstrate that SPLR achieves superior performance in tasks requiring
024            fine-grained temporal dynamics and long-range memory, establishing it as a scal-
025            able and efficient solution for real-time applications such as event-based vision and
026            sensor fusion in neuromorphic computing.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) are an emerging paradigm in neuromorphic computing, offering
033
      significant advantages in terms of energy efficiency, low latency, and event-driven processing. These
      characteristics make SNNs particularly suitable for real-time applications such as event-based vision,
034
      sensor fusion, and neuromorphic signal processing Roy et al. (2019); Davies et al. (2018); Rathi et al.
035
      (2023); Frenkel et al. (2021). Unlike conventional deep learning architectures, SNNs leverage sparse,
036   spike-based communication to process data in an asynchronous and biologically inspired manner.
037   Despite these advantages, SNNs face fundamental limitations in modeling long-range temporal
038   dependencies. Specifically, the reliance on exponentially decaying membrane potentials in typical
039   spiking neuron models like Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) neurons leads to rapid loss of historical
040   information Bengio et al. (1994); Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997); Neftci et al. (2019); Bellec et al.
041   (2018). This results in poor performance on tasks requiring retention of information over extended
042   sequences, such as gesture recognition, activity classification, and spatio-temporal reasoning.
043   Addressing this limitation in SNNs has been a longstanding challenge. Traditional approaches have
044   adapted techniques from recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and deep learning to the spike-based
045   domain, such as incorporating surrogate gradient methods or dense recurrent connections Wu et al.
046   (2018); Shrestha & Orchard (2018). However, these methods often incur significant computational
047   costs, requiring frequent updates that are poorly aligned with the sparse, event-driven nature of SNNs.
048   Furthermore, these approaches fail to fully exploit the inherent advantages of spike-driven dynamics,
049   limiting their efficiency and scalability.
050   Recent advances in State-Space Models (SSMs) provide a promising framework for modeling long-
051   range dependencies. SSMs, such as the Structured State Space (S4) and Mamba models, excel in
052   encoding long-range state representations using continuous-time formulations Gu et al. (2020; 2022).
053   However, these models are typically designed for dense and synchronous systems, and their direct
      integration into the discrete, asynchronous processing paradigm of SNNs has remained challenging.

054
      Specifically, the mismatch between continuous memory updates in SSMs and the event-driven nature
055   of SNNs leads to inefficiencies and incompatibilities Gu et al. (2021); Hasani et al. (2021).
056
057   In this work, we propose SPLR (SPiking Network for Learning Long-Range Relations), a novel
058
      SNN architecture that integrates spike-adapted state-space dynamics to effectively model both short-
      and long-term temporal dependencies in event-driven systems. At the heart of SPLR is the Spike-
059
      Aware HiPPO (SA-HiPPO) mechanism, which dynamically adapts continuous memory retention
060
      frameworks for spike-based inputs, enabling efficient long-range memory retention while preserving
061   the sparsity and low latency of SNNs. The SA-HiPPO mechanism is seamlessly integrated into
062   the SPLR convolutional layer, which leverages state-space principles for efficient spatio-temporal
063   feature extraction.
064
      The SA-HiPPO mechanism within the SPLR convolutional layer adapts continuous-time memory
065
      retention for the sparse, asynchronous nature of SNNs by aligning memory updates with spike
066
      timings through a dynamic decay matrix. This ensures robust long-term dependency modeling while
067   preserving computational efficiency. Building on this, the layer employs FFT-based convolutions
068   and low-rank approximations for scalable spatio-temporal feature extraction. Unlike conventional
069   frame-based methods, SPLR processes spikes event-by-event, maintaining temporal resolution and
070   reducing latency and computational overhead, enabling accurate, efficient event-driven processing for
071   tasks with complex temporal dependencies.
072
      Our contributions are summarized as follows:
073
074          • SPLR Architecture: A novel SNN design that integrates spike-adapted state-space dynam-
075            ics to overcome the limitations of traditional SNNs in modeling long-range dependencies.
076          • Spike-Aware HiPPO (SA-HiPPO): An adaptation of the HiPPO framework for spike-
077            driven inputs, introducing dynamic memory retention mechanisms aligned with inter-spike
078            intervals.
079          • SPLR Convolutional Layer: A state-space-inspired convolutional layer combining FFT-
080            based operations and low-rank approximations for efficient spatio-temporal feature extrac-
081            tion in event-driven systems.
082          • Scalability and Efficiency: A unified architecture that achieves superior performance on
083            event-based benchmarks while maintaining computational efficiency, scalability, and low
084            latency for real-time applications.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
087
088
      SSMs have emerged as a powerful framework for capturing long-range dependencies by encoding
089
      state information over extended sequences Gu et al. (2020; 2022). However, while continuous-time
090   SSMs excel in dense and synchronous settings, adapting them to the sparse, asynchronous nature of
091   SNNs poses significant challenges Gu et al. (2021); Hasani et al. (2021). Existing solutions, such
092   as SpikingLMU Liu et al. (2024b) and BinaryS4D Stan & Rhodes (2024), attempt to address this
093   gap but fall short in fully leveraging the unique characteristics of SNNs. SpikingLMU incorporates
094   Legendre Memory Units (LMUs) into SNNs for long-range dependency modeling but relies on
095   dense recurrent computations, which undermine the event-driven efficiency of SNNs. Similarly,
096   BinaryS4D integrates state-space dynamics into spiking architectures but employs floating-point
097   matrix multiplications, resulting in a hybrid model that deviates from the sparse, fully spiking
098
      paradigm and incurs computational overhead, limiting its suitability for real-time applications.
099   One of the critical limitations in existing approaches is the inability to effectively handle the irregular
100   and sparse timing of spikes while maintaining efficient and robust long-range temporal modeling.
101   Continuous-time memory mechanisms like HiPPO Gu et al. (2020) have demonstrated success in
102   dense systems by optimizing memory retention over continuous sequences. However, their reliance
103   on continuous updates and dense matrix computations makes them ill-suited for asynchronous,
104
      event-driven systems like SNNs.
105   To overcome these limitations, we propose the Spike-Aware HiPPO (SA-HiPPO) mechanism, a
106   novel adaptation of HiPPO for spiking systems. SA-HiPPO introduces a dynamic decay matrix that
107   adjusts memory retention based on inter-spike intervals, allowing it to align memory updates with the
      sparse and asynchronous nature of spike events. This innovation eliminates the need for dense updates,


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      preserving the computational efficiency and latency advantages of SNNs while enabling robust long-
109   range temporal modeling. Unlike prior approaches, SA-HiPPO operates entirely in an event-driven
110   manner, making it uniquely suited for real-time neuromorphic applications such as dynamic vision
111   Gehrig & Scaramuzza (2024) and temporal reasoning Xiao et al. (2024), where irregular timing and
112   low latency are critical. Event-driven systems in neuromorphic vision have explored hybrid strategies
113   that combine frame- and event-based approaches to process high-speed temporal data Gehrig &
114   Scaramuzza (2024); Schöne et al. (2024). While these methods are effective for specific tasks, they
115   often fail to scale for long-range temporal dependencies in asynchronous data streams. Similarly,
116   dendritic-inspired models like DH-LIF Zheng et al. (2024b) improve temporal processing through
117
      heterogeneous dynamics but introduce significant computational overhead, limiting their scalability
      for large datasets and real-time applications.
118
119   Our proposed SA-HiPPO addresses these limitations by introducing a dynamic decay matrix that
120   adjusts memory retention based on inter-spike intervals. Unlike prior approaches, SA-HiPPO operates
121   entirely in an event-driven manner, aligning memory updates with the sparse and asynchronous nature
122   of spike events. This innovation preserves the computational efficiency and latency advantages
123
      of SNNs while enabling robust long-range temporal modeling, making it particularly suitable for
      real-time neuromorphic applications such as dynamic vision and temporal reasoning.

146   The SPLR Model is designed to process asynchronous, sparse data in a biologically-inspired manner.
147   This model combines several novel components, including dendritic attention mechanisms and SSMs,
148   to efficiently handle event-based spiking inputs and capture long-range temporal dependencies. Figure
149   1(a) provides a high-level architecture of the model. The Dendrite Attention Layer first extracts
150   spatio-temporal features from input spikes, which are then reduced spatially in the Spatial Pooling
151   Layer. The SPLR Convolution Layer captures temporal dynamics and long-range dependencies,
152
      while the Spike-Aware HiPPO (SA-HiPPO) mechanism dynamically manages memory retention.
      Finally, the Readout Layer aggregates information for downstream tasks.
153
154   1. Input Representation: The input to the model is represented as a sequence of spike events, each
155   defined by the tuple (x, y, t, p), where (x, y) are the spatial coordinates, t is the timestamp, and p
156   is the magnitude or polarity of the spike. These events are streamed asynchronously, reflecting the
157   sparsity of the data.
158   2. Dendrite Attention Layer: The model begins by passing the input through the Dendrite Attention
159   Layer, constructed using DH-LIF neurons Zheng et al. (2024a), as shown in Figure 1(b). Each
160   DH-LIF neuron has multiple dendritic branches, each characterized by a different timing factor τd ,
161   enabling it to capture temporal dynamics across various scales. This is essential for accommodating
      the diverse timescales present in asynchronous spike inputs.

519   6 C ONCLUSION
520   This work presents the SPLR model, which integrates the novel SA-HiPPO mechanism with
521   fully event-driven processing to overcome the limitations of existing approaches in SNNs. By
522   dynamically adapting memory retention to inter-spike intervals, SA-HiPPO enables precise modeling
523   of long-range dependencies while preserving the sparsity and efficiency inherent to SNNs. Empirical
524   evaluations highlight SPLR’s superior performance across a range of benchmarks. On the Long
525
      Range Arena (LRA), SPLR demonstrates significantly higher accuracy than methods like BinaryS4D
      and SpikingLMU, achieving state-of-the-art results with lower computational cost compared to dense
526
      Transformer-based architectures. On real-world event datasets such as DVS Gesture and HAR-DVS,
527
      SPLR leverages its efficient spatio-temporal feature extraction to outperform other state-of-the-art
528   models like EventMamba. Furthermore, SPLR scales effectively on high-resolution benchmarks such
529   as Celex-HAR, maintaining high performance under increased spatial and temporal complexities
530   where traditional methods degrade.
531
      The entire SPLR pipeline, including components such as the Dendrite Attention Layer, Spatial Pooling,
532
      and SPLR Convolution, is critical for enabling the precise modeling of long-range dependencies in
533
      event-driven systems. Each component plays a complementary role in achieving robust, scalable,
534   and efficient processing. The key novelty lies in the formulation of the SA-HiPPO mechanism,
535   which addresses a major challenge in the field by overcoming scalability limitations in SNNs
536   while preserving their asynchronous, low-latency nature. By preserving the fully asynchronous,
537   event-driven nature of SNNs, SPLR achieves a transformative balance of scalability, low latency,
538   and computational efficiency. These results establish SPLR as a robust and scalable solution for
539   neuromorphic computing, unlocking new capabilities for long-range dependency modeling and
      real-time event-driven systems.

       8.1   DATASETS AND TASKS
1191
1192
       In this study, we evaluate the performance of the SPLR model across a diverse set of datasets, each
1193
       presenting unique challenges in event-driven processing. The datasets include Sequential CIFAR-
1194   10, Sequential CIFAR-100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009), DVS Gesture Amir et al. (2017), HAR-DVS
1195   Wang et al. (2024b), Celex-HAR Wang et al. (2024a), Long Range Arena (LRA) Tay et al. (2020),
1196   Spiking Heidelberg Digits (SHD) Cramer et al. (2020), and Spiking Speech Commands (SSC). For
1197   all experiments, the SPLR model processes inputs on an event-by-event basis, leveraging its temporal
1198   dynamics to handle fine-grained temporal dependencies without accumulating events into frames.
1199   Below, we provide detailed descriptions of each dataset and the corresponding experimental setups.
1200   Sequential CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100: The CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets Krizhevsky et al.
1201   (2009) consist of 32 × 32 RGB images across 10 and 100 classes, respectively. To simulate a temporal
1202   sequence, each image is divided into 16 non-overlapping patches of size 8 × 8 pixels. These patches
1203   are presented to the model sequentially in a raster-scan order, from top-left to bottom-right. Each
1204   patch is treated as an independent event in the sequence. The task involves classifying the image based
1205   on the full sequence of patches, requiring the model to integrate information over the entire sequence.
1206   This setup evaluates the model’s ability to process spatial information in a temporal context.
1207   DVS Gesture Dataset: The DVS Gesture dataset Amir et al. (2017) comprises recordings from a
1208   Dynamic Vision Sensor (DVS), capturing 11 hand gestures performed by 29 subjects under varying
1209   lighting conditions. Each event is characterized by its spatial location (x, y), timestamp t, and polarity
1210   p (on/off). The dataset provides a challenging benchmark for models to recognize dynamic gestures
1211   from sparse, asynchronous event streams. In our experiments, the SPLR model processes each event
1212   individually as it occurs, without accumulating them into temporal frames, thereby maintaining high
1213   temporal resolution and reducing latency.
1214   HAR-DVS Dataset: The HAR-DVS dataset Wang et al. (2024b) contains neuromorphic event
1215   streams representing human activities, recorded with a DVS. Activities include walking, running, and
1216   other movement-based tasks. Each event is defined by its spatial coordinates, timestamp, and polarity.
1217   The dataset tests the model’s ability to recognize complex human activities from sparse event streams.
1218   The SPLR model processes each spike event-by-event, dynamically updating its internal state for
1219   each incoming spike, enabling precise temporal modeling of the activity sequences.
1220   Celex-HAR Dataset: The Celex-HAR dataset Wang et al. (2024a) consists of high-resolution event
1221   streams captured with a CeleX camera for human activity recognition. Activities include actions such
1222   as sitting, standing, and walking. Each event is represented by its spatial coordinates, timestamps, and
1223   polarity. The dataset provides a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating models on high-resolution
1224   event-based data. The SPLR model processes each spike event-by-event, allowing it to capture the
1225   fine-grained temporal dynamics of human activities.
1226   Long Range Arena (LRA): The Long Range Arena benchmark Tay et al. (2020) evaluates a model’s
1227   ability to process long sequences and capture dependencies over extended temporal horizons. Tasks
1228   such as ListOps and Path-X involve sequence lengths ranging from hundreds to thousands of tokens.
1229   Although these tasks involve discrete tokens rather than spikes, we simulate event-driven processing
1230   by treating each token as an individual event presented sequentially. The SPLR model leverages its
1231   temporal dynamics to capture long-range dependencies efficiently.
1232   Spiking Heidelberg Digits (SHD) and Spiking Speech Commands (SSC): The SHD and SSC
1233   datasets Cramer et al. (2020) are benchmarks for spiking neural networks, containing neuromorphic
1234   spike streams derived from speech datasets. SHD consists of spoken digit recordings converted to
1235   spike trains using the CochleaAMS model, while SSC contains spiking representations of spoken
1236   command audio, representing keywords like "yes," "no," and "stop." Each event is characterized by
1237   its spatial location, timestamp, and polarity. The datasets evaluate the model’s performance on tasks
1238
       involving complex spatio-temporal patterns in speech data. The SPLR model processes each spike
       event as it occurs, dynamically updating its state, ensuring high temporal resolution and efficient
1239
       processing for speech recognition tasks.
1240
1241   Across all datasets, the SPLR model processes inputs on an event-by-event basis. This approach
       allows it to maintain high temporal resolution and capture fine-grained spatio-temporal patterns,

1732   State-Space Models: A state-space model (SSM) is a mathematical framework for modeling systems
1733   that evolve over time. The dynamics of such systems are described by a set of first-order differential
1734   equations, often expressed in continuous time as:
1735                               ẋ(t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t),       y(t) = Cx(t) + Du(t)
1736
       where:
1737
1738            • x(t) ∈ RN is the hidden state vector, representing the internal state of the system at time t,
1739
                • u(t) ∈ RM is the input signal, such as sensory data or external stimuli,
1740
1741            • y(t) ∈ RP is the output signal or observable state,
1742            • A ∈ RN ×N , B ∈ RN ×M , C ∈ RP ×N , and D ∈ RP ×M are learned system matrices.
1743
1744   State-space models are often used in signal processing and control systems to model systems with
1745
       temporal dependencies. In many practical scenarios, however, the continuous-time formulation is
       discretized:
1746

1747
1748
       where Ad , Bd , Cd , and Dd are the corresponding discrete-time matrices, and k indexes the discrete
       time steps.

1750   Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs): SNNs are a class of neural networks that more closely mimic
1751   biological neurons. In SNNs, information is transmitted as spikes, or binary events, at discrete times,
1752   as opposed to continuous activations in traditional neural networks. A typical neuron in an SNN,
1753   such as the Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) neuron, is governed by the following dynamics:
1754                                            dVi (t)

1755                                              dt

1757   the input current, typically derived from presynaptic neurons or external stimuli.
1758
       A spike is emitted when the membrane potential exceeds a threshold θi . After a spike, the membrane
1759
       potential is reset, and a refractory period prevents immediate re-firing.
1760
1761   Despite their potential for efficient temporal data processing, SNNs are difficult to train due to the
1762   non-differentiability of spikes and the complex membrane potential dynamics.

1766   a state vector x(t), allowing the model to retain relevant temporal dependencies over long time scales.

       Spike-Aware HiPPO Mechanism: The Spike-Aware HiPPO (SA-HiPPO) (Fig. 8) mechanism is a
1980   core component of the SPLR model, designed to efficiently capture long-term temporal dependencies
1981   in the presence of sparse, event-based spiking inputs. The HiPPO (Highly Optimized Polynomial
1982   Projection) framework, originally developed to approximate continuous input signals, projects them
1983   onto polynomial bases, enabling efficient temporal compression of input history. However, when
1984   dealing with spike-driven dynamics, where inputs are discrete and irregular, the conventional HiPPO
1985   formulation must be adapted to properly address these challenges. The SA-HiPPO adapts the HiPPO
1986   framework to efficiently handle discrete, spike-driven inputs by introducing a decay matrix F (∆t).
1987   This matrix adjusts memory retention based on the time elapsed between spikes (∆t), ensuring more
1988
       recent spikes have a greater influence while older information gradually decays. The Hadamard
       product with the original HiPPO matrix enables adaptive modulation of memory, making it more
1989
       stable and suitable for asynchronous events. In a spike-driven scenario, the input signal is represented
1990
       as a vector of spike trains S(t) ∈ RM , with each element Si (t) defined by Si (t) = ∑ δ(t − tki ),
1991                                                                                                 k

1994   (SA-HiPPO) matrix AS that extends the dynamics of the standard HiPPO to efficiently process spikes.
1995   The SA-HiPPO matrix AS modifies the original HiPPO dynamics to adapt to the nature of spiking

2228   Spiking Neural Networks
2229   Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) are biologically inspired models that process information through
2230   discrete spike events, offering a more energy-efficient alternative to traditional artificial neural
2231   networks (ANNs) Ponulak & Kasinski (2011). They employ learning mechanisms such as spike-
2232   timing-dependent plasticity (STDP) for unsupervised training Gerstner & Kistler (2002); Chakraborty
2233   & Roy (2023) and surrogate gradient descent for supervised learning Neftci et al. (2019). These
2234   approaches have enabled SNNs to be deployed in neuromorphic hardware like TrueNorth Akopyan
2235   et al. (2015) and Loihi Davies et al. (2018), achieving substantial energy savings compared to ANNs.
2236   Recent efforts to improve the learning capacity of SNNs for long-range temporal dependencies
2237
       have explored architectures that integrate heterogeneous neuronal dynamics, achieving significant
       improvements in spatiotemporal tasks Perez-Nieves et al. (2021); Chakraborty & Mukhopadhyay
2238
       (2022; 2023); She et al. (2021a).
2239
2240   However, many traditional SNNs still struggle to model long-range dependencies effectively. This
2241   limitation is often due to the short-term memory characteristics of spiking neuron models, which
2242   focus on local temporal processing and struggle with maintaining information over extended periods
2243
       Bellec et al. (2018); Fang et al. (2023). To address this, recent research has explored the integration of
       state-space dynamics within SNNs. For instance, Stan and Rhodes Stan & Rhodes (2024) proposed a
2244
       model that combines state-space models (SSMs) with spiking architectures, demonstrating improved
2245
       performance in sequence modeling tasks compared to other SNN models. Our work builds on this by
2246   introducing a novel state-space approach specifically tailored for efficient, asynchronous processing
2247   in neuromorphic contexts, enabling accurate and scalable temporal modeling.
