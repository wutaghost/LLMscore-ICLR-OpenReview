                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Achieving seamless coordination between AI agents and humans is crucial for
010
               real-world applications, yet it remains a significant open challenge. Hanabi is
011            an established, fully cooperative benchmark environment that involves imper-
012            fect information, limited communication, theory of mind, and the necessity for
013            coordination among agents to achieve a shared goal. These characteristics, in
014            principle, make Hanabi an ideal testbed for exploring human-AI coordination.
015            However, one key issue is that evaluation with human partners is both expen-
016            sive and difficult to reproduce. To address this, we first develop human proxy
017            agents via a combination of behavioural cloning on a large-scale dataset of human
018            game play and regularised reinforcement learning. These proxies serve as robust,
019
               cheap and reproducible human-like evaluation partners in our Ad-Hoc Human-
               AI Coordination Challenge (AH2AC2). To facilitate the exploration of methods
020
               that leverage limited amounts of human data, we introduce a data-limited chal-
021
               lenge setting, using 1,000 games, which we open-source. Finally, we present
022            baseline results for both two-player and three-player Hanabi scenarios. These
023            include zero-shot coordination methods, which do not utilise any human data,
024            and methods that make use of the available human data combined with rein-
025            forcement learning. To prevent overfitting and ensure fair evaluation, we in-
026            troduce an evaluation protocol that involves us hosting the proxy agents rather
027            than publicly releasing them, and a public leaderboard for tracking the progress
028            of the community. We make our code available as an anonymous repository:
029            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ah2ac2-E451/

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      The interaction between humans and artificial intelligence (AI) systems is rapidly evolving, driven
034
      by significant AI progress and the increasing integration of AI into various aspects of our lives
035
      (Rawas, 2024). As AI agents become more sophisticated, capable and prevalent, effective coordina-
036   tion between humans and AI becomes crucial. This interaction spans a wide range of domains, from
037   collaborative decision-making in healthcare (Asan et al., 2020) to shared control in autonomous ve-
038   hicles (Bansal et al., 2018) and robotics (Haarnoja et al., 2018; Ahn et al., 2024) as well advanced
039   digital assistants (Gemini Team, 2024; Bai et al., 2022). The ultimate goal is to create AI agents
040   that are not limited to solving problems independently but can also work effectively with humans to
041   complete tasks in human-compatible ways (Russell, 2019; Carroll et al., 2019).
042   Traditional approaches to training AI agents frequently employ self-play (SP), where agents learn by
043   interacting with copies of themselves (Tesauro, 1994). While SP has been successful in competitive
044   games like chess and Go (Silver et al., 2016), this approach can lead to agents that overfit to specific
045   strategies, limiting their ability to generalise to novel partners (Carroll et al., 2019). In human-AI
046   coordination scenarios, forming rigid conventions is particularly problematic, as doing so can pose
047   safety risks and humans may be unable to adapt appropriately (Bard et al., 2019). Instead, AI agents
048   must adapt to human partners, taking into account their different skill levels, preferences, and ways
049   of communicating (Hu et al., 2021; 2022).
050   Hanabi is an established, fully cooperative benchmark environment that involves imperfect infor-
051   mation, limited communication, theory of mind, and the necessity for coordination among different
052   players to achieve a shared goal (Bard et al., 2019). These characteristics, in principle, make Hanabi
053   an ideal testbed for evaluating human-AI coordination. However, the evaluation of AI agents when
      paired with human partners is both expensive and difficult to reproduce. Therefore, many previ-

      2.1   H ANABI
111
112   Hanabi is a cooperative card game designed for 2-5 players, but we restrict ourselves to two- and
113   three- player settings in this work due to the availability of human gameplay data for these configu-
114   rations. In Hanabi, players aim to collectively build five ascending stacks of cards, one for each of
115   the five colours. The deck comprises 50 cards, with 10 cards of each colour. These include three
116   cards of rank 1, two each of ranks 2, 3, and 4, and a single card of rank 5. A unique feature of the
117   game is that players can see each other’s cards, but not their own. Information about one’s own cards
118   is gained through hints from teammates or by interpreting their actions.
119   The team starts with eight information tokens and three lives. On their turn, players can choose one
120   of the following three actions: (1) A player can choose a teammate and provide information about all
121   the cards of a specific colour or rank in that teammate’s hand. The hint must be complete, meaning
122   all cards of the specified colour or rank must be indicated. Each hint consumes one of the limited
123   information tokens. If no information tokens remain, this action is unavailable. (2) If the team has
124   fewer than eight information tokens, a player can discard a card face-up, revealing it to all players.
125
      This action replenishes one information token and the player draws a new card. (3) A player can
      attempt to play a card from their hand onto the corresponding colour stack. If the card’s rank is the
126
      next in the sequence for that colour, it is successfully played. Successfully playing a rank five card
127
      earns the team an additional information token. However, an incorrect play results in the loss of one
128   of the team’s limited lives. Regardless of the outcome, the player draws a new card from the deck.
129
130   The game concludes when one of the following three conditions is met: (1) the team loses all their
131
      lives, (2) all five stacks are completed, or (3) the deck is depleted. The final score reflects the team’s
      success in building the stacks. The maximum score is 25, and it is determined by summing up all
132
      the played cards. When all lives are lost, the score is zero.

      2.3   Z ERO - SHOT C OORDINATION AND A D -H OC T EAMPLAY
154
155
      In many real-world scenarios, AI agents need to coordinate with unknown partners, including hu-
156   mans. Traditional cooperative multi-agent RL often uses SP, where agents train together to max-
157   imise joint returns (Bard et al., 2019; Carroll et al., 2019). However, SP training can lead to agents
158   developing arbitrary conventions that work only with their training partners, failing when paired
159   with independently trained agents—a critical issue for human-AI coordination (Carroll et al., 2019;
160   Strouse et al., 2021). Zero-shot coordination (ZSC) addresses this challenge by training agents to
161   coordinate effectively with new partners using the same algorithm (Hu et al., 2020; Treutlein et al.,
      2021). In ZSC, each agent independently trains a joint policy using a general-purpose learning al-


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      gorithm. After training, agents from different runs are paired, and their performance is evaluated
163   through cross-play. The objective is to maximise the expected return when independently trained
164   agents are matched. Ad-hoc teamplay focuses on an agent’s ability to cooperate with unfamiliar
165   teammates at test time (Stone et al., 2010). Similar to ZSC, agents are evaluated based on their
166   performance with partners they have not trained with. However, unlike ZSC, ad-hoc teamplay does
167   not require these teammates to utilise the same training algorithm. In this work, we are specifically
168   interested in the scenario of ad-hoc teamplay with human and human-like agents.
169
170
171   3     R ELATED W ORK
172
173   While Hanabi was introduced as a benchmark encompassing both SP and ad-hoc teamplay (Bard
174   et al., 2019), significant progress has been primarily confined to SP, with methods like SPARTA
175   (Lerer et al., 2019) achieving near-perfect 24.61/25 points on average. However, agents trained in
176   SP often rely on specialised conventions, leading to poor generalisation when paired with novel
177
      teammates. Consequently, ad-hoc teamplay, especially with humans, presents a more demanding
      and unsolved challenge.
178
179   A central issue when evaluating agent abilities for ad-hoc coordination is the selection of test-time
180   policies. One approach to tackle this issue is to ensure the diversity of policies, thereby minimising
181   the possibility of favouring any specific agent. For example, Cui et al. (2023) introduce ADVER-
182   SITY that aims to produce highly skilled and reasonable policies that play according to diverse con-
183
      ventions. Another approach is to focus on a set of policies that hold intrinsic value, with the most
      natural choice being human policies. Coordinating with humans can be seen as a specialised form
184
      of ad-hoc team play, where the set of test policies comprises human strategies, which are inherently
185
      valuable due to their real-world relevance.
186
187   Ad-hoc human-AI coordination in Hanabi is explored in many previous works, with each presenting
188   a different methodology for acquiring human proxy agents and evaluating human-AI coordination
189
      capabilities (Hu et al., 2021; 2020; Cui et al., 2023). However, there is no standard approach for
      ad-hoc human-AI coordination evaluation in the existing literature. Therefore, our work addresses
190
      this gap and proposes the AH2AC2.
191
192   Recent works have empirically shown that augmenting imitation learning methods with regularised
193   RL creates stronger and more reliable policies. Multiple works have shown that regularised RL leads
194   to policies that are more compatible with existing social conventions of the human reference group
195
      (Jacob et al., 2022; Bakhtin et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2022). Recently, (Cornelisse & Vinitsky, 2024)
      extended these works to the driving setting, where the authors show that data-driven regularisation
196
      leads to human-compatible policies. Our work builds upon these findings to build strong and human-
197
      like human proxy agents that enable AH2AC2.
198
199   Nekoei et al. (2023) motivates the multi-agent RL community to shift focus towards the few-shot
200   adaptation problem, alongside the well-studied ZSC problem. The authors provide empirical ev-
201
      idence highlighting the limitations of current state-of-the-art ZSC algorithms in adapting to new
      partners. By introducing data limit settings, we aim to tie AH2AC2 and few-shot coordination.
202
      However, it is crucial to note a key distinction, in our setting, the few-shot-like effort is offline and
203
      one-sided. Human proxy agents do not adapt during test time, maintaining fixed behaviour. Instead,
204   we allow for the usage of a small sample of the human play data that we used for training human
205   proxy agents. This contrast underscores the unique challenges of coordinating with human-like
206   agents, where adaptation must occur primarily on the part of the AI agent, given the constraints of
207   human behaviour.

249   H UMAN P ROXIES
250
      We develop four human proxy agents for evaluating ad-hoc human-AI coordination: two for two-
251
      player Hanabi and two for three-player. These agents are trained using HDR-IPPO, a procedure
252   combining BC and regularised IPPO (de Witt et al., 2020). First, BC policies are trained on a
253   large-scale dataset of human gameplay - 101,096 two-player games and 46,525 three-player games.
254   Because BC alone struggles to generalise to unseen game states (Carroll et al., 2019; Hu et al.,
255   2022; Bakhtin et al., 2022; Cornelisse & Vinitsky, 2024), we then refine them through regularised
256   SP using IPPO. The regularisation ensures the final policies remain close to human play styles. The
257   four proxy agents use varied architectures, hyperparameters and seeds, to capture a range of human
258   playing strengths while maintaining human-like behaviour. We provide further details regarding
259   PPO and IPPO in Appendix A.1.
260   When training human proxy agents using HDR-IPPO, we learn a parameterised policy, denoted as
261   πθHP . As a first step, we train a BC policy, πθBC . Given Hanabi’s discrete action space, this translates
262   into a classification task. To capture the sequential nature of the observations, we adopt an LSTM-
263   based architecture (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). Our model takes a single player’s trajectory
264   as input and predicts an action for each timestep, conditioned on the AOH. Hence, for each player
265   i, we maintain a memory component, ϕit , which represents their observation history, τti . The hidden
266
      state is updated for the next timestep using a function hθi , ϕit+1 = hθi (oit , ϕit ).
267   We train the BC model by minimising the standard cross-entropy loss between the predicted action
268   distribution and the ground truth human actions. At each timestep, the agent acts greedily, selecting

      ACTION P REDICTION C HALLENGE
312
313   In addition to the primary human-AI ad-hoc coordination challenge, we introduce an action pre-
314   diction challenge. While human-compatible play does not necessarily imply strong action predic-
315   tion capabilities, accurate prediction of human actions provides further evidence of human-like be-
316   haviour. This challenge serves as a complementary evaluation metric, assessing how well an agent
317   can anticipate human decisions in Hanabi. The action prediction dataset consists of a held-out por-
318   tion of the human gameplay data used to train the human proxies. This data is distinct from both the
319   training and validation sets used in the data-limited sub-challenges and is not open-sourced.
320   We evaluate performance using the teacher-forced cross-entropy loss since we want to quantify
321   the difference between the predicted action distribution and the true human actions. Lower cross-
322   entropy loss indicates better alignment with human decision-making. Participating agents receive
323   a sequence of observations representing a game trajectory and must predict the action taken by the
      human player at each timestep.

360   5.1   VALIDATING H UMAN P ROXIES
361
362   Table 2 presents the SP performance of our final human proxy agents and their improvement over
363   the initial BC policies. The performance gains from regularised RL are particularly pronounced in
364   the three-player setting, where the BC policies trained on limited data frequently lose all their lives,
365   resulting in a large proportion of zero-score games and overall bad performance. For instance, in a
366   three-player SP evaluation, BC agents scored zero in 70.92% of games. With the help of regularised
367
      RL, human proxies score zero points only on 0.27% of the games. This highlights the robustness
      achieved through regularised SP RL. Moreover, we observe a larger number of perfect-score games
368
      for all agents, compared to BC counterparts. Next, we provide evidence that our agents produce
369
      human-like strategies, aligning with those observed in our dataset.
370
371
      Cross-Play Analysis with BC Policies. We know that BC policies closely adhere to human con-
372   ventions but lack generalisation capabilities. Therefore, we would expect the cross-play scores with
373   HDR-IPPO to be high (and even higher than BC SP). Also, median scores should be higher than

448   5.2   BASELINES
449
450
451   We consider a variety of baseline methods, informed by previous research (Hu et al., 2021; Carroll
452   et al., 2019). IPPO: Policy trained in SP, without human data. BC: Policy trained only on available
453   human data. Acts as a reference policy. HDR-IPPO: Policy builds upon the BC reference by
454   incorporating regularised RL. BR-BC (Carroll et al., 2019): Policy is trained by embedding the BC
455   policy in the environment, treating its actions as a part of environment dynamics during SP learning.
456   OBL (Hu et al., 2021): Strong approach for ZSC that doesn’t utilise any human data during training.
457   We use this agent only in the two-player setting since we do not have access to three-player weights.
458   Other-Play (OP) (Hu et al., 2020): A ZSC method that prevents agents from learning equivalent but
459
      mutually incompatible policies across independent training runs. OP accomplishes this by enforcing
      the equivariance of the policies under the symmetries of the Dec-POMDP, which must be provided
460
      as an input of the algorithm.
461
462   We adopt the IPPO configuration from Rutherford et al. (2023) and adapt the BR-BC training pro-
463   cedure from Carroll et al. (2019). These baseline approaches utilise feed-forward architectures. Our
464   BC and HDR-IPPO baselines, in contrast, employ an LSTM-based architecture. While based on
465
      the same backbone as the human proxy agents, we use different hyperparameters optimised for the
      data-limited challenge settings. Additionally, we utilise different random seeds than those used for
466
      training the human proxies.
467
468   For each of the BC, BR-BC, and HDR-IPPO baselines, we train with three different random seeds.
469   The best-performing agent, based on cross-entropy loss on the validation set, is selected for evalua-
470   tion. Notably, we observe minimal performance variance across different seeds on the validation set
471
      (see Appendix A.2). The IPPO baseline, intended to showcase the limitations of SP in ad-hoc coor-
      dination, is trained with a single seed. Finally, we utilise pre-trained weights for the OBL baseline
472
      and respective hyperparameters (Hu et al., 2021).
473
474   Table 4 presents the initial AH2AC2 leaderboard. OBL (L4) achieves the highest performance,
475   despite not using any human data at all. BR-BC performs significantly better than HDR-IPPO in
476   the two-player setting. In the three-player setting, HDR-IPPO achieves the best results and the gap
477
      compared to human-proxy SP is considerably larger. Unfortunately, there are no pre-trained weights
      for OBL available for this setting. The BC and IPPO baselines perform poorly in both the two- and
478
      three-player settings, as expected. Our findings validate the strength of OBL, which outperforms BC,
479
      BR-BC, and HDR-IPPO, despite not utilizing any human data. OP, which aims to break symmetries
480   to avoid arbitrary conventions, is insufficient for successful human-AI coordination. Furthermore,
481   our results reveal that current methods designed to leverage limited human data as an inductive bias
482   underperform compared to SOTA ZSC algorithms like OBL. This indicates a gap in the current
483   research landscape: existing approaches like BR-BC and HDR-IPPO are inadequate for effectively
484   integrating very small datasets from human populations to enhance coordination. Consequently,
485   there is a pressing need for new methods capable of efficiently utilizing limited human data to guide
      learning.

      6   C ONCLUSION
510
511
      We introduced the Ad-Hoc Human-AI Coordination Challenge (AH2AC2), evaluating human-AI
512
      ad-hoc team play in the context of the cooperative card game Hanabi. By leveraging a large-
513   scale human play dataset to generate human-like agent policies, we provide a meaningful evalu-
514   ation framework for assessing AI agents’ ability to coordinate with human partners. We released
515   a comprehensive set of baselines, including agents trained with and without human data, to pro-
516   vide a reference point for evaluating novel approaches. Our results highlight the inherent challenge
517   of human-AI coordination. We believe that AH2AC2 represents a significant step forward in the
518   field of human-AI coordination. By providing a standardised evaluation protocol, human proxy
519   agents, and a diverse set of baselines, we aim to foster further research and development. We in-
520   vite researchers and practitioners alike to participate, pushing the boundaries of what’s possible in
521
      human-AI collaboration.
522   Several open challenges and questions remain. We highlight some promising directions for future
523   research:
524
525   • Theoretical Analysis of HDR-IPPO: While our experiments and previous works provide strong
526
        empirical evidence for the effectiveness of regularised RL in generating human-like agents, a
        deeper theoretical understanding of the methodology is crucial.
527
528   • Generalisation of the benchmark We currently only cover 2 and 3 players as well as “standard”
529     Hanabi, ignoring the large set of possible variations of the game that are created e.g. by the
530
        “rainbow cards”. Extending the benchmark to cover those scenarios would be a great way to
        measure the generalisation ability of agentic systems.
531
532   • Direct Human-AI Play with Human Proxy Agents: The ultimate validation of our human proxy
533     agents requires direct human-AI play. Future work should involve conducting play experiments
534
        with human participants, comparing their experiences and performance when playing with human
        proxies versus playing with other humans.
535
536   • Extension to Agentic Large Language Models (LLMs): The conventions used in our dataset
537     are documented using human language. This opens up the possibility of extending our benchmark
538
        to include agentic LLMs as baselines. Excitingly, in combination with our human proxies Hanabi
        turns into a fantastic benchmark for the theory of mind and human coordination of agentic LLMs
539
        in complex partially observable settings.

       A.7   HDR-IPPO: A BLATION S TUDY
1081
1082   We conduct an ablation study to investigate the impact of the human data regularisation term on the
1083   performance and behaviour of agents trained with the HDR-IPPO. By systematically varying the
1084   strength of the regularisation, we aim to gain insights into its role in guiding policy learning towards
1085   strategies learned during BC (in this case, human-like strategies).
1086
1087   M ETHODOLOGY
1088
       We focus on the two-player setting, where BC has demonstrated strong performance as a baseline.
1089
       Due to the computational and time constraints associated with training HDR-IPPO agents, it was not
1090   feasible to extend this study to the three-player setting. To systematically analyse the effect of the KL
1091   regularisation term introduced in the HDR-IPPO algorithm, we adopt the following methodology:
1092
1093         1. We train a new BC policy that serves as both a starting point for subsequent HDR-IPPO
1094            training and a baseline for comparison in this ablation study.
1095         2. From the baseline BC policy, we train multiple HDR-IPPO agents. These agents share
1096            identical architectures, hyperparameters, and training procedures, with the sole exception
1097            of the human data regularisation weight, λ. We vary the weight of regularisation term
1098            across a range of values, from no regularisation (λ = 0.00) to very high regularisation
1099            (λ = 0.70) to comprehensively investigate the effects of this term during both training and
1100            evaluation. The final policy weights for each agent are stored for subsequent analysis.
1101         3. We evaluate, analyse and compare the trained HDR-IPPO agents and the baseline BC pol-
1102            icy. Precisely, we:
1103             (a) Assess the SP performance of each agent to understand how the strength of KL regu-
1104                 larisation influences its ability to play Hanabi effectively on its own.
1105             (b) Evaluate the cross-play performance of each HDR-IPPO agent when paired with the
1106                 baseline BC policy. Higher scores in this setting indicate that the HDR-IPPO agent
1107                 has learned strategies compatible with the human-like conventions exhibited by the
1108                 BC agent.
1109             (c) Conduct cross-play evaluations among different HDR-IPPO agents to identify poten-
1110                 tial coordination patterns.
1111             (d) Evaluate each agent on the held-out validation and test sets of human data. Good per-
1112                 formance on this data indicates that the agent has effectively retained the conventions
1113                 observed in the human demonstrations.
1114             (e) Plot the KL divergence between the action distributions of each HDR-IPPO agent and
1115                 the baseline BC policy throughout the training process. This visualisation allows us
1116                 to identify potential convergence or divergence patterns for the KL divergence term.
