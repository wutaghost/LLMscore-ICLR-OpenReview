009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Phylogenetic tree inference, crucial for understanding species evolution, presents
012            challenges in jointly optimizing continuous branch lengths and discrete tree topolo-
013            gies. Traditional Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods, though widely adopted,
014            suffer from slow convergence and high computational costs. Deep learning meth-
015            ods have introduced more scalable solutions but still face limitations. Bayesian
016            generative models struggle with computational complexity, autoregressive models
017            are constrained by predefined species orders, and generative flow networks still fail
018
               to fully leverage evolutionary signals from genomic sequences. In this paper, we
               introduce MDTree, a novel framework that redefines phylogenetic tree generation
019
               from the perspective of dynamically learning node orders based on biological priors
020
               embedded in genomic sequences. By leveraging a Dynamic Ordering Network to
021            learn evolutionarily meaningful node orders, MDTree autoregressively positions
022            nodes to construct biologically coherent trees. To further push its limits, we propose
023            a dynamic masking mechanism that accelerates tree generation through parallel
024            node processing. Extensive experiments show that MDTree outperforms existing
025            methods on standard phylogenetic benchmarks, offering biologically interpretable
026            and computationally efficient solutions for tree generation.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Phylogenetic trees serve as essential tools for deciphering evolutionary relationships among species,
032   enabling researchers to trace lineages from common ancestors to present-day organisms through
033
      DNA or protein sequences (Brocchieri, 2001; Munjal et al., 2019). Their applications permeate
      diverse fields such as taxonomy, evolutionary biology, and medicine, where they unlock pivotal
034
      insights into species origins, decode the genetic blueprints behind biodiversity, and map the intricate
035
      evolutionary pathways of pathogens and cancer cells (Hugenholtz et al., 2021; Hosner et al., 2016),
036   driving transformative discoveries in adaptation and survival mechanisms.
037
038   Traditional statistical approaches like Maximum Likelihood Estimation (Izquierdo-Carrasco et al.,
039
      2011; Huelsenbeck et al., 2001) and Bayesian Inference (Zhang & Matsen IV, 2018a; Wang et al.,
      2020) via Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) have long been the cornerstone of phylogenetic
040
      inference. However, as species numbers grow, these methods face significant computational hurdles
041
      due to the exponential growth in possible tree topologies—(2N − 5)!! for unrooted bifurcating
042   trees—and the complexity of optimizing both continuous branch lengths and discrete tree structures.
043
044   Leveraging deep learning, breakthroughs in phylogenetic inference have burst onto the scene, address-
045
      ing long-standing computational challenges in the field (Nesterenko et al., 2022; Smith & Hahn, 2023;
      Tang et al., 2024). Research efforts primarily follow two main directions: representation learning on
046
      known tree structures and generative models. The former, exemplified by VBPI-GNN (Zhang, 2023),
047
      optimizes performance based on predefined topologies but struggles when the topology is unknown
048   and both topology and branch lengths must be inferred. These methods also underutilize evolutionary
049   information from biological sequences, impacting accuracy and flexibility (Penny, 2004). On the
050   other hand, generative models, which infer tree structures directly from data, can be further divided
051   into three types: Bayesian generative models (e.g., Geophy (Mimori & Hamada, 2024)) leverage
052   probabilistic frameworks to capture uncertainty but are computationally intensive; autoregressive
053   models (e.g., ARTree (Xie & Zhang, 2024)) sequentially add nodes, offering flexibility yet relying
      on predefined orders that overlook true evolutionary relationships, while their stepwise nature leads

123   2        R ELATED W ORKS
124
125   Phylogenetic inference methods are generally classified into traditional and deep learning-based
126   approaches, further divided into graph structure generation and graph representation models. For a
127   detailed background, refer to Appendix A, and for related work, refer to Appendix B.
128
      Traditional Methods rely on predefined evolutionary models and statistical inference. Graph
129
      Structure Generation Models: MrBayes (Ronquist et al., 2012) utilizes Bayesian inference to generate
130   trees but struggles with high-dimensional combinatorial spaces, requiring large sample sizes for
131   accuracy. VaiPhy (Koptagel et al., 2022) combines SLANTIS sampling strategy (Diaconis, 2019) with
132   biological models (e.g., JC model (Munro, 2012)) to estimate branch lengths and generate accurate
133   tree structures. Graph Structure Representation Models: SBN (Zhang & Matsen IV, 2018a) models
134   the probability distribution of tree topologies from existing trees, focusing on subsplit relationships
135   without directly estimating branch lengths. VBPI (Zhang & Matsen IV, 2018b) extends SBNs to
136   estimate posterior distributions and optimize branch lengths through variational inference.
137   Deep Learning-based Methods offer more flexible and scalable solutions. Graph Structure Genera-
138   tion Models: (1) Bayesian Generative Models like GeoPhy (Mimori & Hamada, 2024) learn latent
139   tree representations to generate diverse topologies. (2) Autoregressive Models such as ARTree (Xie
140   & Zhang, 2024) sequentially generate trees, well-suited for hierarchical data. (3) Generative Flow
141   Networks like PhyloGFN (Zhou et al., 2023a) optimize tree generation paths using Markov decision
142   processes. Graph Structure Representation Models: VBPI-GNN (Zhang, 2023) combines SBNs with
143   variational inference to optimize topology and branch lengths.

      Formulation. Given N species sequences S = {si }N         i=1 and a corresponding set of genomic
148
      representations G = {gi }Ni=1 , we can model  the phylogenetic tree as a graph GT = (VT , ET ), where
149
      each node vi ∈ VT represents a species si ∈ S and each edge eij ∈ ET reflects the evolutionary
150   relationship between species i and j. Our goal is to autoregressively generate the unrooted binary
151   tree topology τ and its branch lengths Bτ , representing evolutionary distances. We reformulate the
152   phylogenetic inference problem as the DART task, aiming to learn a mapping F : S → (τ, Bτ )
153   that adjusts node orders and insertion positions based on genomic representations, overcoming the
154   limitations of fixed node orders (Xie & Zhang, 2024).
155
      Framework. To optimize node addition orders in phylogenetic tree generation, we propose MDTree
156   as shown in Fig. 2, leveraging biological priors embedded in genomic sequences. A Genomic LM
157   (e.g., DNABERT2 (Zhou et al., 2023b)) encodes each sequence si into gi , which serve as inputs for
158   the DON q(·) to learn evolutionarily meaningful orders using an absorbing diffusion model (Austin
159   et al., 2021). The optimized order guides the autoregressive generation process, while a dynamic
160   masking mechanism facilitates parallel processing. Finally, branch lengths are refined via a dual-
161   pass traversal, ensuring biological coherence and accuracy in the generated tree to serve multiple
      downstream tasks.

164   As discussed, the biologically relevant node addition order is crucial for phylogenetic inference, as
165   species with closer ancestry should be prioritized (Penny, 2004; Gregory, 2008). Despite evidence
166   of robustness across different taxa orders (Xie & Zhang, 2024), the influence of node orders on
167   phylogenetic accuracy has not been thoroughly examined, which is the focus of our work. To
168   this end, we propose DON to learn optimal node orders by leveraging both genomic information
169   and evolutionary relationships between species. The process begins by using genomic LMs (e.g.,
170
      DNABERT2 (Zhou et al., 2023b)) to encode each species sequence si into representations gi ,

171
      ancestry will have more similar genomic features (Franceschi et al., 2019; Delsuc et al., 2005). These
172
      representations serve as inputs to a Relational Graph Convolutional Network (RGCN) (Schlichtkrull

257   3.4   MDT REE I NFERENCE FOR T REE T OPOLOGY AND B RANCH L ENGTH E STIMATION

259   As mentioned, phylogenetic inference involves optimizing both continuous branch lengths and
260
      discrete tree topologies. To assess MDTree’s performance, we design two tasks following (Zhang,
      2023; Xie & Zhang, 2024): Tree Topology Density Estimation (TDE) optimizes the tree topology by
261
      maximizing the marginal log-likelihood (MLL), while Variational Bayesian Phylogenetic Inference
262
      (VBPI) approximates the joint posterior distribution of tree topology and branch lengths using VI via
263   the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO).
264
265   Task1: Tree Topology Estimation for TDE. TDE assesses the model’s ability to estimate tree
266
      topologies by maximizing the log-likelihood of known structures from MrBayes. To further validate
      model performance, we compare the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the model-generated
267
      tree topology distribution qθ (τ ) and the true posterior p(τ ).
268
269   Task2: Joint Optimization of Topology and Branch Lengths for VBPI. VBPI extends TDE by
      jointly optimizing tree topology and branch lengths using VI. Unlike TDE, VBPI does not rely

      4.1     E XPERIMENT S ETUP

326   Evaluation Tasks and Datasets. We assess MDTree’s performance on two key tasks: TDE, which
327   focuses on optimizing tree topologies with MLL metric, and VBPI, where tree topologies and branch
328   lengths are jointly inferred, using ELBO and MLL. These evaluations span eight diverse benchmark

422
423
      To evaluate the parsimony of tree structures generated by the model, we follow established method-
      ologies (Zhou et al., 2023a), minimizing the genetic mutations required to infer the optimal tree.
424
      The parsimony score evaluates how well the generated tree adheres to the principle of minimizing
425
      evolutionary changes, where fewer mutations are assumed to explain the observed genetic data
426   better. We compare the results against the most parsimonious tree identified by the traditional
427   PAUP* tool (Swofford, 1998). The parsimony score in Fig. 5 denotes the minimum mutations of
428   genetic changes needed to account for the evolutionary relationships in the data. Since scores are
429   plotted as negative values, lower scores indicate more complex trees and, consequently, poorer model
430   performance. MDTree and ARTree achieved higher scores (approaching -4000) in fewer steps,
431   reflecting simpler and more parsimonious trees. In contrast, PhyloGFN exhibited early fluctuations
      and ultimately stabilized around -5000, indicating suboptimal performance compared to others.

      Furthermore, the Top Frequency metric remains notably low, further reinforcing the diversity and

530   5      C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
531
532
      Contributions. In this work, we introduce MDTree, a novel framework redefining phylogenetic
533
      tree generation as a Dynamic Autoregressive Tree Generation task. MDTree leverages a Dynamic
534   Ordering Network to learn biologically informed node orders from genomic sequences, overcoming
535   fixed or random order limitations. It integrates GNNs and Language Models to capture complex
536   topologies, while a Dynamic Masking Mechanism enables parallel node processing, improving
537   efficiency. Experiments show MDTree achieves SOTA performance on phylogenetic benchmarks.
538   Limitations and Future Work: MDTree has yet to be applied to other sequence types, such as
539   protein sequences. Future work will explore multimodal approaches and scaling the model for
      complex evolutionary scenarios. Additional details are in Appendix F.

      A     BACKGROUND
760
      Bayesian Methods Traditional statistical approaches like Maximum Likelihood Estimation
761
      (MLE) (Izquierdo-Carrasco et al., 2011; Solı́s-Lemus & Ané, 2016) and Bayesian Inference via
762
      Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) (Zhang et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020) have been central to
763   phylogenetic inference. However, as species numbers increase, these methods face severe computa-
764   tional limitations. The exponential growth in tree topologies—(2N − 5)!! for unrooted bifurcating
765   trees—leads to combinatorial complexity, making the simultaneous optimization of continuous branch
766   lengths and discrete tree structures infeasible for large datasets. Additionally, MCMC-based methods,
767   in particular, struggle with the multimodal nature of posterior distributions in high-dimensional tree
768   spaces (Tjelmeland & Hegstad, 2001). Their reliance on local proposal mechanisms limits their
769   ability to transition between distant peaks, leading to slow convergence and sampling inefficien-
770   cies (Whidden & Matsen IV, 2015; Zhang & Matsen IV, 2018b). Recent advancements, such as
771
      Variational Inference approaches, aim to improve efficiency but often involve simplifying assumptions
      that compromise the robustness of marginal likelihood estimation.
772
773
      Phylogenetic Posterior and Variational Inference (VI) Variational Autoencoders (VAE) Kingma
774
      & Welling (2013) is a deep generative model that learns input data distribution by encoding it into a
775
      latent space. In this process, the encoder maps each input x to a latent space defined by parameters:
776   mean µ and variance σ. Latent variables z are then sampled from this distribution for data generation.
777
778   VI is employed within VAE to handle the computational challenges of estimating marginal likelihoods
779
      of observed data. This involves computing the log of the marginal likelihood:
780                                                  XN      Z
781                                max log pθ (X) =      log    pθ (X, Z)dz                           (17)
                                      θ                           Z
782                                                      i=1

783   where pθ (X, Z) represents the joint distribution of the observable data x e.g. a Gaussian distribution,
784   N (x|µ, σ) and its latent encoding Z under the model parameter θ.
785
      Since the direct estimation of marginal likelihoods is typically infeasible, VI introduces a variational
786   distribution qϕ (z|x) to approximate the true posterior. The goal of VI is to maximize the Evidence
787   Lower Bound (ELBO), formulated as:
788
789
                              ELBO = Eqϕ (z|x) [log pθ (x|z)] − KL[qϕ (z|x)||p(z)]                          (18)
790   The first term is the reconstruction log-likelihood, log pθ (x|z) can be considered as a decoder, i.e., the
791   log-likelihood between the reconstructed data and the original data given the potential representation.
792   The second term, the KL divergence, quantifies the difference between the variational posterior
793   qϕ (z|x) and the latent prior p(z). Usually, VAE utilizes a reparameterization trick for gradient
794   backpropagation through non-differentiable sampling operations. Once trained, VAEs can generate
795   new data by sampling directly from the latent space and processing it through the decoder.
796
797   B     R ELATED W ORK
798
799   Phylogenetic inference methods can be broadly categorized into two major classes: traditional
800   methods and deep learning-based methods. Each class can be further divided into graph structure
801   generation and representation models. In this section, we review these approaches in detail.
802
803   B.1    T RADITIONAL M ETHODS
804
805   Traditional phylogenetic inference methods primarily rely on predefined evolutionary models and
806
      statistical inference techniques. These methods typically assume specific evolutionary processes and
      use statistical approaches to search and optimize within a given tree structure space. They can be
807
      classified into graph structure generation and representation models.
808
809   Graph Structure Generation Models: MrBayes Ronquist et al. (2012) generates phylogenetic
      trees using Bayesian inference, estimating posterior probabilities based on sample relative frequency


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      (SRF). However, the high-dimensional combinatorial space poses accuracy challenges, particularly
811   for low-probability trees, requiring large sample sizes for stability. VaiPhy Koptagel et al. (2022)
812   introduces the SLANTIS sampling strategy Diaconis (2019) to generate tree structures by learning
813   phylogenetic tree topologies. This approach combines basic biological models, such as the JC model,
814   to estimate branch lengths, producing more accurate tree structures.
815
      Graph Structure Representation Models: SBN (Structured Bayesian Networks) Zhang & Mat-
816
      sen IV (2018a) focuses on learning the probability distribution of tree topologies from existing
817   phylogenetic trees. By modeling subsplit relationships within a given set of trees, SBN captures
818   the probabilistic structure of the entire tree space without directly estimating branch lengths. VBPI
819   (Variational Bayesian Phylogenetic Inference) Zhang & Matsen IV (2018b) builds on the tree topol-
820   ogy probability distributions provided by SBN, using variational inference to estimate the posterior
821   distribution of tree structures. This method further optimizes branch lengths, offering a precise
822   approximation of the posterior distribution.
823   While traditional methods provide a solid theoretical foundation, they often struggle with the com-
824   plexity of high-dimensional data and intricate evolutionary relationships. The emergence of deep
825   learning has introduced new approaches to address these challenges.
826
827
      B.2   D EEP L EARNING -BASED M ETHODS
828
829   In recent years, deep learning techniques have demonstrated significant potential in phylogenetic
830   inference, especially when dealing with complex, high-dimensional genomic data. These methods
831   excel in generating and representing phylogenetic trees by learning latent representations or structural
832   features from the data. They can be categorized into graph structure generation and representation
833   models.
834
      Graph Structure Generation Models:
835
836          • Bayesian Generative Models (e.g., VAE): These models learn latent representations of
837            graphs using variational inference, from which new tree structures can be sampled. Geo-
838            Phy Mimori & Hamada (2024) exemplifies this approach by leveraging VAE to model the
839            latent space of phylogenetic trees, generating diverse structures that accommodate complex
840            evolutionary histories.
841
             • Autoregressive Models: Autoregressive models generate tree structures incrementally,
842            making them suitable for tasks with well-defined sequences or hierarchies. ARTree Xie &
843            Zhang (2024) employs a graph autoregressive model to generate detailed topologies, with
844            branch lengths independently estimated using classical evolutionary models.
845
             • Diffusion Models: Although diffusion models have not been widely applied in phylogenetic
846
               tree generation, our study integrates diffusion models with autoregressive models to generate
847
               the node addition order, enhancing the accuracy of tree structures. This demonstrates the
848            potential of diffusion models in high-quality phylogenetic inference.
849
850          • Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets): As illustrated by PhyloGFN Zhou et al. (2023a),
851            GFlowNets Hu et al. (2023) combined with Markov decision processes optimize the genera-
852
               tion path, progressively constructing complex phylogenetic tree structures.
853
      Graph Structure Representation Models: VBPI-GNN Zhang (2023) leverages pre-generated
854
      candidate tree structures and SBN-provided tree topology probability distributions, combined with
855   variational inference, to optimize branch lengths and tree topologies, ultimately providing a precise
856   approximation of the posterior distribution.

900   We focus on the most challenging aspect of the phylogenetic tree inference task: the joint learning of

902   independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) exponential prior (Exp(10)) for the branch lengths. We
903
      evaluate all methods across eight real datasets (DS1-8) frequently used to benchmark phylogenetic
      tree inference methods. These datasets include sequences from 27 to 64 eukaryote species, each
904
      comprising 378 to 2520 sites. For our Monte Carlo simulations, we select K = 2 samples and apply
905
      an annealed unnormalized posterior during each i-th iteration, where λn = min{1.0, 0.001 + i/H}
906   acts as the inverse temperature. This parameter starts at 0.001 and gradually increases to 1 over H
907   iterations, effectively simulating a cooling schedule commonly used in annealing algorithms, similar
908   to the approach in Zhang & Matsen IV (2018a), with an initial temperature of 0.001, which gradually
909   decreases over 100,000 steps.
910

911
      million Monte Carlo samples, employing K samples at each training step. The stepping-stone (SS)
912   algorithm Xie et al. (2011) in MrBayes is viewed as the gold-standard value. All models were

914   MLL estimate is derived by sampling the importance of 1000 samples, with the larger mean value
915   being better. The learning rate is initially set to 1e-4 and is reduced by 0.75 every 200,000 training
916   steps. Momentum is set at 0.9 to prevent the optimization process from becoming trapped in local

993    F    L IMITATION
994
995    While MDTree demonstrates significant advances in phylogenetic inference, including improved
996    accuracy and efficiency in tree structure generation, several limitations remain. First, MDTree
997    leverages pretrained genomic language models to initialize sequence representations, which enhances
998    performance. However, alternative representations, such as one-hot encoding, can also be used,
999    albeit with reduced accuracy and efficiency. Additionally, MDTree has so far been validated only on
1000   genomic sequences and has not been extended to other sequence types, such as protein sequences
1001
       or non-biological data. Finally, while MDTree improves computational efficiency, its performance
       on extremely large-scale datasets or sequences with complex multimodal dependencies, such as
1002
       integrating genomic and proteomic data, remains unexplored.
