009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            To reduce reliance on labeled data, learning with noisy labels (LNL) has garnered
012            increasing attention. However, most existing works primarily assume that noisy
013            datasets are dominated by closed-set noise, where the true labels of noisy samples
014            come from another known category, thereby overlooking the widespread presence
015            of open-set noise—where the true labels may not belong to any known category. In
016
               this paper, we refine the LNL problem by explicitly accounting for the presence of
               open-set noise. We theoretically analyze and compare the impacts of open-set and
017
               closed-set noise, as well as the differences between various open-set noise modes.
018
               Additionally, we examine a common open-set noise detection mechanism based on
019            prediction entropy. To empirically validate our theoretical insights, we construct
020            two open-set noisy datasets—CIFAR100-O and ImageNet-O—and introduce a
021            novel open-set test set for the widely used real-world noisy dataset, WebVision. Our
022            findings indicate that open-set noise exhibits distinct qualitative and quantitative
023            characteristics, underscoring the need for further exploration into how models can
024            be fairly and comprehensively evaluated under such conditions.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      In recent years, the remarkable success of machine learning has largely relied on the assumption that
029
      data labels are accurate and noise-free. However, in real-world scenarios, label noise—arising from
030   factors such as annotation errors and label ambiguity—is pervasive, posing significant challenges to
031   model performance and generalization. To address this issue, various approaches have been proposed
032   for learning with noisy labels (LNL), including noise transition matrix estimation (Goldberger and
033   Ben-Reuven, 2017; Xia et al., 2019; 2022), noisy label correction (Song et al., 2019; Cascante-Bonilla
034   et al., 2021), robust loss functions (Ghosh et al., 2017; Zhang and Sabuncu, 2018; Wang et al., 2019),
035   and, more recently, dominant sample selection-based methods (Han et al., 2018; Arazo et al., 2019;
036   Li et al., 2020; Xia et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2022).
037   Most current efforts primarily focus on closed-set noise, where the true labels of noisy samples belong
038   to another known class. This includes common noise models such as symmetric noise, where sample
039   labels are randomly flipped to any other known class with a certain probability, and asymmetric
040   noise, where label confusion is influenced by class similarity (e.g., ’cat’ is more likely to be confused
041   with ’dog’ than with ’airplane’). Recent advances have also explored instance-dependent noise
042   models (Chen et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022), where label confusion is directly influenced by the
043   semantics of individual instances.
044   However, unlike the extensive research on closed-set noise, there is significantly less focus on open-
045   set noise, where the true labels of noisy samples do not belong to any known category. This gap
046   is particularly noteworthy given that one of the primary motivations for learning with noisy labels
047   is to manage datasets collected through web crawling. By examining one of the most commonly
048   used benchmarks, the WebVision dataset (Li et al., 2017), we confirm the prevalence of open-set
049   noise (fig. 1).
050   In fact, the "open-world" assumption, which involves open-set samples, has received considerable
051   attention in other weakly supervised learning problems, such as open-set recognition and outlier
052   detection. However, it remains underexplored in the context of learning with noisy labels. To address
053   this gap, this paper focuses on a comprehensive theoretical analysis of open-set noise. The main
      findings are outlined as follows:

077   2    R ELATED WORKS
078
079   In this section, we provide a brief overview of mainstream LNL methods, relevant research connected
080   to this work, and the key motivations derived from them. Briefly speaking, methods for learning with
081   noisy labels can be roughly categorized into two main types.
082
083   Statistical-consistent methods The first type is often referred to as statistical-consistent methods,
084   such as estimating noise transition matrix (Chen et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2019;
085   Goldberger and Ben-Reuven, 2017; Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) or designing robust loss
086
      functions (Zhang and Sabuncu, 2018; Wang et al., 2019; Ghosh et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2023;
      Mao et al., 2023; Patel and Sastry, 2023a; Wilton and Ye, 2024), aiming to achieve theoretically
087
      risk-consistent or probabilistically-consistent models. However, most of these works often assume
088
      an ideal scenario where the model can learn to fit the sampled distribution well, overlooking the
089   over-fitting issues arising from excessive model capacity and insufficient data in practical situations.
090   In this paper, we introduce the the concept of complete noise transition matrix that accounts for
091   open-set noise and conduct theoretical analyses and experimental validations for both ideal case and
092   over-fitting case, namely the fitted case and overfitted case.
093
094   Statistical-approximate methods The second type, often referred to as statistical-approximate
095   methods, includes dominant sample selection-based approaches that incorporate various regularization
096   terms and off-the-shelf techniques such as semi-supervised learning and model co-training to achieve
097   state-of-the-art performance. Most sample selection methods rely on the model’s current predictions,
098   such as the popular ‘small loss’ mechanism (Arazo et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020; Han et al., 2018; Yu
099
      et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2018), and various improved variants upon it (Song et al., 2019; Malach
      and Shalev-Shwartz, 2017; Yi and Wu, 2019; Xia et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
100
      2022; Cordeiro et al., 2021b; Patel and Sastry, 2023b). Some other works attempt to use feature
101
      representations for sample selection. Wu et al. (2020) and Wu et al. (2021) try to construct a graph
102   and identify clean samples through connected subgraphs, while Feng et al. (2022) and Ortego et al.
103   (2021) suggest directly using kNN in feature space to mitigate the impact of noisy labels. Moreover,
104   as hybrid methods, sample selection approaches often involve additional auxiliary techniques, such
105   as model co-training (Han et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2024; Sun et al.,
106   2021; Cordeiro et al., 2021a), semi-supervised learning (Li et al., 2020; Arazo et al., 2019), and
107   contrastive learning (Li et al., 2022; Ortego et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023; Zheltonozhskii et al.,
      2021; Ghosh and Lan, 2021; Karim et al., 2022).

108
      Exploration of open-set noise Research on open-set noise remains relatively limited. Wang et al.
109   (2018) use the Local Outlier Factor algorithm to detect open-set noise in the feature space, while
110   Wu et al. (2021) propose identifying open-set noise through subgraph connectivity. Both Sachdeva
111   et al. (2021) and Albert et al. (2022) focus on entropy-related dynamics to identify open-set noise. In
112   contrast, Feng et al. (2022) avoid explicitly identifying open-set noise and instead prevent relabeling or
113   including it in the training process. More closely related to our work, Xia et al. (2022) also investigate
114   noise transition matrices that account for open-set noise but assume all open-set noise belongs to a
115   single meta-class. In this paper, we extend this idea by considering that open-set noise may originate
116   from multiple classes. Based on this premise, we analyze two distinct modes of open-set noise. Wei
117
      et al. (2021) suggest leveraging open-set noise to mitigate the impact of closed-set noise, as it helps
      reduce overfitting. However, our focus is on providing a thorough theoretical analysis of the effects
118
      of different noise modes, including open-set noise versus closed-set noise, as well as comparisons
119
      between different types of open-set noise.

      3.4    R ETHINKING OPEN - SET NOISE DETECTION
389
390   In addition to examining the impact of various types of open-set label noise on model generalization,
391   we also assess the performance of current learning with noisy labels (LNL) methods when confronted
392   with different types of open-set noise. Most current LNL methods primarily address closed-set
393   noise, while the few sample selection approaches that target open-set noise generally focus on ‘easy’
394   open-set noise only. In this section, we evaluate the effectiveness of current methods in handling
395   different forms of open-set noise, including the newly introduced ‘hard’ open-set noise.
396   We specifically focus on an entropy-based open-set noise detection mechanism, which has been
397   widely applied in prior out-of-distribution (OOD) detection works (Chan et al., 2021; Xing et al.,
398   2024). Within the sample selection framework, several methods (Albert et al., 2022; Sachdeva et al.,
399   2021) have sought to extend closed-set noise detection techniques to identify open-set noise based on
400   similar principles. These methods are generally founded on the empirical observation that samples
401   with less confident and more averaged predictions often correspond to open-set instances, as indicated
402   by high entropy in the model’s predictions.
403   Similar to general sample selection methods, entropy-based open-set noise detection also occurs
404   in the early stages after the model has undergone a few epochs training, commonly referred to as
405   model warm-up training. At this point, the model is expected to have learned meaningful information
406   while avoiding overfitting. In this context, we assume that the current model used for open-set noise
407   detection is consistent with the fitted case described earlier.
408   Specifically, for a given sample x, we consider three variants for comparison: the original sample
409   without noise transition, treated as a clean sample ; the ‘hard’ open-set noise sample, following the
410   T hard open-set noise mode; and the ‘easy’ open-set noise sample, following the T easy open-set
411   noise mode. We denote the prediction entropy values corresponding to these three variants as Heasy ,
412   Hhard , and Hclean , respectively, and we have4 :

516   5     C ONCLUSIONS
517
518   This paper investigates the impact of open-set label noise on model performance. Although the "open
519   world" setting, involving open-set samples, has been widely discussed in other weakly supervised
520   learning contexts, its application in learning with noisy labels (LNL) remains underexplored. In
521   response, we revisit the LNL problem, specifically examining the effects of open-set noise in
522   comparison to closed-set noise, as well as the differences among various types of open-set noise
523   in terms of classification performance. We find that open-set noise has a smaller impact on model
524   classification performance compared to common closed-set noise, and different modes of open-set
525
      noise exhibit notable differences. Recognizing the limitations of existing evaluation frameworks
      in handling open-set noise, we explore the out-of-distribution (OOD) detection task to address
526
      shortcomings in model assessment and conduct preliminary experiments.
527
528   Additionally, we examine a common mechanism for detecting open-set noise based on prediction
529   entropy, finding that it may only be effective for ‘easy’ open-set noise. Overall, our theoretical and
530   empirical findings highlight the need for further investigation into open-set noise and its intricate
531
      effects on model performance.

706   Previous works involving open-set noise also attempted to build synthetic noisy datasets, typi-
707   cally treating different datasets as open-set noise for each other to construct synthetic noisy data-
708   set (Sachdeva et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021). In this scenario, potential domain gaps could affect a
709   focused analysis of open-set noise. In this work, we propose selecting inlier/outlier classes from
710   the same dataset to avoid this issue. Besides, in previous works, the consideration of open-set noise
711   modes often focused on random flipping from outlier classes to all possible inlier classes, which
712   is indeed the ‘easy’ open-set noise adopted in this paper. However, our theoretical analysis and
713
      experimental findings demonstrate that ‘easy’ open-set noise and ‘hard’ open-set noise exhibit distinct
      characteristics. Therefore, relying solely on experiments with ‘easy’ open-set noise is insufficient,
714
      emphasizing the necessity to explore and understand the complexities associated with different types
715
      of open-set noise.

      CIFAR100-O For the original CIFAR100 dataset, in addition to the commonly-used 100 fine
718
      classes, there exist 20 coarse classes each consisting of 5 fine classes. To build CIFAR100-O, we
719

      A.3   OOD DETECTION EVALUATION PROTOCOL
780
781   We employ the maximum softmax probability as proposed by Hendrycks and Gimpel (2016) for
782   out-of-distribution (OOD) detection. Specifically, let the trained model f output a softmax vector
783   pi for each sample xi . A threshold value t, ranging between 0 and 1, is selected. For evaluation,
784   we assign binary labels to indicate whether a sample belongs to a known class (closed-set) or an
785   unknown class (open-set), transforming the OOD detection task into a binary classification problem.
786
      Samples with a maximum softmax value pmaxi    below the threshold are considered potential open-set
      examples, as a low maximum value suggests that the model has low confidence in assigning the
787
      sample to any specific class.

       Zeroshot open-set noise detection with CLIP Due to its multi-modality nature, we further try to
1445   utilize CLIP for zero-shot open-set noise detection. Specifically, we design a simple algorithm to
1446   compute an intuitive indicator value for identifying open-set noise. For each sample x with annotated
1447   label y,
1448
       1. Generate Text Prompts: For the target class y, we create a text prmopt: "A photo of class y.". For
1449
       non-target classes, we consider a set of prompts: ["A photo of class i." for i ∈ Ly ]. Here, we denote
1450
       as Ly the possible source classes to which the sample x may belong. Practically, Ly can be a broad
1451   set of classes, such as the 1K classes from ImageNet-1K dataset, or it can be manully defined to
1452   include semanticlly-challenging classes; for example, [’tiger’, ’cheetah’] for class ’cat’. In below
1453   experiments, we default to the first option.
1454
       2. Calculate Similarities: We first compute similarity to the target class: Sy = sim(vx , ty ). Here,
1455
       vx and ty denotes the visual and textual representation, respetively. We also compute a maximum
1456
       similarities to non-target classes: Sother = max{sim(vx , ti ) | i ∈ Ly }.
1457
       3. Compute the Difference: Dx = Sy − Sother .

1489   In this section, we provide additional examples of open-set noise within the ‘Tench’ class of the
1490   WebVision dataset. By tracing the origins of web pages hosting these open-set noise images, we
1491   found that the term "Tench" or related keywords frequently appeared on these pages. We attribute
1492   this to the data collection process on the web, where images were inadvertently included during
1493   keyword searches and web crawling due to the presence of relevant keywords in image descriptions
1494   or surrounding text, such as people with "Tench" in their name or related fishing tools.
1495   As noted earlier, the prevailing belief in the current LNL community is that real-world noise pre-
1496   dominantly stems from semantic similarity. Consequently, recent research has largely focused on
1497   instance-dependent noise and its theoretical analysis. However, our findings suggest that, in real-
1498   world scenarios—particularly in web-crawled datasets—noise may not always be linked to semantics,
1499   but may also arise from latent high-dimensional factors, such as accompanying text. Addressing
1500   different types of real-world noise also warrants increased attention and further exploration.
