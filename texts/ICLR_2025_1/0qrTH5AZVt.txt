009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            With the rapid advancements of various machine learning models, there is a sig-
012            nificant demand for model-agnostic explanation techniques, which can explain
013            these models across different architectures. Mainstream model-agnostic explana-
014            tion techniques generate local explanations based on basic features (e.g., words
015            for text models and (super-)pixels for image models). However, these explana-
016
               tions often do not align with the decision-making processes of the target models
               and end-users, resulting in explanations that are unfaithful and difficult for users
017
               to understand. On the other hand, concept-based techniques provide explanations
018
               based on high-level features (e.g., topics for text models and objects for image
019            models), but most are model-specific or require additional pre-defined external
020            concept knowledge. To address this limitation, we propose ConLUX, a general
021            framework to provide concept-based local explanations for any machine learning
022            models. Our key insight is that we can automatically extract high-level concepts
023            from large pre-trained models, and uniformly extend existing local model-agnostic
024            techniques to provide unified concept-based explanations. We have instantiated
025            ConLUX on four different types of explanation techniques: LIME, Kernel SHAP,
026            Anchor, and LORE, and applied these techniques to text and image models. Our
027
               evaluation results demonstrate that 1) compared to the vanilla versions, ConLUX
               offers more faithful explanations and makes them more understandable to users,
028
               and 2) by offering multiple forms of explanations, ConLUX outperforms state-
029
               of-the-art concept-based explanation techniques specifically designed for text and
030            image models, respectively.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   As machine learning models become more complex and popular, it has become an emerging topic to
036   explain the rationale behind their decisions. In particular, as the structure of machine learning mod-
037   els diversifies and evolves rapidly, and effective closed-source models (e.g., GPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
038   2023) and Gemini (et al., 2024b)) become more prevalent, model-agnostic explanation techniques
039
      show their appeal due to their adaptability to various models and tasks (Wang, 2024). These tech-
      niques consider target models as black boxes, so they can explain any machine learning model with-
040
      out requiring any knowledge of the model’s internal structure. This paper addresses the challenge
041
      of incorporating high-level concepts into local model-agnostic techniques to explain the decision-
042   making processes of various machine learning models, including large language models (LLMs).
043
044   To faithfully explain the behavior of machine learning models, it is essential to provide explanations
045
      built from language components aligned with the decision process of the target models; to make
      explanations easy to understand, it is also crucial to provide explanations built from user-friendly
046
      language components (Poeta et al., 2023a). Unfortunately, mainstream model-agnostic explana-
047
      tion techniques often fail to meet both requirements, as they provide explanations built from basic
048   features (e.g., words for text models and (super-)pixels for image models) (Ribeiro et al., 2016;
049   Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Ribeiro et al., 2018; Guidotti et al., 2018). In contrast, many concept-based
050   techniques provide explanations based on high-level features (e.g., topics in texts and objects in
051   images) (Poeta et al., 2023a). These techniques either utilize the internal information of the tar-
052   get models like gradients, activations, and attention weights (Zhang et al., 2021b; Yeh et al., 2020;
053   2019b; Cunningham et al., 2023; Ghorbani et al., 2019b; Crabbé & van der Schaar, 2022; Fel et al.,
      2023), or pre-defined external knowledge (El Shawi, 2024; Widmer et al., 2022) to build high-level

      To elevate the explanations provided by existing local model-agnostic techniques from feature-level
169   to concept-level, we modify these techniques in a uniform and lightweight way based on their two
170   commonalities: 1) these techniques use basic features as language components to build explanations;
171   2) these techniques use a perturbation model, which generates samples similar to the given input by
172   changing some of its feature values, to capture the local behavior of the target model at the feature
173   level. To this end, by only elevating the language components to high-level concepts and extending
174   the perturbation model to generate samples by changing high-level concepts, ConLUX extends these
175   techniques to provide concept-level explanations.
176   We evaluated ConLUX on explaining two sentiment analysis models (BERT, Llama 3.1(et al.,
177   2024a)) and three image classification models(YOLOv8, Vision Transformer (Oquab et al., 2023;
178   Darcet et al., 2023), and ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016)). Our evaluation results demonstrate that
179   ConLUX improves the fidelity of Anchors, LIME, LORE, and Kernel SHAP explanations by
180   82.21%, 48.59%, 149.93%, and 48.27% respectively, and by considering various forms of explana-
181   tions together, ConLUX outperforms state-of-the-art concept-based explanation techniques specif-
182   ically designed for text models (TBM (Ludan et al., 2023)) and image models (EAC (Sun et al.,
183
      2023)), respectively.

280   3.1   C ONCEPT-BASED L OCAL E XPLANATIONS
281
282   As we discussed in Section 2, though the form of the explanations varies, they are all built from
283   predicates in P. Elevating the predicates to concept level is the key to providing concept-based
284
      explanations.
285   Definition of high-level concepts varies, as Molnar (2020) mentions, “A concept can be any abstrac-
286   tion, such as a color, an object, or even an idea.” Here, to provide explanations that are easier to
287   understand by end-users, we define a concept predicate as follows:
288   Definition 1 (Concept Predicate). Given an input x, a concept predicate pc is a function that maps
289   x to a binary value, i.e., pc : X → {0, 1}, and satisfies the following properties:
290
291         1. Descriptive: The concept predicate pc can be concisely and intuitively described in natural
292            language.
293
294
            2. Human Evaluable: The truth of pc (x) can be readily assessed by a human user.
295
      The preceding two properties ensure that the concepts are easy to understand. Here, we provide two
296
      examples of concept predicates for text and image models in the following:

325   based on the input x and the target task. For text models, following the approach of Ludan et al.
326   (2023), we provide GPT-3.5 with task-specific information, the given input, the corresponding out-

354
355   In this section, we demonstrate the generality of ConLUX, its improvement of explanation fidelity,
356   and the fidelity of ConLUX unified explanations by empirical evaluation. We show the general-
357   ity of ConLUX by applying it to four mainstream local model-agnostic explanation techniques:
358
      Anchors, LIME, LORE, and Kernel SHAP (KSHAP for short), which provide three types of expla-

359
      text and image models. We show the improvement of explanation fidelity by comparing the vanilla
360
      feature-level explanations with ConLUX-augmented the concept-based explanations. Moreover, we
361   compare the fidelity of ConLUX unified explanations with two state-of-the-art concept-based ex-
362   planation techniques: Textual Bottleneck Model (TBM) (Ludan et al., 2023) for text models and
363   Explain Any Concept (EAC) (Sun et al., 2023) for image models.

389   explanation provides more fidelity than a single form, we used the combination ConLUX-augmented
390   KSHAP and LORE explanations as local surrogate models. Specifically, if an input is covered by
391   LORE’s rule, we use the LORE output; otherwise, we use the KSHAP explanation.
392   More details can be referred to Appendix B.

396
397   4.2.1    E VALUATION M ETRICS
398
      Fidelity reflects how faithfully the explanations describe the target model. As these techniques
399
      provide explanations in different forms, we used different metrics to evaluate their fidelity.
400
401   Following the setup in the original papers of Anchors and LORE, we used coverage and pre-
402   cision as fidelity metrics (which are named differently in the LORE paper). Given a target
403
      model f , an input x, and a distribution Dx derived from the perturbation model, and the cor-
      responding explanation g, we defined the coverage as cov(x; f, g) = Ez∼Dx [g(z)], which indi-
404
      cates the proportion of inputs in the distribution that match the rule; we defined the precision as
405
      prec(x; f, g) = Ez∼Dx [1f (z)=y |g(z)], where y is the consequence of the rules in g with y = f (x)
406
      for factual rules and y ̸= f (x) for counterfactual rules. Precision indicates the proportion of covered
407   inputs that g correctly predicts the model outputs.
408
409   As LIME and KSHAP are attribution-based local surrogate, we used Area Over most relevant first
410
      perturbation curve (AOPC) (Samek et al., 2016; Modarressi et al., 2023), and accuracya as fidelity
      metrics (Balagopalan et al., 2022; Yeh et al., 2019a; Ismail et al., 2021). Given a target model f , an
411
      input x, its corresponding model output y = f (x), their corresponding explanation g, and x(k) that
412
      is generated by masking the k% most important predicates in x, AOPC and accuracya are defined
413
      as follows:
414
                                    1
                                      PT                      (k)
415    • AOPC: Let AOPCk = |T|           x pf (y|x) − pf (y|x     ), where pf (y|x) is the probability of f to
416      output y given the input x, and T is the set of all test inputs. AOP Ck indicates the average
417      change of the model output when masking the k% most important predicates. A higher AOP Ck
418      indicates a better explanation. We calculate the AOPC curve by varying k from 0 to 100.
419    • Accuracya : Accuracya indicates the proportion of inputs among all x(k) that the target model
420      gives the same output as the original input x, i.e. E(f (x(k) ) = f (x)). Specifically, accuracya is
421      different from the standard accuracy, and a lower accuracya indicates a better explanation.
422   Specifically, we only considered the predicates that positively contribute to f (x), and we did not
423   use AOPC when explaining Llama 3.1, as it does not directly provide the probability for each output
424   token.
425
      For TBM, EAC, and ConLUX unified explanations, considering that they can all serve as local
426   surrogate models, i.e. g : X → R, we defined the metrics as follows: Given a target model f ,
427   an input x, a perturbation distribution Dx , and their corresponding explanation g, a performance
428   metric L (e.g. accuracy, F1 score, MSE, etc.), we define the (in-)fidelity as Ez∼Dx L(f (z), g(z)),
429   which indicates the performance of using g to approximate f . Here, we used the accuracy as the
430   performance metric. Specifically, considering the complexity of the original task, we reduced the
431   image classification task for local surrogates to predicting whether the target model f assigns the
      same classification to x′ as it does to x.

514   hard to understand.
515   Concept-based explanation techniques provide explanations in terms of high-level concepts, which
516   align with the decision-making process of the model better and are more interpretable to end-users.
517   To our knowledge, existing concept-based explanation techniques are all model-specific or task-
518   specific (Poeta et al., 2023b). We categorize them into three groups: (1) techniques that extract
519   concepts from the model’s internal structure (Zhang et al., 2021b; Yeh et al., 2020; 2019b; Cun-
520   ningham et al., 2023; Ghorbani et al., 2019b; Crabbé & van der Schaar, 2022; Fel et al., 2023),
521
      which are limited to specific types of models, (2) techniques that use external knowledge to define
      concepts (El Shawi, 2024; Widmer et al., 2022), which are limited to specific types of tasks since
522
      their methods based on the knowledge for a specific task, and (3) techniques that use pre-trained
523
      models to extract concepts (Ludan et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023). Ludan et al. (2023) propose TBM,
524   which is a surrogate model specifically designed for text data, while Sun et al. (2023) propose EAC,
525   which also utilizes internal information of the target model. Therefore, these techniques are only for
526   specific types of tasks. In addition, these techniques mainly focus only on attributions which limits
527   their use cases (Poeta et al., 2023b).

      6   C ONCLUSION
531
      We have proposed ConLUX, a general framework that automatically extracts high-level concepts
532
      and incorporates them into existing local model-agnostic explanation techniques to provide concept-
533
      based explanations, which are more faithful and easier to understand by end-users. ConLUX offers
534   unified explanations that combine attributions, sufficient conditions, and counterfactuals. This sat-
535   isfies diverse user needs and fills the current gap in concept-based explanations, which lack forms
536   beyond attributions. ConLUX achieves this by utilizing large pre-trained models to extract high-
537   level concepts, elevating language components from feature level to concept level, and extending
538   perturbation models to sample in the concept space. We have instantiated ConLUX on Anchors,
539   LIME, LORE, and Kernel SHAP, and provide unified explanations. We have constructed empirical
      evaluations to demonstrate the effectiveness of ConLUX.
