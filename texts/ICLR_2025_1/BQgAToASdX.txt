                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Data Attribution (DA) methods quantify the influence of individual training data
010
               points on model outputs and have broad applications such as explainability, data
011            selection, and noisy label identification. However, existing DA methods are of-
012            ten computationally intensive, limiting their applicability to large-scale machine
013            learning models. To address this challenge, we introduce the Generalized Group
014            Data Attribution (GGDA) framework, which computationally simplifies DA by
015            attributing to groups of training points instead of individual ones. GGDA is a gen-
016            eral framework that subsumes existing attribution methods and can be applied to
017            new DA techniques as they emerge. It allows users to optimize the trade-off be-
018            tween efficiency and fidelity based on their needs. Our empirical results demon-
019
               strate that GGDA applied to popular DA methods such as Influence Functions,
               TracIn, and TRAK results in upto 10x-50x speedups over standard DA meth-
020
               ods while gracefully trading off attribution fidelity. For downstream applications
021
               such as dataset pruning and noisy label identification, we demonstrate that GGDA
022            significantly improves computational efficiency and maintains effectiveness, en-
023            abling practical applications in large-scale machine learning scenarios that were
024            previously infeasible.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In the era of data-driven machine learning, the impact of training data on model performance has
030   become increasingly evident. As such, the ability to discern and quantify the influence of individual
031   training examples on model behavior has emerged as a critical area of research. Data Attribution
032   (DA) methods have risen to powerful tools designed to address this need, which identify the in-
033
      fluence of each training data point for specific test predictions, offering insights into the complex
      relationships between training data and model behavior (Koh & Liang, 2017; Ghorbani & Zou, 2019;
034
      Pruthi et al., 2020; Schioppa et al., 2022; Jia et al., 2021; Kwon & Zou, 2022; Ilyas et al., 2022; Park
035
      et al., 2023; Wang & Jia, 2023). The applications of DA are far-reaching and diverse, encompassing
036   crucial tasks such as model debugging, strategic data selection, and the identification of mislabeled
037   instances. These capabilities hold immense potential for enhancing model reliability.
038
039
      However, existing DA techniques face significant challenges that impede their practical application.
      Foremost among these is the issue of computational efficiency. These methods incur prohibitive
040
      computational costs, for example, by requiring training a large number of models in the order of
041
      the training data size, which is computationally intractable for large-scale machine learning mod-
042   els (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Ilyas et al., 2022). On the other hand, many existing methods struggle
043   with attribution fidelity, often failing to faithfully represent the true impact of training data on model
044   outcomes (Basu et al., 2020a). This lack of fidelity can lead to misinterpretations and potentially
045   misguided model development and deployment decisions. These efficiency and fidelity limitations
046   combined have severely restricted the widespread adoption and utility of DA techniques in large-
047   scale applications.
048   To address these critical issues, we propose a novel approach called Generalized Group Data Attribu-
049   tion (GGDA). This method shifts the paradigm from attributing influence to individual data points to
050   considering groups of data points collectively. By aggregating data into meaningful groups, GGDA
051   substantially reduces the computational overhead of attribution, making it particularly well-suited
052   for large-scale applications where traditional DA methods falter. Importantly, this improvement in
053   efficiency comes at little cost to attribution fidelity. Overall, this framework improves the scalability
      of individual-point attribution techniques while maintaining and, in some cases, improving the at-


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      tribution fidelity. GGDA also offers a unique advantage in allowing users to fine-tune the trade-off
055   between efficiency and fidelity through adjustable group sizes. This adaptability empowers practi-
056   tioners to optimize the balance between computational efficiency and attribution fidelity based on
057   their specific requirements. While other approaches have been proposed to accelerate DA methods,
058   such as subsampling and gradient approximations (Guo et al., 2021; Schioppa et al., 2022), these are
059   often method-specific and focus on optimizing computational aspects of existing DA algorithms. In
060   contrast, GGDA proposes a fundamentally novel attribution paradigm, allowing the acceleration of
061   arbitrary DA methods.
062   To validate the effectiveness of GGDA, we conduct extensive experimental evaluations across vari-
063   ous datasets (HELOC, MNIST, CIFAR10, QNLI), model architectures (ANN, ResNet, BERT), and
064   DA methods (Influence Functions, TRAK, TracIn). Our results show that: (1) GGDA significantly
065   improves the computational efficiency of DA methods, resulting in upto 10x-50x speedups depend-
066   ing on the setting, (2) GGDA favourably trades off fidelity with efficiency compared to standard DA
067   approaches, and (3) For downstream applications such as dataset pruning and noisy label identifica-
068   tion, GGDA outperforms standard DA methods, while maintaining significant speedups.

070   2   R ELATED W ORK
071
072   Data Attribution quantify the influence of training samples on model predictions. One type of
073   DA is retraining-based, which systematically retrain models with varying subsets of training data to
074   measure sample influence. Leave-One-Out (LOO) influence Tukey (1958) measures the prediction
075   difference between models trained on the full dataset and those trained with one sample removed.
076   Data Shapley (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Jia et al., 2019) extends this concept by considering all
077   possible subsets of training data, providing a more comprehensive measure of sample importance.
078
      DataModels (Ilyas et al., 2022) attempts to learn model predictions for each subset of the training
      dataset, requiring significant computational resources. While these methods can capture complex
079
      data interactions, they are computationally expensive due to the need for multiple model retrainings.
080
      Another type of gradient-based method offers more scalable solutions by providing closed-form
081   attribution scores using gradients. Influence functions (Koh & Liang, 2017) approximate the effect
082   of upweighting a training sample on the loss function, providing a foundation for many subsequent
083   studies. TracIn (Pruthi et al., 2020) traces loss changes on test points during the training process,
084   offering insights into sample influence throughout model training. TRAK (Park et al., 2023) employs
085   the neural tangent kernel with random projection to assess influence, presenting a novel approach
086   to attribution. These gradient-based methods significantly reduce computational costs compared to
087   retraining-based approaches but may suffer from performance degradation on non-convex neural
088   networks due to their reliance on convexity assumptions and Taylor approximations.
089   Grouping in Data Attribution. Several prior works considered attributing to groups, emphasizing
090   different aspects of the problem than ours. For example, Koh et al. (2019) examines influence
091   functions when applied to large groups of training points, and their empirical analysis revealed a
092   surprisingly strong correlation between the predicted and actual effects of groups across various
093   datasets. While it focuses primarily on linear classification models, we particularly focus on large-
094   scale non-linear models and study the fidelity and computational efficiency of group attribution.
095
      Basu et al. (2020b) proposed a second-order influence function to identify influential groups of
      training samples better. While their estimator results in improved fidelity estimates, their runtime is
096
      also strictly worse than first-order influence methods. On the other hand, our focus is on improving
097
      computational efficiency, even at slight costs to fidelity. To overcome the computational cost of Data
098   Shapley, Ghorbani & Zou (2019) also briefly mentioned the idea of grouping data points to manage
099   large datasets more effectively as an experimental design choice. On the other hand, our work
100   studies group attribution more systematically and shows that (1) it is possible to generalize both re-
101   training-based and gradient-based attribution methods to the group setting and (2) it demonstrates
102   experimentally the superiority of group approaches.
103
      Data Attribution Acceleration. Various approaches have been proposed to accelerate data attribu-
104   tion methods, including subsampling (Guo et al., 2021) and better gradient/Hessian approximations
105   (Schioppa et al., 2022). These techniques focus on optimizing existing attribution algorithms to im-
106   prove computational efficiency. While valuable, these methods are orthogonal to our GGDA frame-
107   work, which fundamentally alters the attribution paradigm by considering groups of data points. As

108
      such, GGDA can potentially be combined with these acceleration techniques to achieve even greater
109   efficiency gains, highlighting the complementary nature of our approach.

112   3       G ENERALIZED G ROUP DATA ATTRIBUTION F RAMEWORK
113
114   This section introduces the formal definitions of Data Attribution (DA) and our proposed General-
115
      ized Group Data Attribution (GGDA) frameworks. We examine the limitations of the conventional
      DA methods, which attributes importance to individual training points, and show that they often
116
      result in prohibitive computational costs. We then present the rationale behind the development of
117
      GGDA as a solution to these challenges. We begin by establishing the notations and definitions.
118
119   Notation. Given a dataset of n training data points D = {(x0 , y0 ), (x1 , y1 ), ..., (xn , yn )} in the
120   supervised learning setting1 , and a learning algorithm A(D) : 2D → Rp that outputs weights
121
      θ ∈ Rp , where f (xtest ; θ) ∈ R is the model prediction function for a given test input xtest , we define
      a counterfactual dataset DS = D \{(xi , yi ; i ∈ S)} as the training dataset with points in S removed.
122
      We use θS ∼ A(DS ) to denote the model weights trained on DS .
123
124   Definition 1 (Data Attribution) Given a learning algorithm A, dataset D and test point xtest , data
125   attribution for the ith training point (xi , yi ) is a scalar estimate τi (A, D, xtest ) ∈ R representing the
126   influence of (xi , yi ) on xtest .
127
128   To ground our discussion with a concrete example, we first introduce the conceptually most straight-
129   forward DA method, namely the Leave-One-Out (LOO) estimator. This computes the influence of
130   individual training points, by the average prediction difference between the model trained on the
131   original dataset, and the one trained on the dataset with a single training point excluded.
132
      Definition 2 (Leave-One-Out estimator) The LOO estimator of a learning algorithm A, dataset D
133
      and test point xtest , w.r.t. to training point (xi , yi ) is given by τi (A, D, xtest ) = Eθ∼A(D) f (xtest ; θ)−
134
      Eθi ∼A(D\{(xi ,yi )}) f (xtest ; θi )
135
136   The LOO estimator forms the conceptual basis for methods in the DA literature. For example, the
137   influence function (Koh & Liang, 2017) based methods are gradient-based approximations of the
138   LOO estimator. On the other hand, methods like datamodels (Ilyas et al., 2022) and TRAK (Park
139   et al., 2023) aim to generalize beyond LOO estimators by estimating importance of training points
140   by capturing the average effect of excluding larger subsets that include that training point. However,
141   the underlying paradigm of measuring the importance of individual training points (xi , yi ), for a
142   given test point xtest , remains common among DA methods. However, as we shall now discuss, this
143   paradigm of data attribution has several critical drawbacks.
144   Drawback: Attribution Computation Scales with Data Size. A critical challenge in data at-
145   tribution is its substantial computational cost. From an abstract perspective, the definition of DA
146   necessitates computing attributions for each training point individually, resulting in computational
147   requirements that scale with the size of the training dataset n. This scaling becomes prohibitive
148   in modern learning settings with large n. The computational burden can be even more severe in
149   practice. For instance, the LOO estimator demands training at least n + 1 models, with this number
150
      increasing significantly in non-convex settings due to Monte Carlo estimation for the expectation in
      Definition 2 (Jia et al., 2019). Gradient-based methods, such as influence functions, aim to reduce
151
      attribution computation by avoiding retraining. However, these methods still require (1) computa-
152
      tion of per-training-sample gradients w.r.t. parameters and (2) computation of the Hessian inverse
153   of models parameters. Both these steps are computationally intensive, as (1) requires n forward and
154   backward passes, which is prohibitive when n is in the billions, and (2) requires manipulating the
155   Hessian matrix whose dimensions are p × p for a model θ ∈ Rp , which is again intractable when p
156   is in the billions.
157
      Drawback: Pointwise Attribution may be Ill-Defined. Another issue with DA is that estimates
158
      of individual training point importance may not always be meaningful. For example, recall that the
159   LOO estimator involves repeatedly training models on nearly identical training sets, with merely a
160   single point missing. However, for training data involving billions of data points, a single missing
161

      example out of billions is unlikely to lead to any statistically significant change in model parameters
163   or outputs. This intuition is captured in the machine learning literature via properties such as (1)
164   stability of learning algorithms (Elisseeff et al., 2005), or (2) differential privacy (Dwork, 2008).
165   These capture the intuition that the models output by learning algorithms must not change when
166   individual points are removed, and these have been linked to (1) model generalization for the case
167   of stability and (2) privacy guarantees on the resulting model. Thus, when we employ learning
168   algorithms that satisfy these conditions, the LOO estimator, which is the conceptual basis for many
169   DA methods, may be ill-defined.
170   Drawback: Focus on Individual Predictions Limits Scope. A primary motivation for DA is to
171   explain individual model predictions via their training data. However, there exist many applications
172   beyond explainability of individual predictions that require reasoning about the relationship between
173   model behaviour and training data. For instance, we may wish to identify training points that cause
174   models to be inaccurate, non-robust, or unfair, all of which are “bulk” model properties as opposed
175   to individual predictions. One way to adapt DA to analyze such bulk model properties is to analyze
176   pointwise the test points that are inaccurate or non-robust, for example, but this is undesirable for
177
      two reasons. First, many model properties, such as fairness and f-measure, are non-decomposable
      (Narasimhan et al., 2015), i.e., they cannot be written in terms of contributions of individual test
178
      points. Second, even with decomposable metrics, it may be computationally wasteful to identify
179
      important training instances for the bulk metric by aggregating results across test points instead of
180   directly identifying important training data points for the metric itself. We are thus interested in
181   developing tools to directly analyze the impact of training data on bulk model behavior.
182
183
      To resolve these drawbacks, we propose to modify the DA framework by partitioning the training
      dataset into groups of training points and attributing model behaviour to these groups instead of
184
      individual data points. Similar data points affect models in similar ways, so it is meaningful to con-
185
      sider attribution to groups consisting of similar data points. When no such meaningful groups exist,
186   we can define groups as singleton training points, subsuming the standard DA setting. Note that
187   our expanded scope of DA can handle not just individual predictions but arbitrary functions of the
188   model parameters, which we call “property functions”. This can help efficiently reason about bulk
189   model properties such as accuracy, robustness, and fairness. When we wish to attribute individual
190   model predictions, we can simply set the property function to be equal to the loss for an individ-
191   ual test sample, thus subsuming the case of standard DA. We now formally define these two new
192   components: groups and property functions, before using these to define our GGDA framework.
193   Group Z: Given a dataset D, we define a partition of the dataset into k groups suchSthat Z =
194   {z0 , z1 , ...zk }, where each zj is a set of inputs xi such that zj ∩ zi = ∅ for i ̸= j, and i zi = D.
195   In general, the cardinality of each group |zi | can be different. When the group sizes = 1, this reverts
196   to the usual case of attributing to individual training data points.
197
      Property function g: Given a model with weights θ ∈ Rp , we define a property function g(·) :
198   Rp → R. Property functions generalize (1) pointwise functions like log probability of a given test
199   point, (2) aggregate functions like accuracy on a test set. Given these two quantities, we are ready
200   to define GGDA.
201
202   Definition 3 (Generalized Group Data Attribution) Given a learning algorithm A, dataset D with
203   groups Z and property function g, GGDA wrt to the j th group zj estimates τj (A, D, Z, g) ∈ R
204   representing the influence of group zj on model property g
205
206
      To ground this with a concrete example, we present the GGDA variant of the LOO estimator below.
      The idea is very similar to the usual LOO estimator, except here that we leave the j th group out, and
207
      measure the resulting change in the property function g.
208
209   Definition 4 The (GGDA LOO) estimator of a learning algorithm A, dataset D, groups Z and
210   property function g, wrt to the j th group zj is given by τj (A, D, Z, g) = Eθ∼A(D) g(θ) −
211   Eθj ∼A(D\Zj ) g(θj )
212
213   Comparing this to the usual LOO attributor, we observe that the GGDA variant requires training of
214   k+1 models as opposed to n+1 models, which is advantageous when k ≪ n. In summary, the main
215   advantage of GGDA methods is that they scale as the number of groups k, whereas DA methods
      scale with the number of data points n. While extending LOO was fairly simple, it is, in general,

216
      non-trivial to extend arbitrary DA methods to GGDA methods, particularly in a way that makes the
217   computation scale with the number of groups k, reflecting the advantage that group attribution offers.
218   For example, while Koh et al. (2019) study group influence functions, they compute these group
219   influences by summing the influences of individual training points, thus retaining the computational
220   cost of standard DA. In the next section, we shall propose GGDA variants of popular DA methods
221   in a manner that exactly improves their computational efficiency.

226   computational benefits of attribution in groups. In particular, we focus on deriving GGDA variants
227   of gradient-based influence methods, given their inherent computational superiority over re-training-
228   based methods. To facilitate the derivation of the group setting, we unify two gradient-based DA
229   methods — TRAK and TracIn — as instances of influence function methods, even as this differs
230   with the original motivation presented in these papers. To derive these group variants, there are two
231   principles we follow:
232   • the GGDA variants must scale with k, the number of groups, and not n, the number of datapoints;
233   • the GGDA variants must subsume the ordinary DA variants when groups are defined as singleton
234     datapoints, and the property function as the loss on a single test point.

332   5.1   DATASETS AND M ODELS
333
334   We evaluate across four diverse datasets encompassing tabular (HELOC), image (MNIST and
335   CIFAR-10), and text data (QNLI). These datasets represent a range of task complexities, data types,
336   and domains, allowing us to assess the generalizability of our approach across different scenarios.
337
      The models we fit on these datasets range from simple logistic regression (LR) to medium-sized ar-
      tificial neural networks (ANNs, e.g., MLPs and ResNet) and pre-trained language models (BERT).

      5.2   G ROUPING M ETHODS
341   We employ several grouping methods to evaluate the effectiveness of our GGDA framework. For
342   each grouping method, we also experiment with various group sizes to analyze the trade-off between
343   computational efficiency and attribution effectiveness. The choice of grouping method and group
344   size can significantly impact the performance of GGDA, as we subsequently demonstrate.
345
      Random We uniformly randomly assign data points to groups of a specified size. This serves as
346
      our baseline method from which we may assess the impact of more sophisticated methods.
347
348   K-Means We use the standard K-means algorithm to cluster data points based on their raw features.
349   Representation K-Means (Repr-K-Means) We first obtain hidden representations of the data
350   points from a model trained on the full training set. We then apply K-means clustering on these
351   representations. This approach groups data points that are similar in the model’s learned feature
352   space, potentially capturing higher-level semantic similarities.
353
      Gradient K-Means (Grad-K-Means) We compute the gradient of the loss with respect to the
354   activations (NOT parameters) of the pre-classification layer of the model for each data point, and
355   then apply K-means on the gradients. This approach groups instances with similar effects on the
356   model’s learning process.
357
358   5.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
359
360   To illustrate the effectiveness of GGDA at identifying important datapoints, especially in comparison
361   to DA, it is critical to have metrics that allow us to compare both on equal terms. To this end, we
362   utilize the following metrics:
363   Retraining Score (RS) This metric quantifies the impact of removing a proportion of training data
364   points on model performance. The process involves first identifying the most influential data points
365   or groups using GGDA. A given percentile of the high-importance data points are then removed from
366   the training set, and the model is retrained on the remaining data. We then compare the performance
367   of this retrained model to the original model’s performance on the test set. A significant decrease
368   in performance (i.e., an increase in loss or decrease in accuracy) after removing influential points
369   indicates that they were indeed important for the model’s learning process. This metric provides a
370
      tangible measure of the effectiveness of GGDA in identifying highly useful training instances.
371   Noisy Label Detection (AUC) This metric quantifies the effectiveness with which GGDA methods
372   can identify mislabelled training data points. This involves randomly flipping a proportion of the
373   training set labels, and training a model on the corrupted dataset. We then examine the labels of the
374   training data points that belong to groups with the lowest scores, and plot the change of the fraction
375   of detected mislabeled data with the fraction of the checked training data, following Ghorbani &
376
      Zou (2019); Jia et al. (2021). The final metric is computed as the area under the curve (AUC).

      5.4.1                         R ETRAINING S CORE
388   To evaluate the retraining score metric, we compute Test Accuracy upon removal of 1%, 5%, 10%,
389   and 20% of the most important points in the train set. For groups, this translates to sequentially
390   removing the most important groups up until the desired number of data points (points are randomly
391   selected from the final group in the case of overlap). Lower test accuracies are thus more favorable,
392   indicating that the removed points were indeed important.
393
      Which Grouping Method to Use? Before presenting our results, we first investigate the effect that
394   the particular group arrangement has on GGDA methods, to select an appropriate grouping method.
395   We plot a subset of group sizes that decreases from left to right as runtimes simultaneously increase,
396   up unto the point where our GGDA framework subsumes traditional individual DA at group size 1.
397   Among the grouping methods, we find that across the board, Grad-K-Means consistently demon-
398   strates superior performance. Figure 1 illustrates this for the MNIST dataset (similarly conclusive
399   plots for the remaining datasets and models are provided in Appendix C.1).
400   While random grouping necessarily provides orders of magnitude speedups in runtime, it is inferior
401   at grouping together points that similarly impact model performance. For instance, the most impor-
402   tant Random groups of size 64 consisted of a rough 50:50 split between points that had positive and
403   negative individual attributions, while the most important Grad-K-Means group of size 64 consisted
404   only of points with positive individual attributions (across all three attribution methods shown). In
405   terms of preserving group integrity, the Grad-K-Means method outperforms other approaches by
406   consistently grouping together data points that influence model performance in similar ways.
407   Results across Removal Percentages. Based on our results above, we employ Grad-K-Means
408   as the primary grouping method, and trial incremental increases on the removal percentage for the
409   retraining metric (using values of 1%, 5%, 10%, 20%). Our results in Figure 2 show that GGDA,
410   in general, has a favorable accuracy efficiency tradeoff. In particular, we find that the performance
411   levels are roughly equivalent across group sizes {1, 4, 16, 64}, but with orders-of-magnitude im-
412   provement in runtime.

528   6    C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we have proposed a new framework, generalized group data attribution, that offers
531   computational advantages over data attribution while maintaining similar fidelity. While our work
532   has focussed more on influence-based approaches that are more non-trivial to extend to the group set-
533   ting, any data attribution approach can, in principle, be extended to the group setting to obtain these
534
      computational benefits. The key insight here is that many downstream applications of attribution
      involve manipulating training datasets at scale, such as dataset pruning, or noisy label identification,
535
      and a fine-grained approach of individual point influence may be unnecessary in such cases.
536
537   In terms of future work, it is desirable to conduct a formal analysis of the fidelity tradeoffs in the
538   group setting for various methods of interest, analytically quantifying the fidelity loss due to group-
539   ing.

      A.3   BACKGROUND ON F ISHER INFORMATION MATRIX
757
758   One of the popular techniques for Hessian approximation in machine learning is via the Fisher
759   Information Matrix. When the quantity whose Hessian is considered is a log-likelihood term, it
760   turns out that the Hessian has a simple form:
761

763                    Hθ = −            Ey′ ∼p(y|x) ∇2θ log p(y ′ | x, θ)

768   Here, the term Fθ is the Fisher Information Matrix. Thus, when the loss term is a log-likelihood term,
769   ℓ(x, θ) = Ey′ ∼p(y|x;θ) log p(y ′ | x; θ), the Hessian can be written in this manner (Barshan et al.,
770   2020). In practical applications involving the Fisher, it is common to use the so-called Empirical
771
      Fisher matrix, which is given by:
772
773              X
         F̂θ =       ∇θ log p(y | x, θ)∇θ log p(y | x, θ)⊤          (where y is the ground truth label for x)   (6)

775
776   While the limitations of using the empirical Fisher approximation have been well-documented (Kun-
777   stner et al., 2019), primarily owing to issues with the bias of the estimate, it is employed practically
778   as a convenient approximation. Computationally, while the Hessian of log-likelihood requires both a
779   double-backpropagation and a Monte Carlo expectation, the Fisher only requires a single backprop-
780   agation for gradient computation with the Monte Carlo estimate. On the other hand, the empirical
781   Fisher approximation forgoes the Monte Carlo estimate.
