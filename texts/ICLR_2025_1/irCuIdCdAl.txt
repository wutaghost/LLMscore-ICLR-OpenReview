009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformers have revolutionized AI research, particularly in natural language
012            processing (NLP). However, understanding the decisions made by transformer-
013            based models remains challenging, which impedes trust and safe deployment in
014            real-world applications. While activation-based attribution methods have proven
015            effective in explaining transformer-based text classification models, our findings
016
               suggest that they may suffer from class-irrelevant features within activations, po-
               tentially degrading the quality of their interpretations. To address this issue, we
017
               introduce Contrast-CAT, a novel activation contrast-based attribution method that
018
               improves token-level attribution by filtering out class-irrelevant features from ac-
019            tivations. Contrast-CAT enhances interpretability by contrasting the activations of
020            input sequences with reference activations, allowing for the generation of clearer
021            and more faithful attribution maps. Our experiments demonstrate that Contrast-
022            CAT consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods across various datasets and
023            models, achieving significant gains over the second-best methods with average
024            improvements in AOPC and LOdds by ×1.30 and ×2.25, respectively, under the
025            MoRF setting. Contrast-CAT provides a promising step forward in enhancing the
026            interpretability and transparency of transformer-based models.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The success of transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), particularly in natural language processing
031   (NLP), has been remarkable in recent years. This success has transcended both academic and in-
032   dustrial boundaries, integrating them more into our daily lives. Unfortunately, this integration has
033   also increased the risk of direct exposure to AI errors, heightening the need to ensure the safety,
034   security, and trustworthiness of AI models by promoting transparency in AI systems (The White
035   House, 2023; Dunietz et al., 2024; European Commission, 2024). As a result, developing methods
036   to interpret the decision-making processes of transformer-based models has become essential.
037   To meet this need, numerous methods have been proposed for interpreting transformer-based mod-
038   els, particularly for text classification, where they have demonstrated remarkable performance.
039   These methods often provide attribution maps telling the relative contributions of input tokens to the
040   model’s decisions; in Section 2, we categorize them into attention-based, LRP-based, and activation-
041   based attribution methods. This work focuses on activation-based attribution, which leverages a
042   model’s activation information to generate attribution maps, achieving state-of-the-art performance
043   in attribution quality thus far.
044   In essence, activation-based attribution maps are created using activations from a certain layer or
045   multiple layers of a neural network corresponding to an input sequence. Then, the output gradient
046   of the prospective class with respect to the activations is imposed on the activations to extract only
047   class-relevant features (Selvaraju et al., 2017; Qiang et al., 2022).
048   However, we found that this procedure can still be affected by class-irrelevant features present in
049   activations, hindering the creation of accurate class-specific interpretations. For example, Figure 1
050   shows attribution maps generated by AttCAT in panel (A), one of the state-of-the-art activation-
051   based attribution method (Qiang et al., 2022), for a movie review ‘It is very slow.’ classified as
052   negative. We expect the word ‘slow’ to be detected as relevant, with a positive attribution value for
053   the negative review. However, AttCAT fails to detect the word, being confused by the punctuation
      mark. To the contrary, our proposed method Contrast-CAT puts the highest attribution on ‘slow’.

071
      In this paper, we propose Contrast-CAT, a novel activation-based attribution method for transformer-
072   based text classification models. Contrast-CAT is designed to produce high-quality token-level at-
073   tribution maps by filtering out class-irrelevant features from activations through our new activation-
074   contrasting framework. Our experiments demonstrate that Contrast-CAT significantly improves the
075   quality of token-level attribution.
076
077   Contributions Our contributions can be summarized as follows. (1) We observe that activation-
078   based attribution methods for transformer-based text classification models may incorporate class-
079   irrelevant features within activations, potentially degrading attribution quality. (2) We propose
080   Contrast-CAT for generating token-level attribution maps based on a novel activation-contrasting
081   framework tailored for transformer architecture. Unlike existing activation-based attribution meth-
082   ods, Contrast-CAT leverages differences between target and multiple reference activations to reduce
083
      class-irrelevant features in the target activation, thereby improving attribution quality. (3) We pro-
      vide experimental results demonstrating that Contrast-CAT significantly outperforms state-of-the-
084
      art methods, achieving average improvements of ×1.30 and ×2.25 in AOPC and LOdds under the
085
      MoRF setting, and ×1.34 and ×1.03 under the LeRF setting, compared to the second-best methods.

088   2   R ELATED W ORK
089
090   We describe attribution methods for interpreting transformer-based text classification models, cate-
091   gorizing them into attention-based, LRP-based, and activation-based approaches.
092
093   Attention-based Attribution Attention-based attribution methods rely on attention scores, a key
094   component of transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017). Under the assumption that input tokens with
095   high attention scores significantly influence model outputs, numerous studies (Martins & Astudillo,
096
      2016; Mullenbach et al., 2018; Clark et al., 2019; Abnar & Zuidema, 2020; Modarressi et al., 2022;
      Mohebbi et al., 2023) have employed attention scores for interpretative purposes of a model. Specifi-
097
      cally, (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020) proposed Rollout, which integrates attention scores across multiple
098
      layers while accounting for skip connections in transformer architectures to capture information
099   flow. Additionally, there have been many papers (Chrysostomou & Aletras, 2021; Barkan et al.,
100   2021) that introduce the gradient of attention weight for interpretation. Despite advances in attention-
101   based methods, significant debate remains about whether attention scores truly reflect the relevance
102   of model predictions, as highlighted in (Jain & Wallace, 2019; Wiegreffe & Pinter, 2019).
103
104   LRP-based Attribution Layer-wise relevance propagation (LRP) (Bach et al., 2015) is a tech-
105   nique for backpropagating relevance scores through a neural network, with the scores reflecting
106   our specific interest in the model’s prediction. Building on LRP, several studies have derived ex-
107   planations for model behavior (Gu et al., 2018; Voita et al., 2019; Chefer et al., 2021). In (Voita
      et al., 2019), LRP was partially used to determine the most important attention heads within a spe-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      cific transformer’s encoder layer, utilizing relevance scores for the attention weights. (Chefer et al.,
109   2021) introduces TransAtt, which propagates relevance scores through all layers of a transformer,
110   combining these scores with gradients of the attention weights and utilizing the Rollout technique
111   for multi-layer integration. However, LRP-based methods are limited by certain assumptions, known
112   as the LRP rules, designed to uphold the principle of relevance conservation (Montavon et al., 2019).
113
114   Activation-based Attribution In contrast to the methods discussed above, activation-based at-
115   tribution primarily relies on activation information from each layer of a transformer model. These
116   methods are based on core ideas originally developed for convolutional neural networks (CNNs),
117   which have been shown to be effective for generating high-quality interpretations with simple im-
118   plementations and broad versatility (Selvaraju et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2020; Lee & Han, 2022).
119   In (Qiang et al., 2022), the authors introduced AttCAT as the first adaptation of Grad-CAM (Sel-
120   varaju et al., 2017), one of the most popular activation-based methods for CNNs, to interpret the
121
      decisions of transformer-based text classification models. AttCAT generates token-level attribution
      maps by merging activations and their gradients in relation to the model’s predictions, following
122
      Grad-CAM’s essential approach, which uses gradients to reflect class-relevant information. Simi-
123
      larly, (Englebert et al., 2023) introduced TIS adapting Score-CAM (Wang et al., 2020): TIS uses the
124   centroids of activation clusters identified from the activation from all layers to compute relevance
125   scores in a manner akin to Score-CAM.
126
127
      Although there are existing attribution methods for transformer-based text classification models that
      use gradients to extract class-relevant features from activations, no approach has yet focused on
128
      filtering out class-irrelevant features through activation contrasting to improve attribution quality.

219   Extraction from Multiple Layers As discovered in previous studies (Jawahar et al., 2019; Turton
220   et al., 2021; Pascual et al., 2021), the semantic information of given input token sequences processed
221   by transformer-based models varies across different layers, ranging from phrase-level information
222   to deeper semantic meanings. Therefore, unlike traditional activation-based attribution methods for
223   CNNs, which only use activations extracted from a single, usually the penultimate (Selvaraju et al.,
224   2017; Lee & Han, 2022), layer, we use the activations Aℓ in Eq. (2) from multiple layers, where
225   ℓ = 1, . . . L, along with their layer-wise attention scores αℓ,h in Eq. (1) to capture layer-specific
226   meanings for each token across various layers. This design allows us to reflect dominantly attended
227
      token-level information of the target activations across multiple layers by combining α̂iℓ .
228   Finally, by incorporating the gradient information ∂f∂A c (x)

232   4.2   U SE OF M ULTIPLE C ONTRASTS
233
234   The activation subtraction in Eq. (4) is done with a single reference belonging to a certain class.
235   However, it would be beneficial to contrast with multiple references of various classes, considering
236   that the target activation Aℓ may contain features of more than one non-target class. Furthermore,
237   features within the target activation that persist after subtraction with various reference activations
238   are more likely to represent class-relevant features unique to the target activation. For this purpose,
239
      we create a set of attribution maps D by conducting the previous procedure in Section 4.1 with
      multiple reference activation, where D := {IR(r) (x) : r ∈ training set, fc (R(r)) < γ}.
240
241   These reference activations can be sampled and cached during training and used later to generate
242   attribution maps – we call this the reference library. We used such a reference library with 30 pre-
243   computed references per class.
244
245   Refinement with Multiple Contrasts We refine Contrast-CAT using the set D. Our refinement
246
      process involves selectively filtering out maps from D that likely contain incorrect attributes. For
      this purpose, we assess the attribution quality of each map in the set D and exclude those that do not
247
      meet our established criteria based on the assessed scores.
248
249   To evaluate the attribution quality of each map, we utilize a deletion test (Petsiuk, 2018; Wang et al.,
250   2020; Lee & Han, 2022). This approach is adapted here as a token-wise deletion test. For each
251   map in D, we calculate the average probability drop score by sequentially removing the top-ranked
252
      tokens based on their attribution values and comparing the model’s output before and after each
      modification. This measures the decrease in the model’s predictive probability due to the removal
253
      of each token. This procedure is conducted on a token-by-token basis, where each token’s removal
254
      is individually assessed to determine its impact on the model’s output. The average probability drop
255   score is then computed by taking the mean of these individual probability drops, thereby quantifying
256   the average quality of the attribution map for each token.
257
258
      Finally, we generate Contrast-CAT by averaging over all the attribution maps:

263   drop score of each map IR (x). In our experiments, we set the value of ρ as the mean plus standard
264
      deviation of these scores from the set of attribution maps.

308   Evaluation Metrics We used the area over the perturbation curve (denoted by AOPC) (Nguyen,
309   2018; Chen et al., 2020) and the log-odds (LOdds) (Shrikumar et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2020)
310   metrics for assessing the faithfulness of attribution following the previous research (Qiang et al.,
311   2022). Faithfulness refers to the accuracy with which an attribution map’s scores reflect the actual
312   influence of each input token on the model’s prediction. The AOPC and LOdds metrics    c   are defined
                                    1
                                      PN       c    c                          1
                                                                                 PN          ỹ
313   as follows: (1) AOPC(k) := N i=1 (yi − ỹi ), and (2) LOdds(k) := N i=1 log yic . Here, N is
                                                                                               i
314   the total number of data points used for evaluation, and yic denotes the model’s prediction probability
315   for the class c of a given input token sequence x, while ỹic indicates the probability after removing
316   the top-k% of input tokens based on relevance scores from an attribution map.
317
      To evaluate attribution quality more precisely using the AOPC and LOdds metrics, and to address
318   inconsistencies in evaluation results caused by the order of token removal (i.e., removing the most
319   relevant tokens first versus the least relevant tokens first) (Rong et al., 2022), we conducted experi-
320   ments under two settings: one where tokens were removed in descending order of relevance scores
321   (MoRF: Most Relevant First), and another in ascending order (LeRF: Least Relevant First). Consis-
322   tently achieving high-quality attribution under both conditions indicates superior attribution quality.
323   Specifically, under the MoRF setting, higher AOPC and lower LOdds indicate better attribution,
      while under the LeRF setting, lower AOPC and higher LOdds suggest better performance.

495   6   C ONCLUSION
496
497   In this work, we reported that activation-based attribution methods for interpreting transformer-
498   based text classification models may incorporate class-irrelevant features into attribution maps, po-
499   tentially leading to a degradation in attribution quality. To address this challenge, we introduced
500   Contrast-CAT, a novel activation-based attribution method that leverages activation contrasting to
501   reduce class-irrelevant features within activations, thereby generating high-quality token-level attri-
502   bution maps. Our extensive experiments demonstrated that Contrast-CAT significantly outperforms
503   state-of-the-art methods in terms of faithfulness, as measured by AOPC and LOdds metrics, under
504
      both MoRF and LeRF settings.
505   Despite its effectiveness, Contrast-CAT requires reference points whose activations must be avail-
506   able during the creation of attribution maps. We have minimized the computational overhead using
507   a pre-built reference library; however, it will require larger storage as the number of classes and the
508   size of activation maps increase. To address this, we plan to explore replacing the reference activa-
509   tions with alternative tensors that can be computed and stored at a lower cost, ideally without relying
510
      on training data in future work.
511   Nevertheless, given the growing need to interpret AI models’ decisions to ensure their safety, se-
512   curity, and trustworthiness, we believe that Contrast-CAT serves as a meaningful advancement in
513   improving the interpretability and transparency of transformer-based models.
