011                                                      A BSTRACT
012
013                We study the reinforcement learning (RL) problem with trajectory feedback. The
014                trajectory feedback based reinforcement learning problem, where the learner can
015                only observe the accumulative noised reward along the trajectory, is particularly
016
                   suitable for the practical scenarios where the agent suffers extensively from query-
                   ing the reward in each single step. For a finite-horizon Markov Decision Process
017
                   (MDP) with S states, A actions and a horizon √ length of   H, we develop an algo-
018
                   rithm that enjoys an optimal regret of Õ             3
                                                                   SAH K in K episodes for suffi-
019
020                ciently large K.1 To achieve this, our technical contributions are two-fold: (1)
021                we incorporate reinforcement learning with linear bandits problem to construct
022                a tighter confidence region for the reward function; (2) we construct a reference
023                transition model to better guide the exploration process.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      In the standard reinforcement learning (RL) formulation, it is assumed that the agent acts in an
      unknown environment, and in each step, the agent receives feedback in the form of a state-action
029
      dependent reward signal, and then transits to the next state. Although such an interaction model
030
      might be reasonable when a simulator is available, for real-life applications, such reward feedback
031   model could be hard to realize. For practical scenarios, querying the reward function could be costly,
032   or even impossible in certain circumstances.
033
034
      As a motivating example, in healthcare, a doctor repeatedly interacts with a patient for the purpose
      of treatment. In each step, the doctor decides an action (e.g., taking some medicine) and observes
035
      the new state (including information like body temperature or blood pressure). On the other hand,
036
      the state-action dependent reward signal could be costly to observe, since the extent to which the
037   disease has been cured might be expensive to measure as it requires comprehensive medical tests. In
038   this case, in order to apply the RL framework, it is more reasonable to assume that in each step, the
039   agent observes only the current state, and the cumulative reward value is revealed only after a whole
040   trajectory is finished.
041
      As another example, in autonomous car driving, defining a state-action dependent reward function
042
      could be a challenging task, as it requires associating all possible state-action pairs with a real
043   number. A possible workaround is to have human experts involved to produce the reward signals.
044   However, defining reward signals could be a highly subjective matter, and waiting for reward values
045   from human experts could take unacceptable amount of time from the perspective an a RL algorithm.
046
      To circumvent issues mentioned above, practitioners often rely on heuristics (e.g., reward shap-
047
      ing (Ng et al., 1999) or reward hacking (Amodei et al., 2016)). RL with trajectory feedback has
048
      been recently proposed in Efroni et al. (2021) as a more principled framework to the deal with the
049   aforementioned issues. In this framework, the agent no longer has access to a per state-action re-
050   ward function. Instead, it receives the cumulative reward on the trajectory as well as all the visited
051   state-action pairs on the trajectory. Clearly, this new feedback model is weaker than the standard RL
052   setting and could be more applicable for real-life scenarios. In Efroni et al. (2021), new algorithms

182   In this section, we give an overview of the technical challenges associated with obtaining the min-
183

187   Connection with Linear Bandits. As observed in prior work on RL with trajectory feed-
188   back (Efroni et al., 2021), when the transition model, RL with trajectory feedback can be seen as an
189   instance of linear bandits. More specifically, in each round, suppose the trajectory sampled by the
190   agent is τ , the expected trajectory reward feedback would be ϕ⊤  τ R, i.e., a linear function with respect
191   to ϕτ . Based on this observation, Efroni et al. (2021) showed how to build appropriate confidence
