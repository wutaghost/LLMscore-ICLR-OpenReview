011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We address the challenge of solving multi-objective bandit problems, which are
014            increasingly relevant in real-world applications where multiple possibly conflict-
015            ing objectives must be optimized simultaneously. Existing multi-objective algo-
016            rithms often rely on complex, computationally intensive methods, making them
017            impractical for real-world use. In this paper, we propose a novel perspective by
018            showing that objective diversity can naturally induce free exploration, allowing
019            for simpler, near-greedy algorithms to achieve optimal regret bounds up to log-
020
               arithmic factors with respect to the number of rounds. We introduce simple and
               efficient algorithms for multi-objective linear bandits, which do not
                                                                                  √ require con-
021
               structing empirical Pareto fronts and achieve a regret bound of O(
                                                                                e T ) under suf-
022
               ficient objective diversity and suitable regularity. We also introduce the concept
023
               of objective fairness, ensuring equal treatment of all objectives, and show that
024            our algorithms satisfy this criterion. Numerical experiments validate our theoreti-
025            cal findings, demonstrating that objective diversity can enhance algorithm perfor-
026            mance while simplifying the solution process.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Multi-objective decision-making problems have become increasingly prevalent in today’s complex,
032   real-world applications. From recommendation systems to robotics, decision-makers often face the
033   need to optimize multiple conflicting objectives simultaneously. This complexity naturally leads to
034
      the formulation of multi-objective bandit problems (Drugan & Nowe, 2013; Turgay et al., 2018; Lu
      et al., 2019; Xu & Klabjan, 2023; Cheng et al., 2024; crepon et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2023), a general-
035
      ization of the single-objective bandit framework. However, solving multi-objective bandit problems
036
      is particularly challenging due to the added complexity of balancing the exploration-exploitation
037   tradeoff across multiple objectives.
038
039   To address this challenge, many multi-objective bandit algorithms often resort to complex, some-
040
      times computationally intractable methods (Yahyaa & Manderick, 2015; Turgay et al., 2018; Lu
      et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2023), especially when compared to their simpler single-objective coun-
041
      terparts (Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011; Chu et al., 2011; Chapelle & Li, 2011; Agrawal & Goyal,
042
      2013; Abeille & Lazaric, 2017). Many of these methods often involve constructing empirical Pareto
043   fronts in each round, leading to significant computational overhead, making them less practical for
044   real-world deployment.
045
046
      Despite the additional complexity that multiple objectives introduce, we aim to address the following
      intriguing research question:
047
048    Can the presence of multiple diverse objectives actually facilitate learning rather than hinder it?
049
050   To our best knowledge, this question has never been addressed. To some, it may appear even some-
051   what counter-intuitive since a larger number of objectives usually implies a more challenging prob-
052   lem setup. Yet, we ask whether the diversity in objectives can induce exploration, enabling simpler
053   near-greedy algorithms to achieve performance guarantees of statistical efficiency which is typically
      obtained by more complex approaches.

      In this work, we propose a novel perspective on this problem by showing that objective diversity—
055   rather than feature diversity, which is commonly assumed in the literature—can drive exploration
056   in multi-objective linear bandits. This new insight allows us to demonstrate the statistical efficiency
057   of simple round-robin greedy algorithms, such as the algorithms we introduce, MORR-Greedy and
058   MORO-Greedy. Remarkably, our algorithms achieve optimal regret bounds with respect to the num-
059   ber of rounds T without the need for the context (feature) diversity assumption that underpins most
060   of the existing greedy bandit literature (Kannan et al., 2018; Raghavan et al., 2018; Hao et al., 2020;
061   Bastani et al., 2021).
062   While most previous works on greedy bandits (Kannan et al., 2018; Raghavan et al., 2018; Bastani
063   et al., 2021) rely on context diversity to induce exploration, we do not assume any such diversity
064   in the features. Our algorithms even perform effectively in fixed feature settings, where previous
065   greedy approaches and their proof techniques fail. To our best knowledge, this finding represents
066   the first result where a greedy-type algorithm achieves no regret results without relying on feature
067   diversity in parametric bandits. Furthermore, this work is the first to study the provable efficiency of
068   simple round-robin algorithms for multi-objective linear bandits.
069   We rigorously analyze our algorithms in terms of both regret performance and objective fairness,
070   demonstrating their theoretical and empirical superiority under objective diversity and suitable reg-
071   ularity, even without feature diversity. Our work introduces a new perspective in multi-objective
072   bandit research, showing that, in certain cases, more and diverse objectives can help rather than
073   complicate the learning process.
074   Our main contributions are summarized as follows.

      that can arise from the diversity of objectives, particularly in the absence of context diversity. Re-
140
      cent research on single-objective linear contextual bandits with stochastic contexts has shown that
141
      if context diversity is sufficiently high, greedy algorithms can achieve near-optimal regret bounds
142   (Bastani et al., 2021; Kannan et al., 2018; Raghavan et al., 2018; Hao et al., 2020). However, the
143   extension of these results to multi-objective bandits has been limited by an assumption of context
144   diversity, leaving a gap in understanding how exploration can occur without it.
145
      Our work addresses this gap by focusing on free exploration driven by objective diversity, even in
146
      the absence of context diversity. While Bayati et al. (2020) demonstrated that greedy algorithms
147
      perform well in non-contextual single-objective settings when the number of arms is large, they
148   relied on a β-regularity assumption related to the reward distribution. In contrast, we introduce the
149   concept of γ-regularity (Definition 6), which extends the notion of regularity to feature spaces in
150   the multi-objective setting. Unlike previous work, which provided only Bayesian regret bounds,
151   we rigorously establish worst-case regret bounds for our proposed algorithms, MORR-Greedy and
152   MORO-Greedy, under this new regularity assumption.
153

154   tings, driven solely by the diversity of the objectives. This is the first time that a theoretical guarantee
155   has been provided for such exploration in multi-objective linear bandits, without relying on context
156   diversity—a significant departure from existing literature.

      2.2.2    O BJECTIVE FAIRNESS
211
212   Pareto regret minimization is a central goal in multi-objective bandit algorithms, but it does not fully
213   capture the multi-objective nature of the problem. Focusing solely on Pareto regret minimization
214   allows algorithms to optimize for a single specific objective, potentially neglecting others, while
215   achieving regret bounds comparable to those in single-objective settings (Xu & Klabjan, 2023).
      Therefore, meaningful multi-objective bandit algorithms should aim to balance multiple objectives,


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      typically incorporating additional considerations such as fairness, alongside Pareto regret minimiza-
217   tion.
218
219   In the context of multi-objective bandits, fairness reflects the algorithm’s impartial treatment of
220
      multiple equivalent objectives. The earliest notion of fairness, to the best of our knowledge, was
      introduced by Drugan & Nowe (2013) and emphasizes equal representation across all Pareto fronts.
221
      This concept is closely linked to Pareto front approximation and remains a key objective for many
222
      existing algorithms (Drugan & Nowe, 2013; Yahyaa & Manderick, 2015; Turgay et al., 2018; Lu
223   et al., 2019). However, in practice, the true Pareto front is often unknown, requiring most Pareto-
224   efficient algorithms to rely on empirical Pareto front approximations during the selection process.
225   This reliance introduces two significant challenges: the lack of theoretical guarantees concerning the
226   fairness of true Pareto optimal arms, and the computational overhead associated with constructing
227   the empirical Pareto front in each round.
228
      To address these limitations, we propose a new notion of fairness in multi-objective bandit problems,
229   focusing on the fairness of objectives. Specifically, we advocate for algorithms that consistently
230   consider all optimal arms for each objective, ensuring that no objective is neglected over time. This
231   perspective shifts the focus from approximating the Pareto front to ensuring that each objective is
232   adequately and equitably addressed throughout the decision-making process.

234   the arm that has the largest reward for objective m, and µ∗m := µa∗m ,m . For all ϵ > 0, define the
235   objective fairness index pϵ,T of an algorithm as

292   3.2   F REE E XPLORATION INDUCED BY O BJECTIVE D IVERSITY
293
294   The MORR-Greedy algorithm (Algorithm 1) is built on the insight that exploration can arise naturally,
295   even when the algorithm is focused solely on exploitation, provided the bandit problem involves
296   sufficiently diverse objectives. In most of the existing multi-objective bandit literature, increasing
297   the number of objectives complicates the problem setup and leads to more complex algorithms,
298   especially compared to single-objective bandits.
299
      However, we observe a surprising and beneficial side effect: the diversity of objectives can induce
300   free exploration, enabling simple near-greedy algorithms like MORR-Greedy to achieve statistically
301   efficient performance (see Theorem 1).
302
303
      The core idea is that, for each objective, rounds in which greedy selections are made for other
      objectives can simultaneously serve as exploration rounds for the remaining objectives. In the round-
304
      robin process, exploitation occurs for one objective, while the other objectives naturally benefit from
305
      exploration. This dynamic allows for automatic exploration without incurring additional Pareto
306   regret, providing a significant performance advantage.
307
308   This phenomenon is intuitive, yet it has not been rigorously examined in multi-objective settings
309
      until now. Our work is the first to formalize the conditions under which natural exploration can
      occur in the presence of objective diversity, paving the way for simpler, more efficient algorithms in
310
      multi-objective bandit problems.

496
497   In this work, we introduced MORR-Greedy, a near-greedy algorithm for multi-objective bandits.
498   We identified sufficient conditions where free exploration arises from objective diversity, enabling
                                    √
499   our algorithm to achieve O(e dT ) regret bounds under objective diversity and feature regularity.
                                     λ0
500   We also introduced the concept of objective fairness, ensuring equal treatment of all objectives,
501   and demonstrated that MORR-Greedy satisfies this criterion. Our findings offer a new perspective,
502   showing that diverse objectives can actually enhance learning in multi-objective bandits.

      B.1   S ETTING
908
909   In each round t = 1, 2, . . . , T , the set of feature vectors χ(t) = {xi (t) ∈ Rd , i ∈ [K]} is drawn
910   from some unknown distribution Pχ (t). Each arm’s feature xi (t) ∈ χ(t) for i ∈ [K] need not be
911   independent of each other and can possibly be correlated. In this case, we denote xa(t) (t) as x(t).
912   Other settings are identical to the fixed arms case.
913
914   B.2   R ESULTS FOR STOCHASTIC CONTEXTS
915
916   In this section, we first present the regret bound of MORR-Greedy and the results on objective fairness
917   when played in a stochastic context. The proofs of each theorem are provided in the subsequent
      sections.

      To analyze the MORR-Greedy algorithm in the case of stochastic contexts, it is necessary to modify
919   the definition of γ-regularity slightly. In the stochastic version, it is necessary to assume the exis-
920   tence of a near-optimal arm not only near the true objective parameter but also in all directions to
921   ensure that the selected arms during the exploration process are sufficiently diverse. Instead, there
922   is a clear advantage in that, even with the assumption of a small positive bound on the probability of
923   the existence of near-optimal arms allows us to obtain a regret bound that differs only by a constant
924   factor in the results under fixed contexts.

       C.2   γ-R EGULARITY VS β-R EGULARITY
1194
       In Bayati et al. (2020), they assume the prior distribution Γ of the expected reward µ of each arm
1195
       satisfies Pµ [µ > 1 − ϵ] = Θ(ϵβ ) for all ϵ > 0 in non-contextual MAB setting. Let’s compare this
1196
       with γ-regularity when m = d = 1. We claim that γ-regularity can be considered weaker than
1197   β-regularity from three perspectives.
1198
1199   The most significant difference is that in β-regularity, the probability that the expected reward µi
1200
       exceeds 1 − ϵ is required for all arm i ∈ [K], along with the assumption that µi ’s are drawn
       independently from prior Γ. In contrast, in γ-regularity, it is sufficient to ensure that the prob-
1201
       ability that one of the K arms satisfies xi (t)⊤ β ≥ γ, without the need for the independence
1202
       assumption between arm vectors. Secondly, unlike β-regularity, γ-regularity does not require a
1203   specific relationship like Θ(1 − γ) between the probability of the existence of near-optimal arms
1204   Pχ(t) [∃i ∈ [K], xi (t)⊤ β ≥ γ] and the threshold γ ; instead, it focuses on the existence of a pos-
1205   itive lower bound qγ . Lastly, in Bayati et al. (2020), the β-regularity assumes the probability of
1206   µ > 1 − ϵ for all ϵ > 0. In contrast, this study does not mandate γ-regularity for γ very close to 1;
1207   it is sufficient to hold γ-regularity only for γ ≥ 1 − ( λ80 )2 .

       C.3   α-R EGULARITY VS C ONTEXT D IVERSITY
1210
1211   In recent years, there has been significant attention on the optimality of the Greedy algorithm in
1212   1-objective bandit problems (Bastani et al., 2021; Kannan et al., 2018; Raghavan et al., 2018; Hao
1213   et al., 2020). A common theme among these studies is assuming that the feature vector follows a con-
1214   tinuous distribution that satisfies specific diversity conditions. MORR-Greedyalways achieve optimal
1215   regret under any form of context diversity assumptions which can drive λmin (E[x(t)x(t)⊤ |Ht−1 ]) ≥
1216   λ0 . Given the results in the single-objective setting, this is not surprising at all.
1217   The γ-regularity condition is fundamentally different from context diversity condition. Naturally,
1218   in the single-objective setting, we cannot guarantee that the greedy algorithm will perform well
1219   under the γ-regularity condition. However, when the number of objectives is sufficiently large, the
1220   intersection of the multi-objective setting and the γ-regularity condition leads to free exploration,
1221   resulting in optimal regret. The following example highlight cases where the assumption of context
1222   diversity is not met, yet the regularity condition remains valid, enabling the MORR-Greedyoptimal
1223   performance in many-objective bandit problems.
1224   Example 1 (Containing Fixed Arms) Imagine a situation where one feature vector is a continuous
1225   variable while the other arms are fixed. For example, let x1 (t) be uniformly distributed over Bd
1226   while x2 (t) = x2 , . . . , xK (t) = xK are fixed at some points in Sd−1 . By Lemma C.1, Pχ (t)
1227   satisfies γ-regularity for all γ ∈ (0, 1). However, it is easy to see that diversity is not satisfied
1228   because λmin (E[x(t)x(t)⊤ |θ̂(t) = x2 ]) = λmin (x2 x⊤
                                                            2 ) = 0.
1229
       Example 2 (Low-Randomness Distribution) Consider a scenario where the feature vectors are drawn
1230
       from a finite set of discrete points. Despite the lack of diversity, if these points are strategically
1231
       chosen to cover Sd−1 adequately, the regularity condition can     p still be satisfied. For example, suppose
1232
       there is a set of points P = {a1 , a2 , . . . , aN } that contains 1 − γ 2 -net of Sd−1 . Assume that x1 (t)
1233   be chosen uniformly from the N1 < d points in P with the largest first coordinates, and other arms
1234   x2 (t), . . . , xK (t) be chosen from the remaining points. Obviously, Pχ (t) satisfies γ-regularity with

1584
1585
       We compare the empirical Pareto regret of MORR-Greedy in a linear bandit setting with other
                                                                                            ∗
       multi-objective algorithms. We experiment with a linear bandit, ym (t) = N (xTi θm     , 0.12 ) for all
1586
       i ∈ [K] and m ∈ [M ]. For each problem instance, M objective parameters are selected randomly
1587
       uniformly from the positive part of Sd−1 and then K(> 2M ) feature vectors are drawn from Bd .
1588   In the case of fixed arms, to ensure a certain degree of regularity, the first M feature vectors are
1589   drawn from the multivariate normal distribution with the true objective parameter as the mean and
1590   a covariance matrix of 0.1Id . Subsequently, we use the vectors were scaled to ensure that their
1591   magnitudes fall within the range of (3/4, 1). The other K − M vectors are drawn uniformly at
1592   random from Bd . Among M of K − M vectors are scaled to have length longer than 3/4, while
1593   the rest of them to have their magnitudes smaller than 3/4. Limiting the magnitudes of the feature
1594   vectors prevents meaningless results caused by excessively large Pareto fronts. For the experiment
1595   with varying arms, contexts are drawn from uniform distribution on Bd . Our results are averaged
1596
       over 10 different instances for each (d, K, M )-combination, and we conduct 10 reputations for 1
       problem instance.
1597

1599   and MOGLM-UCB (Lu et al., 2019) with tuned parameters for contextual algorithms. We evaluated the
1600   performance of each algorithm in both cases of fixed arms and stochastic arms. When playing with
1601   stochastic arms, the feature vectors are drawn the uniform distribution on Bd and only contextual
1602
       algorithms MORR-Greedyand MOGLM-UCB are compared. For MOGLM-UCB, we use the confidence

1762   {β1 , · · · , βM }-sets. First, we note that the performance of the low-diversity {β1 , · · · , βM }-set is

1765
       vectors located near the center of the positive part of Sd−1 , and there is a high probability that at
       least one true objective parameter exists close to these vectors. In contrast, when initializing with
1766
       a high-diversity {β1 , · · · , βM }-set, the regret is larger, but it can be observed that exploration ter-
1767
       minates earlier in the same λ setting. In this case, by selecting diverse features, we can accelerate
1768   the increase of the minimum eigenvalue of the Gram matrix. Consequently, we confirmed that our
1769   algorithm performs well with initial objective parameters of various diversity levels.
