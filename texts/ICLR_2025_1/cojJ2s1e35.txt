009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Various world model frameworks are being developed today based on autoregres-
012            sive frameworks that rely on discrete representations of actions and observations,
013            and these frameworks are succeeding in constructing interactive generative models
014            for the target environment of interest. Meanwhile, humans demonstrate remark-
015            able generalization abilities to combine experiences in multiple environments to
016            mentally simulate and learn to control agents in diverse environments. Inspired by
017            this human capability, we introduce World modeling through Lie Action (WLA),
018
               an unsupervised framework that learns continuous latent action representations to
               simulate across environments. WLA learns a control interface with high controlla-
019
               bility and predictive ability by simultaneously modeling the dynamics of multiple
020
               environments using Lie group theory and object-centric autoencoder. On bench-
021            mark synthetic and real-world datasets, we demonstrate that WLA can be trained
022            using only video frames and, with minimal or no action labels, can quickly adapt
023            to new environments with novel action sets.
024

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Originally proposed as a framework to support automatic planning and decision-making, world mod-
029   els (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) predict future states conditioned on actions, allowing agents to antic-
030   ipate the consequences of their interactions. There have been extensive efforts in the development
031   of world models and related learning methods. Many of them rely on discrete representations of
032   actions and/or images (Hafner et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023) in order to leverage the techniques of
033
      autoregressive inference that are extensively developed in language modeling. Recent works such as
      Genie (Bruce et al., 2024) and LAPO (Schmidt & Jiang, 2023) have advanced the field by learning
034
      interactive world models with few/no explicit action labels. These models have shown promising
035
      results in capturing complex dynamics and discrete actions in specific environments.
036
037   Humans, however, possess an extraordinary ability to generalize learned skills across diverse envi-
038   ronments by leveraging continuous and compositional representations of actions. For example, after
039
      mastering basic movements in a few 2D action-adventure games, a person can quickly adapt to a
      game of a different type(e.g. Pac-Man) by leveraging the knowledge of common 2D concepts such
040
      as moving in continuous directions (Schmidt, 1975; Poggio & Bizzi, 2004). Continuous action rep-
041
      resentations allow for smooth transitions and fine-grained control, while compositionality enables
042   complex actions to be built from simpler primitives, facilitating generalization and transfer learning
043   (Flash & Hochner, 2005; Botvinick & Plaut, 2004).
044
045
      Inspired by this human capability, we hypothesize that, in order to learn an interactive world model
      that generalizes across environments, it is essential to construct an environment-agnostic simula-
046
      tor that embraces continuous and compositional action representations. In this paper, we introduce
047
      World modeling through Lie Action (WLA), an unsupervised framework that aims to learn such
048   a simulator. Our approach models the nonlinear and continuous transition operators in the obser-
049   vation space with a Lie group that acts linearly on partitioned latent vector spaces, where each
050   partition corresponds to different objects and fundamental action axes. This allows us to capture the
051   continuous and compositional nature of actions, enabling seamless generalization across different
052   environments. Our method extends the capabilities of existing world models by introducing the con-
053   tinuity and compositionality in the form of Lie group structure. We demonstrate the effectiveness of
      WLA on 2D game and 3D robotics environments in terms of generalization and adaptability.

      2     P ROBLEM S ETTING
074
075   Before introducing our framework, we formalize the problem that WLA is meant to solve. WLA is
076   a mathematical framework for learning an interface to interact with a family of diverse environments
077
      sharing common basic rules of composition and continuity, such as a family of 2D games. To further
      elaborate, we introduce our definition of environment.
078
079
080   2.1   E NVIRONMENT
081
082   Definition 1. An environment E is a pair (X , T ) where X is a space of observations, and T is a set
083   of nonlinear transition operators on X . Let us use XE and TE to denote the space of observations
084   and the set of transitions of E, respectively. Thus, if x : I → XE is a sample trajectory of E with
085
      time domain I ⊂ R+ , then for all t, t + δ ∈ I, we have gt,δ (x(t)) = x(t + δ) for some gt,δ ∈ TE .
086
      We assume that the dataset consists of a set of trajectories sampled from all environments {Ej }j . For
087
      example, in the family of simple 2D action games, the transitions in one environment may be related
088
      to jumping and running. In another environment, actions may contain rotations and and powerups.
089
090   Strictly speaking, because gt,δ differs for each x, we should use the notation gt,δ,x to denote the
091   transition of x(·), and in cases where all trajectories in E can be enumerated, we should use the
092
      notation gt,δ,i to denote the transition of xi (·). However, for brevity, we may use gt,δ in place of
      gt,δ,i with the understanding that it is a variable that differs across trajectories. Also, because not
093
      all the observation sequences in the real world are realized by a finite set of discrete actions like
094
      buttons, we do not assume in this definition that the transitions in the environment can be grounded
095   on such actions (e.g., button presses).
096

098   2.2   C ONTROLLER I NTERFACE P ROBLEM
099
100
      We aim to construct an interactive interface for the world model that approximates a given (set of)
      environment(s), which in this study is/are assumed deterministic. We propose constructing such
101
      an interface through an inter-environmental simulator like WLA. In what follows, we rephrase this
102
      goal with the definition of environment introduced above. If A is a topological space of all possible
103   instantaneous inputs to the interface, we informally define the Controller Interface Problem (CIP)
104   for the environment E as below:
105

112   In more informal words, CIP is a problem of building a controller interface that approximates the rule
113   of dynamics in a target environment. There are two settings for CIP, which we call (1) unstructured
114   CIP and (2) structured CIP. In unstructured CIP, the problem includes that of specifying the space of
115   A itself. In structured CIP, the problem is to find the map with A that is prespecified, for example,
116   by hardware restrictions. When A is chosen to be finite, unstructured CIP may be analogous to
117
      building a set of controller buttons for a game E, and structured CIP may be analogous to finding
      the correspondence map between a given controller input and transitions in the environment. Once
118
      the CIP is solved for E, the user may iteratively apply the action sequence of choice to x ∈ X to
119
      generate a new trajectory within E.
120
121   Bruce et al. (2024) solve unstructured CIP through a Inverse Dynamic model fI : TE → A and a
122   Forward Dynamic model fF : X × A → TE . Meanwhile, fF is trained so that fF (x(t), fI (gt,1 )) =
123
      x(t + 1), where the transition gt,1 is represented as the pair (x(t), x(t + 1)). This way, fF serves as
      CtrlE itself in our formulation of unstructured CIP. However, in this paper, we will mainly focus on
124
      structured CIP. The greatest challenge in structured CIP is the deficiency of action labels. We show
125
      that WLA can efficiently solve the structured CIP with few labels by solving unstructured CIP with
126   Lie group action as part of the process. From this point onward, we use ”CIP” to imply structured
127   CIP unless described otherwise.

130   3        M ATHEMATICAL C ONCEPT OF WLA FRAMEWORK
131
132   WLA is a framework for solving CIP for multiple environments by leveraging the power of an inter-
133   environmental simulator to aggregate the compositional and continuity rules of the dynamics that
134   are shared across all environments. Our inter-environmental simulator uses the algebraic structure
135   of the Lie group and an object-centric autoencoder to represent the continuity and compositionality
136
      of the group elements that realize the trajectories x, which are sampled across all environments.
137   Through the Lie-group theoretic structure of transitions learned across multiple environments, WLA
138   solves CIP for all environments by first mapping the input action to the transition operators in the
139   inter-environmental simulator and then by mapping the transitions in the simulator to the observation
140   space to generate the ”imagined” trajectory based on the user inputs. The map Ctrladapt from the
141   action signals to the transitions in the latent space is trained separately. By constructing Ctrl through
142
      our inter-environmental simulator instead of the black-box autoregressive model, we can improve
      the performance of Ctrl on multiple environments.
143
144   The core of the WLA framework is the environment-agnostic simulator, which consists of an
145   encoder-decoder pair (Φ, Ψ) that relates every observation to a state in a vector latent space. While
146   the previous approaches solve CIP separately for each environment, WLA solves the problem
147   through the simulator that describes, in the form of Lie group action, the common composition-
148
      ality and continuity rules of dynamics in all environments. In the following subsections, we provide
      an informal explanation to convey the rough idea of each component in our framework. Please see
149
      Appendix section A for the formal explanation.

      4.1    T IME DISCRETIZATION
257
258   In an implementation, the time series dataset is discretized, and an interval I is represented as an
259   increasing subset {τ0 < τ1 < · · · < τT } ⊂ R+ . As such, a trajectory x(·) is represented by the
260   discrete signal [x(τt )]t . From here on forward, whenever we use the “hard bracket” x[t] := x(τt ),
261   we assume that the signal is discretized and that t is a natural number. Also, just for brevity, we
262   assume in this section that the observations are evenly spaced, so that τt − τt−1 = ∆. We also
263   denote the past observations as x[: t] := (x[0], . . . , x[t − 1]). We can similarly define the discretized
264   version of other variables such as a[t], A[t], λ[t], θ[t].
265   In CIP, CtrlE was designed to take (x[: t], a[: t]) to compute λk [t] and θk [t]. Likewise, we implement
266   IDM FΦ,Ψ as a function that takes two consecutive frames (x[t], x[t + 1]) as an input instead of the
267   actual transition gt , because the operator itself is not observable. This strategy has been successfuly
268   taken in the past (Mitchel et al., 2024; Koyama et al., 2024; Miyato et al., 2022). Because this
269   section pertains to implementation, we will use this discretized notation throughout this section. See
      Appendix A for a more formal explanation.

330   5     R ELATED W ORK
331
332   Several works have been done on (latent) dynamics models that can generalize across environments,
333   many of them purporting to solve a reinforcement learning problem. Hafner et al. (2023) propose
334   a method designed for the purpose of solving reinforcement learning tasks in various environments
335   and uses a recurrent latent space and discretized states learned with action and reward-labeled se-
336
      quences. Lee et al. (2022) also use multiple environments to train generalist reinforcement learning
      agents. Our method, on the other hand, is an unsupervised generative interactive framework trained
337
      to generate a video itself in response to user action inputs. Raad et al. (2024) use a language-
338
      annotated dataset to build a world model that can be used to solve tasks in multiple environments.
339   Mondal et al. (2022) associate group actions with the transition as in our approach but focus on
340   embedding-invariant reward optimization and do not generate future observations.
341
342
      The three major features of WLA are that it is (1) unsupervised, (2) object-centric, and (3) able
      to capture the continuous dynamics in a way that all transitions are compositional by design. We
343
      borrow much of the idea from (Wu et al., 2022), an object-centric framework with slot attention ar-
344
      chitecture and temporal position embedding. It preserves the temporal consistency through residual
345   connections, in contrast to our algebraic structure. To encourage the compositionality of dynamics,
346   Rybkin et al. (2018) combine past frames through an MLP to predict future frames. Valevski et al.
347   (2024) use conditional diffusion to model the dynamics autoregressively, providing a high-quality
348   continuous simulator. Because the effect of the action is modeled in a black box, the input of ”no
349   action” does not necessarily map to an identity operator in the observation space, which corrupts the
350   generation in the long run. To mitigate this undesired effect, they use the technical trick of noise
351   augmentation. For the algebraic design of our Controller map that guarantees the compositional-
352   ity, we build on the theory of (Koyama et al., 2024; Mitchel et al., 2024), which are unsupervised
353
      methods that learn the underlying symmetry structure by predictions. Their method, however, does
      not consider continuous dynamics that are non-autonomous (time-dependent). Mondal et al. (2024)
354
      consider Koopman-based modeling with complex diagonal latent transition operators related to our
355
      A, but their latent actions are additive, and ambient transitions are time-homogeneous.
356
357   As an effort to learn an interactive generative environment, (Watter et al., 2015) have advocated to
358   control dynamical systems in latent space. However, our method has much more in common with
359
      VPT (Baker et al., 2022). VPT learns FI : X × TE → A and CtrlF : X × A → TE separately
      as noncausal and causal maps, and it learns the noncausal part with action labels. WLA is different
360
      in that it learns without labels the E-agnostic noncausal structure in the form of an underlying Lie
361
      group via equivariant autoencoder (Φ, Ψ). Genie (Bruce et al., 2024) and LAPO (Schmidt & Jiang,
362   2023) also share a similar philosophy as VPT, except that they do not use labels in learning CtrlI .
363   Unlike WLA, their controller interface does not map actions through the latent space that reflects the
364   structure of compositional and continuous action. (Goyal et al., 2021) took the approach of separat-
365   ing object files from schemata to describe the dynamics. However, in their context, our schemata is
366   modeled with much stronger inductive bias of compositionality and continuity. Chen et al. (2023)
367   build an interactive system by conditioning the diffusion generation on user stroke inputs. However,
368   they do not consider the dynamics behind the system with objects and compositionality.

524   7        C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we introduced WLA for the CIP, a method that uses a simulator to capture the rules
527   of composition and continuity between environments through the algebraic relations of a Lie group
528   and object-centric modeling. Using the structured latent model, we can improve the controllability
529
      and predictive power of the controller interface. The empirical results demonstrate that WLA is
      capable of modeling real-world robot actions in 3D environments, in addition to 2D game environ-
530
      ments. Importantly, it is the first of its kind as a generative interactive framework that is based on
531
      a state-space model. However, there are still several limitations that need to be resolved to scale up
532   the framework further. Firstly, our method does not account for the possible randomness of the en-
533   vironment. This problem might be addressed by utilizing stochastic process modeling, for example.
534   Secondly, in our model, we assume a priori that transitions in the environment commute with each
535   other, and the number of rotations in the latent dynamics is specified by the user. NFT (Koyama
536   et al., 2024), one source of our inspiration, however, learns the group dynamics without relying on
537   such an assumption. Future work, therefore, includes the development of methods to build a latent
538   state-space model with fewer prior assumptions and more freedom.

      A.5.2    A REMARK REGARDING THE REPRESENTATION POWER OF OUR MODEL
852
853   Because our fundamental assumption is that all G is a the group, every g ∈ G is assumed invert-
854   ible. Also, as we have mentioned earlier, we assume G to be abelian as well. Therefore, strictly
855   speaking, transitions operators that are nilpotent or idempotent cannot be expressed. While this
856   might seem to restrict our modeling capability, our model can construct a transition that mimics

857                                                    L                cos Θk (t) − sin Θk (t)
      these operators. Firstly, because M (g(t)) =        k exp(Λ(t))                              with
858                                                                     sin Θk (t) cos Θk (t)
                Rt                     Rt
859   Λk (t) = 0 λk (s)ds, Θk (t) = 0 θk (s)ds, we can create a sequence M (g(t)) → 0 by considering
860   Λ(t) → −∞. Likewise, because limt→t∗ a(t) → 0 for some fixed t∗ ∈ I is plausible for equa-
861   tion 12, we can also model a sequence similar to g(t) → g∗ for some fixed g∗ ∈ G as well. In fact,

863   1X World model dataset and simulated world setting like ProcGen which are both likely to involve
      transitions like nilpotent and idempotent operations.

867
868   The encoder was configured with a depth of 9 layers, a width of 216, a patch size of 8, 12 attention
869   heads, and 6 slots. The decoder had a depth of 3 layers and a patch size of 16, converting the latent
870   slots to images using the mechanism from slot attention (Locatello et al., 2020). For the attention
871   modules on the patch tokens, we employed GTA (Miyato et al., 2023).
872   We trained the model using the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 5 × 10−4 and a weight
873   decay of 0.1 for 100 epochs. We set the sparsity regularizer scale α to 0.01. Linear warmup cosine
874   annealing was applied with 10 warmup epochs and an initial learning rate of 1 × 10−5 .
875
876   B.2   P ROC G EN
877
878   For the encoder and the decoder, we used the same setting as for the Phyre experiment, except
879   that the number of slots is 20. The IDM module, which converts latent states z into latent action
880   parameters (λ, θ), was implemented using a 1D convolutional architecture with SiLU activation and
881
      LayerNorm. We trained the model using the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 5×10−4 over
      200 epochs. We trained the model with 16 frames: 12 frames for prediction, followed by 4 burn-in
882
      frames.

884   When training Ctrladapt with action labels (as described in Section 4.3), we input the action label
885   sequences a(t) together with the latent states z(t) into a spatio-temporal transformer (Xu et al., 2020)
886   configured with a depth of 4 layers and 4 attention heads. Ctrladapt was trained using the AdamW
887   optimizer with a learning rate of 5 × 10−4 for 100 epochs. The encoder-decoder pair (Φ, Ψ) was
888
      frozen during the training of Ctrladapt .

891
      In this experiment, each 256×256 RGB video frame was converted into an 8×8 grid of 18-bit binary

      tokens using MAGVIT2. Consequently, we trained our model on the time series of these tokens
893   and replaced the reconstruction losses with logistic losses, as the tokens are binary. Additionally,

895   architecture to model the time evolution and alignment of the encoded tokens. Specifically, since
896   there are 8 × 8 = 64 tokens in total, we treat them as if they are “slot” tokens. Also, we set the
897   number of rotation angles to J = 16.
