009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The alignment of Large Language Models (LLMs) is achieved through fine-tuning
012            with human preference data, where preference optimization has become a critical
013            part of the process. Many methods have scaled LLM performance by incorporat-
014            ing self-judgement, highlighting the importance of unifying LLM-as-a-judge with
015            the alignment process. One such method, called Self-rewarding LLMs, iteratively
016            samples new data from the model to improve alignment using self-judgement.
017            Since this additional data is generated by the LLM, we argue that similar im-
018
               provements can be achieved without new data. We propose a method that reuses
               alignment data in the form of a self-judgement classification task and defines a
019
               multi-objective optimization problem. Our self-judgement task is derived from a
020
               simple transformation of the primary alignment data, asking the LLM to select the
021            superior response. It does not introduce new data beyond existing alignment data.
022            Thus, we claim that the improvements are due to positive interference between the
023            two tasks. We focus on a direct preference optimization method called Odds-Ratio
024            Preference Optimization (ORPO). We conduct a thorough study of linear scalar-
025            ization on two objectives and introduce two alternative approaches that vary the
026            emphasis on alignment versus self-judgement objectives. Our results in Mistral
027            7B indicate a promising direction for fine-tuning LLMs on multiple objectives,
028            particularly to improve performance on related tasks without additional natural
029
               language data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) acquire the majority of their linguistic knowledge and capabilities
034
      during the pre-training phase, where they learn to predict subsequent tokens from vast amounts of
035
      text data, encompassing trillions of tokens Cao et al. (2024). To further refine their abilities and
036   adapt to new tasks, LLMs undergo supervised fine-tuning, a transfer learning method that enhances
037   their performance on downstream applications. One such application is alignment of LLMs.
038
039
      Alignment of LLMs to human values, and instruction-following is achieved by a combination
      of supervised fine-tuning and preference optimization. Once an LLM is fine-tuned using super-
040
      vised labels, additional training on pairs of responses—where one response is preferred over the
041
      other—significantly improves alignment performance. This process, known as preference optimiza-
042   tion, was first introduced through Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) on GPT-3
043   Ouyang et al. (2022). In RLHF, the model is rewarded for generating the preferred response and pe-
044   nalized for the less desirable one, enabling the LLM to align with human values such as helpfulness
045   and truthfulness based on these preferences. A prominent method for achieving preference opti-
046   mization is Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) Rafailov et al. (2023), which eliminates the need
047   for explicit reward models or reinforcement learning. Instead, DPO directly approximates a loss
048   function based on implicit rewards. One work we use as a baseline further simplifies DPO. Odds
049   Ratio Preference Optimization (ORPO) Hong et al. (2024) integrates preference optimization and
050
      supervised fine-tuning into a single loss function using the concept of odds ratio, improving align-
      ment performance while reducing computational requirements by half.
051
052   Self-rewarding Yuan et al. (2024) offers a novel approach to enhancing the performance of LLMs
053   on alignment tasks through iterative fine-tuning. In this method, responses are sampled from the
      LLM after each iteration to generate fresh data. A key innovation in this approach is training the

128   2.1   P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
129
130   To align pretrained Large Language Models (LLMs) with human intents and teach them to follow
131   instructions, they are fine-tuned on instruction-following datasets. The first example of instruction-
132   following datasets came with the Flan family Chung et al. (2022), which introduced a family of
133   language models that were fine-tuned using supervised fine-tuning (SFT). Supervised Fine-Tuning
134   (SFT) optimizes the log likelihood of the target outputs in the language model. Since then, GPT-
135
      3 Ouyang et al. (2022) introduced Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) on a
      then-novel kind of data for language models: ”human preferences.” By providing contrasting ex-
136
      amples, GPT-3, along with its vast scale, earned LLMs their reputation as instruction followers.
137
      Since then, preference optimization has become a critical component of aligning language models
138   to human intent. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) Rafailov et al. (2023) provided a significant
139   breakthrough in this process by simplifying preference optimization. They removed Reinforcement
140   Learning components from the pipeline, such as training reward models, and approximated rewards
141   directly in terms of the LLM’s probabilities of producing output labels. DPO uses a supervised loss
142   for preference optimization and performs as well as RLHF in most cases. Building on the same
143   principles, Odds Ratio Preference Optimization (ORPO) Hong et al. (2024) further simplifies the
144   pipeline by removing the reference LLM, and by combining SFT and preference optimization into
145   a single loss function made of multiple objectives. Due to its efficiency benefits over DPO, we use
146
      ORPO as our baseline.
147
148   2.2   I TERATIVE F INE -T UNING
149
150   Iterative fine-tuning Yuan et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2024) achieves stellar scaling of performance
151   across various tasks, including alignment. By generating new data, either as preferences or as
152   both instructions and preferences, iterative fine-tuning efforts strive to improve LLMs continuously.
153
      However, they rarely show any improvements after three iterations, and the creation of new data is
      an expensive ordeal. One notable work that we provide an alternative to is self-rewarding Yuan et al.
154
      (2024). They train an LLM across two dimensions: LLM-as-a-judge and alignment to human pref-
155
      erences. This is very similar to what we do with our multi-objective ORPO Loss. This contrasts with
156   other works that utilize the RLHF pipeline and use LLM-as-a-judge as part of the reference model
157   Lee et al. (2024). In both works, LLM-as-a-judge and alignment objectives are initially fine-tuned
158   without preference optimization, and no improvements in alignment performance are reported dur-
159   ing this stage. Only after performing self-sampling iterations with preference optimization—where
160   both chosen and rejected responses are utilized—do these approaches show improvements in align-
161   ment performance. In contrast, during supervised fine-tuning, only the chosen responses are used
      to adjust the model. Our approach eliminates the need for their self-sampling stages by simulta-

      2.3   LLM- AS - A -J UDGE
166
167   It’s extremely expensive to collect human preference data. Replacing humans with LLMs to act as
168   judges would automate the alignment process. However, fine-tuning LLMs to be judges does not
169   come without its challenges. Various biases, such as verbosity bias, position bias, and format bias,
170   hinder the wide adoption of LLM-as-a-judge Thakur et al. (2024); Zheng et al. (2023). Position
171   bias causes the judges to prefer the first-mentioned response with more probability Shi et al. (2024),
172   while verbosity bias occurs when LLMs tend to prefer verbose responses over succinct and accurate
173   ones. LLM-as-a-judge models are trained to produce elaborate explanations and scores to judge
174
      responses. Our work only uses pairwise selection tasks without using the explanations, which is
      similar to On-policy self-judgment Lee et al. (2024). Self-rewarding is also inspired by LLM-as-a-
175
      judge and enhances their prompt to elicit scores with a breakdown of important components. This
176
      simple innovation proves pivotal as their approach is unable to harness similar improvements without
177   the prompt enhancements.

      2.4   M ULTI -O BJECTIVE AND M ULTI -TASK L EARNING IN LLM S
180
181   Recent advancements have seen a surge in the use of multi-objective preference optimization for
182   large language models (LLMs). Methods like Multi-Objective DPO (MODPO) Zhou et al. (2023)
183   adapt the original DPO approach into a multi-objective problem using techniques akin to linear
184   scalarization Hu et al. (2023). Other studies integrate multiple objectives within the alignment task
185   Wang et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2024), focusing on factors like helpfulness, truthfulness, honesty,
186   and instruction adherence. These methods manage trade-offs between objectives, ensuring the LLM
187   operates on the Pareto front where no other optimization outperforms it.
188   However, as illustrated in Figure 2, our work differs by introducing a separate task—self-
189   judgment—that is significantly distinct from the original instruction-following task. While existing
190   multi-objective (MO) methods aim to align the model across different sub-goals within the same
191   task, our inclusion of self-judgment data broadens the scope, placing our work firmly within the
192   multi-task learning (MTL) framework. In MTL, the objective is to learn shared representations
193   across multiple tasks to enhance generalization through positive interference. Although MTL of-
194
      ten grapples with challenges like negative interference due to gradient conflicts Shi et al. (2023);
      Yu et al. (2020); Sener & Koltun (2018), we employ MO optimization techniques as a method to
195
      achieve effective MTL without the computational overhead of mitigating these conflicts.

202   Pareto Optimality in Multi-objective Optimization
203   Pareto optimality Miettinen (1999) is a key concept in multi-objective optimization, where the goal is
204   to optimize several objectives simultaneously. A solution is said to be Pareto optimal if no objective
205   can be improved without causing a deterioration in at least one other objective. In mathematical
206   terms, consider a multi-objective optimization problem with k objective functions:
207
208                                   min F (x) = (f1 (x), f2 (x), . . . , fk (x))
209                                   x∈X

210
      where F (x) is a vector of objective functions f1 , f2 , . . . , fk , and X is the feasible set of solutions.
211
      A solution x∗ ∈ X is said to be Pareto optimal if there is no other solution x ∈ X such that:

389   4.2   A PPROACH BASED C OMPARISON
390
391   Since we observed a rapid increase in self-judgement (SJ) log odds during scalarization experiments
392
      (as shown in Figure 3 right), we concluded that the model overfits the SJ data due to its simplicity or
      underfits the alignment task by comparison. To improve the fit on the alignment task, we employed
393
      the conjoined approach; to reduce overfitting on the self-judgement task, we used the conditional
394
      approach.
395
396   These two methods are based on conflicting hypotheses. Our results indicate that the conditional
397   hypothesis is correct. Specifically, our linear scalarization models overfit the SJ data while fitting
398
      the alignment data reasonably well. In the conjoined approach, the loss for alignment data will be
      higher if their probabilities are lower than those for the easily maximized SJ data. Therefore, the
399
      conjoined method leads to very high probabilities for chosen responses compared to baseline and
400
      linear scalarization approaches. For example, we found that the odds ratio for conjoined alignment
401   examples exceeds 50, whereas the same ratio remains below 7 for scalarization experiments, as
402   shown in Figure 3 (left).
403
404
      Our approach-wise results in Table 2 demonstrate that the epoch-by-epoch and task-by-task strate-
      gies, where we alternated between alignment and self-judgement ORPO training, performed signif-
405
      icantly worse than the baseline. We believe this is due to the use of longer context lengths and the
406
      clash between the simplicity of the binary self-judgement task and the complexity of the alignment
407   objective. However, the low win rate in task-by-task training suggests that explanations used in
408   the self-rewarding LLM-as-a-judge framework are crucial for maintaining alignment performance.
409   Nonetheless, our simultaneous training approach exhibited less performance degradation, likely due
410   to positive interference between the two tasks and the control offered by assigning extra weight to
411   the alignment task.
412
      We consider scalarization (SJ-SFT), applied epoch-by-epoch, as control experiments that justify the
413   superior results of the scalarization and conditional approaches. By replacing the ORPO loss for
414   SJ data with supervised fine-tuning on chosen data (SFT), we observe considerable performance
415   degradation. This indicates that we have incurred overfitting due to the usage of the same data for
416   both SJ and alignment tasks. Through simultaneous training, we avoid such performance degra-
417   dation caused by data duplication. This further indicates the importance of our results in seeking
418   simultaneous scaling of two differing objectives in the multi-task learning framework. We observe
419   that careful tweaking of their importance results in better-than-baseline performance.
420
421   4.3   V ERBOSITY B IAS
422
423
      All of our multi-objective experiments (with the exception of conjoined method) tend to generate
      longer responses compared to the baseline, which diminishes the potential benefits of our approach.
424
      We attempted to tackle this with prompting, but it caused no change in the final win-rate. We
425
      attribute this to two factors:
426
427          • Padding used for self-judgment data: Since self-judgment data contains very lengthy
428            prompts, and LLMs are usually trained with a consistent length for the entire dataset for
429            efficiency, padding is necessary. For self-judgment data, padding is more abundant than for
430            the baseline. In our results, we observe that this impacts the average length of the trained
431            models’ outputs on Alpaca Eval. There is no direct way to address this issue, as LLMs
               require padding during training.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
            • Verbosity Bias from LLM-as-a-judge training: Verbosity serves as a useful heuristic for
433           predicting winning responses over losing ones. LLM-as-a-judge learns quickly to rely on
434           such measures to reduce training loss. This trap is the biggest challenge we faced, and we
435           have not found a consistent solution for it.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
439
      Our results indicate a promising direction for fine-tuning LLMs. The reliance on high-quality data
440
      to scale LLM performance and achieve AGI will eventually reach a ceiling limited by human per-
441
      formance. Therefore, methods like multi-objective optimization inspired by human learning, are
442   needed to scale LLMs without relying on additional high-quality data. Our self-judgment task pro-
443   vided no extra information beyond what was already available in the dataset; rather, it aimed to
444   constrain the solution space to a subset where two objectives are satisfied instead of one. This also
445   introduced unhelpful noise in form of verbosity. We believe that future work will unlock the full
446   potential of this approach by addressing the tendency of multiple objectives to interfere and fit to
447   noise. Perhaps, we may see improvements in performance that surpass what self-rewarding LLMs
448   have achieved with their iterative fine-tuning approach.
