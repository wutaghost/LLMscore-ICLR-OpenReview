009                                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Knowledge tracing (KT) aims to predict students’ future knowledge levels based
012                on their historical interaction sequences. Most KT methods rely on interaction
013                data between students and questions to assess knowledge states and these ap-
014                proaches typically assume that the interaction data is reliable. In fact, on the
015                one hand, factors such as guessing or slipping could inevitably bring in noise in
016                sequences. On the other hand, students’ interaction sequences are often sparse,
017                which could amplify the impact of noise, further affecting the accurate assess-
018
                   ment of knowledge states. Although data augmentation which is always adopted
                   in KT could alleviate data sparsity, it also brings noise again during the process.
019
                   Therefore, denoising strategy is urgent and it should be employed not only on the
020
                   original sequences but also on the augmented sequences. To achieve this goal, we
021                adopt a plug and play denoising framework in our method. The denoising tech-
022                nique is adopted not only on the original and the augmented sequences separately
023                during the data augmentation process, but also we explore the hard noise through
024                the comparison between the two streams. During the denoising process, we em-
025                ploy a novel strategy for selecting data samples to balance the hard and soft noise
026                leveraging Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). This approach optimizes the
027                ratio of explicit to implicit denoising and combines them to improve feature rep-
028                resentation. Extensive experiments on four real-world datasets demonstrate that
029
                   our method not only enhances accuracy but also maintains model interpretability.

      the original interaction sequences on the final question may be incorrect due to some factors, e.g., the
055   mistake made by the carelessness of the student or the unreliable knowledge states. These outliers
056   can be regarded as noise inevitable in interactions. Furthermore, sparse problem which always ex-
057   isted in interaction sequences could amplify the impact of noise and affect the representation of the
058   students’ final knowledge states. To address this issue, recent researches have utilized various deep
059   models (Nakagawa et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b) to capture the sequentiality
060   within sequences to against the risk of noise and employed contrastive learning for data augmen-
061   tation to mitigate the problem of data sparsity. These approaches aim to uncover unique learning
062   patterns or regularities among students, but they are always not involved in the noise generated in
063
      data augmentation, which is harmful to learn robust sequence representations, too.
064   In Figure 1, we assume that each question is answered some times. Question q1 was answered
065   correctly three times at first and incorrectly two times latter, indicating that q1 has been gradually
066   mastered. Based on this, the impact of the incorrect responses should be reduced on the target
067   question. While for question qm , it was answered correctly nine times and incorrectly once. This
068   single incorrect response very likely might be a mistake, so we want to treat it as noise and ignore
069
      its impact on the target question. The comparison shows that the answers to the target question
      are completely different before and after denoising. Without denoising, the noise might lead to a
070
      decline in the student’s knowledge states, resulting in incorrect answers. Besides noise, sparse is
071
      also an important problem in KT. Previous methods which leverage the data augmentation strategy
072   may amplify the influence of noise due to the unreliability in original sequences, which is harmful to
073   the performance of the model. Considering the two problems, we proposed the Sequence Denoising
074   with Self-Augmentation for Knowledge Tracing method, which aims to address the noise in both
075   the original sequences and augmented sequences from the explicit and implicit perspectives.
076
      Specifically, after data augmentation to expand sparse interaction sequences, we employ a combined
077
      strategy of soft and hard denoising (Lin et al., 2023a; Yuan et al., 2021). Intuitively, the sample that
078   is off center has the high probability is the outlier, that is to say, the noise usually has the high
079   sharpness. To measure the degree of outlier, inspired by Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
080   (Zhai et al., 2024), we adopt the singular value to reflect the informative signals. Obviously, the
081   larger the singular value, the more smooth. To make full use of the singular value, on the one hand,
082   we maximize the largest singular value to reduce the sharpness, then the impact of noise could
083   be weakend. On the other hand, to fully explore the noise, considering that the augmented data
084   are randomly generated, the greater the feature difference, the more likely it is to be hard noise.
085   Therefore, we not only leverage the SVD as the regularization to reduce the impact of noise, but
086
      also is helpful to the mining of noisy samples. Specifically, we apply SVD to explicitly explore the
      hard sequence data, while performing implicit denoising on the remaining data, and then merged
087
      the denoised sequences. This approach not only enhances the robustness of the model in noisy
088
      environments but also improves its interpretability.
089
090   The main contributions are summarized as follows. Firstly, we introduce a denoising module that
091   combines explicit and implicit denoising methods and integrate them for the first time in the Knowl-
092
      edge Tracing (KT) task. This not only improves the prediction accuracy but also enhances the in-
      terpretability of the model. Secondly, our method is based on data augmentation, which has already
093
      been utilized in KT. We further utilize data augmentation to denoise both the original sequence and
094
      the augmented sequence and combine them to obtain a better data representation, which improves
095   the reliability of the data while addressing the issue of data sparsity. Thirdly, we make use of Sin-
096   gular Value Decomposition (SVD) (Chen et al., 2019) not only for individual data sequences as a
097   regularization term, but also to distinguish between explicit and implicit denoising samples, effec-
098   tively extracting the underlying patterns and structures from the data and improving the data quality.

141   Sequence Denoising Recent KT studies have explored many methods to learn better feature rep-
142   resentations. However, in practice, historical sequences usually contain some inherently noisy
143   items (such as guessing or slipping) (Zhang et al., 2023), which are always ignored, resulting in
144   inaccurate final predictions. Although few approaches utilize denoising to achieve better data rep-
145   resentation in KT field, the denoising methods are widely applied in sequence-related tasks, which
146   could inspire our research. Some studies addressed this challenge in a ”soft” way (Zhang et al.,
147
      2022), trying to implicitly reduce the noise on the learned sequence representation, i.e., assigning
      lower weights to those interactions that are less important relative to the final interactions, but in this
148
      way, noise still existed in sequence and may affect performance. Furthermore, some studies directly
149
      use explicit denoising (Tong et al., 2021; Han et al., 2023) to delete irrelevant items in the sequence.
150   However, the historical sequence usually contains some interactions that are irrelevant to the next
151   interaction, which may not be inherent noise, so eliminating them without carefully selected may
152   lose useful information. Inspired by these observations, we want to combine explicit and implicit
153   denoising, balancing the influence of primary and secondary features on the next interaction. This
154   strategy not only ensures performance but also enhances the model’s interpretability.

164   The student’s historical learning interactions are defined as V , where V = (v1 , v2 , ..., vm , vn , ..., vt ).
165   Each interaction consists of a tuple: vt = (qt , rt ). Where qt represents the tth question, and rt
166   represents the response result, which is either 0 or 1, with 1 indicating a correct response and 0
167   indicating an incorrect response. Given the interaction sequence and the next question qt+1 , KT
168   aims to determine the probability of correctly answering the next question:

173   Data-Augmentation Due to the complexity and uniqueness of the KT task, directly applying ex-
174   isting data augmentation methods from CV (Chen et al., 2020; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997)
175   and NLP (Gao et al., 2021) is challenging. Therefore, we follow the data augmentation approach
176   in contrastive learning and use various data augmentation methods to generate relevant views of
177
      students’ learning histories. These methods include: 1. Question Masking: Replacing some ques-
      tions in the original history with a special mask without changing their responses. 2. Interaction
178
      Cropping: Randomly extracting a subsequence from the original history. 3. Question Replacement:
179
      Transforming the original question into a simpler or more difficult one based on its response. 4.
180   Interaction Shuffling: Reordering interactions within a subsequence of the original history. Each
181   of these data augmentation methods is applied with different probabilities. Ultimately, this results
182   in augmented question sequences: Q+              + +            + +            +

192   Denoising Module To obtain better sequence representations, we applied data augmentation to both
193   question and interaction sequences. However, since the original sequences may contain inherent
194   noise, the same denoising process was applied to both the augmented and original sequences. On
195   one hand, the augmented and original sequences usually share similar data distributions and noise
196
      characteristics. On the other hand, the augmented sequences provide richer and more diverse data,
      helping prevent the model from overly focusing on specific noise patterns. This approach allows
197
      us to effectively remove noise while preserving data diversity, thereby improving the quality of
198
      sequence representations. In the denoising process, we adopted the denoising method fden proposed
199   in (Zhang et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023b). This method utilizes information from both augmented
200   and original sequences to generate noiseless sub-sequences through a specific denoising mechanism.
201   Specifically, fden filters noise by leveraging intra-sequence information:

298   4       E XPERIMENTS
299
300   Datasets and Baselines We use four widely-used public datasets to evaluate the performance of the
301   model including Algebra051 , Algebra061 , Assistment092 and Slepemapy3 . These methods not
302   only include DKT (Piech et al., 2015), DKT+ (Yeung & Yeung, 2018), DKVMN (Zhang et al.,
303   2017) which leveraging the deep learning for KT, but also contain SAKT (Vaswani, 2017; Pandey
304   & Karypis, 2019),AKT (Ghosh et al., 2020), CL4KT (Lee et al., 2022) and DTransformer (Yin
305   et al., 2023) which leveraged the attention mechanism into KT task.
306   Experimental Setup and Results We adopt the data augmentation parameters from CL4KT for
307   fairness in the experiments. To rigorously evaluate the model’s performance, we apply five-fold
308   cross-validation by dividing the data into five subsets and sequentially assessing the model’s perfor-
309   mance on each subset. We also adopt the baseline strategy of applying early stopping when the AUC
310   on the validation set does not increase over 10 epochs, providing a reliable quantitative evaluation.
311   This experiment is conducted on an NVIDIA 3090 GPU with 24GB of memory.
312   Table 1 summarizes the evaluation results. After integrating the denoising module, our method
313   achieved the best performance across all four datasets. We also tested other denoising methods on
314   the baseline models: -ID represents implicit denoising, -ED represents explicit denoising, and -DA
315   represents our combined method. The results show that neither implicit nor explicit denoising alone
316   matches the performance of our combined method. Explicit denoising tends to degrade performance
317   due to excessive filtering, while implicit denoising struggles to handle sparse interactions, negatively
318
      impacting the representation of knowledge states. Specifically, due to the sparse student interaction
      data, using only explicit denoising for DKT-ED may lead to over-denoising, especially in a base
319
      model like DKT, where the risk of performance degradation is greater. Our method effectively
320
      solves these denoising issues, boosting performance.

      Denoising Robustness Analysis Table 2 illustrates the impact of different denoising methods on
350   model performance under Gaussian noise. Gaussian noise is introduced to simulate the random
351   behavior of students during answering, allowing us to assess the robustness of the model. The ex-
352   perimental results indicate that as the noise intensity increases, the model’s performance gradually
353   declines. We compared two datasets with shorter runtimes. For the baseline model CL4KT, per-
354   formance degrades more significantly as the noise ratio rises, attributed to the model’s sensitivity
355   to noisy data, which impacts predictive accuracy. In contrast, our denoising module shows greater
356   stability under varying noise conditions. This is mainly because explicit denoising alone can lead
357   to the loss of important behavioral information crucial for accurate predictions. Implicit denoising
358
      reduces the impact of noisy data and better reflects students’ true behavioral patterns. However, re-
      lying solely on implicit denoising may inadequately address certain low-probability behaviors that
359
      significantly affect the student’s knowledge state updates. Our combined approach, integrating both
360
      explicit and implicit denoising methods, balances these challenges, enhancing model interpretability
361   and stability while ensuring accurate performance. This demonstrates the robustness of our denois-
362   ing strategy in handling diverse noise levels while maintaining reliable results.

481   In this paper, we present a plug and play framework for KT that incorporates a denoising module.
482   To address the noise problem and interaction sparsity, we apply both explicit and implicit denoising
483   during the data augmentation preocess, effectively reducing noise in both augmented and original
484   data, which enhances sequence representations. Our comprehensive experiments demonstrate that
485   our model significantly outperforms current state-of-the-art methods in terms of prediction accuracy
      and data representation quality.
