009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Continuous space-time video super-resolution (C-STVSR) aims to simultaneously
012
               enhance video resolution and frame rate at an arbitrary scale. Recently, im-
               plicit neural representation (INR) has been applied to video restoration, repre-
013
               senting videos as implicit fields that can be decoded at an arbitrary scale. How-
014
               ever, the highly ill-posed nature of C-STVSR limits the effectiveness of current
015            INR-based methods: they assume linear motion between frames and use inter-
016            polation or feature warping to generate features at arbitrary spatiotemporal po-
017            sitions with two consecutive frames. This restrains C-STVSR from capturing
018            rapid and nonlinear motion and long-term dependencies (involving more than
019            two frames) in complex dynamic scenes. In this paper, we propose a novel C-
020            STVSR framework, which captures both holistic dependencies and regional mo-
021            tions based on INR. It is assisted by an event camera – a novel sensor renowned
022            for its high temporal resolution and low latency. To fully utilize the rich tem-
023
               poral information from events, we design a feature extraction consisting of (1) a
               regional event feature extractor – taking events as inputs via the proposed event
024
               temporal pyramid representation to capture the regional nonlinear motion and (2)
025
               a holistic event-frame feature extractor for long-term dependence and continuity
026            motion. We then propose a novel INR-based decoder with spatiotemporal embed-
027            dings to capture long-term dependencies with a larger temporal perception field.
028            We validate the effectiveness and generalization of our method on four datasets
029            (both simulated and real data), showing the superiority of our method.
030
031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The real world’s visual information, e.g., edge and object motion, is continuous, spanning both time
034   and space dimensions. However, the limited I/O bandwidth and sensor size of modern systems (Del-
035   bracio et al., 2021; Parker, 2010) confines us to record videos at low frame rates and fixed resolutions.
036   This limitation has profound repercussions across various computer vision applications, e.g., en-
037   compassing immersive experiences in virtual reality (Zhang, 2020; Lee et al., 2020), traffic analysis
038   in autonomous driving (Zou et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2019). To address this limitation, recent research
039
      works (Chen et al., 2022; 2023b) have explored restoring videos with continuous resolutions and frame
      rates, referred to as Continuous Space-Time Video Super-Resolution (C-STVSR).
040
041   Recently, implicit neural representation (INR) has been applied to video restoration: it represents
042   images or videos as neural fields that can be decoded at any resolution with a pointwise MLP de-
043   coder (Cao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a). One of the seminal INR approaches is LIIF (Chen et al.,
044   2021), which is designed for arbitrary-scale image SR. This line of research soon extended to the
045
      video domain. In this context of C-STVSR, VideoINR (Chen et al., 2022) employs a fixed STVSR
      model (Xiang et al., 2020) that extracts features from two consecutive video frames. Then, it in-
046
      troduces a temporal INR to generate inverse backward warping optical flow (Niklaus & Liu, 2020)
047
      to warp features. Lastly, it employs a spatial INR, similar to LIIF, to decode the RGB frame with
048   arbitrary resolution. Building upon this, MoTIF (Chen et al., 2023b) improves VideoINR by using for-
049   ward motion estimation, reducing gaps and holes in the temporal INR, which are typically caused
050   by the randomness and discontinuities associated with backward warping (Park et al., 2021). These
051   methods depend solely on two successive RGB frames, rendering the task of predicting inter-frame
052   motions ill-posed. Consequently,, it becomes challenging to accurately capture highly dynamic mo-
053   tion (e.g., regional high-speed or nonlinear movements) and to model long-term dependencies that
      extend across more than four frames.

072   Motivation and Contributions. Event cameras are bio-inspired sensors, known for their high tem-
073
      poral resolution and low latency (< 1us) (Zheng et al., 2023; Gallego et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020a).
      Recent research has demonstrated the potential of events in guiding various video super-resolution
074
      (VSR) (Lu et al., 2023; Jing et al., 2021) and video frame interpolation (VFI) tasks (Tulyakov et al., 2021;
075
      2022; He et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021). However, utilizing events to facilitate joint video super-resolution
076   and frame interpolation is a challenging area yet to be explored.
077
078   This paper introduces HR-INR, a novel INR-based method that leverages events for jointly guiding
079
      VSR and VFI. It adeptly captures regional, rapid motion and holistic, long-range motion dependen-
      cies, as shown in Fig. 1. To capture the regional motion, we propose Temporal Pyramid Repre-
080
      sentation (TPR) to construct a time-series pyramid structure around the pivotal timestamp of events
081
      (Sec. 3.1). Different from the evenly divided timeline representations, like voxel grids (Tulyakov
082   et al., 2021; 2022; Gallego et al., 2020), time surfaces (Sironi et al., 2018), time moments (Han et al., 2021;
083   Lu et al., 2023) and symmetric cumulative (Sun et al., 2022), TPR offers finer temporal granularity with
084   less complexity and effectively captures rapid motion changes, as shown in Fig. 2 (a). Additionally,
085   our method is capable of processing multiple frames and their associated events, which empowers it
086   to estimate holistic, long-range motion that involves more than two frames.
087
      Accordingly, we design two specialized feature extractors: the regional event feature extractor (RE)
088   and the holistic event-frame feature extractor (HE), see Sec. 3.2. Both extractors are grounded in the
089   Swin-Transformer architecture (Liang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022a; Liang et al.), renowned for its effi-
090   cacy and efficiency in video enhancement tasks. RE is a lightweight network designed specifically
091   for extracting local information from our event TPR. Meanwhile, HE employs a more sophisticated
092   approach, utilizing long-term and multi-scale fusion strategies to integrate both events and frames.
093   Consequently, our training and inference strategy requires HE to be invoked only once for multi-
094   frame interpolation. Subsequently, the extracted features from RE and HE are fused as the output of
095   the feature extraction module.
096   After fusing the regional and holistic features, we propose a novel INR-based spatial-temporal de-
097   coding module (Sec. 3.3). Our motivation is to avoid gaps and holes typically found in optical
098   flow-based warping and multi-frame fusion, as identified in previous research (Chen et al., 2023b;
099   2022; He et al., 2022; Tulyakov et al., 2022). To accomplish this, we propose an implicit temporal
100   embedding designed to transform timestamps into focused attention vectors on long-term features.
101   This approach ensures attention is also given to long-distance dependencies, which is crucial for
102
      effectively modeling long-term temporal dependencies. Subsequently, inspired by LIIF (Chen et al.,
      2021), we employ spatial embedding to achieve arbitrary up-sampling in the spatial dimension.
103
104   We conducted experiments on two simulated and two real-world datasets. The results validate the
105   superiority of our method and its excellent generalization capabilities on real-world datasets. Our
106   approach is the first event-based method to achieve continuous space-time video super-resolution,
107   surpassing frame-based methods, as shown in Fig. 1. It also excels in individual VSR and VFI
      metrics compared to previous event-based methods.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      Space-time Video Super-Resolution aims to enhance the resolution and frame rate of a video si-
111
      multaneously (Haris et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2020; Xiang et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021). In comparison to
112   two-stage solutions, where VFI (Jiang et al., 2018; Xue et al., 2019; Niklaus & Liu, 2020; Niklaus et al.,
113   2017; Cheng & Chen, 2020) and VSR (Liu et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2021; Yue et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
114   2021; Tian et al., 2020; Isobe et al., 2020) methods are applied sequentially, simultaneous space-time
115   video super-resolution reduces cumulative errors and leverages the natural relations between VFI
116   and VSR methods. Zooming Slow-Mo (Xiang et al., 2020) uses temporal interpolation to generate
117   missing frames and aligns temporal information using a deformable ConvLSTM network. Simi-
118   larly, TMNet (Xu et al., 2021) extracts short-term and long-term motion cues in videos by modulating
119   convolution kernels. However, these methods cannot simultaneously achieve spatiotemporal resolu-
120
      tion across arbitrary scales.
121   INR for VFI and VSR have achieved space-time video super-resolution with arbitrary resolu-
122   tions (Chen et al., 2022; 2023b) by learning videos implicit neural representations (INRs). These
123   methods primarily estimate optical flows from two consecutive frames to warp features into arbi-
124   trary space-time coordinates, which are then decoded using MLP layers. However, by relying on just
125   two consecutive frames, these methods cannot inherently model long-term motions (involving three
126
      or more frames) and fail to accurately capture local, inter-frame, non-linear motions due to missing
      inter-frame information.
127
128   Event-guided VFI and VSR seek to boost performance by incorporating the biologically inspired
129   event cameras (Zheng et al., 2023). Previous works have demonstrated the potential of event-guided
130   VFI, which mainly focus on modeling non-linear motion with events (Paikin et al., 2021; Tulyakov et al.,
131   2021; Wu et al., 2022; Tulyakov et al., 2022; He et al., 2022; Song et al., 2022; 2023). EFI (Paikin et al., 2021)
132
      exclusively adopts the synthesis approach for intermediate frame generation. TimeLens (Tulyakov
      et al., 2021) and TimeLens++ (Tulyakov et al., 2022) employ events to model nonlinear motion correla-
133
      tions, integrating both synthesis and warping-centric approaches. Building on these advancements,
134
      CBM-Net (Kim et al., 2023), introduces a motion field to handle complex movements. However,
135   while these VFI methods utilize events to capture inter-frame motion, they fail to establish long-term
136   dependencies beyond two frames and support simultaneous VSR. The realm of event-guided video
137   super-resolution has also been explored. E-VSR (Jing et al., 2021) highlights that high-frequency
138   temporal information from events is beneficial to recovering high-frequency spatial information.
139   Like our work, EG-VSR (Lu et al., 2023) employs events to comprehend INR, allowing for video
140   ups-sampling with arbitrary scale. However, the INR of EG-VSR cannot interpolate frames. Con-
141   trasting these methods, we pioneer using events to enable concurrent VSR and VFI across arbitrary
142   spatial-temporal scales, i.e.,, C-STVSR.
143   Video Long-term Dependence Modeling is a crucial aspect of VSR and VFI. For instance, Ba-
144   sicVSR (Chan et al., 2021) and BasicVSR++(Chan et al., 2022) enhance VSR performance by pro-
145   cessing multi-frame inputs through an alignment module to model long-term motion correlations.
146   Similarly, in the VFI domain, many methods (Suzuki & Ikehara, 2020; Nah et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020)
147   employ RNN or LSTM to model sequences of frames, capturing long-term dependencies effectively.
148   Furthermore, Zooming Slow-Mo (Xiang et al., 2020), TMNet (Xu et al., 2021), and RSST (Liang et al.)
149
      leverage multi-frame inputs in the joint task of VSR and VFI, showcasing the importance of in-
      tegrating multiple frames for improved modeling of video dynamics. However, current C-STVSR
150
      methods (Chen et al., 2022; 2023b), and event-based VFI methods (Tulyakov et al., 2021; He et al.,
151
      2022; Tulyakov et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023), primarily rely on estimating optical flow between two
152   consecutive frames. Therefore, they are challenging to handle multi-frames as inputs, inherently
153   undermining their capability to model long-term dependencies.

206   3.1      T EMPORAL P YRAMID R EPRESENTATION
207
208   Firstly, we represent the event stream E into a frame-like form that the network can process. To
209   capture holistic motion, we partition all events during [0, 1] of the timeline into M equal intervals
210   using a voxel grid (Tulyakov et al., 2021; Gallego et al., 2020), denotes as Ev with dimensions M × H ×
211   W . In practice, event representation methods like voxel grid (Tulyakov et al., 2021; Gallego et al., 2020)
212
      and its extended structure, symmetric cumulative event representation (Sun et al., 2022), achieve time
      granularity by uniformly dividing time into intervals with resolutions of 1/M .
213
214   However, for C-STVSR, capturing intricate motion and edges requires a finer granularity. Merely
215   increasing M to enhance detail sharply raises computational costs; for instance, capturing 1/1000
      second intervals within a second necessitates expanding M to 1000, which is computationally im-

      3.3    INR- BASED S PATIAL -T EMPORAL D ECODING
297
298   In this section, we employ INR-based spatial-temporal decoding to effectively retrieve RGB frames
299   at any desired time and resolution. To achieve this, we leverage a temporal INR to generate features
300   at any timestamp and a spatial INR to upscale the features to any spatial resolution.
301
      Temporal Embedding: We utilize learned temporal embedding as attention vectors to aggregate
302   the fused feature in the channel dimension in a time-specific manner. At a given timestamp t, we
303   first use a two-layer MLP to increase its dimension to Ct , resulting in a temporal attention vector
304   (as illustrated in Fig. 4 (a)). The visualization of the learned temporal attention is depicted in Fig. 4
305   (b), exhibiting variations across both time and channel dimensions. This attention vector is then
306   multiplied directly with Rt to generate the temporal embedded feature Rts . This temporal INR
307   allows for a larger temporal perception field without the need for estimating optical flows. Next,
308   a 1 × 1 convolution is applied to compress Rts to Cts dimensions, reducing the complexity of the
309   spatial embedding and decoding.
310   Spatial Embedding and Decoding: To upscale the temporal embedded features to any desired
311   spatial scale, we utilize a similar approach to previous works (Chen et al., 2022; 2023b; 2021). We
312   query the four nearest neighbors in the temporal embedded feature for each spatial coordinate and
313   concatenate these with their distances for spatial embedding. A four-layer MLP decoder computes
314   the RGB values, which are then aggregated through area-weighted interpolation for arbitrary-scale
315   super-resolution. Similar to (Lu et al., 2023), we use the Charbonnier loss (Lai et al., 2018) as the
316
      fundamental loss function for training.

      The main contributions are: (I) Event temporal pyramid representation for capturing short-term dy-

877   B     M ORE D ETAILS ABOUT N ETWORK S TRUCTURE
878
879   Owing to space constraints in the main text, this section provides a more detailed account of the net-
880   work’s results. The description begins with the Regional Event Feature Extraction component, cov-
881   ering its preprocessing steps and the architecture of the Swin Transformer Encoder Blocks (STEB).
882   Subsequently, the Holistic Event-Frame Feature Extraction is detailed, which, besides STEB, incor-
883   porates the Swin Transformer Decoder Block (STDB). In summary, this section elaborates on the
884   preprocessing for both feature extraction components and details the structures of STEB and STDB.
885
886   B.1   R EGIONAL E VENT F EATURE E XTRACTION :
887
888
      This section delves into Regional Event Feature Extraction, denoted as fre . fre receives Tempo-
      ral Pyramid Representation (TPR) Ep corresponding to timestamp t, formatted as RL×Mp ×H×W .
889
      Here, L signifies the number of TPR layers, while Mp indicates the count of moments within each
890
      layer. fre involves two primary stages: preprocessing and feature extraction via STEB. Specifically,
891   this preprocessing first increases its dimension to Cr through a 1 × 1 convolution operation. This
892   preprocessing alters the TPR’s shape to L × Cr × H × W . Feature extraction is then performed
893   using STEB, where both the input and output retain their shape throughout the process.
894
      Swin Transformer Encoder Blocks (STEB): The STEB structure, proven effective in video super-
895
      resolution and frame interpolation (Liu et al., 2022b; 2021; Geng et al., 2022), is adopted in our design.
896
      The input and output shapes of STEB are represented as L × C × H × W for clarity. Initially, for
897   a window size of M × M , specifically 4 × 4 in our implementation, the input is partitioned into
898   disjoint windows of (M × M ) × N × (H/M ) × (W/M ) × C dimensions. Then, each window
899   is compressed to form a feature map of shape (M × M ) × (N × H × W/M 2 ) × C. Following
900   this, Layer Normalization (Ba et al., 2016) and window-based multi-head self-attention (Liu et al.,
901   2021; Geng et al., 2022) are computed for each window, succeeded by further transformation via
902   another Layer Normalization and a Multi-Layer Perception. Shifted window-based multi-head self-
903   attention (Liu et al., 2021; Geng et al., 2022) is then employed to establish cross-window connections.
904   After applying one STEB structure, a second STEB is introduced with an identical configuration,
905
      except the input feature window is offset by (M/2) × (M/2). In total, four STEBs are utilized for
      comprehensive feature extraction in the Regional Event Feature Extraction.
906
907
908   B.2   H OLISTIC E VENT-F RAME F EATURE E XTRACTION :
909
      This section details the Holistic Event-Frame Feature Extraction (fhe ) module. The fhe module
910
      processes inputs vIin and vEv to generate the output F g . Input vIin has a shape of N ×3×H ×W ,
911
      whereas vEv is structured as M ×H ×W , with M indicating the count of moments derived from the
912   events. The output, F g , has a structure of Cg ×H ×W . Initially, fhe transforms vIin and vEv . vIin
913   and vEv are transformed into features with dimensions N × C × H × W and N − 1 × C × H × W
914   using 1×1 convolution and reshaping, followed by processing through a STEB module. The process
915   then employs a combination of downsampling and STEB to extract features across multiple scales,
916   each possessing a distinct view field. Notably, STEB maintains the same resolution and channel
917   count in both its input and output. Next, the Swin Transformer Decoder (STDB) is utilized for
      fusing and decoding the dual-modal features. Each STDB block receives three types of inputs: the


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      preceding STDB’s output and the outputs from the vIin and vEv encoder sections, all at a matching
919   resolution. In practice, the outputs from the vIin and vEv encoders are concatenated, transformed
920   into queries via an MLP layer, and then fused utilizing a multi-head attention mechanism. Notably,
921   the first STDB module lacks a preceding STDB output, hence defaulting this input to zero. The
922   final output, F g , is derived from the last STDB’s output, post reshaping and 1 × 1 convolution. In
923   summary, fhe employs a sequence of intricate yet efficacious transformation and fusion procedures,
924   designed to extract multifaceted features from inputs vIin and vEv for subsequent tasks in video
925   super-resolution and frame interpolation.

937   this dataset into 100 training, 16 validation, and 17 test sets. Upon frame extraction, we employed
938   the widely-used event simulation method vid2e (Gehrig et al., 2020), which accounts for real noise
939   distribution, ensuring robust neural network training with enhanced generalization. In generating
940   the inputs and ground truth of the network, we adopted and extended the previous works (Chen et al.,
941   2023b; 2022) to accommodate multi-frame input. Specifically, input and ground truth (GT) frames

1476   D.9            BAD CASE ANALYSIS
1477
       We have observed that our method has certain limitations in some cases (Fig. 14). For example,
1478   when restoring color information, although our model can accurately reconstruct the contours of
1479   objects, the color information is often distorted or missing. This issue primarily arises due to the
1480   lack of color information in the event stream. We believe that with future advancements in color
1481   event technology, this problem will be effectively addressed.
