009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities but
012            often struggle to align with human preferences, leading to harmful or undesirable
013            outputs. Preference learning, which trains models to distinguish between preferred
014            and non-preferred responses based on human feedback, has become a crucial com-
015            ponent for ensuring that LLMs align with human values. Despite the widespread
016            adoption in real-world systems, a thorough theoretical understanding of the general-
017            ization guarantees for these models remains lacking. This paper bridges that gap by
018            introducing a new theoretical framework to analyze the generalization guarantees of
019
               models trained with direct preference optimization. While existing generalization
               theory often focuses on overparameterized models achieving near-optimal loss or
020
               models independent of the training process, our framework rigorously assesses
021
               how well models generalize after a finite number of gradient steps, reflecting real-
022            world LLM training practices. By analyzing the reward margin associated with
023            each sample and its trajectory throughout training, we can effectively bound the
024            generalization error. We derive learning guarantees showing that, under specific
025            conditions, models trained with DPO can correctly discern preferred responses
026            on unseen data with high probability. These insights are empirically validated on
027            contemporary LLMs, underscoring the practical relevance of our theory.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable abilities to generate human-like text
033   and acquire diverse capabilities (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022; Anil et al., 2023). However,
034   these models are not necessarily aligned with human preferences and can inadvertently produce
035   harmful or undesirable outputs. Thus, aligning language models with human preferences has emerged
036   as a crucial problem, which aims to harmonize AI behaviors with human intentions and ensure safe
037   and desirable behavior. At the heart of this alignment process lies preference learning, where the goal
038   is to train a language model policy that can distinguish, according to some reward model, preferred
039
      vs. non-preferred responses based on human feedback. Specifically, preference learning involves
      optimizing a language model policy to produce higher rewards for more preferred responses, guided
040
      by preference data provided in the form of comparative judgments. Despite the empirical success in
041
      real-world systems (OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), theoretical analysis of
042   preference learning, particularly in the context of alignment, is still in its early stages and remains
043   largely underdeveloped. A rigorous understanding of how preference learning affects LLM behaviors
044   and generalization guarantees has not been studied. This paper aims to fill the critical gap.
045
      In particular, theoretically analyzing the generalization behavior of preference learning is a highly non-
046
      trivial task due to the complexity of modeling language. Existing generalization theories (Attias et al.,
047
      2019; Dziugaite & Roy, 2017; Lei et al., 2019; Valle-Pérez & Louis, 2020) are not directly applicable
048   because they typically consider simpler learning tasks such as regression and classification, where the
049   output is either a scalar or categorical label. In contrast, training language models entails dealing with
050   the output space of sentences, which is considerably more complex. Moreover, existing generalization
051   theory typically considers overparameterized models that achieve near-optimal loss (Allen-Zhu et al.,
052   2019; Cao & Gu, 2020; Subramanian et al., 2022; Arora et al., 2019) or are independent of the training
053   process (Arora et al., 2018; Lotfi et al., 2022; 2023). This does not match real-world practices, where
      large language models are often fine-tuned for a limited number of gradient steps. This discrepancy


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      suggests the need for a new theoretical framework that can capture the intricacies of preference
055   learning methods and the unique challenges posed by aligning language models.
056
057   To address the challenges, we provide a new theoretical framework designed to analyze the general-
058
      ization guarantees for models trained with preference optimization loss (Rafailov et al., 2023). Our
      framework focuses on the generalization of models after finite gradient steps when the loss is within
059
      a constant factor of its initial value, which matches more closely with the real-world practices of
060
      aligning LLMs. To the best of our knowledge, generalization results in this setting have not been
061   obtained before. Under our framework, we can rigorously characterize the conditions under which the
062   model can correctly discern between preferred and non-preferred outcomes on future unseen sample.
063   Central to our framework, we characterize the generalization error through the lens of reward margin,
064   which quantifies the log-likelihood difference between the preferred and non-preferred responses. A
065   sample’s generalization error is zero when the reward margin is positive and vice versa. The key to
066   our framework lies in analyzing the reward margin associated with each sample and its dynamics
067   throughout the training process. By bounding the trajectory of the reward margin, we can effectively
068   quantify the generalization error of preference learning.
069   To summarize our results, we provide conditions under which we can guarantee with high probability
070   that the reward margin for all training samples is positive (Theorem 4.1), meaning that the loss can
071   correctly predict all training samples into the preferred vs. non-preferred categories within finite
072   gradient steps. Building on the results, we provide guarantees and bound the generalization error for
073   new inputs drawn from the preference distribution (Theorem 4.2). Our theorems indicate that the
074   conditions under which the guarantees hold with high probability depend on the number of preference
075
      concepts (e.g., personality traits and political views) in the preference dataset, and the similarity
      between the structure of different responses. Additionally, the results indicate that as the number
076
      of samples per concept increases, the time needed to achieve a given training loss or generalization
077
      bound decreases. These results shed light on practical aspects of aligning LLMs, helping explain the
078   benefit of scale and characterizing the behavior of alignment loss on new samples. We empirically
079   validate these theoretical insights in Section 5, affirming their relevance to real-world LLMs.
080

      We summarize our key contributions in the following:

            1. To our knowledge, this work represents the first attempt to comprehensively analyze the gen-
083
               eralization behavior of finite-step preference learning from a rigorous theoretical standpoint.
084            We introduce a novel theoretical framework specifically designed to examine the generaliza-

            2. We provide new learning guarantees on how DPO can correctly distinguish the preferences

135   Framework overview under practical considerations. We provide a theoretical framework for
136   analyzing the generalization guarantees of learning preferences using DPO. Under this framework,
137
      we can rigorously characterize the conditions under which the model can correctly predict preferred
      responses for new input prompts. While existing generalization theory typically considers overpa-
138
      rameterized models that achieve near-optimal loss (Allen-Zhu et al., 2019; Arora et al., 2019; Cao &
139
      Gu, 2020; Subramanian et al., 2022) or are independent of the training process (Arora et al., 2018;
140   Lotfi et al., 2022; 2023), we consider the generalization of models after finite gradient steps when the
141   loss is within a constant factor of its initial value. This scenario closely matches real-world practices,
142   where LLMs are often fine-tuned for a few epochs. The crux of our framework thus lies in analyzing
143   the reward associated with each sample and its evolution throughout training. Finding bounds on
144   the trajectory of the reward directly allows us to quantify the generalization error, which we show
145   formally in Section 4. We proceed to describe our setup in detail.

261   4.1   C HARACTERIZING THE P REFERENCE D ISTRIBUTION
262
      We characterize the preference distribution by modeling the input feature to the unembedding
263
      layer. Importantly, the features we model are designed to reflect the characteristics of the real-
264   world transformer backbone, ensuring that our theoretical analysis remains grounded in the specific
265   inductive biases and structures that are typical of such models (see careful verification in Section 5).
266   Specifically, we consider a preference distribution that consists of K pairs of clusters that correspond
267   to different concepts. In the context of alignment, the concepts can be broadly associated with different
268   personality traits, political views, moral beliefs, etc. For example, the concepts may encompass
269   common properties such as helpfulness, honesty, and harmlessness (Bai et al., 2022a), and can also
      represent much more diversified and nuanced ones like conscientiousness, non-racism, compassion,


                                                                5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      and so on (Perez et al., 2022). For each concept, we have a pair of clusters containing samples aligned
271   vs. misaligned with that concept.
272
273   To formalize, we consider a distribution P of (x, yw , yl ) that represents the set of clusters as a
274
      mixture of Gaussians with K equally weighted pairs of clusters labeled with i ∈ [K]. Each cluster is
      distributed as N (±ci + b, v 2 Id ), where ci is a unit vector representing the concept vector for cluster
275
      pair i and b is a vector with norm lb representing the shared aspect of all embeddings. Let Ci,+ be
276
      the cluster corresponding to samples aligned with concept i and Ci,− be the cluster corresponding to
277   samples misaligned with concept i. For simplicity, we can assume without loss of generality that
278   b = lb e1 in the standard basis e1 , . . . , ed for Rd . Additionally, we let each ci correspond to a standard
279   basis vector eci such that the ci are pairwise orthogonal and are all orthogonal to b. The preferred
280   and rejected response for all samples in a given cluster is fixed, and no two pairs of clusters have the
281   exact same set of responses. We define Z as the maximum number of times a token appears across
282   all preference responses. To construct the empirical training data, we sample Q i.i.d. samples from
283   each cluster and there are total N = 2KQ samples across K clusters. We will verify in Section 5
284   that our data assumption matches closely the characteristics of real-world alignment datasets.

      Practical implications. The generalization guarantee uses the fact that samples seen in training are
325   predicted correctly to ensure that a new sample from the distribution is also likely to be classified
326   correctly. This implies that the conditions for generalization are similar to those needed to guarantee
327   strong training performance, which means less interaction between different types of preferences
328   and a smaller number of clusters would also benefit the generalization error. In order to have less
329   interaction between types of preferences or clusters, it would be necessary for the cluster directions
330   to have smaller inner products which are only possible for a large number of clusters when the
331   dimension is sufficiently large. This points to one reason as to why an increase in scale can allow for
332   better model capabilities. Another aspect of the guarantees to consider is that they are for samples
333
      within the training distribution. As we see in Equation (9), the model behavior on new samples

334

335
      scale and diversity of data can bolster a model’s ability to generalize. We present a simplified bound

409   Implications. We can see that after decomposing the reward for multi-token responses into token-
410   wise terms, the gradient as seen in Equation (15) resembles that of the single-token case, albeit
411   with additional interaction terms. Notably, similar to those terms in the single-token gradient, these
412   additional terms also involve an inner product between the given embedding and the embedding of
413
      tokens in the dataset, suggesting that the correlations between embeddings continue to play a key
      role in multi-token responses. This shared structural aspect between the decomposition for multi-
414
      token and single-token reward gradients, coupled with our existing understanding of single-token
415
      guarantees, points towards a promising avenue for understanding preference learning. Considering
416   the importance of embedding correlations, as evidenced in the single-token scenario, we should
417   expect that having clusters of embeddings corresponding to different contexts along directions with
418   small inner products would help the model learn preferences within the training distribution. Given
419   the inherent complexity of learning multi-token responses, we expect the scale of the data and the
420   model to have an even more substantial influence.

474   6    R ELATED W ORKS
475
476   Alignment of LLMs. A key aspect of training and deploying large language models is ensuring
477
      the models behave in safe and helpful ways (Ji et al., 2023; Casper et al., 2023; Hendrycks et al.,
      2021; Leike et al., 2018). This is an important problem due to the potential harms that can arise in
478
      large models (Park et al., 2023; Carroll et al., 2023; Perez et al., 2022; Sharma et al., 2023; Bang
479
      et al., 2023; Hubinger et al., 2019; Berglund et al., 2023; Ngo et al., 2022; Shevlane et al., 2023;
480   Shah et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2022). A wide range of methods have been developed that utilize human
481   feedback or human preference data to train models to avoid harmful responses and elicit safer or more
482   helpful responses (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2021;
483   Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a; Nakano et al., 2022; Glaese et al., 2022; Snell et al., 2023;
484   Yuan et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022b; Lee et al., 2023; Munos
485   et al., 2023; Hejna et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Khanov et al., 2024). Particularly, the Reinforcement
      Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) framework has proven effective in aligning large pre-trained


                                                                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      language models (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a).
487   However, given its computational inefficiency, recent shifts in focus favor closed-form losses that
488   directly utilize offline preferences, like Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023)
489   and related methodologies (Azar et al., 2023; Pal et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Ethayarajh et al.,
490   2024a; Xiong et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024b; Zeng et al.,
491   2024; Calandriello et al., 2024; Muldrew et al., 2024; Ray Chowdhury et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a;
492   Gao et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Chakraborty et al., 2024). Despite the empirical success and
493   wide adoption in real-world systems (OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), fewer
494   works provide theoretical underpinnings (Azar et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024; Im & Li, 2024;
495
      Tang et al., 2024; Ray Chowdhury et al., 2024; Tajwar et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Nika et al.,
      2024; Xiong et al., 2024). In this work, we make an initial attempt to comprehensively analyze the
496
      generalization behavior of preference optimization from a rigorous theoretical standpoint. Our work
497
      considers offline preference optimization which differs from the setting of other theoretical works on
498   preference-bases reinforcement learning (Chen et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023). We introduce a new
499   theoretical framework specifically designed to examine the generalization properties of LLMs by
500   approximating their reward dynamics, providing insights into practical aspects of aligning LLMs.
501
502   Generalization of deep neural networks. Understanding how and why deep models generalize
503   has been a subject of extensive research. One approach is through the lens of feature learning,
504   attempting to understand how models learn data-dependent features and how these features are
505   structured (Izmailov et al., 2022; Fort et al., 2020; Yang & Hu, 2021; Shi et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020;
506   Ba et al., 2022; Mousavi-Hosseini et al., 2022; Aghajanyan et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2022; Tian
507   et al., 2023). Another approach is through providing generalization bounds that quantify the expected
508   performance of the model beyond the training samples and over a data distribution (Allen-Zhu et al.,
509
      2019; Cao & Gu, 2020; Subramanian et al., 2022; Arora et al., 2019; 2018; Lotfi et al., 2022; 2023;
      Attias et al., 2019; Dziugaite & Roy, 2017; Valle-Pérez & Louis, 2020; Lei et al., 2019). While
510
      existing generalization theories typically consider simpler learning tasks such as regression and
511
      classification, our work provides generalization analysis in the context of aligning language models,
512   which entails dealing with the complex output space of sentences. Moreover, existing generalization
513   theory typically considers overparameterized models that achieve near-optimal loss (Allen-Zhu et al.,
514   2019; Cao & Gu, 2020; Subramanian et al., 2022; Arora et al., 2019) or are independent of the
515   training process (Arora et al., 2018; Lotfi et al., 2022; 2023). One line of works considers algorithmic
516   stability which allows for generalization bounds that are dependent on the number of steps (Hardt
517   et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2017). In contrast, our framework focuses on the generalization of models by
518   directly following and analyzing the reward dynamics after finite gradient steps, which matches more
519   closely with the real-world practices of aligning LLMs. Our theoretical insights are further supported
520
      by empirical validations on contemporary LLMs, as shown in Section 5.
521
522   7    C ONCLUSION
523   Our work theoretically analyzes the generalization behavior of preference learning, which remains
524   an open problem in the field of AI safety. We base our theoretical analysis on a popular alignment
525   loss, direct preference optimization, which implicitly learns a reward model. Key to our framework,
526   we analyze the reward margin associated with each sample and its trajectory throughout the training
527   process, which allows us to effectively bound the generalization error. Through rigorous analysis,
528   we establish conditions under which the model trained with DPO loss generalizes to new inputs
529   with provably high accuracy. Empirical validation on contemporary LLMs and real-world alignment
530   datasets confirms the practical relevance of our framework, offering insights crucial for developing
531
      AI systems that align with human intentions and preferences. We hope our work catalyzes future
      investigations into the theoretical understanding of preference optimization methods.

535   While our study primarily focuses on DPO as a representative case, it is important to acknowledge
536   that our analysis may not fully capture the nuances of other emerging preference learning methods.
537   We envision that our theoretical framework and insights can be extended to these methods, which
538   we discuss in Appendix C. Future work should investigate the applicability and adaptability of our
539   framework to these newer approaches, ensuring a comprehensive understanding of generalization
      across a broader spectrum of preference learning methodologies.
