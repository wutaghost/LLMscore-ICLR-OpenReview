013
014
                   In many multi-objective reinforcement learning (MORL) applications, being able
015                to systematically explore the Pareto-stationary solutions under multiple non-convex
016                reward objectives with theoretical finite-time sample complexity guarantee is an
017                important and yet under-explored problem. This motivates us to take the first
018                step and fill the important gap in MORL. Specifically, in this paper, we propose
019                a weighted-Chebyshev multi-objective actor-critic (WC-MOAC) algorithm for
020                MORL, which uses multi-temporal-difference (TD) learning in the critic step and
021                judiciously integrates the weighted-Chebychev (WC) and multi-gradient descent
022                techniques in the actor step to enable systematic Pareto-stationarity exploration
023
                   with finite-time sample complexity guarantee. Our proposed WC-MOAC algorithm
024
                   achieves a sample complexity of Õ(ϵ−2 p−2   min ) in finding an ϵ-Pareto-stationary
                   solution, where pmin denotes the minimum entry of a given weight vector p in the
025
                   WC-scarlarization. This result not only implies a state-of-the-art sample complexity
026
                   that is independent of objective number M , but also brand-new dependence result
027                in terms of the preference vector p. Furthermore, simulation studies on a large
028                KuaiRand offline dataset, show that the performance of our WC-MOAC algorithm
029                significantly outperforms other baseline MORL approaches.

033
034   1) Motivation: As a foundational machine learning paradigm for sequential decision-making,
035
      reinforcement learning (RL) has found an enormous success in many applications (e.g., healthcare
      (Petersen et al., 2019; Raghu et al., 2017b), financial recommendation (Theocharous et al., 2015),
036
      ranking system (Wen et al., 2023), resources management (Mao et al., 2016) robotics (Levine et al.,
037
      2016; Raghu et al., 2017a), and recently in generative AI (Franceschelli & Musolesi, 2024)). Also,
038   as more complex applications emerge, RL has increasingly evolved from single-objective to multi-
039   objective settings. For instance, in RL-driven short video streaming platforms (Cai et al., 2023), the
040   system sequentially displays short videos to optimize multiple rewards at the same time, including but
041   not limited to “WatchTime”, “Subscribe”, “Like”, “Dislike”, “Comment”, etc. As another example,
042   to attract diverse customers and maximize long-term total benefits, an e-commerce recommender
043   system sequentially ranks and displays products by balancing the conflicting preferences of different
044   user groups (e.g., some prefer low prices and can tolerate slow delivery, while others prefer quick
045   delivery over low prices). All of these applications entail the need for multi-objective reinforcement
046
      learning (MORL) (Stamenkovic et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021a).

065   In light of the fact that MORL is a special class of multi-objective optimization (MOO) problems, in
066   this paper, we propose a weighted-Chebyshev multi-objective actor-critic (WC-MOAC) method by
067   drawing inspirations and insights from the MOO literature. More specifically, to enable systematic
068   Pareto-front exploration with low sample complexity in MORL, our proposed WC-MOAC method
069
      uses temporal-difference (TD) learning in the critic component and judiciously integrates the weighted-
      Chebyshev (WC) and multi-gradient-descent algorithmic (MGDA) techniques in the actor component.
070
      The rationale behind our approach is three-fold: (i) Combining the strengths of value-based and policy-
071
      based RL approaches, the actor-critic framework has been shown to offer state-of-the-art performance
072   in RL; (ii) in the MOO literature, it has been shown that an optimal solution under the WC-based
073   scalarization approach (also known as hypervolume scalarization) provably achieves the Pareto front
074   even when the Pareto front is non-convex (Zhang & Golovin, 2020); and (iii) for MOO problems, the
075   MGDA method is an efficient approach for finding a Pareto-stationary solution (Désidéri, 2012). 2
076   Finally, the connection between gradient information in optimization and TD-error in RL leads us to
077   generalize the WC and MGDA approaches from MOO to our WC-MOAC method for MORL.
078   2) Challenges: However, to show that WC-MOAC enjoys systematic Pareto-stationarity exploration
079   with provable low finite-time sample complexity remains highly non-trivial due to multiple challenges:
080
       1) In the MOO literature, WC- and MGDA-based techniques are developed with very different goals
081       in mind: facilitating Pareto-front exploration and achieving Pareto-stationarity, respectively. To
082       date, it remains unclear how to combine them to achieve systematic Pareto-stationarity exploration
083       with low finite-time sample complexity simultaneously even for general MOO problems, not to
084       mention generalizing them to the more specially structured MORL problems and the associated
085       theoretical performance analysis. Indeed, to our knowledge, there is no such result in the literature
086       on integrating WC- and MGDA- techniques for designing MORL policies.
087    2) In WC-MOAC, the critic and actor components evaluate and improve the policies, respectively,
088       with an intricate dependence between these two components. Such a complex dependence
089
          between actor and critic further renders standard convergence analysis in MOO irrelevant to our
          proposed WC-MOAC methods. Thus, it remains an open question whether one can design a
090
          multi-objective actor-critic algorithm to facilitate Pareto-stationarity exploration with a provable
091
          finite-time sample complexity guarantee.
092    3) In WC-MOAC, both critic and actor components update their parameters through stochastic
093       TD-errors based on directions guided by a WC-scalarization weight vector and finite-length state-
094       action trajectories. All of these inject cumulative biases in policy parameter updates. If not handled
095       properly, such biases could significantly affect the performance of our WC-MOAC method for
096       MORL or could even lead to a divergence of policy parameter updates.

099   Pareto-stationary convergence and sample complexity guarantees. Collectively, our results provide
100   the first building block toward a theoretical foundation for MORL. Our main contributions are

      2      R ELATED W ORK
128
129   In this section, we provide an overview on three closely related areas, namely multi-objective
130
      optimization, multi-objective reinforcement learning, and RL problems with multiple rewards, thereby
      putting our work in comparative perspectives.
131
132   1) Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO): Generally speaking, MOO approaches can be broadly
133   classified into four main categories (Miettinen, 1999): 1) no-preference methods, 2) a priori methods,
134   3) a posteriori methods, and 4) interactive methods. While the latter three categories all involve
135   preference weight information from a decision maker either directly or indirectly, the first category
136
      does not require any preference information. A line of work (Fliege et al., 2019; Liu & Vicente,
      2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Sener & Koltun, 2018; Yang et al., 2024; Fernando et al., 2022; Xiao
137
      et al., 2023) has utilized the MGDA (Désidéri, 2012) technique to characterize the finite-time
138
      convergence/sample complexity of MOO problems, including one recent work on no-preference
139   MORL (Zhou et al., 2024). However, in this paper, we are concerned with the finite-time convergence
140   and effectiveness in practical MORL setting that comes with given preference weight information
141   and further enabling Pareto-stationarity exploration. A closely related work in MOO can be found
142   in (Momma et al., 2022), where the authors studied MOO problem with pre-defined preference
143   weight incorporated by proposing a WC-based MGDA approach to align the Pareto solution with
144   the preference direction. However, this work only showed the empirical effectiveness and did not
145   provide finite-time convergence results. Another closely related work in (Xiao et al., 2024) proposed a
146   direction-oriented MOO algorithm based on a weighted sum of the MGDA and the linear scalarization
147
      approaches. This is in stark contrast to the WC-scalarization technique in our approach. Extensive
      empirical comparisons are provided in Section 5 to show the superiority of our WC-MOAC method
148
      over the RL counterpart of (Xiao et al., 2024).
149
150   2) Multi-Objective Reinforcement Learning (MORL): MORL is a type of sequential decision-
151   making problems endowed with multiple rewards. Different from conventional RL problems with
152   scalar-valued rewards (e.g., Sutton & Barto (2018); Konda & Tsitsiklis (1999); Xu et al. (2020);
153
      Guo et al. (2021)), MORL is concerned with optimizing vector-valued rewards, either directly or
      through various types of scalarization. Although the studies on MORL are not new (see, e.g., Gábor
154
      et al. (1998); Parisi et al. (2016); Van Moffaert & Nowé (2014); Abels et al. (2019); Yang et al.
155
      (2019); Abdolmaleki et al. (2020); Reymond et al. (2023); Roijers et al. (2013); Ruadulescu et al.
156   (2020); Hayes et al. (2022)), finite-time convergence results for multi-objective actor-critic (MOAC)
157   algorithms remain quite limited. To our knowledge, the first MOAC algorithm was proposed in
158   (Chen et al., 2021a), which is based on deterministic policy gradients. Subsequently, a two-stage
159   constrained actor-critic algorithm was proposed in (Cai et al., 2023), where the MORL formulation is
160   different from ours and takes an ϵ-constrained scalarization approach (i.e., all except one objective are
161   reformulated as ϵ-constraints and the only remaining objective is set as the system objective). Also,
      none of the above MORL works offers finite-time convergence rate or sample complexity results.

      3) RL Problems with Multi-Reward Scalarization: We note that several RL paradigms bear some
163   similarities with MORL in the sense of having multiple rewards. The first such RL paradigm is
164   cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) (Zhang et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2021b;
165   Hairi et al., 2022), where each agent has a scalar-valued reward. However, the global objective of
166   cooperative MARL is a static weighted sum of all agents’ rewards. Similarly, many MORL problems
167   are often scalarized to enable the use of single-objective RL techniques (e.g., linear scalarization in
168   (Stamenkovic et al., 2022)). Another multi-reward RL paradigm is the constrained (also known as
169   safe) RL Cai et al. (2023), which balances multiple RL objectives with a set of predefined parameters
170   associated with the constraints to indicate the constraint levels. Due to different problem structures,
171
      these multi-reward RL problems are often concerned with other goals rather than Pareto-stationarity.

      As mentioned in Section 1, due to the fact that the objectives in MORL are conflicting in general, the
217   more appropriate and relevant learning goal and optimality notions in MORL are the Pareto-optimality
218   and the Pareto front, which are defined as follows:
219
      Definition 1 ((Weak) Pareto-Optimal Policy and (Weak) Pareto Front). We say that a policy πθ
220
      dominates another policy πθ′ if and only if J i (θ) ≥ J i (θ ′ ), ∀i ∈ [M ] and J i (θ) > J i (θ ′ ), ∃i ∈ [M ].
221   A policy πθ is Pareto-optimal if it is not dominated by any other policy. A policy πθ is weak Pareto-
222   optimal if and only if there does not exist a policy πθ′ such that J i (θ ′ ) > J i (θ), ∀i ∈ [M ]. Moreover,
223   the image of all (weak) Pareto-optimal policies constitute the (weak) Pareto front.
224
225   In plain language, a Pareto-optimal policy identifies an equilibrium where no reward objective
226   can be further increased without reducing another reward objective, while a weak Pareto-optimal
227   policy characterizes a situation where no policy can simultaneously improve the values of all reward
228
      objectives (i.e., ties are allowed). However, since MORL problems are often non-convex in practice
      (e.g., using neural networks for policy modeling or evaluation), finding a weak Pareto-optimal policy
229
      is NP-hard. As a result, finding an even weaker Pareto-stationary policy is often pursued in practice.
230
      Formally, let ∇θ J i (θ) represent the policy gradient (to be defined later) direction of the i-th objective
231   with respect to θ. A Pareto-stationary policy is defined as follows:
232
      Definition 2 (Pareto-Stationary Policy). A policy πθ is said to be Pareto-stationary if there exists no
233
      common ascent direction d ∈ Rd2 such that d⊤ ∇θ J i (θ) > 0 for all i ∈ [M ].
234
235   Since MORL is a special-structured MOO problem, it follows from the MOO literature that Pareto
236   stationarity is a necessary condition for a policy to be Pareto-optimal(Désidéri, 2012). Note that in
237   convex MORL settings where all objective functions are convex functions, Pareto-stationary solutions
238   imply Pareto-optimal solutions.

      As mentioned in Section 1, our WC-MOAC algorithm is motivated by two key observations: (i) actor-
244   critic approaches combine the strengths of both value-based and policy-based approaches to offer
245   the state-of-the-art RL performances; and (ii) an optimal solution under the WC-based scalarization

535   In this paper, we proposed a weighted Chebyshev multi-objective actor-critic (WC-MOAC) algorithm
536   for multi-objective reinforcement learning (MORL). Our proposed WC-MOAC method judiciously
537   integrates weighted Chebyshev and multi-policy-gradient techniques to facilitate systematic Pareto-
538   stationary solution exploration with provable finite-time sample complexity guarantee. Our numerical
539   experiments with real-world datasets also verified the theoretical results of our WC-MOAC method
      and its practical effectiveness.
