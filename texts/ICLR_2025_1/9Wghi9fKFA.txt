011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In the realm of neuroscience, identifying distinctive patterns associated with neu-
014            rological disorders via brain networks is crucial. Resting-state functional mag-
015
               netic resonance imaging (fMRI) serves as a primary tool for mapping these net-
               works by correlating blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD) signals across differ-
016
               ent brain regions, defined as regions of interest (ROIs). Constructing these brain
017
               networks involves using atlases to parcellate the brain into ROIs based on vari-
018            ous hypotheses of brain division. However, there is no standard atlas for brain
019            network classification, leading to limitations in detecting abnormalities in dis-
020            orders. Some recent methods have proposed utilizing multiple atlases, but they
021            neglect consistency across atlases and lack ROI-level information exchange. To
022            tackle these limitations, we propose an Atlas-Integrated Distillation and Fusion
023            network (AIDFusion) to improve brain network classification using fMRI data.
024            AIDFusion addresses the challenge of utilizing multiple atlases by employing a
025            disentangle Transformer to filter out inconsistent atlas-specific information and
026
               distill distinguishable connections across atlases. It also incorporates subject- and
               population-level consistency constraints to enhance cross-atlas consistency. Ad-
027
               ditionally, AIDFusion employs an inter-atlas message-passing mechanism to fuse
028
               complementary information across brain regions. Experimental results on four
029            datasets of different diseases demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of AID-
030            Fusion compared to state-of-the-art methods. A case study illustrates AIDFusion
031            extract patterns that are both interpretable and consistent with established neuro-
032            science findings.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   In the field of neuroscience, a key objective is to identify distinctive patterns associated with neuro-
038   logical disorders (e.g., Alzheimer’s, Parkinson’s, and Autism) by the brain networks (Poldrack et al.,
039
      2009). Resting-state functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) is widely employed among
      various neuroimaging techniques to characterize the connectivities among brain regions (Worsley
040
      et al., 2002). This results in brain networks where each node represents a specific brain region,
041
      referred to as a region of interest (ROI). Each edge indicates a pairwise correlation between the
042   blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD) signals of two ROIs (Smith et al., 2011), revealing the con-
043   nectivity between brain regions and indicating which areas tend to be activated synchronously or
044   exhibit correlated activities.
045
      Brain networks model neurological systems as graphs, allowing the use of graph-based techniques
046
      to understand their roles and interactions (Kawahara et al., 2017; Lanciano et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
047
      2023). Constructing these brain networks involves using a specific atlas to parcellate the brain into
048   ROIs. Various atlases based on different hypotheses of brain parcellation, such as anatomical and
049   functional divisions, have been proposed to group similar fMRI regions and create ROIs (Tzourio-
050   Mazoyer et al., 2002; Makris et al., 2006; Schaefer et al., 2018a). Although proper brain parcellation
051   is essential for detecting abnormalities in neurodegenerative disorders (Long et al., 2022), there is
052   no golden standard atlas for brain network classification. Relying on a single atlas for brain net-
053   work analysis has two main drawbacks. First, some voxels may not be assigned to any specific ROI,
      potentially leading to the loss of important information. Second, each atlas is based on a different


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      parcellation hypothesis. The BOLD signal of an ROI is averaged from all voxels within it, possibly
055   missing detailed information. To address these limitations, recent works have proposed using mul-
056   tiple atlases with different parcellation modes to enhance multi-atlas brain network analysis. Some
057   methods (Chu et al., 2022; Mahler et al., 2023) independently encode brain networks from various
058   atlases and then aggregate the graph representations as a late feature fusion scheme for the final
059   prediction. Another approach (Lee et al., 2024) incorporates early feature fusion by incorporating
060   multi-atlas information from the raw data and using the fused feature for representation learning.
061   However, these methods (1) neglect the need of consistency across atlases, potentially leading to the
062   under-utilization of cross-atlas information; and (2) lack ROI-level information exchange through-
063
      out the entire representation learning process, which could hinder the models’ ability to discern
      complementary information across different atlases.
064
065   In this paper, we propose an Atlas-Integrated Distillation and Fusion network (AIDFusion) to ad-
066   dress the aforementioned limitations by utilizing atlas-consistent information distillation and cross-
067   atlas complementary information fusion. Specifically, AIDFusion introduces a disentangle Trans-
068   former to filter out inconsistent atlas-specific information and distill distinguishable connections
069
      across different atlases. Subject- and population-level consistency constraints are applied to enhance
      cross-atlas consistency. Furthermore, to facilitate the fusion of complementary information across
070
      ROIs in multi-atlas brain networks, AIDFusion employs an inter-atlas message-passing mechanism
071
      that leverages spatial information. In summary, our key contributions are:
072
073          • We propose a multi-atlas solution for brain network classification with fMRI data. AIDFu-
074            sion takes full advantage of multi-atlas brain networks by enhanced atlas-consistent infor-
075            mation distillation and intense fusion of cross-atlas complementary information.
076
             • We evaluate AIDFusion on four resting-state fMRI brain network datasets for different
077            neurological disorders. Our results demonstrate the superiority of AIDFusion over state-
078            of-the-art baseline methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency in brain network clas-
079            sification.
080
             • We present a case study that underscores the intriguing, straightforward, and highly in-
081
               terpretable patterns extracted by our approach, aligning with domain knowledge found in
082            neuroscience literature.

087   2.1   B RAIN N ETWORK A NALYSIS WITH VARIOUS ATLASES
088
      Multi-atlas methods introduce multiple brain atlases for each neuroimage, which can provide in-
089
      formation that complements each other and offers ample details without being restricted by the par-
090
      cellation mode. MGRL (Chu et al., 2022) pioneered the construction of multi-atlas brain networks
091   using various atlases. It applied graph convolutional networks (GCNs) to learn multi-atlas repre-
092   sentations and perform graph-level fusion for disease classification. METAFormer (Mahler et al.,
093   2023) proposed a multi-atlas enhanced transformer approach with self-supervised pre-training for
094   Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) classification. A graph-level late fusion was utilized to aggregate
095   the representations of different atlases. Lee et al. (2024) employed a multi-atlas fusion approach
096   that integrates early fusion on the raw feature to capture complex brain network patterns. STW-
097   MHGCN (Liu et al., 2023a) constructs a spatial and temporal weighted hyper-connectivity network
098   to fuse multi-atlas information, and Huang et al. (2020) adopt a voting strategy to integrate the clas-
099
      sification results of different classifiers (each corresponding to a different atlas) for ASD diagnosis.
      CcSi-MHAHGEL (Wang et al., 2024) introduces a class-consistency and site-independence Multi-
100
      view Hyperedge-Aware HyperGraph Embedding Learning framework to integrate brain networks
101
      constructed on multiple atlases in a multisite fMRI study. However, these studies did not con-
102   sider the inherent consistency between atlases. Independently encoding multi-atlas brain networks
103   without constraints might extract atlas-specific information, distracting from disease-related pattern
104   modeling. Moreover, existing works only incorporate primitive early or late feature fusion between
105   atlases. This absence of intermediate ROI-level interaction could hinder their models’ ability to dis-
106   cern complementary information in each atlas. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to
107   introduce information distillation with consistency constraints and employ intermediate ROI-level
      interaction for complementary information fusion. Note that in our work, multiple atlases are ap-

      plied to preprocessed images for parcellation, meaning our method is based on a single template.
109   The difference between multi-atlas methods and multi-template methods are discussed in Appendix
110   A.1. The discussion about various atlases is included in Appendix A.2.

112
      Multi-modal and multi-resolution methods also explore brain networks using various atlases. Re-
      search about multi-modal brain networks (Zhou et al., 2019; 2020; Zhu et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
113
      2023; Qu et al., 2024) employed multiple modalities of neuroimaging data, including fMRI, Diffu-
114
      sion Tensor Imaging (DTI) and Positron Emission Tomography (PET), with various atlases to en-
115   hance brain network classification, as different modalities provide abundant information compared
116   to a single modality. However, these multi-modal methods focus on fusing structural and functional
117   connectivity information instead of trying to capture the whole picture of the single modality data.
118   Another line of research (Zhang et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; 2023b; Wen et al., 2024) focuses
119   on applying multi-resolution atlases to fMRI data to capture individual behavior across coarse-to-
120   fine scales. However, the technical design of these approaches focuses on extracting information
121   from both fine and coarse scales under the same parcellation mode. Although multi-modal and
122   multi-resolution methods employ various atlases, they focus on different objectives from multi-atlas
123
      approaches, and the field of brain network analysis with multi-atlas is still in its infancy stage.

125   2.2   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS (GNN S )
126
      In recent years, there has been a surge of interest in employing Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for
127
      the analysis of brain networks. Ktena et al. (2017) utilized graph convolutional networks to learn
128
      similarities between pairs of brain networks (subjects). BrainNetCNN (Kawahara et al., 2017) intro-
129   duced edge-to-edge, edge-to-node, and node-to-graph convolutional filters to harness the topological
130   information within brain networks. MG2G (Xu et al., 2021) utilizes a two-stage method where it
131   initially learns node representations using an unsupervised stochastic graph embedding model based
132   on latent distributions, which are then used to train a classifier, allowing for the identification of
133   significant ROIs with Alzheimer’s disease (AD)-related effects. Zhang et al. (2022) incorporated
134   both local ROI-GNN and global subject-GNN guided by non-imaging data. ContrastPool (Xu et al.,
135   2024) introduced a dual attention mechanism to extract discriminative features across ROIs for sub-
136   jects within the same group.
137   An alternative method for graph representation learning involves Transformer-based mod-
138   els (Vaswani et al., 2017), which adapt the attention mechanism to consider global information
139   for each node and incorporate positional encoding to capture graph topological information. Graph
140   Transformers have garnered significant attention due to their impressive performance in graph rep-
141   resentation learning (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020; Ying et al., 2021; Rampášek et al., 2022). A se-
142   ries of brain network Transformer methods have emerged for brain network analysis. One such
143
      method (Kan et al., 2022) applied Transformers to learn pairwise connection strengths among brain
      regions across individuals. Com-BrainTF (Bannadabhavi et al., 2023) uses a hierarchical local-
144
      global transformer for community-aware node embeddings, while GBT (Peng et al., 2024) employs
145
      an attention weight matrix approximation to focus on the most relevant components for improved
146   graph representation. THC (Dai et al., 2023) introduced an interpretable Transformer-based model
147   for joint hierarchical cluster identification and brain network classification. DART (Kan et al., 2023)
148   utilized segmenting BOLD signals to generate dynamic brain networks and then incorporated them
149   with static networks for representation learning. Most GNN- and Transformer-based methods for
150   brain network analysis are designed for single-atlas thus may lead to a dependency on specific par-
151   cellation mode.

189   3.2          P ROBLEM D EFINITION
190
191   Multi-atlas brain network classification aims to predict the distinct class of each subject by us-
192   ing various atlases for the same fMRI data. Given a dataset of labeled brain networks D =
193   {(X a , X b , yX )}, where yX is the class label of brain networks X a and X b , the problem of brain
194
      network classification is to learn a predictive function f : (X a , X b ) ↑ yX , which maps input brain
      networks to the groups they belong to, expecting that f also works well on unseen brain networks.

201   Distillation and Fusion network (AIDFusion), depicted in Figure 1. Two brain networks constructed

256   The features at different atlases originate from totally different parcellation modes. Pulling those
257   highly correlated features of two different atlases into a shared space allows their effective fusion.

259

345
346
      We use 9 single-atlas methods and 6 multi-atlas methods as baselines to evaluate our proposed
      AIDFusion, including: (1) Conventional machine learning (ML) models: Logistic Regression
347
      (LR) and Support Vector Machine Classifier (SVM) from scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011).
348
      These models take the flattened upper-triangle connectivity matrix as vector input, instead of us-
349   ing the brain network. (2) General-purposed GNNs: GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016) and Trans-
350   former (Vaswani et al., 2017). (3) Single-Atlas Models tailored for brain networks: Brain-
351   NetCNN (Kawahara et al., 2017), MG2G (Xu et al., 2021), ContrastPool (Xu et al., 2024), BNT (Kan
352   et al., 2022) and GBT (Peng et al., 2024). (4) Multi-atlas models: MultiLR (multi-atlas version of
353   LR, concatenate the flatten feature of multiple atlases as input), MultiSVM (multi-atlas version of
354   SVM, similar with MultiLR); MGRL (Chu et al., 2022); MGT (a multi-atlas version of Transformer
355   with the same fusion mechanism as MGRL), METAFormer (Mahler et al., 2023) and LeeNet (Lee
356   et al., 2024).

523   6     C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this paper, we presented the Atlas-Integrated Distillation and Fusion network (AIDFusion), a
526   novel approach to multi-atlas brain network classification. The disentangle Transformer mecha-
527   nism, combined with inter-atlas message-passing and consistency constraints, effectively integrates
528   complementary information across different atlases and ensures cross-atlas consistency at both the
529   subject and population levels. Our extensive experiments on four fMRI datasets demonstrate that
530   AIDFusion outperforms state-of-the-art methods in terms of classification accuracy and efficiency.
531
      Moreover, the patterns identified by AIDFusion align well with existing domain knowledge, show-
      casing the model’s potential for providing interpretable insights into neurological disorders. Cur-
532
      rently, our discussion of multi-atlas brain networks is restricted to 3 atlases, i.e., AAL, Schaefer,
533
      and HO. It is also worth extending the exploration to more atlases to find out which one benefits
534   our model the most. Besides, experiments for atlases with various numbers of ROIs (Appendix F)
535   indicate that AIDFusion performs better when the two atlases have a similar number of ROIs. How
536   to utilize atlases with different resolutions remains a problem. Exploring datasets with more neu-
537   roimaging modalities and more atlases with different resolutions will provide insights into how atlas
538   resolution affects the performance of multi-atlas methods. We plan to advance multi-scale brain
539   network analysis by creating public benchmarks and leveraging inter-scale information to capture
      the brain’s hierarchical organization more effectively.
