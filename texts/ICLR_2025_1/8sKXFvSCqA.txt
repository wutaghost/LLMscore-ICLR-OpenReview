009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural time-series analysis has traditionally focused on modeling data in the time
012            domain, often with some approaches incorporating equivalent Fourier domain
013            representations as auxiliary spectral features. In this work, we shift the main focus
014            to frequency representations, modeling time-series data fully and directly in the
015            Fourier domain. We introduce Neural Fourier Modelling (NFM), a compact yet
016            powerful solution for time-series analysis. NFM is grounded in two key properties
017            of the Fourier transform (FT): (i) the ability to model finite-length time series as
018
               functions in the Fourier domain, treating them as continuous-time elements in
               function space, and (ii) the capacity for data manipulation (such as resampling and
019
               timespan extension) within the Fourier domain. We reinterpret Fourier-domain
020
               data manipulation as frequency extrapolation and interpolation, incorporating
021            this as a core learning mechanism in NFM, applicable across various tasks. To
022            support flexible frequency extension with spectral priors and effective modulation of
023            frequency representations, we propose two learning modules: Learnable Frequency
024            Tokens (LFT) and Implicit Neural Fourier Filters (INFF). These modules enable
025            compact and expressive modeling in the Fourier domain. Extensive experiments
026            demonstrate that NFM achieves state-of-the-art performance on a wide range of
027            tasks (forecasting, anomaly detection, and classification), including challenging
028            time-series scenarios with previously unseen sampling rates at test time. Moreover,
029
               NFM is highly compact, requiring fewer than 40K parameters in each task, with
               time-series lengths ranging from 100 to 16K.

      Given its well-known prevalence in the field of conventional signal processing, the adoption of
055   frequency domain analysis to neural-based modelling is, unsurprisingly, not a unique idea in itself.
056   There have been a number of research efforts across multiple areas from time-series analysis to
057   computer vision (CV). Spectral representations are often harnessed as an alternative (Trabelsi et al.,
058   2018; Choi et al., 2019) or as a complement (Yang & Hong, 2022; Woo et al., 2022) to time- or spatial-
059   domain representations to capture information in a more compact and overarching form. Moreover,
060   several recent studies have extended its applications to an efficient form of global convolution (Huang
061   et al., 2023; Lee-Thorp et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023; Alaa et al., 2020; Yi et al., 2024a) and a
062   means of data augmentation (Xie et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2021b; Zhou et al., 2022b) for facilitating
063
      invariances conducive to generalization in learning. While the existing works have successfully
      utilized the frequency representation, it still remains under-explored with the lack of study on
064
      frequency interpolation and extrapolation as a direct means of modelling data fully in the Fourier
065
      domain. More specifically, our work is motivated by two aspects of frequency representations. (i)
066   Simply learning directly in the Fourier domain enables finding a function-to-function mapping - an
067   inductive bias towards learning resolution-invariance property. (ii) Fourier-domain manipulation and
068   a fundamental connection to its time-domain counterparts can provide a general means of learning in
069   the Fourier domain.
070
071   Fourier-domain Manipulation. As shown in Figure 1, there are two ways to manipulate time
072   series in the Fourier domain – 1) padding/truncating and 2) interleaving the original Frequency
073   representation with zero coefficients, each of which resulting in resampling and extending the original
074   time-domain representation, respectively. Taking this into a view where a resultant time-domain
075   representation caused by the frequency manipulation to a given input sequence is a desired target,
076   we can naturally reformulate the manipulation with zero frequency coefficients into a constructive
077   process of learning meaningful coefficients towards the target - i.e., frequency extrapolation and
078   interpolation. Notably, this view provides a comprehensive learning framework requiring no archi-
079
      tectural modification to models. For example, time series can be readily modelled for forecasting
      task from the frequency interpolation. Moreover, context learning (e.g., classification and regression)
080
      or representation learning can be made through the frequency extrapolation or directly imposing
081
      reconstruction practice with an auxiliary modelling scheme such as Fourier-domain (Xie et al., 2022;
082   Zhang et al., 2022) masking.
083
084   Adopting the above insight as a core learning mechanism, we frame time series modelling into finding
085
      an interpolation or extrapolation solution between input and target directly in the Fourier domain
      and propose NFM. To achieve this, we introduce two main learning modules that operate directly
086
      in the Fourier domain and equip NFM with them. (i) A complex-valued learnable frequency token
087
      (LFT) is proposed to capture effective spectral priors and enable flexible frequency extension for
088   the frequency interpolation and extrapolation. (ii) Implicit neural Fourier filter (INFF) as a principal
089   processing operator is designed to realize an expressive continuous global convolution for learning
090   the interpolation or extrapolation in the Fourier domain. We apply NFM to various datasets and
091   distinct tasks of scenarios with both normal (constant) and unseen discretization rate, and show that
092   NFM achieves state-of-the-art performance in a remarkably compact form - forecasting with 27K,
093   anomaly detection with 6.6K, and classification with 37K parameters.

151

152

153

154   x[n ∈ IN ]|Di = x(n/fx )|Di as a time series with N -point discretization at rate fx over the timespan

247   3.2       L EARNABLE FREQUENCY TOKENS
248
249
      In our framework, we decouple the process of extending the spectral representations of original
      sequence to that of target domain (i.e., N → L and KN → KL ), from learning the abstract
250
      coefficients of Fourier interpolation/extrapolation. We denote such extended spectral representation
251
      with absence of the weighting coefficients Z̄0 ∈ CKL ×d , and it is obtained by simply initializing
252   the Z̄0 with zeros and rearranging X̄ = F(x̄) onto it with scaling for the extension such that
253   Z̄0 [⌊mt k⌋] = mτ mf X̄[k] for all k ∈ IKN , where ⌊·⌋ is floor operation.
254
255
      The result from the above extension is equivalent to the ones from applying zero-padding and/or
      zero-interleaving to X̄. The operation itself is non-parametric and allows handling variable-length
256
      input sequence. However, directly adopting Z̄0 is not effective for learning since extending spectral
257
      representations with zero coefficients itself does not bring in any information gain. One natural
258   solution to this is introducing extra embeddings that are learned to encapsulate certain abstraction
259   and priors in data during optimization. Indeed, it has become a canonical practice, especially in many
260   Transformer models (Devlin et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2022b; 2023; Wang & Chen, 2020), to enrich
261   the models’ learning capability and performance.
262
      Inspired by this, we introduce LFT that can be learned without a-priori and applied directly in
263
      the complex Fourier domain as expressive spectral priors across sequences. Especially, we design
264   the LFT by representing the desired spectral priors as a composition of F and an implicit neural
265   representation (INR) (Sitzmann et al., 2019b; Chen et al., 2021) ϕ : R → Rd that maps a temporal
266   location τ ∈ [0, Ty ) to abstract temporal priors v corresponding to that time location. Notably, the
267   LFT can be characterized as samples from continuous-time Fourier transform (CTFT) of the temporal
268   priors, allowing sampling of the frequency tokens within bandwidth from any arbitrary temporal
269   locations without a need of re-training. We first obtain the learnable frequency tokens V [k] ∈ Cd by
      sampling v[n] = ϕ(τn ) at temporal locations τn = {n/fy |n ∈ IL } and add it to Z̄0 to get Z0 . This

285   defined in the Fourier domain with arbitrary resolution and its size being as large as the length
286   of inputs. This, then, gives rise to a way for instantiating an efficient global convolution operator
287   K : (D, Rd ) → (D, Rd ) as follows (Guibas et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020):
288                                           Z
289                               K(z)(τ ) =      κ(τ − s)z(s) ds,      ∀τ ∈ D                          (6)

291                               K(z)(τ ) = F −1 (R · F(z))(τ ),        ∀τ ∈ D                           (7)

      where Eq.(6) and Eq.(7) express convolution operator on physical space of the signals and its
293   equivalent form with convolution theorem and the FT applied, respectively. R ≡ F(κ) is a Fourier
294   filter defined directly in the Fourier domain and parameterized by a neural network. While a shallow
295   fully-connected network is generally sufficient to parameterize R, much design concerns are put in
296   how to achieve properties of NFFs that are desirable for modelling. We summarize them and compare
297   the existing NFFs from the designing perspective in Appendix B. In short, it is highly desirable
298   to have a NFF that is memory-efficient, length-independent (i.e., flexible), instance-adaptive, and
299   mode-adaptive (i.e., expressive and generalized across spectrum). We design INFF that satisfy these
300   properties below.
301
302   INFF. INFF is formulated for modulating the embedding tokens z globally in the Fourier domain
303
      to ẑ through a Fourier filter, R[k] ∈ Cd , as follows:
304                                         ẑ = F −1 (R(z0 ) ⊙ F(z))                                     (8)
305
306
      where ⊙ denotes Hadamard product. The computation of R is conditioned on the initial spectral
      embedding Z0 , for which a reason will be clarified later. Here, the use of an INR for encapsulating
307
      abstract spectral priors in Section 3.2 is extended to define R with implicit filter coefficients.
308
309                              R(z0 ) := W(F(InstanceNorm(ϕ(τn ) + z0 )))                               (9)
310
      Recalling that F(ϕ(τn )) implicitly models CTFT, in this formulation the Fourier filter has filter
311
      coefficients defined uniquely for each period in spectrum, but with a single parameterization. That is,
312   the designed Fourier filter is in a compact form and can readily handle variable-length sequence with
313   unique frequency coefficients (i.e., mode-adaptive). Unfortunately, the INFF with the filter solely
314   defined by the F(ϕ(τn ) would lack expressivity due to the reliance on depth-wise convolution (i.e.,
315   no channel mixing) and struggle to generalize across different instances. We further improve the filter
316   on both factors by aggregating its temporal coefficients with the initial temporal embedding tokens
317   z0 and processing them through a complex-valued MLP, W : Cd → Cd . Note that we use ReLU
318   for non-linearity and have no bottleneck or expansion factor for the intermediate dimension. With
319   this, each feature of the updated kth filter coefficient now represents a mixture of all features of kth
320
      implicit filter coefficient and all features of the kth spectral representation of the input embedding.
321   Finally, INFF is put together with a plain channel-mixing block and Layer Normalization to form a
322   complete Mixer block. We configure the components as pre-channel-mixing and post-normalization
323   with a skip connection in each Mixer block and stack multiple Mixer blocks followed by a final
      channel-mixing block to constitute a NFM backbone network M(·) as shown in Figure 3.

336   4     E XPERIMENTS
337
338   We conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of NFM and its competence as a
339   general solution to time series modelling. Note that we provide only a brief description about the
340   setting for each experiment below, but one can find all details in the supplementary section (Appendix
341   C∼E). Our code is publicly available at: https://github.com/minkiml/NFM.
342
343   Implementation. A general-purpose time-series model allows one to address a range of tasks as
344   well as various time series modalities without significant architectural modifications (i.e., no injection
345   of task-specific inductive bias). To this end, we implement a single NFM backbone M(·) and use
346   it for all tasks (only differ by some hyper-parameters such as hidden size and the number of mixer
347   blocks) by equipping it with a task-specific linear predictor P(·) (simply a fully-connected layer).
348
      For all experiments, we use a single NVIDIA A100 GPU.

515         5                                             C ONCLUSION
516
517      In this work, we have introduced, NFM, modelling time series directly in Fourier domain by for-
518      mulating the Fourier-domain data manipulation into Fourier interpolation and extrapolation. NFM
519      with learnable frequency tokens and implicit neural Fourier filter enjoys the intriguing properties of
520      the FT in learning, resulting in a remarkable compactness and continuous-time characteristics. Our
521      experiments demonstrate that NFM can be a powerful general solution to time series analysis across
522
         a range of datasets, tasks, and scenarios and show that it is possible to achieve the state-of-the-art
         performance with a remarkably compact model, compared to models with hundreds thousand and
523
         million parameters.
524
525
526
         Limitations and Future Work. Despite the fact that NFM models time series in function space
         through Fourier lens, the current implementation is not directly suitable for some other dynamic
527
         scenarios such as handling irregular time series. A reason for this is that the FFT for efficient
528
         transformation requires uniformly-sampled sequence thus not applicable while a naive algorithm
529      with computing a DFT matrix and its pseudo inverse is not only too slow but also memory-intensive
530      for every long and multivariate but irregular time series. While addressing this challenging scenario
531      is highly valuable in time series analysis, we are currently improving NFM on this matter.

785   We provide an additional discussion on a recent time series model, Frequency Interpolation Time
786   Series analysis baseline (FITS), that is introduced on the same principle of frequency-domain
787   manipulation as NFM.
788   FITS is designed with a single complex-valued linear layer to directly learn the linear interpolation
789   or extrapolation from input frequency spectrum as analysed in Section 3.1. It is equipped with a
790   heuristic low-pass filter which restricts the spectrum and further reduces the model complexity. While
791   FITS achieves an elevated degree of lightweight-ness with competing performance in some time
792   series tasks, there present several limitations to be improved for broader utilization.
793
794
                    • FITS cannot model multivariate time series. With its simplified architecture, it relies on
                      channel-independent modelling (Nie et al., 2022) which does not model correlation between
795
                      channels, thus its application is limited to univariate scenarios only.
796
797
                    • FITS is a linear model operating directly in frequency domain, and thus its expressiveness
                      and learning capacity are largely limited. This is especially disadvantageous when it comes
798
                      to modelling large-scale dataset and more complex patterns. Moreover, the complexity of
799
                      FITS itself without a low-pass filter increases exponentially with the length of inputs and
800                   target outputs (there is a trade-off between giving up some information and resolving the
801                   complexity with the use of a low-pass filter). This characteristic does not allow FITS to
802                   stay compact in many practical scenarios with more complex and/or long time series whose
803                   major frequencies spread in wide spectrum. This is well shown from the foreacsting results
804                   on electricity and traffic datasets (Table 7). In Figure 6, we also provide the overall number
805                   of parameters in NFM and FITS used in forecasting task. Taking any lower cut-off frequency
806                   for the low-pass filters in the datasets like ETTh2, elctricity, and traffic to make FITS more
807                   compact leads to considerable performance drops.
808                 • As discussed in the main body of this work, one natural advantage gained from modelling
809                   time series directly in the Fourier domain is that the learned function can be more robust to
                      dealing with change in discretization of input data. These features, however, are difficult to

                   be fully exploited in FITS due to the static nature of the model (not able to deal with variable
811                length of time series). Although the adoption of a low-pass filter mitigates this issue by
812                allowing to handle any length of input sequence longer than the period of cut-off, defaulting
813                a model with a heuristic low-pass filter is not always practical. It discards the information of

815                the performance with full spectrum is consistently better than that with restricted spectrum.

        NFFs can mainly be found in the context of operator learning to solve PDEs and global token mixing
        as an efficient alternative to attention mechanism in vision tasks. Here, we summarize the existing
855
        NFFs from design perspective as shown in Figure 7 and compare them with NFM.
856
857     FNO (Li et al., 2020) is designed with per-mode matrix multiplication. Such per-mode param-
858     eterization allows the model to be expressive with large learning capacity. However, the model
859     can also be easily over-parameterized and thus the advantage comes with a high risk of overfittig.
860
        Moreover, the inhibitive number of parameters can be incurred when the model needs to deal with
        long sequences. Besides, the per-mode parameterization essentially prevents the model from being
861
        adaptive to handling variable-size inputs unless a heuristic low-pass filter is integrated.
862
863     GFN (Rao et al., 2021) also adopts per-mode multiplication but of entry-wise operation (i.e., depth-
        wise global convolution). While this features GFN with more efficient parameterization than FNO it


                                                                 16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      raises another concern of landing no channel-mixing operation in learning and may limit the model’s
865   expressivity. Additionally, due to the per-mode parameterization, GFN is limited to a scenario where
866   the input length is static as well, hence it is less suitable for time-series tasks. Note that FEDformer
867   (Zhou et al., 2022b) and FourierGNN (Yi et al., 2024a) are good examples of GFN adapted to time
868   series problems with random frequency masking and just a single set of filter coefficients, respectively,
869   which resolve the issue with the static input length but would still suffer from the lack of expressivity.
870
      AFNO (Guibas et al., 2021) handles variable-size inputs and achieves channel mixing simply by
871   having a single parameterization (1×1 convolution + non-linearity + soft-shrinkage function) and
872   sharing its weights across all frequency modes in spectrum. AFNO can essentially be seen as a
873   generalization of FNO and GFN with a block-diagonal (i.e., multi-head) structure, Note that FreTS
874   (Yi et al., 2024b) adopts AFNO. However, it trades off expressivity (i.e., there is no principled way
875   for AFNO with a single shared network to learn discriminative feature across frequency modes)
876   over efficiency and flexibility to handling variable-size inputs. Besides, a soft shrinkage function is
877   adopted in AFNO as a regularization to encourage sparsity, with which we, however, consistently
878   observe a performance drop on time series tasks in all NFFs including INFF as well.
879   The weights of the filter (i.e., filter coefficients) in the above works are all shared across different
880   instances. Unfortunately, this poses a concern that the models can struggle to learn complex patterns
881   across different instances that reside upon different underlying spectrum.
882
      AFF (Huang et al., 2023) addresses this by altering the use of neural network from directly parameter-
883   ized filter coefficients to a hypernetwork (Ha et al., 2017) that yields the filter coefficients dynamically
884   according to each input instance.
885
886
      Based on the above analysis, one remaining gap in the current NFFs we found is how to design a
      filter that is aware of each mode separately in spectrum, namely "mode-adaptive", and modulate them
887
      locally in efficient way. As discussed, FNO and GFN achieve such mode-adaptivity by instantiating
888
      the filter coefficients separately for each mode. However, this way not only makes the operation static
889   (due to fixed parameterization) but also come at the cost of significantly increasing the number of
890   parameters (especially, when operating matrix multiplication as in FNO) with the input length. On
891   the other hand, AFNO and AFF do not account for the mode-adaptivity while simply sharing filter
892   coefficients for all modes.
893
      Our proposed INFF essentially gives a solution to this matter without sacrificing other favourable
894
      NFFs’ characteristics. We achieve this by leveraging an INR (Sitzmann et al., 2019a; Tancik et al.,
895   2020b; Sitzmann et al., 2020), a neural-based technique to reformulate a direct representation of
896   interests into a more compact form of implicit representation. Especially, in INFF, the per-mode
897   parameterization of a Fourier filter is turned into a process of learning to encode an abstract and
898   implicit representation of the whole filter coefficients in spectrum. Thus, it is possible to draw
899   unique filter coefficients for any frequency modes within the bandwidth at the cost of only a single
900   parameterization, which largely contributes to achieving high compactness in NFM.

1221
1222   Discussion on the number of parameters. With the prevalence of chunk-to-chunk prediction in the
1223   forecasting community, one practice in the deep forecasting baselines is to adopt a prediction head
1224   that acts on temporal dimension. Note that the linear models (FITS and N-Linear) naturally fall in
1225   this as they by themselves are the prediction head operating on the input time series. Due to this, they
1226   scale poorly with the length of horizons as well as the length of lookback window. For example, more
1227   than 95% of the learnable weights in PatchTST for L = 720 surprisingly belongs to the single "wide"
1228   prediction head of 8.3M parameters against 0.4M parameters in its Transformer-based backbone.
1229
       In contrast, the prediction head used in NFM is feature-to-feature projection and the number of
       parameters in NFM is completely independent from the length of input sequence and prediction
1230
       horizon. Importantly, we highlight that this aspect completely decouples the contribution of the linear
1231
       head in modelling sequence and further validates the effectiveness of NFM to modelling temporal
1232   dependency unlike the other deep forecasting baselines that are equipped with a wide linear predictor.

1291   in sampling rate for signals being monitored by a model. Such change does not affect the underlying
1292   temporal dynamic of the signals (i.e., the same solution) but brings in a positional alternation in
1293   the sequences of our observations, greatly affecting the performance of the model. To this end, we
1294   conduct forecasting on the input time series sampled at "unseen" discretization rate (this experiment
1295   is made for the first time in our work). Overall, the full results in Table 9 demonstrates the resolution-
       invariance property of NFM that can be highly valuable in practical applications.

1321   Analysis. Especially, we observe that the models operating fully on frequency domain (NFM and
1322   FITS) are much more robust to the change in sampling rate as learning a function-to-function mapping,
1323   than those operating on time domain (PatchTST and N-Linear). Interestingly, the performance
1324   degradation with unseen sampling rate tends to be more significant in forecasting over short horizons
1325   (with the same lookback length) and relatively minor over long horizons in all models. This tendency
1326   could be a strong indication that the forecasting models, including NFM, become more reliant on
1327   the global context (or similarly, low frequency regime) of the lookback window and less focusing
1328
       on the local details (or similarly, high frequency regime) in the window as the predicting horizon
       gets longer. In this sense, the results imply that NFM is not the one that leverages local features
1329
       in optimal way but is less prone to the local variations than the others. In the future, integrating a
1330
       mechanism that encourages learning more of local features (high-frequency information) into models
1331   could potentially improve the forecasting ability in the long horizon cases as well as the short horizon
1332   cases.
