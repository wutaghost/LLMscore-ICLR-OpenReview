009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Independent learning is a straightforward solution for fully decentralized learning in
012            cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL). The study of independent
013            learning has a history of decades, and the representatives, such as independent Q-
014            learning and independent PPO, can obtain good performance in some benchmarks.
015            However, most independent learning algorithms lack convergence guarantees or
016
               theoretical support. In this paper, we propose a general formulation of independent
               policy optimization, f -divergence policy optimization. We show the generality
017
               of this formulation and analyze its limitations. Based on this formulation, we
018
               further propose a novel independent learning algorithm, TVPO, which theoretically
019            guarantees convergence. Empirically, we show that TVPO outperforms state-of-
020            the-art fully decentralized learning methods on three popular cooperative MARL
021            benchmarks, which verifies the efficacy of TVPO.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has shown great potential in many areas
027
      including power control (Zhang & Liang, 2020), autonomous vehicle (Han et al., 2022), and robot
      control (Sartoretti et al., 2019). The main framework for cooperative MARL is centralized training
028
      with decentralized execution (CTDE) (Kraemer & Banerjee, 2016), while the MARL community pays
029
      less attention to fully decentralized learning, also known as decentralized training with decentralized
030   execution (DTDE). Fully decentralized learning is still significant in cooperative MARL due to
031   its simplicity. From the perspective of applications, fully decentralized learning is useful in many
032   industrial applications where agents may belong to different parties, e.g., autonomous vehicles or
033   robots. From the perspective of theory, fully decentralized algorithms rely on less information during
034   training and hence are more general and worth further study.
035
      For DTDE or fully decentralized settings, independent learning is a straightforward but effective
036   way that enables agents to directly execute a single-agent RL algorithm. The representatives are
037   independent Q-learning (IQL) (Tan, 1993) and independent actor-critic (IAC) (Foerster et al., 2018;
038   Papoudakis et al., 2021). Recently, independent PPO (IPPO) (de Witt et al., 2020) extended PPO
039   (Schulman et al., 2017) to MARL and shows good performance on several benchmarks. However,
040   these independent learning algorithms are still troubled by the non-stationarity problem and lack
041   convergence guarantees or theoretical support.
042   In this paper, we propose a general formulation of independent policy optimization, f -divergence
043   policy optimization. We show the generality of such a formulation for independent learning in
044   cooperative MARL. We also analyze the policy iteration of this formulation and discuss its limitation
045   by a two-player matrix game. Based on this formulation, we further propose a novel independent
046   learning algorithm, total variation policy optimization (TVPO). To theoretically study the property
047   of TVPO and prove its convergence, we introduce a new set of value functions and policy iteration
048   specifically for fully decentralized learning and prove the monotonicity of this policy iteration. The
049   practical algorithm of TVPO can be effectively realized by an adaptive coefficient, similar to PPO
050
      (Schulman et al., 2017).
051   Empirically, we verify our discussion about the limitation of f -divergence policy optimization in the
052   two-player matrix game and show the joint policy may converge to the sub-optimum with different
053   f -divergences. Moreover, we evaluate the performance of TVPO in three popular benchmarks of
      cooperative MARL including SMAC (Samvelyan et al., 2019), multi-agent MuJoCo (Peng et al., 2021)


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and SMACv2 (Ellis et al., 2023). We compare TVPO with four representative fully decentralized
055   learning methods: IQL (Tan, 1993), IPPO (de Witt et al., 2020), I2Q (Jiang & Lu, 2022), and DPO
056   (Su & Lu, 2022b). The empirical results show that TVPO outperforms these baselines in all evaluated
057   tasks, which verifies the effectiveness of TVPO in fully decentralized cooperative MARL.

060   2    R ELATED W ORK
061
062   CTDE. The popular framework to address cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL)
063   problems is centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE) (Lowe et al., 2017; Foerster
064   et al., 2018; Sunehag et al., 2018; Rashid et al., 2018; Iqbal & Sha, 2019; Wang et al., 2021a; Zhang
065
      et al., 2021; Su & Lu, 2022a; Wang et al., 2023a). CTDE successfully mitigates the challenge of
      non-stationarity via centralized training. This line of research can be categorized into two types: value
066
      decomposition algorithms (Sunehag et al., 2018; Rashid et al., 2018; Son et al., 2019; Yang et al.,
067
      2020; Wang et al., 2021a), where the centralized Q-function’s optimum aligns with the decentralized
068   Q-functions’ optima, allowing the learning of the centralized Q-function to be factorized into the
069   learning process of the decentralized Q-functions; and multi-agent actor-critic algorithms (Foerster
070   et al., 2018; Iqbal & Sha, 2019; Wang et al., 2021b; Zhang et al., 2021; Su & Lu, 2022a; Wang et al.,
071   2023a; Wen et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023), which leverage a centralized Q-function to facilitate the
072   learning of decentralized stochastic policies. HAPPO (Kuba et al., 2021) and MAPPO (Yu et al.,
073   2021) extend the applicability of TRPO (Schulman et al., 2015) and PPO (Schulman et al., 2017),
074   respectively, to the MARL setting through a centralized state value function. HASAC (Liu et al., 2023)
075   combines the heterougeneous-agent decomposition with the entropy regularization in SAC. MAT
076
      (Wen et al., 2022) introduce Transformer and sequencial modeling into the heterougeneous-agent
      decomposition. Nevertheless, it is important to note that these approaches remain constrained by
077
      the CTDE paradigm and are therefore unsuitable for fully decentralized learning.
078
079   Fully Decentralized Learning. There have recently been several different views about fully decen-
080   tralized learning or decentralized learning. Some works study decentralized learning specifically
081   with communication (Zhang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2020) or parameter sharing (Terry et al., 2020).
082
      Both communication and parameter sharing exchange information among agents (Terry et al., 2020).
      However, in this paper, we consider fully decentralized learning in the strictest sense – with each
083
      agent independently learning its policy while being not allowed to communicate or share param-
084
      eters as in Tampuu et al. (2015); Mao et al. (2022b); Wang et al. (2023c). Additionally, there are
085   several studies (Zhan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Mao & Başar, 2023) considering general-sum
086   games in decentralized MARL, these studies focus on episodic Markov game(Jin et al., 2021), which
087   is non-cooperative and assumes the reward function, transition probability, and policy are related
088   to the time step. The objective of finding an equilibrium in this setting is different from the fully
089   decentralized learning in this paper. Independent learning (OroojlooyJadid & Hajinezhad, 2019) has
090   been extensively studied in the field of cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) as a
091   straightforward approach for fully decentralized learning. Representatives of this approach include
092   independent Q-learning (IQL) (Tan, 1993; Tampuu et al., 2015), independent actor-critic (IAC)
093
      (Foerster et al., 2018; Papoudakis et al., 2021), and independent proximal policy optimization (IPPO)
      (de Witt et al., 2020). It should be noted that all these independent learning algorithms deviate from
094
      the stationary condition of the Markov decision process (MDP) and lack convergence guarantees,
095
      even though IQL and IPPO perform well in various benchmarks (Papoudakis et al., 2021). Recent
096   studies have emerged with convergence guarantees in fully decentralized MARL, namely I2Q (Jiang
097   & Lu, 2022) and DPO (Su & Lu, 2022b). I2Q introduces the concept of QSS-value (Edwards et al.,
098   2020) into independent Q-learning, achieving convergence guarantees. However, its applicability is
099   restricted to deterministic environments. On the other hand, a novel decentralized surrogate of the
100   joint TRPO objective is proposed by DPO to ensure convergence. In terms of empirical performance,
101   I2Q demonstrates superior performance compared to IQL, while DPO outperforms IPPO. Thus,
102   in our empirical studies, we comprehensively compare our TVPO with the two state-of-the-art
103   methods.
104   Mirror Descent in RL. Recently, mirror descent (Blair, 1985) or similar ideas have been applied
105   in single-agent RL (Wang et al., 2019; Lan, 2023; Tomar et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022; Vaswani
106   et al., 2021) or CTDE algorithms in MARL (Su & Lu, 2022a; Kuba et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023) for
107   theoretical guarantees. Mirror descent is a method related to Bregman divergence (Bregman, 1967).
      Although Bregman divergence is a general divergence class, KL-divergence is most frequently used


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      in previous mirror descent studies (Wang et al., 2019; Lan, 2023; Tomar et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022;
109   Vaswani et al., 2021). On the other hand, KL-divergence is at the intersection of Bregman divergence
110   and f -divergence and our analysis of KL-divergence shows it is trapped in the sub-optimum even in
111   a simple matrix game. Moreover, Bregman divergence in mirror descent and f -divergence are two
112   different divergence classes, and the latter can provide us with more useful properties for theoretical
113   guarantees in fully decentralized learning. Extending mirror descent in fully decentralized learning
114   with theoretical guarantees is still open and beyond the scope of the discussion in this paper.

      Cooperative Markov Game. The cooperative Markov Game serves as a general model for
119
      cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL). It is a special case of Markov Game
120
      (Littman, 1994) where the reward functions of all agents are the same. It is represented by the
121   tuple G = {S, A, P, I, N, r, γ}, where N denotes the number of agents, and I = {1, 2 · · · N } refers
122   to the set of all agents. The state space is denoted as S, and the joint action space is denoted
123   as A = A1 × A2 × · · · × AN , where Ai represents the individual action space for agent i. The
124   transition function P (s′ |s, a) : S × A × S → [0, 1] defines the probability of transitioning from
125   state s to s′ given joint action a. The discount factor is denoted as γ ∈ [0, 1), and the reward
126   function r(s, a) : S × A → [−rmax , rmax ] assigns reward to state s and joint action a, with
127   rmax serving Pas the reward function’s upper bound. The objective of Dec-POMDP is to maximize
128   J(π) = Eπ [ t=0 γ t r(st , at )]. Thus, the optimal joint policy π ∗ = arg maxπ J(π) needs to be
129
      determined. In fully decentralized learning, each agent independently learns an individual policy
      denoted as π i (ai |s). The joint policy π of all agents can be represented as the product of each
130
      individual policy π i .

151   Independent Learning. Independent learning is a straightforward method to solve cooperative
152   MARL problems, which makes each agent learn through the same single-agent RL algorithm, such
153   IQL (Tan, 1993), IAC (Foerster et al., 2018), and IPPO (de Witt et al., 2020). Though independent

      independent policy optimization, and try to find an independent learning algorithm that can guarantee
158
      convergence in cooperative MARL.

501

502   limited, which may result in relatively low performance. If d is large, corresponding to the learning
503   curves d = 1.0, the policy update may exceed the trust region, which is away from the convergence
504   condition and results in oscillating curves. There is a trade-off for d. Therefore, the appropriate

508   In the second line, we control δ to be the same in each plot. Intuitively, α represents the adjustment
509   strength of the coefficient β i and δ represents the tolerance of the expected distance. A smaller δ
510   means more frequent adjustments. The empirical results show that α should match δ, i.e., a smaller
511   adjustment strength (a smaller α) should correspond to more frequent adjustments (a smaller δ) and
512   vice versa. A good combination of (α, δ) means a good ability to keep the coefficient β i close to the
513   expected distance d. Specifically, among the values of α and δ we chosen, from the perspective of α,
514   α = 1.1 and α = 1.5 are small values corresponding to the best value δ = 2; from the perspective of
515   δ, δ = 4 and δ = 6 are large values corresponding to the best value α = 3.

517   6                            C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
518
519   In this paper, we propose f -divergence policy optimization, a general formulation of independent
520   policy optimization in cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning, and analyze the policy iteration
521   of such a formulation. We discuss the limitation of this formulation, i.e., convergence to only
522   sub-optimal policy, and verify it by the empirical results in a two-player matrix game. Based on
523   f -divergence policy optimization, we propose a novel independent learning algorithm, TVPO, and
524   prove its convergence in fully decentralized learning. Empirically, we evaluate TVPO against four
525
      baselines in three environments. The empirical results show that TVPO outperforms all the baselines,
      which verifies the effectiveness of TVPO.
526
527   The main limitation of our work is the approximations in the practical algorithms which may not
528   preserve the theoretical properties including the convergence. Additionally, though the learning of
529   decentralized critic is unbiased, it may be troubled with the variance especially in multi-agent settings.
530   Moreover, TVPO still requires on-policy updates which is inconvenient especially in multi-agent
531
      settings.

       F.1     A B RIEF INTRODUCTION OF BASELINE ALGORITHMS
1150
1151   We select these four baseline algorithms as representatives of fully decentralized algorithms. IQL
1152   (Tan, 1993) is a basic value-based algorithm for decentralized learning. IPPO is a basic policy-
1153   based algorithm for decentralized learning. Both IQL and IPPO (de Witt et al., 2020) do not have
1154   convergence guarantees, to the best of our knowledge. DPO (Su & Lu, 2022b) and I2Q (Jiang & Lu,
1155   2022) are the recent policy-based algorithm and value-based algorithm respectively, and both of them
1156
       have been proved to have convergence guarantee.
1157   IQL, IDDPG, and IPPO are relatively simple to understand, where each agent updates its policy
1158   through an independent Q-learning, DDPG, or PPO. These algorithms simply extend the single-agent
1159   RL algorithms into the MARL setting. They are heuristic algorithms without convergence guarantees
1160   in fully decentralized MARL.
1161   The idea of DPO is to find a lower bound of the joint policy improvement objective as a surrogate
1162   which can also be optimized in a decentralized way for each agent. The formulation of DPO is as
1163   follows:

1434
       Using the global state s for theoretical analysis has been a common practice in the study of multi-agent
       reinforcement learning, especially in the setting of decentralized learning. There are many previous
1435
       works containing theoretical results in decentralized learning, which include both cooperative settings
1436
       (Jiang & Lu, 2022) and non-cooperative settings (Arslan & Yüksel, 2016; Mao et al., 2022a; Zhang

1438   has been studied for decades in Papadimitriou & Tsitsiklis (1987); Mundhenk et al. (2000); Vlassis
1439   et al. (2012). Additionally, the theoretical analysis of Dec-POMDP will be even more difficult in the
1440   multi-agent setting. If we include partial observability in the analysis, we may not obtain anything
1441   since the problem may be undecidable in Dec-POMDP (Madani et al., 1999).
