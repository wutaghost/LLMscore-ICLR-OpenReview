009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Foundation models (FMs) are pre-trained on large-scale datasets and then fine-
012            tuned on a downstream task for a specific application. The most successful and
013            most commonly used fine-tuning method is to update the pre-trained weights
014            via a low-rank adaptation (LoRA). LoRA introduces new weight matrices that
015            are usually initialized at random with a uniform rank distribution across model
016            weights. Recent works focus on weight-driven initialization or learning of adaptive
017            ranks during training. Both approaches have only been investigated in isolation,
018
               resulting in slow convergence or a uniform rank distribution, in turn leading to sub-
               optimal performance. We propose to enhance LoRA by initializing the new weights
019
               in a data-driven manner by computing singular value decomposition (SVD) on
020
               minibatches of activation vectors. Then, we initialize the LoRA matrices with the
021            obtained right-singular vectors and re-distribute ranks among all weight matrices
022            to explain the maximal amount of variance across layers. This results in our new
023            method Explained Variance Adaptation (EVA). We apply EVA to a variety of
024            fine-tuning tasks ranging from language generation and understanding to image
025            classification and reinforcement learning. EVA exhibits faster convergence than
026            competitors and attains the highest average score across a multitude of tasks per
027            domain while reducing the number of trainable parameters.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2021, FMs) are usually trained on large-scale data and then
033
      fine-tuned towards a particular downstream task. This training paradigm has led to significant
      advancements in the realm of language modeling (OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a; Reid et al.,
034
      2024), computer vision (Dehghani et al., 2023; Oquab et al., 2023), and reinforcement learning
035
      (Brohan et al., 2023; Zitkovich et al., 2023). With an increasing number of model parameters,
036   the process of fine-tuning becomes prohibitively expensive. This results in the need for efficient
037   alternatives to fine-tuning all parameters of the pre-trained model.
038
039
      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) approaches are commonly used as an effective alternative to
      full fine-tuning (FFT). PEFT methods modify the pre-trained model by introducing a small number
040
      of new trainable parameters, while the pre-trained weights remain frozen. This leads to a substantial
041
      reduction in computational cost, both in terms of time and space. A particularly successful approach,
042   LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), introduces new weights in the form of a low-rank decomposition for each
043   weight matrix in the pre-trained model. After training, the new weights can be readily merged
044   into the pre-trained weights without any additional inference latency. Recent research has explored
045   various extensions to LoRA, such as different initialization schemes and adaptive rank allocation
046   (see Table 1). Weight-driven initialization schemes are constrained to the information stored in the
047   pre-trained weights. Further, adaptive rank allocation techniques usually optimize the ranks during
048   the fine-tuning process which results in additional complexity for computing importance scores of
049   ranks. Both approaches have merely been investigated in isolation thus far.
050   We propose a new method that extends LoRA with adaptive rank allocation and data-driven initializa-
051   tion by leveraging information from the downstream task. During the fine-tuning process, information
052   of the downstream task is stored in the newly introduced weights of LoRA. Our aim is to make
053   fine-tuning more efficient by initializing the LoRA weights in a manner such that they already contain
      the maximum possible amount of information from the downstream task. This way, the fine-tuning

084
      and understanding, image classification, and reinforcement learning (RL). EVA consistently improves
085   average performance across a multitude of tasks on each domain compared to LoRA and other
086   recently proposed initialization or rank redistribution methods. For language generation, we fine-tune
087   7B-9B parameter language models on math and reasoning tasks, where EVA attains the highest
088   average performance. Further, on a set of language understanding tasks, EVA improves the average
089   performance compared to competitors. On image classification we fine-tune a pre-trained vision
090   transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) on a set of 19 diverse tasks. We find that EVA attains the
091   highest average score and improves over LoRA and established extensions thereof, with most gains
092   on in-domain data. For our RL experiments we conduct fine-tuning on continuous control tasks
093

      full fine-tuning (FFT) when combined with DoRA (Liu et al., 2024a). Finally, we demonstrate that
094
      EVA is pareto-dominant as our rank re-distribution reduces the amount of trainable parameters while
095
      improving performance. Our contributions are as follows:

099          • We propose a data-driven heuristic for adaptive rank allocation based on explained variance.

             • We demonstrate the effectiveness of EVA across a variety of different domains.

130   Initialization of LoRA matrices Common initialization schemes for neural networks (He et al.,
131
      2015; Glorot & Bengio, 2010) were designed to stabilize training of deep neural networks based on
      activation functions and depth. In the context of PEFT, Hu et al. (2022) and Liu et al. (2022) explored
132
      data-driven initialization by either pre-training on a different task first, or by unsupervised pre-training
133
      on the task at hand. Contrary, EVA does not require any gradient update steps, therefore it is much
134   more efficient. Similarly, Nikdan et al. (2024) utilize a warm-up stage in LoRA fine-tuning, where
135   gradients with respect to LoRA weights are used to initialize a sparse matrix for sparse adaptation
136   (Sung et al., 2021) in combination with LoRA. Alternatively, Babakniya et al. (2023) initialize LoRA
137   matrices using SVD on weight matrices obtained after a few steps of full fine-tuning for federated
138   learning with heterogeneous data. Meng et al. (2024) use the main directions of the pre-trained
139   weights to initialize the LoRA matrices. In contrast, EVA takes a data-driven approach to initialize
140   the LoRA matrices. Similar initialization schemes were proposed for training deep networks from
141   scratch (Mishkin & Matas, 2016; Krähenbühl et al., 2016).
142   Increasing efficiency of LoRA Several works have investigated how to increase efficiency of LoRA
143   fine-tuning. Kopiczko et al. (2024) decrease the memory complexity by keeping both A and B frozen
144   while merely training newly-introduced scaling vectors. This way, only random seeds for initializing
145   A and B need to be stored. Another prominent approach is quantization (Dettmers et al., 2022),
146   which has been successfully combined with LoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023). Other LoRA variants are
147   compatible with quantization (Nikdan et al., 2024; Valipour et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2024). It has
148
      also been shown that initialization can improve fine-tuning quantized models (Li et al., 2023).

152   We aim at initializing LoRA weights in a data-driven manner by leveraging data from the downstream
153   task. Since EVA builds on LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), we first briefly explain LoRA in Section 3.1.

155   initialization for the low-rank decomposition of LoRA matrices via SVD on activation vectors
156   (Section 3.2), and (ii), adaptive assignment of ranks across all layers to maximize the explained
157   variance throughout the pre-trained model (Section 3.3).

      The data-driven initialization of EVA relies on incremental SVD on minibatches of activations in
471   the initial training stage. In Figure 5, left, we show that this process converges for Llama-2-7B on
472   MetaMathQA for different minibatch sizes. Using a minibatch size of 4 the computation for EVA’s
473   initialization lasts for approximately 80 seconds, which corresponds to around 90 minibatches. For a
474   batch size of 32 the computation of the SVD components takes around 500 seconds. In Figure 5, right,
475   we additionally show, that the main components obtained via SVD mostly remain consistent across
476   different batch orders for a batch size of 4, again for Llama-2-7B on MetaMathQA. To this end, we
477   plot cosine similarity between components obtained via incremental SVD after rank redistribution.
478   These results indicate that these models exhibit certain activation patterns that remain consistent
479
      across different batch orders which lead to a robust initialization for EVA. We also show that the
      components for different batch sizes converge to mostly the same final initialization in Appendix F.

482   5                                  D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
483
484   Alternative data-driven initialization schemes. We also investigated alternative data driven ini-
485   tialization schemes. Such alternatives include, but are not limited to, Kernel-PCA (Schölkopf et al.,
      1997) or Linear Discriminant Analysis (Fisher, 1936, LDA). While Kernel-PCA can account for


                                                                                                                     9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      non-linearities in the data, it scales with the number of datapoints, which is impractical in our setting.
487   Further, we observed convergence instabilities for incrementally updating LDA.
488
489   Additional latency of SVD. EVA leads to performance improvements over LoRA, but introduces
490
      additional latency in the beginning of training for computing the data-driven initialization. In Table 23
      we demonstrate that this process constitutes merely 0.2% of the actual training time for Llama-2-7B
491
      on MetaMathQA. Further, in Appendix F we also show that this process is mostly invariant to the
492
      batch size, meaning that smaller batch sizes may be used for the SVD computation, resulting in
493   additional speedup. Since, the SVD computation does not require backpropagation and storing of
494   optimizer states there is no overhead with respect to memory.
495
496
      Effect of rank redistribution. Our experiments on language understanding tasks indicate that the
      effect of rank redistribution strongly depends on the downstream task, i.e. all models benefit from
497
      the redistribution on the common sense reasoning tasks, whereas for the math tasks a uniform rank
498
      distribution appears to perform best. In our experiments on language understanding and image
499   classification, adaptive ranks performed best, while on decision making uniform ranks performed
500   best. Generally the performance gap between the two is not big and since rank redistribution also
501   leads to fewer trainable parameters we recommend to use it by default.
502
      What method performs well on which tasks? We conducted fine-tuning experiments across 51
503
      tasks and four domains and found that EVA or EVA+DoRA performs best on expectation. This is
504
      evidenced by the higher average score across multiple tasks per domain. Despite this finding, there is
505   usually variation in the ranking of methods considering single tasks, i.e. LoRA performed better on
506   specialized, and FFT performed best on structured images. Therefore there is no one algorithm that
507   performs best on every single task, verifying that there is no free lunch (Wolpert & Macready, 1997).
508
      Reproducibility. We provide the source code along with the submission (see Appendix A) to ensure
509
      reproducibility. Further, to make EVA more accessible to the community, we will integrate it into the
510
      widely used PEFT library (Mangrulkar et al., 2022).
511

513   6    C ONCLUSION AND B ROADER I MPACT
514
515   We propose a novel method named Explained Variance Adaptation (EVA), extending the widely
516   used LoRA with data-driven initialization and rank re-distribution. We initialize LoRA matrices
517
      in a data-driven manner by performing SVD on minibatches of activation vectors. Further, we
      re-distribute ranks across weight matrices according to the amount of variance they explain. In this
518
      regard, we also introduce a hyperparameter that allows for a controlled investigation of different
519
      rank distributions. Thereby, in EVA we bind the benefits of adaptive rank allocation and data-driven
520   initialization, resulting in one initialization to rule them all. We demonstrate performance gains of
521   EVA over LoRA and initialization schemes thereof on a variety of domains, ranging from language
522   to vision and RL. Our results demonstrate that EVA variants consistently reach the highest average
523   performance on a wide range of tasks across all domains.
524
      We believe that EVA sheds a novel view on LoRA fine-tuning, where initialization of the newly
525
      introduced weights is guided by the downstream data. As we have shown, this can boost performance
526   on a wide variety of domains. We believe that EVA can have a significant impact on future research
527   on fine-tuning of foundation models, because it inherits all benefits of LoRA while improving
528   performance at no significant additional cost. In the future, we aim at investigating the effect of
529   rank redistribution on other initialization schemes and quantization, as well as alternative data-driven
530   initialization schemes in more detail.

1325   In Table 11 we show the number of trainable parameters for EVA (ρ = 2) compared to LoRA on

1327   improved performance while reducing the parameter count by approximately 1M. The reason for this
1328   is that parameters are usually re-distributed from higher dimensional projections to lower dimensional
1329
       ones, i.e. from non-attention weights to attention weights. This results in improved performance
       while reducing the parameter count.
1330
1331   Finally, to verify our intuition that the LoRA matrix A should be initialized with the projection onto
1332   the components that explain the most variance, we compare its performance to initializing EVA
1333   with the components that explain the least amount of variance. We call this method EVA-minor and
1334   present results for it in Table 12. To implement EVA-minor, we sample 20 minibatches of data and
1335
       perform truncated SVD on those and select the resulting minor components. This incurs substantial
       additional cost, as we must compute all components, whereas for EVA we only approximate the
1336
       components that explain the most variance. Hence, incremental SVD is not beneficial in this case
1337
       anymore and it is also not practical as obtaining the initialization takes hours instead of seconds for
1338   EVA. Moreover, our data-driven heuristic for adaptive rank allocation is not applicable to this case
1339   anymore, therefore we consider uniform ranks. Finally, we find that EVA consistently improves over
1340   EVA-minor, highlighting the importance of initializing EVA with the major components, i.e. the ones
1341   the explain the most variance.

1432   C.3   H YPERPARAMETER SEARCH
1433
1434
       For LoRA and EVA, we search over the number of ranks r ∈ {2, 4, 6, 8} and different learning rates
1435   η ∈ {1e − 3, 4e − 4, 1e − 4} for RoBERTaLarge and η ∈ {4e − 3, 1e − 3, 4e − 4} for DeBERTav3Base .
1436   We report the best hyperparameter settings for both, RoBERTaLarge and DeBERTav3Base for LoRA
1437   and EVA in Table 16. For AdaLoRA, we search over the same ranks and always start initial ranks
1438   with r + 4 that are then redistributed during training. For BOFT we sweep over different combinations
1439   of block sizes b ∈ {2, 4, 8, 16} which determine the number of multiplicative matrices. Additionally,
1440   for both, AdaLoRA and BOFT, we search over the same learning rates as for the other LoRA variants.
1441   Further, we introduce hyperparameters that result in additional speed-up of our initialization, namely
1442   a threshold τ that considers components as converged, and a threshold δ that stops computation of the
1443
       initialization when a certain percentage of components have converged. By default, we set τ = 0.99
       and δ = 1, i.e. we only stop when all components are converged, and they are almost exactly the
1444
       same. These parameters provide additional leeway to speed up the initialization stage of EVA.
1445
1446   We have explored the sensitivity of LoRA to different initialization schemes and found that, similar
1447   to other prominent initialization schemes (He et al., 2015; Glorot & Bengio, 2010), scale plays an
1448   important role along with directions. Originally, (Hu et al., 2022) propose to set α = 2r, however,
1449
       we found that this parameter is quite sensitive as also shown in (Kalajdzievski, 2023). Similarly,
       different ranks lead to very different results on different downstream tasks. Therefore, we suggest to
1450
       always search over more ranks and choose the best performing one if the required compute budget is
1451
       available. We also experimented with different learning rates for the A and B matrices as proposed
1452   in (Hayou et al., 2024), however, this did not result in consistent improvements. Instead, we found
1453   that learning rates for LoRA-style training can be surprisingly high (4e − 3 for DeBERTav3Base ),
1454   while for larger models the learning rate needs to be approximately a magnitude smaller. A simple
1455   recipe that worked consistently well, was setting α = 1, which results in a similar scaling factor as in
1456   Kalajdzievski (2023), and searching over a set of small learning rates for larger models and higher
1457   learning rates for smaller ones. For EVA, the only tunable hyperparameter is the rank budget, which
       we recommend to tune along with the fine-tuning learning rate.

1918   E.2   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
1919
1920   We implemented our pipeline that supports training for Meta-World on top of the code-base provided
1921   by Schmied et al. (2024). Our custom implementation supports training LoRA, DoRA and EVA.
1922   Furthermore, we leverage the peft library (Mangrulkar et al., 2022) to train the remaining methods.
1923
       For our experiments on Meta-World, we use a GPT2-like network architecture (Radford et al., 2019)
1924   with 4 Transformer layers, 8 heads, and hidden dimension of 512 resulting in 16M parameters. We
1925   use a context of 50 time steps, which amounts to a sequence length of 200, as each timestep contains
1926   states, actions, rewards and RTGs. We embed states, actions, rewards and return-to-gos (RTGs) using
1927   separate linear embedding layers per modality, as proposed by Chen et al. (2021a). We train with a
1928   batch size of 128 using a constant learning rate of 1e−4 , 4000 linear warm-up steps followed by a
1929   cosine decay to 1e−6 , using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017). We employ gradient
1930   clipping of 0.25, weight decay of 0.01, and a dropout rate of 0.2. Our DT implementation employs
1931   global position embedding. For every task, we set the target return to the maximum return achieved
1932
       in the respective training datasets, as proposed by (Schmied et al., 2024). Furthermore, we employ
       mixed-precision (Micikevicius et al., 2017) and flash-attention (Dao, 2023) to speed-up training.
1933
1934   We first pre-train a DT on all MT40 tasks (80M transitions) for 1M updates via next-action prediction
1935   by minimizing the mean-squared error. The resulting pre-trained model attains an average success
1936   rate of 80% across all MT40 tasks. Then we fine-tune the DT on each of the CW10 down-stream
1937   tasks for 100K updates with the same set of hyperparameters as used for pre-training. We run all our
1938
       experiments on a public research cluster with 4xA100-40GB GPU nodes. A single fine-tuning run
       with EVA for one task takes roughly 1 hour on one A100.

       F.1   C OMPLEXITY
2086
2087   The computation of SVD introduces computational overhead in the initial training stage. Since we
2088   do not require gradient computation or storing of optimizer states, there is no overhead in terms
2089   of memory. SVD has a time complexity of O(min(b2 d, bd2 )) which can be reduced to O(k 2 b) for
2090   k << d by randomly choosing k columns from X as introduced in Halko et al. (2011). Let T
2091   be the number of minibatches until all components are converged for N weight matrices, then the
2092   time complexity is O(N T k 2 b). In other words, the complexity scales linearly with the number of
2093   weight matrices and the number of minibatches. To speed up the computation of SVD, we provide an
2094
       implementation that runs entirely on GPU.
