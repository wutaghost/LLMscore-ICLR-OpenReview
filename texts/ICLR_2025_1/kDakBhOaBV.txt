011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Current trends in pre-training Large Language Models (LLMs) primarily focus on
014            the scaling of model and dataset size. While the quality of pre-training data is
015
               considered an important factor for training powerful LLMs, it remains a nebulous
               concept that has not been rigorously characterized. To this end, we propose a for-
016
               malization of one key aspect of data quality – measuring the variability of natural
017
               language data – specifically via a measure we call the diversity coefficient. Our
018            empirical analysis shows that the proposed diversity coefficient aligns with the
019            intuitive properties of diversity and variability, e.g., it increases as the number of
020            latent concepts increases. Then, we measure the diversity coefficient of publicly
021            available pre-training datasets and demonstrate that their formal diversity is high
022            compared to theoretical lower and upper bounds. Finally, we conduct a compre-
023            hensive set of controlled interventional experiments with GPT-2 and LLaMAv2
024            that demonstrate the diversity coefficient of pre-training data characterizes use-
025            ful aspects of downstream model evaluation performance—totaling 44 models of
026
               various sizes (51M to 7B parameters). We conclude that our formal notion of di-
               versity is an important aspect of data quality that captures variability and causally
027
               leads to improved evaluation performance.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Current trends in pre-training Large Language Models (LLMs) tend to concentrate on model and
033   dataset size scaling Chowdhery et al. (2022); Nostalgebraist (2022); OpenAI (2023); Google (2023).
034   Therefore, vast amounts of effort have been invested in understanding neural scaling laws—the
035   power-law relationship between the loss of artificial deep networks and the size of the pre-training
036   dataset for a fixed compute budget (Hestness et al., 2017; Rosenfeld et al., 2019; Henighan et al.,
037   2020; Kaplan et al., 2020; Gordon et al., 2021; Hernandez et al., 2021; Jones, 2021; Zhai et al., 2022;
038   Hoffmann et al., 2022; Clark et al., 2022; Neumann & Gros, 2022). In addition, recent work focuses
039
      on training a fixed-size model but using very large, trillion-token datasets (Touvron et al., 2023a;b).
      However, the effectiveness of these systems also fundamentally relies on the quality Longpre et al.
040
      (2023), variability and coverage of the pre-training data Hashimoto (2021); David et al. (2010) and
041
      not only the size. In particular, the richness and variety of data, otherwise known as data diversity,
042   is a key aspect of data quality that plays an important role in researchers’ and practitioners’ choice
043   of pre-training corpora for general capabilities (Gao et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
044   2023a; Eldan & Li, 2023; Gunasekar et al., 2023). In other words, diverse data is high quality data
045   when your goal is to instill general capabilities in a model.
046
      In addition, experimental settings demonstrate that the level of variety and diversity in pre-training
047
      data is likely a strong causal factor in the development of in-context learning (ICL) in LLMs, an
048   essential component of the versatility and generality of LLMs (Xie et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2022;
049   Chan et al., 2022). However, data quality, diversity and coverage David et al. (2010) are often
050   discussed in vague and imprecise ways Longpre et al. (2023). Even in instances of quantitative
051   approaches to data quality, methods are often not interpretable as to why certain datasets are prefer-
052   able over others, or require reference corpora and, thereby, limit the diversity of resulting datasets
053   (Xie et al., 2023a;c). Hence, we propose to ground the discussion of data quality and, in particular,
      data diversity, through the Task2Vec diversity coefficient for natural language—a metric using the


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      expected distance between Task2Vec embeddings of data batches (Achille et al., 2019a; Miranda
055   et al., 2022a) to quantify the level of structural and semantic diversity of natural language data,
056   allowing researchers and practitioners to move beyond scale alone.
057
058
      Hence, our key contributions are:
059
            1. A paradigm shift beyond dataset scale to a data-centric machine learning perspective
060            through a quantitative data diversity metric for data variability—the diversity coefficient.
061
            2. We advance discussions on data quality by developing an interpretable and formal quanti-
062
               tative measure of an important aspect of data quality—data diversity—in the form of the
063            Task2Vec diversity coefficient for natural language.
064
            3. We demonstrate that the diversity coefficient aligns with human intuitions about variability
065
               and diversity through interpretability and relationship analyses. For example, if the number
066
               of latent concepts increases, a richer vocabulary is used, or datasets are concatenated, then
067            the diversity coefficient should increase.
068
            4. We quantitatively demonstrate that public datasets for LLM pre-training exhibit high diver-
069
               sity, by them to well-motivated lower and upper bounds for our diversity metric.
070
071         5. We provide evidence via interventional experiments with various GPT-2 and LLaMAv2
072
               models that pre-training models on data with higher diversity coefficients leads to better
               downstream performance on language modeling for diverse evaluation corpora—totaling
073
               44 models pre-trained from scratch of various sizes (54M to 7B).
074
075   We conclude that the diversity coefficient serves as an effective measure of the diversity of natural
076   language data and, thus, enables researchers and practitioners to more rigorously study this aspect
077   of natural language data quality and move beyond scale alone.

      Our aim is to formalize the concept of data diversity and empirically demonstrate, through inter-
      ventional experiments, its positive relationship with the evaluation performance of the validation
098
      set. We estimate the diversity of a dataset through the Task2Vec diversity coefficient for natural lan-
099
      guage, which embeds randomly sampled batches of data using Task2Vec embeddings (Achille et al.,
100   2019a) and computes the expected cosine distance between these (see Figure 1). It approximates the
101   diversity of the dataset because distances between Task2Vec embeddings approximate the distance
102   between the generative (task) distributions of the batches.

104   2.1   C OMPUTING TASK 2V EC E MBEDDINGS F OR T EXT
105
106   Task2Vec embeddings are defined as the diagonal of an (approximated) Fischer Information Matrix
107   (FIM); the FIM is derived from the parameters of a fixed probe network—in the case of text data, a
      language model—whose final layer has been fine-tuned on the target data to embed (Achille et al.,


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2019a). Since the FIM encodes information on which parameters of the probe network are most
109   important in predicting the text sequence, the diagonal of the FIM encodes a compact and tractable
110   representation of these parameters. Thus, the Task2Vec embedding of text data represents which
111   parameters of the probe network are most important in solving the next token prediction task given by
112   a text sequence. We use a probe network with final layer fine-tuning on the target data in calculating
113   the FIM as this is the only validated method (to our knowledge) for creating Task2Vec embeddings
114   (Achille et al., 2019a). Importantly, the probe network is fixed for all batches in order to make
115   embeddings comparable between tasks (Achille et al., 2019a).
116   Though Task2Vec embeddings are primarily used in visual and meta-learning tasks (Achille et al.,
117   2019a; Miranda et al., 2022a; Wallace et al., 2021), we adapt and repurpose this technique to the
118   novel domain of natural language data. In particular, we use GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) as our
119   fixed probe network and fine-tune its final layer with a next-token prediction objective.
120
      Formally, we compute the FIM as:

      2.2   C OMPUTING THE D IVERSITY C OEFFICIENT FOR NATURAL L ANGUAGE T EXT
129
130   The diversity coefficient is the expected pairwise cosine distance d between Task2Vec embeddings
131   of batches of text B1 , B2 sampled from a natural language dataset D:
132
                                          ˆ
                                         div(D) = EB1 ,B2 ∼D d(f⃗B1 , f⃗B2 )
133
134   By measuring the distance between Task2Vec embeddings of sampled text batches, the diversity
135   coefficient captures the average intrinsic variability of the sampled batches, serving as an efficient
136   estimate for the diversity of information contained in the dataset. It is an effective “soft” count of
137   the number of distinct batches.
138   A useful variation on the concept of the diversity coefficient is the cross diversity coefficient, which
139   calculates the informational variation i.e. diversity between separate datasets. Following the frame-
140   work of the diversity coefficient, the cross diversity coefficient is the expected pairwise cosine
141   distance d between Task2Vec embeddings of batches of text B1 sampled from a natural language
142   dataset D1 and batches of text B2 sampled from a separate dataset D2 :
143

145   We introduce these two definitions (diversity and cross diversity) to show our results hold with
146   respect to two intuitive and logical ways to define data diversity (details in Appendix E). In addition,
147   the cross diversity coefficient also measures the similarity/alignment or difference between datasets
148   (using Task2Vec representations of data).

399   4   R ELATED W ORK
400
401   Existing diversity metrics have concentrated on data produced by Generative Adversarial Networks
402
      (GANs) and involve variations of a precision- and recall-based framework originally proposed in
      (Sajjadi et al., 2018) to measure quality and diversity, respectively (Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019; Si-
403
      mon et al., 2019; Naeem et al., 2020). Similar to our metric, these methods use embedding functions.
404
      These methods argue data quality is not synonymous with data diversity in the context of GANs
405   (Fowl et al., 2020) and take a two-metric approach. Regarding LLMs, we argue that data diversity
406   is a subset of data quality, which is demonstrably important to enable emergent capabilities such as
407   in-context learning Xie et al. (2022); Shin et al. (2022); Chan et al. (2022). Recent work has also
408   confirmed the importance of diversity from the perspective of deduplication (Tirumala et al., 2023),
409   and the general importance of quantitatively informed data selection (Xie et al., 2023a). Hence,
410   diversity metrics capture an important aspect of data quality.
411   A recently proposed diversity metric that doesn’t rely on an embedding function is the Vendi Score
412   (Friedman & Dieng, 2022), which is the exponential of the Shannon entropy of the eigenvalues of a
413   similarity matrix/kernel. For more discussion of related work, please see Appendix B.
414

      5   D ISCUSSION
417
      Our work extends, examines, and thus validates the application of the Task2Vec diversity coeffi-
418
      cient to a new modality—natural language—and demonstrates that open LLMs are pre-trained on
419
      formally diverse data.
420
421   One potential limitation of our method is the need for a data representation. Although the require-
422   ment for a data representation might seem restrictive, we argue that it is an inherent aspect of data
423
      processing. Choosing symbols (e.g., one-hot vectors), raw pixels, etc. is a choice of data repre-
      sentation. We suggest deep learning representations due to their overwhelming success in machine
424
      learning, e.g. in computer vision (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; He et al., 2015), natural language pro-
425
      cessing (Devlin et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023; Google,
426   2023), game playing (Silver et al., 2016; Mnih et al., 2013; Ye et al., 2021), theorem proving (Rabe
427   et al.; Polu & Sutskever, 2020; Han et al.), code (Chen et al.) and more. In addition, widely avail-
428   able open-source pre-trained models (e.g. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
429   2023a), etc.) have made choosing a good embedding method easier. Another potential limitation of
430   the diversity coefficient is the need to fine-tune a probe-network. Though this does introduce some
431   computational overhead, it is relatively small (fine-tuning only the final layer) and outweighed by
      the utility of using information-rich Task2Vec embeddings.

      Common Crawl, Wikipedia), data used to train new models may be curated from such datasets,

      necessitating a metric that captures overall data diversity. These scenarios are thus in favor of using
732   the Task2Vec diversity coefficient. Therefore, our method is likely more general and scalable than
733   the Vendi Score. We leave a detailed comparison with the Vendi Score as future work.
734
735
      Returning to the utility of a practical diversity metric, there is strong empirical support from efforts
      at increasing multi-modal image and text data quality that using semantically deduplicated data (as
736
      opposed to simply exact match deduplication) for training can yield impressive training efficiency
737
      gains and even improve out of domain generalization (Abbas et al., 2023; 2024). Though motivated
738   by the same underlying hypothesis about the importance of diversity in data quality, our work on the
739   diversity coefficient focuses on text-only data, the standard for training LLMs, and yields a more in-
740   terpretable metric that allows practitioners to rigorously understand the quality of datasets, enabling
741   the informed choice of baseline higher quality datasets rather than only being able to improve a cho-
742   sen dataset. Furthermore, the generality of the diversity coefficient allows it to assess and inform the
743   results of traditional data pruning, which has been shown to be a promising method for increasing
744   data quality (Sorscher et al., 2023), while also lending itself naturally as a useful metric for guiding
745   diverse data corpora construction, which has also been seen to be a fruitful endeavor with the release
746
      large, diverse, open source corpora (Gao et al., 2020).
747   More generally, better understanding and clarity about the characteristics of datasets and data qual-
748   ity enable greater control and direction of model capabilities (Mitchell et al., 2023), which become
749   especially important as models become more complex, more powerful, and more important to sci-
750   entific and economic activity.
751   However, the benefits of this more sophisticated aggregation method are not clear, and its compu-
752   tation (O(n3 )) is more expensive than the diversity coefficient (O(n2 )). Moreover, it assumes a
753   suitable similarity function/kernel, and does not provide guidance on data representation, arguably
754   the most important ingredient in machine learning. Furthermore, they suggest that utilizing data
755   representational methods such as embedding networks that require pretrained models may be limit-
      ing. We argue instead that data representation is a fundamental property of data processing that has

836   C.2   T OKEN BASED D EFINITIONS OF D IVERSITY ARE B RITTLE TO PARAPHRASINGS
837
838   One can also consider formalizations of diversity based on token distributions in a batch (or data
839   set). We did not use this because previous work has already demonstrated its limitation as data rep-
840
      resentations for train-test data contamination detection (Jiang et al., 2024). This is highly related
      to our work because contamination detection requires good data representations for detecting dif-
841
      ferences in text. In particular, they say: “these contamination definitions primarily revolve around
842
      n-gram or token overlaps, which only target direct duplications present in both training and evalua-
843   tion datasets and might provide both high false positive rate (since many semantically different texts
844   have overlaps) and false negative rate (since simple paraphrasing can evade detection)”. To make it
845   more concrete, consider an example. Consider the sentences “The great paper” and “the fantastic
846   paper”. These would be binned to entirely different locations in a histogram, while the difference
847   in the sentence is varying much more smoothly. Therefore, token based representation of data are
848   brittle to simple paraphrasing and as our example shows result in overly “harsh” metrics—similar to
849   previous work that criticizes harsh metrics for LLM evaluation (Schaeffer et al., 2023a). In addition,
850   previous work has shown that importance weights of data like text are statistically intractable to
851
      estimate without sufficient additional structure (Xie et al., 2023b; Bengtsson et al., 2008; Gelman
      & Meng, 2004; Snyder et al., 2008). For example, the histograms of different types of text end up
852
      being sparse, even with hashing e.g., consider 50k (GPT2), 30K (LLaMA2) or 10K (DSIR) fea-
853
      ture vectors. We conjecture that if these representations were robust, the transformer architecture
854   (Vaswani et al., 2017) wouldn’t have emerged as the successful architecture that it is today (2024).

      C.3   D ISCUSSION ON M ODEL AGNOSTICS F ORMALIZATIONS OF DATA D IVERSITY
857
858   Theoretical Relation of FIM (Model) and Kolmogorov (Model Agnostic) based metric: Theory
859   can be important to have a better understanding of the world. Therefore, theoreticians might be in-
860   terested in diversity definitions that are model agnostic, since, they might be interested in the inherit
861   diversity of the data or task. We emphasize that Task2Vec was derived with such considerations in
862   mind: “The FIM is also related to the (Kolmogorov) complexity of a task, a property that can be
863   used to define a computable metric of the learning distance between tasks” (Achille et al., 2019a).
      More precisely (Achille et al., 2018) showed that the training dynamics of a deep network minimiz-


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      ing a loss function can be viewed as approximately optimizing an upper bound on the Kolmogorov
865   Structure Function (KSF) of the learning task. This connection arises because the loss function in-
866   corporates a complexity term measured by the Fisher Information Matrix of the network weights.
867   Therefore, Fisher information provides a computable way to relate the geometry of the loss land-
868   scape to notions of task complexity linked to Kolmogorov complexity. Therefore, Task2Vec (via
869   FIM) was derived with Kolmogorov Complexity in mind. In Summary, the relationship between
870   Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) and Kolmogorov Complexity, as discussed in Achille’s works
871   (Achille et al., 2019a; 2018; 2019b; 2021), suggests that FIM can serve as an upper bound for Kol-
872   mogorov Complexity in the context of learning tasks. Specifically, the FIM captures the sensitivity
873
      of the learning model to its parameters, reflecting the amount of information about the data that the
      model encodes. This sensitivity can indirectly bound the Kolmogorov Complexity by quantifying
874
      the model’s complexity and its capacity to learn from data, thus offering a bridge between empirical
875
      measures of complexity (FIM) and theoretical ones (Kolmogorov Complexity). However, explicit
876   relationships quantifying how distances based on Fisher information matrices correlate with true
877   Kolmogorov complexity are not provided.
878
879
      Remarks on approximations to Kolmogorov: The central way we control for our results to
      be model agnostic in practice is by fixing the model. This means that the data is the only thing
880
      being varied, and the features being used for any diversity of computations are consistent. Since,
881
      Kolmogorov Complexity in uncomputable we conjecture this is approximately optimal—given that
882   it’s essential in practice to have a way to represent the data.

      We include the notion of the cross diversity coefficient as it also leverages the ability of distance
931   between Task2Vec embeddings to capture important properties of the data, and allows one to more
932   clearly assess similarity/diversity between datasets (hence the term cross diversity), as opposed sim-
933   ilarity/diversity within datasets (the focus of the “normal” diversity coefficient). Thus, when infor-

935   determine the diversity of the concatenation of the given datasets (to form the total/overall dataset).
936   Thus, the cross diversity coefficient provides us the opportunity to more robustly characterize meth-
937   ods of defining data diversity by offering a different perspective on combined datasets from the
938   diversity coefficient. Note that in the case D1 = D2 , i.e. one is calculating the cross diversity of a
939
      single dataset (with itself), cross diversity becomes equivalent to diversity.

1176   GPT-2 model itself has 12 layers, 12 heads, 768-d hidden size, and 117M total parameters. The LM
1177   head is a linear layer with weights corresponding to the input embedding layers. The model was

       K.1    BACKGROUND
1299
1300   The GINC dataset is generated using the latent concept framework proposed in Xie et al. (2021),
1301   where language models condition on a prompt to infer latent document concepts learned during pre-
1302   training. The pretraining distribution is defined using a uniform mixture of Hidden Markov Models
1303   (HMMs) parameterized over a family Θ of latent concepts.
1304
1305   K.2    D EFINITIONS OF GINC DATASET PARAMETERS
1306
1307   Number of latent concepts: A latent concept θ parameterizes the transitions of a HMM in the
1308   mixture. A latent concept (e.g. a wiki bio) contains document statistics, such as semantics, syntax,
1309   and the formatting of and distribution of tokens.
1310   Vocabulary size: Each HMM in a given mixture outputs a fixed number of tokens, defined as the
1311   vocabulary size. The vocabulary is generated by enumerating combinations of letters from a to z, aa
1312   to az, etc. The delimiter token is designated by a backslash. Sequences are tokenized by whitespace.

1339   L     D ISCUSSION ( CONT.)
1340
1341   Our paper introduces a metric that leverages tunable parameters, such as the number of batches,
1342   batch size, probe network configuration (pre-trained vs. random, fine-tuned vs. not) and depth.
1343   While these elements influence the diversity coefficient’s absolute value and necessitate the recali-
1344   bration of lower and upper bounds (see Appendices I.3 and H), a consistent choice of hyperparame-
1345   ters can mitigate these effects.
1346   Intriguingly, our proposed diversity may not always correlate with model performance, as high
1347   diversity could simply be due to uniform noise. Nevertheless, we contend that a higher diversity,
1348   in the context of a sufficiently large model, likely indicates superior performance and data quality.
1349   Furthermore, our diversity metric is intentionally designed to be widely applicable, albeit concealing
       causal factors, rendering it an effective tool for ablation studies.


                                                        25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       Despite our diversity metric’s broader applicability, it may obscure certain causal factors. This
1351   limitation is intentional to enhance its practical usage—since causality is often difficult to infer and
1352   is out of scope. This can be overcome with data property ablation studies, as we showed in our
1353   GINC dataset experiments.
1354
       Currently, our proposed bounds are specific to sequence data with a symbolic vocabulary, limit-
1355
       ing their applicability across different modalities. To overcome this limitation, we suggest using a
1356
       multimodal embedding method for embedding diversity coefficients and lower/upper bounds across
1357   tasks.
1358
1359   To really clarify why FIM is better than activations, we provide this intuitive explanation. FIM gives
1360
       a weight/feature of which parameter of the generative distribution matters, e.g. the first coordinate
       of Task2Vec corresponds to how artsy the text sequence is. This is a feature of a task or dataset itself.
1361
       Therefore, FIM exactly approximates the (task) data generative distribution we are trying to embed.
1362
       Therefore, we conjecture it results in superior representations for datasets compared to activations
1363   since it directly approximates the data (or task) generative distribution. Our study, and references,
1364   provide positive evidence in favor of this argument.
1365
1366
       The strength of embeddings is their ability to approximate semantics in a way that symbols may
       struggle with, such as distinguishing the equivalence of two sentences with different symbols but
1367
       identical meanings. In NLP there is no easy way to determine this equivalence. In formal mathe-
1368
       matics, symbolic semantics and thus equivalence can sometimes be done exactly. Though it does
1369   not come without its costs, e.g. requires expert knowledge, computationally demanding or (approx-
1370   imately) exhaustive representations like e-graphs. Therefore, embedding methods for data diversity,
1371   quality, etc. have the unique advantage of being more generally applicable.
1372
       Our diversity calculations predominantly utilize a small model (GPT-2). Despite the ongoing dis-
1373
       cussion concerning the emergence of large language models (LLMs), our conjecture extends the
1374
       results to models of all sizes. We base this inference on the fact that the manifestation of emergence
1375   is intimately tied to the specific metric employed, and the sudden unpredictable jumps disappear
1376   when smooth metrics are applied Schaeffer et al. (2023b). The cosine distance is smooth and does
1377   not have this issue.
1378
       Why and when does diversity matter? We propose two central conjectures for the importance of
1379
       diversity and provide the underlying rationale:
1380
1381         1. Conjecture 1: Diversity is essential because it promotes learning-to-learn (a surrogate
1382            for General Intelligence). The main argument is that a significant level of diversity corre-
1383            sponds to a multitude of tasks in the dataset. Therefore, to achieve high (test) performance,
1384            the model must perform well on all tasks. One potential strategy is by learning-to-learn,
1385            thereby allowing transferability when tasked with a new problem. Another alternative could
1386            be to memorize all tasks.
1387         2. Conjecture 2: Diversity is crucial because it enhances the probability that the pre-
1388            training set covers the test set. Diversity is a formal score of coverage—it aims to reflect
1389            the effective number of tasks in a dataset. Thus, increased diversity equates to more tasks in
1390            a dataset. This (could) boosts the chance of the training set covering the test set, hence im-
1391
                proving performance, given a sufficiently large model like an LLM. The direct exploration
                of this conjecture is slated for future investigation, but we provide a suggestive (correlative)
1392
                analysis of one reason why LLMs might perform so well.
1393
1394   Another benefit is that our method does not rely on activations from an arbitrarily selected layer
1395   in a network. Lastly, note that activations may be unreliable for embedding dataset/tasks because
1396   large distances between datasets/tasks may be due to well-separated decision boundaries instead
1397   of intrinsic semantic properties of the dataset/task. In contrast, the diversity coefficient is well-
1398   justified, extensively tested in our work and previous work, e.g. the diversity coefficient correlates
1399   with ground truth diversities, cluster according to semantics, taxonomy etc. (see section F and
1400
       Achille et al. (2019a); Miranda et al. (2022a)). In short, FIM-based representations are motivated
       by information theory (e.g. FIMs are metrics in distributions) and have been extensively tested by
1401
       independent sources (Miranda et al., 2022a; Achille et al., 2019a; Vu et al., 2020).
1402
1403   Data has taken a central role in the success of modern machine learning methods, like GPT4 OpenAI
       (2023), CLIP Radford et al. (2021), and PaLM 2 Google (2023), with special relevance for architec-

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
       tures with few inductive biases, like the popular Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017). Therefore, it is
1405   paramount to understand the pretraining data we use, beyond scale alone. We conclude the diversity
1406   coefficient is a trustworthy metric, and conjecture the diversity coefficient can be used to build qual-
1407   ity, diverse datasets for LLMs. We hope our contributions inspire more effective data collection and
1408   curation in machine learning that goes beyond scale alone to improve performance.

       Limitations:
1410
1411          • The diversity coefficient presents an aggregate measure that masks the underlying causal
1412            factors. Despite this, we illustrate how it might be employed to uncover these factors. We
1413            show this through the use of vocabulary size and latent space, acknowledging that these
1414            experiments could be resource-intensive. Causality is a challenging topic, and we do not
1415            claim to solve it through our experiments. Our experiments in this regime are mostly to
1416            show that the diversity coefficient (might) correlates/captures different sources of diversity
1417            beyond number of concepts or tasks.
1418          • The computation of Task2Vec embeddings requires more resources than computing simply
1419            the activations. However, given the proven correlation with ground truth task generative
1420            parameters from previous studies, we posit that it supersedes activations. Furthermore, we
1421            hypothesize that using activations could result in high distances due to optimization for
1422
                decision boundaries, making it less reliable for measuring distances i.e., high distances in
                activation space might be artificially high. We observed this but plan to give more detailed
1423
                study in the future.
1424
1425          • The choice of an expectation as the aggregation function could be seen as arbitrary. Alter-
1426
                natives such as the Vendi score are promising, but still under-explored and computationally
                demanding compared to expectations/sums. Future work could focus on the comparative
1427
                analysis of the total distance sum and the Vendi score. We hypothesize, in line with the
1428
                Central Limit Theorem (CLT), that the results might not differ significantly e.g., CLT still
1429            converges to (unit) Normal given the proper normalization procedure.
1430
              • We reject the notion that the use of models is a limitation. As discussed earlier, models
1431
                can provide superior data embeddings, and all forms of data representations are always
1432
                required. For example, the identity function or symbols are data representations.
1433
1434   Implications:
1435
1436          • Given the impressive performance of LLMs, our study suggests a correlation with our
1437            diversity measure, potentially providing an explanation for this high level of performance.
1438          • High diversity implies a larger task coverage. Therefore, we conjecture that a highly di-
1439            verse pre-training set could increase the probability of including relevant pre-training data
1440            for the target task/testing. This suggests that collecting more diverse data could be a method
1441            to enhance performance. If the model is sufficiently large, we conjecture this method al-
1442
                ways (stochastically) monotonically increases the performance (as implied by (Zhang et al.,
                2021)).
1443
1444          • The transition from a qualitative to a quantitative measure of diversity can be seen as a
1445            significant advancement in the field because of conceptual transitions about how we think
1446
                and talk about data quality/diversity.
1447          • The use of Task2Vec to embed data implies a method applicable to any modality, potentially
1448            benefiting all areas of machine learning research.
1449
       Importantly, the relationship between pre-training dataset diversity and test performance remains a
1450
       key question. To investigate, we conduct preliminary experiments, pre-training three GPT-2 models
1451
       on datasets of varying diversities, then evaluate their performance on diverse validation datasets. We
1452   observe in Table 2 that increased diversity leads to decreased cross-entropy loss, indicating a positive
1453   relationship between diversity and model performance on diverse tasks. Although the cross-entropy
1454   values are arguably large, Figure 7 shows that even the smallest 51M parameter models were still
1455   pre-trained enough to grasp important aspects of sentence syntax and structure of their respective
1456   dataset, indicating model performance on evaluation represents meaningful improvements in ability.
1457   Hence, we conjecture pre-training on higher diversity data improves test performance on diverse
       tasks, though more extensive experiments are needed to know so conclusively.

       L.1   F UTURE W ORK
1459
1460   Our future research will explore the potential of the Task2Vec distance function for pre-training
1461   dataset curation. Given that the objective of pre-training is to maximize downstream task perfor-
1462   mance, we define high-quality training data as data that facilitates the best achievable performance
1463   on such tasks. We anticipate that higher diversity in the dataset will increase the likelihood of achiev-
1464   ing this objective. The rationale is that a higher data diversity implies a broader coverage of tasks
1465   or batches, thereby increasing the probability of training the model on tasks or data representations
1466
       that are relevant to evaluation tasks. Our focus will be to leverage Task2Vec to assess the similarity
       between individual data points, batches, or datasets to a target task. This assessment will enable us
1467
       to curate the training data by selectively removing tasks that resemble random, noisy, or irrelevant
1468
       sequences, which may adversely affect downstream performance.
