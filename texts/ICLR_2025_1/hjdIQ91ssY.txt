009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Fairness remains a critical concern in healthcare, where unequal access to ser-
012            vices and treatment outcomes can adversely affect patient health. While Feder-
013            ated Learning (FL) presents a collaborative and privacy-preserving approach to
014            model training, ensuring fairness is challenging due to heterogeneous data across
015            institutions, and current research primarily addresses non-medical applications.
016            To fill this gap, we introduce FairFedMed, the first FL dataset specifically de-
017            signed to study group fairness (i.e., demographics) in the medical field. It con-
018
               sists of paired 2D SLO funfus images and 3D OCT B-Scans from 15,165 glau-
               coma patients, along with six different demographic attributes. Existing state-
019
               of-the-art FL models may work well for natural images but often struggle with
020
               medical images due to their unique characteristics. Moreover, these models
021            do not sufficiently address performance disparities across diverse demographic
022            groups. To overcome these limitations, we propose FairLoRA, a novel fairness-
023            aware FL framework based on singular value decomposition(SVD)-based low-
024            rank approximation. FairLoRA incorporates customized singular value matrices
025            for each demographic group and shares singular vector matrices across all demo-
026            graphic groups, ensuring both model equity and computational efficiency. Exper-
027            imental results on the FairFedMed dataset demonstrate that FairLoRA not only
028            achieves state-of-the-art performance in medical image classification but also sig-
029
               nificantly improves fairness across diverse populations. Our code and dataset
               can be accessible via the Github anonymous link: https://github.com/
030
               Anonymouse4Science/FairFedMed-FairLoRA.git

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Fairness is a critical issue in healthcare, as unequal access to services and disparities in treatment
036   outcomes can adversely affect patient health and quality of life. The increasing reliance on artificial
037   intelligence complicates this challenge, as many models are trained on data from specific popula-
038   tions that do not adequately represent all demographic groups. To ensure equitable healthcare, it
039   is essential for models to achieve strong overall performance while consistently performing well
040   across diverse populations, making fairness a key challenge in medical applications.
041
      A major barrier to achieving fairness in the medical field is the challenge of sharing sensitive pa-
042   tient data across institutions due to intellectual property and privacy concerns. Traditional central-
043   ized training methods require aggregating data from multiple sources, which poses significant risks
044   of data breaches and privacy violations. This limitation hinders multi-institution collaborations,
045   where diverse datasets are crucial for developing accurate and fair models. Federated Learning (FL)
046   McMahan et al. (2017); Ezzeldin et al. (2023) has emerged as a promising solution to address this
047   barrier. FL allows institutions to jointly train models in a distributed fashion without sharing raw
048   data, thereby protecting patient data privacy. This decentralized method keeps sensitive information
049   local, reducing privacy risks while enhancing model robustness and generalizability.
050   While FL addresses privacy concerns, it encounters challenges related to fairness due to varying
051   data distributions across institutions. Each client may have different local data and demographic
052   distributions, making it complex to ensure fairness within the FL framework. Research on fairness
053   in FL is categorized into site fairness and group fairness, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Site fairness
      ensures the model performs equally across institutions, preventing any site from suffering negative

070   equitably across various demographic attributes such as different racial groups or ethnic groups.
071   In healthcare, group fairness holds profound significance, as misdiagnosis in certain demographic
072   groups can lead to severe consequences for patients.
073   Unfortunately, current research on group fairness in FL mainly focuses on non-medical tasks, such
074   as recommendation systems (Agrawal et al., 2024) and financial transactions (Badar et al., 2024).
075   In the medical field, cross-institution demographic differences are common due to geographic het-
076   erogeneity (e.g., hospitals in Asia and hospitals in North America), but research on how to manage
077   these differences within the FL framework is relatively limited. Bridging this gap is crucial for
078   developing models that promote health equity and uphold the principle of justice.
079   In this paper, we address the shortage of medical datasets for fairness-aware FL by introducing the
080   FairFedMed dataset for eye disease. This dataset includes 15,165 images from real-world clinics,
081   consisting of paired 2D scanning laser ophthalmoscop (SLO) fundus images and 3D optical coher-
082   ence tomography (OCT) B-Scans from 15,165 patients. To our knowledge, it is the first FL dataset
083   for group fairness that includes both real-world 2D and 3D medical images, including six different
084   demographic attributes: age, gender, race, ethnicity, preferred language, and marital status. The
085   dataset is partitioned into multiple clients with varied demographic distributions reflecting a real-
086
      world FL setting for group fainrness.
087   Existing state-of-the-art FL methods perform well with natural images but often struggle with med-
088   ical images due to their unique characteristics. Moreover, these models fail to sufficiently address
089   group fairness across different demographics. To overcome these limitations, we propose FairLoRA,
090   a fairness-aware FL framework for eye disease classification. This framework is based on CLIP
091   (Radford et al., 2021) and introduces fairness into the singular value decomposition (SVD) low-rank
092
      approximation method. Specifically, to preserve unique intra-group characteristics, FairLoRA cus-
      tomizes singular value matrices for each demographic group while sharing singular vector matrices
093
      across all groups to capture inter-group relationships. Local models train a set of low-rank matri-
094
      ces on their local data, and the global model integrates matrices from different clients, allowing the
095   model to aggregate global knowledge about demographic attributes from distributed data. This col-
096   laborative approach ensures that the model is not biased toward any particular client or demographic
097   group. Experimental results on the FairFedMed dataset demonstrate that our model achieves state-
098   of-the-art performance in medical image classification while ensuring equitable outcomes across
099   demographic groups. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
100
101          • We introduce FairFedMed, the first FL dataset for group fairness that includes real-world
102
               paired 2D and 3D medical images, along with six different demographic attributes.
103          • We create an experimental setup that divides the dataset into multiple distinct clients with
104            varied demographic distributions, simulating a real-world FL environment with group fair-
105            ness. We benchmark several state-of-the-art FL methods under this setting.
106          • We propose FairLoRA, a fairness-aware FL framework for eye disease classification. It
107            customizes singular value matrices for each group to preserve unique characteristics while
               sharing singular vector matrices across groups to capture inter-group relationships.

      2   R ELATED WORK
109
110
      Fairness Learning in Medical Imaging: Fairness learning in medical imaging aims to reduce bi-
111   ases and ensure equitable outcomes for all patient groups, particularly underrepresented minorities.
112   Currently, most research focuses on fairness models and datasets in classification tasks, with limited
113   attention given to cross-domain fairness. Publicly available datasets, such as CheXpert (Irvin et al.,
114   2019), MIMIC-CXR (Johnson et al., 2019), and Fitzpatrick17k (Groh et al., 2021), support fairness
115   studies but often lack comprehensive identity attributes, focus predominantly on 2D images, and
116   do not include federated clients/sites representing diverse demographic distributions, limiting their
117   applicability to federated learning tasks or 3D medical imaging. Recent methods (Quadrianto et al.,
118   2019; Ramaswamy et al., 2021; Zhang & Sang, 2020; Park et al., 2022; Roh et al., 2020; Sarhan
119
      et al., 2020; Zafar et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2019; Lohia et al.,
      2019) have made progress in algorithmic fairness for medical imaging, but these approaches mainly
120
      address bias within individual clients or sites. The performance of multiple clients in a federated
121
      learning setup remains largely unexplored, representing a significant gap in fairness research.
122
123   Federated Learning (FL) is a decentralized machine learning paradigm enabling multiple clients
124   to collaboratively train a global model while keeping their local data private. FL approaches typi-
125
      cally fall into two categories: traditional fully parameter-updated models and prompt learning meth-
      ods. Traditional FL methods (McMahan et al., 2017; Li et al., 2020; 2021; Mendieta et al., 2022)
126
      aggregate model parameters from distributed clients to update a global model, allowing collabora-
127
      tive learning while preserving low communication. FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) averages local
128   model updates, providing a straightforward and effective solution for various tasks. In contrast,
129   FedProx (Li et al., 2020) adds a proximal term to the objective function, improving stability and
130   performance in heterogeneous environments. Prompt learning methods (Guo et al., 2023; Zhao
131   et al., 2022; Su et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024) customize task-specific text prompts for each client,
132   enabling local and global communication without altering model parameters. PromptFL (Guo et al.,
133   2023) allows clients to train soft prompts instead of the entire model, significantly reducing aggre-
134   gation overhead and accelerating local training. FedOTP (Li et al., 2024) balances global consensus
135   and local personalization by learning both global and local prompts, using Optimal Transport to
136
      align local visual features with prompts and address heterogeneities such as label and feature shifts.
      The latest methods (Yi et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024) introduce LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) into FL
137
      models to achieve a balance between performance and communication cost.
138
139   Fairness in Federated Learning: Fairness in FL has gained attention due to its unique challenges
140   compared to centralized learning. Research on fairness in FL can be categorized into two main areas:
141   Site Fairness and Group Fairness, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Site Fairness (Pan et al., 2024b; Cheng
142
      et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2024a) ensures equitable handling of model updates from clients, especially
      when data quality, quantity, or distribution varies. Disparities can lead to models favoring clients
143
      with larger or higher-quality datasets. Techniques have been developed to equalize client influence
144
      during model aggregation, such as FedLF (Pan et al., 2024b), which uses multi-objective optimiza-
145   tion to minimize gradient conflicts and promote equitable model improvements. Group Fairness
146   (Ezzeldin et al., 2023; Agrawal et al., 2024; Badar et al., 2024) focuses on equitable model per-
147   formance across demographic groups within clients’ local datasets. FairFed (Ezzeldin et al., 2023)
148   implements a fairness-aware aggregation method, enabling local debiasing and adjusting aggrega-
149   tion weights based on local and global fairness assessments. FairTrade (Badar et al., 2024) employs
150   multi-objective and Bayesian optimization to balance fairness and accuracy. However, most existing
151   studies focus on non-medical applications, like recommendation systems, where biased outcomes
152   are less critical. Despite the urgent need for fairness in healthcare, research on group fairness in
153
      federated learning for medical applications is limited, revealing a significant gap in this field.

191   Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) Golub & Reinsch (1971) decomposes a matrix into orthogonal
192   components, providing insights into the geometry of linear transformations and offering a powerful
193
      tool for dimensionality reduction. To improve efficiency, the Compact SVD is proposed to reduce the
      size of the decomposed matrix by retaining only the r largest singular values and their corresponding
194
      singular vectors. For a matrix A ∈ Rm×n , the compact matrix representation can be represented as
195
196                                                                               A ≈ U SV ⊤ ,                                                                                              (1)
197   where U ∈ Rm×r and V ∈ Rn×r contain the left and right singular vectors corresponding to the
198   largest r singular values in S ∈ Rr×r . Here, r ≪ min(m, n) is the rank of A.
199
      The SVD-based Adaptation Zhang et al. (2023) extends this concept to model fine-tuning by apply-
200
      ing low-rank updates, thereby reducing the number of trainable parameters while preserving model
201
      performance, making it especially suitable for optimizing large-scale models. The low-rank adapta-
202   tion (LoRA) Hu et al. (2022) is essentially the simplest form of SVD, where the singular value matrix
203   S is omitted. In LoRA, the weight matrix is expressed as: W = W0 + ∆W = W0 + U V ⊤ , where
204   W0 is the full-rank weight matrix, and ∆W = U V ⊤ represents the low-rank update. By contrast,
205   the standard SVD-based LoRA includes the singular value matrix S, whose updated weight matrix
206   can be formulated as W = W0 + ∆W = W0 + U SV ⊤ . The inclusion of the diagonal matrix
207   S enables precise control over the contribution of each singular vector component to the update.
208   Since S contains fewer parameters (just scalars along the diagonal), it offers greater flexibility with
209   minimal parameter overhead.

      Implementation Details. The training process consists of 50 epochs with a batch size of 32. The
329   optimization is performed using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with a learning rate of 0.001,
330   which decays by a factor of 0.1 at the 40th epoch. In each round, two out of three sites are randomly
331   selected to update their local model weights before aggregating to form the global model. FairLoRA
332   is implemented using two representative backbones: ResNet50 (He et al., 2016) and ViT-B (Doso-
333   vitskiy et al., 2020). For the ViT-B backbone, the rank and alpha parameters are set to 12 and 2,
334   respectively, while for the ResNet50 backbone, they are set to 32 and 8. Additionally, all batch nor-
335   malization layers in the ResNet50 architecture are unfrozen. To ensure training stability, we employ
336   the exponential moving average (EMA) strategy to update the global parameters.
337   Comparison Methods and Evaluation Metrics. We compare FairLoRA with traditional fully
338   parameter-updated methods, including FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) and FedHEAL (Chen et al.,
339   2024), as well as prompt learning-based models, such as PromptFL (Guo et al., 2023) and FedOTP
340   (Li et al., 2024), both based on CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). Compared to prompt learning models,
341   traditional fully parameter-updated methods typically involve a larger number of learnable param-
342   eters. To facilitate the assessment of model fairness, we report the overall Area Under the Curve
343
      (AUC), equality-scale AUC (ESAUC) (Luo et al., 2024), and group-wise AUCs across the attributes
      of race, language and ethnicity.

346   5.2   B ENCHMARK : FAIR F ED M ED ON 2D SLO F UNDUS IMAGES
347
348   The experimental results in Tables 1 and 2 demonstrate the performance and fairness of various FL
349   models applied to 2D SLO Fundus images for glaucoma detection within our proposed FairFedMed
350
      benchmark. Traditional FL methods such as FedAvg and FedHEAL, although effective in achieving
      overall AUCs ranging from 73% to 76%, exhibit fluctuating ESAUC scores between 57% and 68%.
351
      Analysis of group-wise AUCs reveals that these methods fall short in ensuring fairness across differ-
352
      ent demographic group. Prompt learning models, such as PromptFL and FedOTP, exhibit relatively
353   lower performance with overall AUCs ranging from 71% to 73%. This decline is primarily due to
354   the frozen model weights of CLIP, pre-trained on natural images, which do not adapt well to medi-
355   cal imaging data. Furthermore, these models struggle to achieve equitable outcomes across different
356   demographic groups. For instance, for the language attribute, which includes a more diverse range
357   of demographic groups, these models achieve only 56% to 68% ESAUC.
358
      In contrast, FairLoRA consistently outperforms other models, delivering higher overall AUC scores

532
533
      This paper addresses the lack of fairness-aware medical datasets in FL by introducing the
534   FairFedMed dataset, which is the first to include both 2D and 3D real-world medical images along-
535   side key demographic attributes. We also propose FairLoRA, a novel framework designed to en-
536   hance fairness through a low-rank approximation method tailored for demographic groups. Fair-
537   LoRA ensures that both intra-group and inter-group characteristics are preserved, and global knowl-
538   edge is shared across clients. Our experimental results confirm that FairLoRA not only achieves
539   superior performance in medical image classification but also promotes fairness across different
      demographic groups, making a significant contribution to fairness-aware FL in healthcare.
