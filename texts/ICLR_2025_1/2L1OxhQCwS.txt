009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012            The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence has seen widespread application of
013            long short-term memory (LSTM), a type of recurrent neural network (RNN), in
014
               time series forecasting. Despite the success of Transformers in natural language
               processing (NLP), which prompted interest in their efficacy for time series predic-
015
               tion, their application in financial time series forecasting is less explored compared
016
               to the dominant LSTM models. This study investigates whether Transformer-based
017            models can outperform LSTMs in financial time series forecasting. It involves
018            a comparative analysis of various LSTM-based and Transformer-based models
019            on multiple financial prediction tasks using high-frequency limit order book data.
020            A novel LSTM-based model named DLSTM is introduced alongside a newly
021            designed Transformer-based model tailored for financial predictions. The find-
022            ings indicate that Transformer-based models exhibit only a marginal advantage in
023            predicting absolute price sequences, whereas LSTM-based models demonstrate
024            superior and more consistent performance in predicting differential sequences such
025
               as price differences and movements.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   LSTM has been proven successful in the application of sequential data. Like LSTM, the Transformer
033
      Vaswani et al. (2017) is also used to handle the sequential data. Compared to LSTM, the Transformer
      does not need to handle the sequence data in order, which instead confers the meaning of the sequence
034
      by the Self-attention mechanism.
035
036   Since 2017, the Transformer has been increasingly used for Natural Language Processing (NLP)
037   problems. It produces more impressive results than RNN, such as machine translation Lakew et al.
038   (2018) and speech applications Karita et al. (2019), replacing RNN models such as LSTM in NLP
039
      tasks. Recently, a surge of Transformer-based solutions for less explored long time series forecasting
      problem has appeared Wen et al. (2022). However, as for the financial time series prediction, LSTM
040
      remains the dominant architecture.
041
042   Investigating whether Transformer-based methods are suitable for financial time series forecasting
043   is the central focus of this paper, which compares the efficacy of Transformer and LSTM-based
044   approaches using LOB data from Binance Exchange across various financial prediction tasks. These
045
      tasks include mid-price prediction, mid-price difference prediction, and mid-price movement predic-
      tion. In the first two tasks, the study assesses existing Transformer and LSTM models; for mid-price
046
      prediction, Transformer methods show a 10% − 25% lower prediction error than LSTM methods,
047
      although the results are not sufficiently reliable for trading. Conversely, LSTM models excel in
048   mid-price difference prediction, achieving an out-of-sample R2 of approximately 11.5%. The paper’s
049   most notable contribution is the development of a new LSTM-based model, DLSTM, specifically
050   designed for mid-price movement prediction by integrating LSTM with a time series decomposition
051   approach. This model significantly outperforms previous methods, with accuracy ranging from
052   63.73% to 73.31%, demonstrating robust profitability in simulated trading scenarios. Moreover, the
053   architecture of existing Transformer-based methods has been modified to better suit the demands of
      movement prediction tasks.

      2    LSTM IN T IME S ERIES P REDICTION
055
056
057   LSTM, introduced by Hochreiter et al. Hochreiter and Schmidhuber (1997), has become a cornerstone
058   for time series prediction, especially in handling long-term dependencies that are beyond the reach of
059   traditional Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN). RNN often struggles with issues like exploding or
060   vanishing gradients, which impede the learning of long-range dependencies Rumelhart et al. (1986);
061   Goodfellow et al. (2016). LSTMs mitigate these problems through a series of gating mechanism
062   that regulates information flow, thus maintaining model stability over extended sequences Gers et al.
063   (1999).
064   In the financial sector, LSTMs have proven particularly effective, being widely applied in predicting
065   stock prices using Open-High-Low-Close (OHLC) data and other financial indices Roondiwala et al.
066   (2017); Cao et al. (2019); Bao et al. (2017); Selvin et al. (2017); Fischer and Krauss (2018). Notably,
067   models such as Bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) and hybrids of LSTM with Convolutional Neural
068   Networks (CNN) have further enhanced prediction accuracy Siami-Namini et al. (2019); Zhang et al.
069   (2019).
070   Zhang et al. expanded LSTM’s capabilities by developing the DeepLOB architecture, which incorpo-
071   rates convolutional blocks for feature extraction, an Inception module for decomposing inputs, and an
072   LSTM layer to capture temporal patterns Zhang et al. (2019). This model excels in complex financial
073   environments, particularly when analyzing high-frequency data from Limit Order Books (LOB).
074   Further adaptations include DeepLOB-Seq2Seq and DeepLOB-Attention models, which integrate
075   Seq2Seq and attention mechanisms, respectively, to improve multi-horizon and long-term predictions
076   Zhang and Zohren (2021). These enhancements allow the models to handle more complex prediction
077
      tasks, achieving better performance by adapting the encoder-decoder framework for dynamic financial
      markets.
078
079   Such innovations demonstrate LSTM’s adaptability and its continuous evolution to meet the specific
080   demands of financial time series prediction, showcasing the model’s robustness and reliability in
081   capturing and analyzing intricate market dynamics.

      3    T RANSFORMER IN T IME S ERIES P REDICTION
086
087
      The Transformer, originally impactful in natural language processing (NLP) Brown et al. (2020),
088
      has been adapted to tackle the unique challenges of time series prediction, particularly in financial
089
      contexts. According to Vaswani et al. Vaswani et al. (2017), the Transformer architecture employs a
090   self-attention mechanism that efficiently processes long sequences without encountering the vanishing
091   gradient problems typical of RNNs. This capability is particularly beneficial in financial markets
092   characterized by long input sequences.
093
      In the financial domain, the deployment of Transformer models is on the rise, with applications in
094
      predicting stock prices using Temporal Fusion Transformers Hu (2021) and in forecasting cryptocur-
095
      rency values, showing notable advantages over LSTMs Sridhar and Sanagavarapu (2021). Innovative
096   uses also include combining Transformers with BERT for sentiment analysis, followed by Generative
097   Adversarial Networks (GANs) for stock price prediction Sonkiya et al. (2021).
098
099
      To address the high computational demands of traditional self-attention, which scales quadratically
      with sequence length, new Transformer models like LogTrans Li et al. (2019), Reformer Kitaev
100
      et al. (2020), Informer Zhou et al. (2020), Autoformer Wu et al. (2021), Pyraformer Liu et al.
101
      (2022), and FEDformer Zhou et al. (2022) have been introduced. These models reduce complexity
102   through innovations including convolutional self-attention, reversible connections, and ProbSparse
103   mechanisms, enhancing efficiency in processing long sequences. They also incorporate advanced
104   decomposition methods and frequency domain transformations, significantly improving forecasting
105   accuracy and efficiency. Originally validated on datasets like electricity consumption and solar energy,
106   these optimized Transformers show great potential for financial time series forecasting, surpassing
107   traditional LSTM models in handling complex dependencies and long data sequences Wen et al.
      (2022).

323   This adaptation involves feeding a sequence of predicted mid-prices into a linear layer, followed by
      a softmax activation function to determine price movements. This approach, illustrated in Figure

      5.3.2   DLSTM: INNOVATION ON LSTM- BASED METHODS
338
339   Inspired by the Dlinear model Zeng et al. (2022) and Autoformer, the DLSTM model combines time
340   series decomposition with LSTM to leverage the strengths of both approaches. DLSTM capitalizes on
341   three key observations: the effectiveness of time decomposition in enhancing forecasting performance
342   as demonstrated in prior works Zhang et al. (2019); Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2022), the robustness
343   of LSTM in handling diverse forecasting tasks, and Dlinear’s success over other Transformer-based
344   models in long time series forecasting due to its decomposition and DMS prediction methods. The
345
      architecture of DLSTM, which replaces the linear layers with LSTM layers as shown in Figure 5,
      incorporates a dual-layer approach where the time series XT = (x1 , x2 , . . . , xT ) is first decomposed
346
      into a Trend series using a moving average:
347
348                                      Xt = AvgP ool(P adding(XT ))                                      (12)
349
350   where AvgP ool(·) is the average pooling operation and P adding(·) is used to fix the input length.
351   The Remainder series is calculated by Xr = XT − Xt . After that, these two series are processed
352
      by separate LSTM layers, whose outputs are combined and passed through a linear and softmax
      activation to predict price movements, effectively handling one-step-ahead predictions without the
353
      error accumulation typically seen in multi-step forecasting.

      6      C ONCLUSION
471
472
      This study conducts a comprehensive comparison of LSTM-based and Transformer-based models
473
      on three cryptocurrency LOB data prediction tasks. In the first task of predicting the LOB mid-
474   price, FEDformer and Autoformer demonstrate lower error rates than other models, although LSTM
475   outperforms Informer, Reformer, and vanilla Transformer. Despite lower prediction errors, the
476   practical utility of these results for high-frequency trading is limited due to insufficient quality. In
477   the second task of predicting the mid-price difference, LSTM-based models showcase superior
478   robustness and performance, achieving the highest R2 of 11.5% within about 10 prediction steps,
479   while state-of-the-art models like Autoformer and FEDformer falter due to their inability to effectively
480   process difference sequences.
481   For the final task, predicting LOB mid-price movement, a novel DLSTM model integrating LSTM
482   with Autoformer’s time decomposition architecture significantly outshines all models in classification
483   metrics, proving its efficacy in trading simulations, especially under transaction costs. Overall, while
484   Transformer-based models may excel in limited aspects of mid-price prediction, LSTM-based models
485   demonstrate consistent superiority across the board, reaffirming their robustness and practicality in
      financial time series prediction for electronic trading.
