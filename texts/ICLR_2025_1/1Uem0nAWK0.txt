009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning Large Language Models (LLM) to address subjectivity and nuanced
012            preference levels requires adequate flexibility and control, which can be a
013            resource-intensive and time-consuming procedure. Existing training-time align-
014            ment methods require full re-training when a change is needed and inference-time
015            ones typically require access to the reward model at each inference step. To ad-
016            dress these limitations, we introduce inference-time model alignment method that
017            learns encoded representations of preference dimensions, called Alignment Vec-
018
               tors (AV). These representations are computed by subtraction of the base model
               from the aligned model as in model editing enabling dynamically adjusting the
019
               model behavior during inference through simple linear operations. Even though
020
               the preference dimensions can span various granularity levels, here we focus on
021            three gradual response levels across three specialized domains: medical, legal, and
022            financial, exemplifying its practical potential. This new alignment paradigm intro-
023            duces adjustable preference knobs during inference, allowing users to tailor their
024            LLM outputs while reducing the inference cost by half compared to the prompt
025            engineering approach. Additionally, we find that AVs are transferable across dif-
026            ferent fine-tuning stages of the same model, demonstrating their flexibility. AVs
027            also facilitate multidomain, diverse preference alignment, making the process 12x
028            faster than the retraining approach.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Aligning LLMs is crucial for adapting them to meet human preferences. Standard training-time
      alignment methods, such as RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022) and DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024), are con-
034
      ducted during model training. However, making nuanced preference adjustments during inference
035
      with these approaches would necessitate retraining, which requires substantial amounts of time, pref-
036   erence data and computational resources. Inference-time LLM alignment, by contrast, delays the
037   alignment process until inference (Wang et al., 2024). While preference alignment can be achieved
038   through training-time methods or targeted prompting, fine-grained control over preferences at in-
039   ference remains largely unexplored in current State-of-the-Art (SOTA) works (Sahoo et al., 2024;
040   Guo et al., 2024). This research introduces an inference-time model editing technique via Alignment
041   Vectors (AV), offering users dynamic preference adjustments without additional computational over-
042   head.
043   Due to their extensive capabilities, LLMs are now employed in different fields, including the ones
044   that requires specialized domain understanding like legal (Guha et al., 2024), healthcare (Yang et al.,
045   2023) and financial (Huang et al., 2023) industry. However, the diverse needs of a broad customer
046   base require that LLM outputs be carefully refined. For instance, while a healthcare provider might
047   need detailed medical responses for professional use, a public health forum may prefer more gener-
048   alized information to avoid misinterpretation. Although prompt engineering can temporarily address
049   these needs, it becomes costly when scaled (Li et al., 2023).
050   Furthermore, managing multiple alignment objectives can be complex. Consider an insurance com-
051   pany that needs expert legal responses, generic financial answers, and to avoid medical responses;
052   balancing these demands poses a significant challenge. A joint training with targeted preference
053   levels can resolve the problem, however, it lacks flexibility, and training can be resource intensive.
      Hence, at present, there is no work that addresses such preference flexibility in the inference time.

054
      Thus, developing flexible, inference-time adjustable model alignment to manage costs and maintain
055   efficiency in the long term remains a major research gap.
056
057   In the current literature, preference dimensions such as helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty are
058
      well-studied (Bai et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2024). Some studies also explore the controllability of these
      dimensions by numerically categorizing preference “levels” (Guo et al., 2024). However, special-
059
      ized dimensions have a finer granularity which gives more control in making adjustments during
060
      inference time. Hence, to simplify the controllability objective, we primarily focus on achieving
061   meaningful preference tunability by emphasizing proficiency levels in responses within special-
062   ized domains. Additionally, we demonstrate preference tunability in a general domain, specifically
063   ‘safety’ in this case.
064
      It is important to note that existing literature lacks specialized preference alignment datasets for
065
      domain-specific Query-Response pairs. To fill this gap, we propose a method to generate both
066
      queries and responses synthetically. Our queries are derived from personas sampled from the Per-
067   sonaHub dataset (Chan et al., 2024) and supplemented by novel personas created through prompts
068   to an LLM. We produce responses at three levels: avoidance (Avd), generic response (Gen), and
069   expert opinion (Exp).
070
      In addition, to achieve inference time preference tunability, we propose a simple model editing
071
      technique called Alignment Vector Arithmetic, which is based on the concept of Task Arithmetic
072
      (Ilharco et al., 2023). AVs can be obtained by directly subtracting the base model parameters from
073   the aligned model, and can be added in the inference time. Hence, our first research question (RQ1)
074   Are alignment vectors valid representation of the preference dimensions? To address this question,
075   we systematically integrate the alignment vector into the base model with varying weights, both
076   positive and negative, and analyze the resulting changes in model behavior. Our second research
077   question is posed as (RQ2) Can we calibrate different alignment vectors to achieve diverse multi-
078   domain preference? We address RQ2 through different domain-specific AV-integration strategy.
079   The key contribution of this work are:
080
081          • We frame LLM alignment in single and multiple domains as a model editing problem
082            and introduce an inference-time tunable mechanism, which allows flexible adjustment of
083            generation output along the preference dimension.
084          • We generate a synthetic dataset with a total of 38k queries, each paired with responses cat-
085            egorized into three levels of specialized subject matter proficiency across three specialized
086            domains: Medical, Financial, and Legal. The dataset will be available through this link.
087          • By adjusting the merging coefficients, we achieve diverse, multidomain behaviors effi-
088            ciently, saving time and resources. Unlike joint training, which requires pD adjustments
089            for D domains and p preference levels, our method only requires D training runs, reducing
090            resource usage by a factor of pD /D.
091
092
      Why inference time alignment over conventional approach? In contrast to conventional train-
093
      ing time approaches, inference time alignment provides flexibility and adaptability by enabling
094   dynamic adjustments to model behavior based on task or user needs without retraining.

097   2   R ELATED W ORKS
098
099   Research on inference time alignment has explored several approaches, with prompt engineering
100   being the simplest and most basic. Techniques, such as zero-shot, few-shot, and Chain-of-Thought
101   (COT) prompting have proven effective in aligning language model responses to user queries dur-
102   ing inference time (Radford et al., 2019; Sahoo et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2022). However, prompt
103   engineering comes with expensive inference time and cost and that could be infeasible when scaled.
104
      Additionally, effective prompt engineering assumes that the user is skilled at interacting with LLMs
      (Meskó, 2023; Oppenlaender et al., 2023). In contrast, our approach does not rely on prompting,
105
      and thus meets the diverse needs of users.
106
107   Li et al. introduced Inference-Time Intervention (ITI), which identifies a sparse set of attention heads
      with high linear probing accuracy for a target task and shifts their activation along task-correlated


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      directions during inference time (Li et al., 2024). However, their methods are largely applicable
109   to truthfulness and not controllable. A related approach involves learning Safety Related Vectors
110   (SRV), to steer harmful model outputs towards safer alternatives (Wang et al., 2024). However, the
111   lack of controllability and input dependency of this technique to determine if the prompt may induce
112   unsafe response limits its applicability in our context. Huang et al. introduced DeAl, an alignment
113   method that treats alignment as a heuristic-guided search process (Huang et al., 2024). However, this
114   approach significantly slows down the decoding process due to the expansion of the search space.
115   Liu et al. studied regularization strength between aligned and unaligned models to have control over
116   generation (Liu et al., 2024). Although closely related to our work, their method lacks clarity on
117
      whether fine-grained preference levels can be achieved, and it appears less flexible in transferring
      alignment behavior across different contexts. Researhers controlled attributes of generated contents
118
      by adding control token in the prompt (Guo et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2023). Despite its effectiveness,
119
      this method requires training LLMs with a particular data format, which restricts the flexibility of
120   control during inference.
121
122   Rame et al.’s work is closely related to our multi-domain preference alignment (Rame et al., 2024).
123
      However, their approach focuses on training-time alignment by interpolating weights from models
      fine-tuned on diverse rewards to achieve Pareto-optimality. In contrast, our work introduces a prefer-
124
      ence adjustment strategy that operates at inference time, in addition to achieving multi-dimensional
125
      alignment. Similarly, while Jang et al. address personalized preference alignment and post-hoc
126   merging, our approach provides a unique capability: preference level adjustment Jang et al. (2023).
127   Our research addresses the existing gaps by exploring model editing techniques to achieve fine-
128   grained control over preferences during inference, without the constraints of prompt engineering or
129   additional training requirements.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
132
133
      3.1   O BTAINING A LIGNMENT V ECTOR
134
135   To obtain the Alignment Vector (AV), we first perform alignment through DPO algorithm, using
136   an ‘ipo’ loss function to create a domain-specific aligned model (Rafailov et al., 2024; Azar et al.,
137   2024). We derive AV using a direct subtraction technique. This method, inspired by the work of
138   Ilharco et al., involves performing task arithmetic to capture alignment vectors (Ilharco et al., 2023).
139   Their approach suggests that by subtracting the base pre-trained model parameters from those of
140   a fine-tuned model (specifically fine-tuned on a given task), a task-specific direction is encoded.
141
      Moving the model along this task direction enhances its performance on that particular task.
142   We build AV by subtracting the weights of an unaligned model from the weights of the same model
143   after alignment fine-tuning on a task. If θaligned denotes the model parameter after fine-tuning on a
144   preference dimension, then the Alignment Vector can be obtained by the following:
145                                       θAV = θaligned − θunaligned                                     (1)
146
147   3.2   S INGLE D OMAIN A LIGNMENT
148
149   To enable preference tunability across different domains, we perform a weighted integration of the
150   alignment vectors into the base (or unaligned) model, where the weights can be both positive and
151   negative. Our hypothesis is that this gradual integration will result in a corresponding gradual in-
152   crease or decrease in the model’s proficiency. This process is governed by the following equation:
153
154                                     θaligned = θunaligned + λ ∗ θAV                                   (2)
155
156   By adjusting the value of λ, we aim to control the proficiency of the model’s generated responses.
157   Assuming when λ = 0, the model remains unaltered and functions as the base, unaligned model.
158
      If the θAV encodes the expert behavior in a certain domain, as λ increases towards 1, the model
      becomes increasingly aligned, achieving full proficiency at λ = 1.
159
160   We further hypothesize that when λ takes on negative values, the model’s behavior tends to reverse
161   the preference ranking. For instance, if the base model typically generates generic responses and
      the aligned model is designed for expert-level responses, moving λ in the negative direction will


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      shift the model towards avoidance behavior. Therefore, to control the proficiency of the responses,
163   adjusting λ is sufficient, eliminating the need to train the model with a new preference configuration.

      3.3   M ULTIDOMAIN A LIGNMENT
166
167   When dealing with multiple domains simultaneously, the interaction between these domains can
168   present a significant challenge. While each individual preference vector encodes domain-specific
169   attributes, they also embed proficiency levels which can easily generalize and negatively affect mul-
170   tidomain diverse behavior. This complexity can make it difficult to integrate multiple domains ef-
171   fectively.
172   Our goal is to achieve a diverse multidomain preference, which we approach by using the following
173   equation:
174
175
176
                      θmultidom aligned = α ∗ θAV dom1 + β ∗ θAV dom2 + γ ∗ θAV dom3                      (3)
177
      In this equation, α, β and γ represent the integration coefficients for the domains in question, re-
178
      spectively. By identifying different sets of these coefficients, we aim to achieve varying levels of
179
      preference across the three domains.

      4.1   Q UERY G ENERATION
209
210   First, we randomly select 7,000 personas from the PersonaHub dataset, which contains 200,000
211   personas (Chan et al., 2024). Using these selected personas, we prompt an LLM, Claude-3-Sonnet

214   par with GPT-4, often ranking among the best foundational models. Second, we opted to use GPT-4

      To enhance the diversity of our dataset and create a more robust hub, we then initiate a hierarchical
217   generation process called “CreatePersona.” We begin by randomly generating a few persona-query
218   pairs using Claude. Our initial investigations revealed that generating too many initial pairs often re-

226   4.2   R ESPONSE G ENERATION
227
      We generate the response from the queries into three distinct levels: avoidance of response (Avd),
228
      generic response (Gen), and expert response (Exp). Detailed instructions are provided to the LLM to
229
      facilitate the generation of these responses (see Appendix C). Furthermore, we observe a progressive
230   increase in response length from the avoidance level to the expert level. To mitigate potential bias
231   associated with response length, we instructed the LLM to produce responses of random lengths.

233   4.3   H UMAN E VALUATION OF MULTI - LEVEL RESPONSE GENERATION
234
235   To evaluate the quality of the generated responses, we conduct a small experiment involving three
236   annotators. Each annotator is asked to categorize a set of LLM-generated responses into one of
237   three categories: Avd, Gen, and Exp. We provide the annotators with clear definitions of these
238   categories. Each annotator reviews 30 queries along with their three-level responses, with at least 15
239   examples shared between every pair of annotators. This allows us to compute the average Cohen’s
240
      kappa score, which is found to be 0.84 (Cohen, 1960), indicating substantial agreement among the
      annotators.
241
242   We also calculate the average annotation agreement for each annotator with the LLM generation.
243   Responses generated with the Avoidance instruction have the fewest disagreements or misclassifi-
244   cations. However, some Gen and Exp responses are occasionally misclassified from one another.
245   We observe that certain responses, although aligned with the expert spectrum, are miscidentified as
246
      generic due to their tone, and vice versa. Additionally, a few avoidance responses provide basic
      information, leading to their misclassification as Gen responses. These findings suggest that the
247
      levels may represent a continuous spectrum rather than distinct categories, highlighting the need for
248
      further research to more precisely define these proficiency levels.

      To assess the performance after alignment, we use a metric called preference accuracy (pref. acc).
255
      This metric reports the accuracy at each alignment level. To calculate it, we first compute the token-
256   level mean log-probability for each of the three response levels across all queries for the aligned
257   model. Then, for each sample in the validation set, we determine which alignment level has the
258   highest log-probability. For example, in proficiency level alignment, it can be among Exp, Gen, and
259   Avd. Finally, we report the percentage of samples where each alignment level had the highest log-
260   probability in the validation set. A higher preference accuracy in an alignment spectrum indicate the

282   5.2   BASELINE A PPROACHES
283
284   Since no existing model-editing methods currently support inference-time controlled alignment, we
285   use a ‘prompting’ approach as our baseline. This method involves manually instructing the LLM
286   through prompts to generate responses at different proficiency levels based on predefined definitions.
287   Notably, unlike model editing, the ‘prompting’ approach can help the model achieve discreet levels
288   instead of spectrums.
289   Our second baseline, aimed at achieving multidomain diverse behavior, is the ‘Joint Training’ ap-
290   proach. In this method, we combine data from various domains to create a preference dataset tai-
291   lored to different proficiency levels. Although this approach is applied during training rather than at
292   inference time, it provides valuable insights for establishing diverse alignment objectives.
293   Additionally, we report the performance of the model when we simply prompt the query without
294   providing any additional instruction or performing model editing. We refer to this as the ‘default’
295   performance.
296
297   5.3   M ODEL AND T RAINING C ONFIGURATION
298
299   While we define three primary preference levels for specialized domain proficiency, our approach
300   can be extended to accommodate additional levels if needed. For DPO training, we employ a full
301   fine-tuning strategy, using a fixed beta parameter of 0.1. During alignment training, we focus on
302   tuning our model to the “expert” proficiency level within each domain, where “expert” is preferred
303   over “generic,” and “generic” is preferred over “avoidance.” To show the preference tunability, we
304
      experiment with different λ values, and we choose an interval of 0.1. We empirically found that
      an interval of 0.1 provides a fine-grained and practical resolution, allowing us to capture significant
305
      shifts in the model’s behavior.
306
307   As a base model for alignment, we use Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023). We conducted
308   our training on NVIDIA A100 GPUs and we utilize 80% of the generated data in each domain for
309   training and 20% for testing. For the validation process, we allocated 3% of the data for training
310
      time validation. We used a batch size of 4 and trained each model for one epoch, monitoring the
      validation loss to determine when to stop training.

551   7   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
552
553   Our work has several limitations and areas for future research. First, we used a basic approach for
554   obtaining alignment vectors, but more advanced techniques like parameter thresholding, zeroing,
555   or SVD-based separation should be explored (Yadav et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024). Second, our
556   method works only for LLMs with the same architecture, so applying it to different architectures is
557   a potential direction for future study. Third, we tested our approach only on Mistral-7b, so validation
558
      with other open-source LLMs and SLMs is necessary. Fourth, we did not evaluate the correctness of
      the specialized domain responses, which we plan to do in the next version of our dataset. Lastly, we
559
      relied on an extensive grid search for multidomain alignment, and we plan to streamline this process
560
      in future work.

563   8   C ONCLUSION
564
565   In this research, we address the important research gap of inference-time preference alignment tun-
566   ability through model editing. We introduce a novel synthetic dataset designed to represent three lev-
567   els of response proficiency across three specialized domains. Our approach enables single-domain
568   preference tunability at inference time without incurring additional costs or resource usage. This
569   allows users to select different response proficiency levels without the need for extra training. Fur-
570
      thermore, our method offers tailored configurations for diverse multidomain behaviors, significantly
      reducing both training time and resource demands. In future work, we will explore preference tun-
571
      ability in more open-source models like Llama and Qwen (Touvron et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023).
572
      Furthermore, we want to explore the transferability of alignment vectors across different LLMs.
