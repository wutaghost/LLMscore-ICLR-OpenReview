                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Machine Learning models are often trained on proprietary and private data that
010
               cannot be shared, though the trained models themselves are distributed openly
011
               assuming that sharing model weights is privacy preserving, as training data is not
012            expected to be inferred from the model weights. In this paper, we present Training-
013            Like Data Reconstruction (TLDR), a network inversion-based approach to recon-
014            struct training-like data from trained models. To begin with, we introduce a com-
015            prehensive network inversion technique that learns the input space corresponding
016            to different classes in the classifier using a single conditioned generator. While in-
017            version may typically return random and arbitrary input images for a given output
018            label, we modify the inversion process to incentivize the generator to reconstruct
019            training-like data by exploiting key properties of the classifier with respect to the
020
               training data. Specifically, the classifier is expected to be relatively more confident
               and robust in classifying training samples, and the gradient of the classifiers out-
021
               put with respect to the classifier’s weights is also expected to be lower for training
022
               data than for random inverted samples. Using these insights, along with some
023            prior knowledge about the images, we guide the generator to produce data closely
024            resembling the original training data. To validate our approach, we conduct empir-
025            ical evaluations on multiple standard vision classification datasets, demonstrating
026            that leveraging these robustness and gradient properties enables the reconstruction
027            of data semantically similar to the original training data, thereby highlighting the
028            potential privacy risks involved in sharing machine learning models.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Machine learning models have become an essential tool across a wide range of domains, including
034   healthcare, finance, and security, where the need for data privacy is paramount. These models are
035   often trained on proprietary or sensitive data, which cannot be shared openly, yet the trained models
036   themselves are commonly distributed to facilitate various applications. In federated learning, for
037   example, model weights are shared under the assumption that they do not expose the underlying
038
      training data, thereby preserving privacy. However, recent research suggests that this assumption
      may not be valid, as it may be possible to infer and reconstruct training or similar data by analyzing
039
      the model weights.
040
041   This potential privacy risk arises from the fact that trained ML models implicitly encode informa-
042   tion about the data they were trained on. In model inversion attacks, adversaries aim to exploit this
043   information to reconstruct training data from the model parameters. While these attacks have been
044
      demonstrated in controlled settings, where models are typically over-parameterized or overly sim-
      plistic, the risks associated with sharing models trained on large, complex and multi-class datasets
045
      are yet been fully explored.
046
047   Previous research to reconstruct training data has primarily focused on restricted scenarios, such
048   as binary classifiers with fully connected layers trained on a few hundred samples in a dataset.
049   However, these settings are far removed from real-world applications, where models are typically
050
      trained on larger, more complex, and multi-class datasets with regularization techniques like dropout
      and weight decay that prevent over-fitting and memorization.
051
052   In restricted settings, over-parameterized models can easily memorize portions of the training data,
053   leading to successful reconstructions. For under-parameterized models, where there is no possibility
      of memorization and the models generalize well, reconstructions are typically more difficult as these

054
      models have limited capacity to store detailed representations of individual samples from the training
055   data, making it harder to exploit the model’s learned parameters to reconstruct the data. Also in fully
056   connected layers, each input feature is assigned dedicated weights, which may make reconstruction
057   easier as the model captures more direct associations between inputs and outputs. While as in
058   convolutional layers, due to the weight-sharing mechanism, where the same set of weights is applied
059   across different parts of the input, the reconstruction becomes more challenging.
060
      In this paper, we introduce Training-Like Data Reconstruction (TLDR), a novel approach to recon-
061   struct training-like data from vision classifiers with convolutional layers trained on large, complex,
062   and multi-class datasets. We specifically explore reconstruction in the context of convolutional
063   neural networks (CNNs) that incorporate commonly used non-linearities like ReLU, batch normal-
064   ization, regularisation techniques like dropout and weight decay and demonstrate that training-like
065   data reconstruction is still possible, even in these realistic and unrestricted settings.
066
      At the core of our approach is a network inversion technique that learns the input space correspond-
067   ing to different classes within a classifier using a single conditioned generator trained to generate a
068   diverse set of samples from the input space with desired labels guided by a combination of losses
069   including cross-entropy, KL Divergence, cosine similarity and feature orthogonality. Inverted sam-
070   ples generated through network inversion are often random, and while inversion may occasionally
071   produce training-like data, our goal is to specifically encourage the generator to reconstruct training-
072   like data. To achieve this, we leverage several key insights and properties of the classifier in relation
073   to its training data.
074   First, model confidence is a crucial signal. The classifier, having been trained on the data, is expected
075   to be more confident in its predictions on training samples compared to randomly generated, inverted
076   samples. This stems from the fact that training data is considered in-distribution for the model,
077   while random inverted samples tend to be out-of-distribution. Thus, by generating samples that
078   the classifier is more confident in, we can guide the generator toward producing data similar to the
079   training set. Mathematically, this can be expressed as:
080
081                                      P (yin |xin ; θ) ≫ P (yood |xood ; θ)
082   Where P (y|x; θ) represents the softmax output of the classifier for a given input x, θ are the
083   model’s parameters, xin refers to in-distribution data (i.e. training samples), and xood refers to out-
084   of-distribution data (i.e. randomly generated, inverted samples).
085
      Second, the robustness to perturbations is another important property. During training, the model
086
      learns to generalize across slight variations in the training data, making it relatively more robust to
087
      perturbations around these samples compared to random inverted samples. Hence we specifically
088   encourage the generator to generate samples that are robust to perturbations when passed through
089   the classifier. Formally, we express this as:
090
                                              ∂fθ (xin )     ∂fθ (xood )
091                                                      ≪
                                                ∂xin           ∂xood
092
093   This equation highlights that the classifier is less sensitive to perturbations for xin compared to xood ,
094
      where fθ (x) is the model output for input x.
095   Finally, we exploit gradient properties of the model. Since the classifier has already been optimized
096   on the training data, the gradient of the loss with respect to the model’s weights is expected to be
097   lower for training data compared to random inverted samples. By minimizing the gradient with
098   respect to the model’s weights for the generated samples, we can guide the generator to produce
099   samples that more closely resemble the training data, since those would exhibit lower gradient mag-
100
      nitudes. This can be formalized as:
101
102                              ∥∇θ L(fθ (xin ), yin )∥ ≪ ∥∇θ L(fθ (xood ), yood )∥
103
104
      Where L represents the loss function, fθ (x) is the model output for input x, and ∇θ is the gradient
      with respect to the model weights θ.
105
106   By combining these three signals—model confidence, robustness to perturbations, and gradient be-
107   havior—along with prior knowledge about the images we guide the inversion process to reconstruct
      samples that semantically resemble the original training data.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
128
129   Network inversion has emerged as a powerful method for exploring and understanding the internal
130
      mechanisms of neural networks. By identifying input patterns that closely approximate a given out-
      put target, inversion techniques provide a way to visualize the information processing capabilities
131
      embedded within the network’s learned parameters. These methods reveal important insights into
132
      how models represent and manipulate data, offering a pathway to expose the latent structure of neu-
133   ral networks. While inversion techniques primarily began as tools for understanding models, their
134   application to extracting sensitive data has sparked significant concerns. Neural networks inherently
135   store information about the data they are trained on, and this has led to the potential for training
136   data to be reconstructed through inversion attacks. Early works in this space, particularly on over-
137   parameterized models with fully connected networks, demonstrated that it was possible to extract
138   portions of the training data due to the model’s tendency to memorize data. This raises significant
139   privacy concerns, especially in cases where models are trained on proprietary or sensitive datasets,
140   such as in healthcare or finance.
141   Early research on inversion for multi-layer perceptrons in (Kindermann & Linden, 1990), derived
142   from the back-propagation algorithm, demonstrates the utility of this method in applications like
143   digit recognition highlighting that while multi-layer perceptrons exhibit strong generalization capa-
144   bilities—successfully classifying untrained digits—they often falter in rejecting counterexamples,
145   such as random patterns. Subsequently (Jensen et al., 1999) expanded on this idea by proposing
146   evolutionary inversion procedures for feed-forward networks that stands out for its ability to iden-
147
      tify multiple inversion points simultaneously, providing a more comprehensive view of the network’s
      input-output relationships. The paper (Saad & Wunsch, 2007) explores the lack of explanation ca-
148
      pability in artificial neural networks (ANNs) and introduces an inversion-based method for rule
149
      extraction to calculate the input patterns that correspond to specific output targets, allowing for the
150   generation of hyperplane-based rules that explain the neural network’s decision-making process.
151   (Wong, 2017) addresses the problem of inverting deep networks to find inputs that minimize certain
152   output criteria by reformulating network propagation as a constrained optimization problem and
153   solving it using the alternating direction method of multipliers.
154
      Model Inversion attacks in adversarial settings are studied in (Yang et al., 2019), where an attacker
155
      aims to infer training data from a model’s predictions by training a secondary neural network to
156   perform the inversion, using the adversary’s background knowledge to construct an auxiliary dataset,
157   without access to the original training data. The paper (Kumar & Levine, 2020) presents a method
158   for tackling data-driven optimization problems, where the goal is to find inputs that maximize an
159   unknown score function by proposing Model Inversion Networks (MINs), which learn an inverse
160   mapping from scores to inputs, allowing them to scale to high-dimensional input spaces. While
161   (Ansari et al., 2022) introduces an automated method for inversion by focusing on the reliability of
      inverse solutions by seeking inverse solutions near reliable data points that are sampled from the


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      forward process and used for training the surrogate model. By incorporating predictive uncertainty
163   into the inversion process and minimizing it, this approach achieves higher accuracy and robustness.
164
165   The traditional methods for network inversion often rely on gradient descent through a highly non-
166
      convex loss landscape, leading to slow and unstable optimization processes. To address these chal-
      lenges, recent work by (Liu et al., 2022) proposes learning a loss landscape where gradient descent
167
      becomes efficient, thus significantly improving the speed and stability of the inversion process. Sim-
168
      ilarly Suhail (2024) proposes an alternate approach to inversion by encoding the network into a
169   Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) propositional formula and using SAT solvers and samplers to find
170   satisfying assignments for the constrained CNF formula. While this method, unlike optimization-
171   based approaches, is deterministic and ensures the generation of diverse input samples with desired
172   labels. However, the downside of this approach lies in its computational complexity, which makes it
173   less feasible for large-scale practical applications.
174
      In reconstruction (Haim et al., 2022) studies the extent to which neural networks memorize training
175   data, revealing that in some cases, a significant portion of the training data can be reconstructed
176   from the parameters of a trained neural network classifier. The paper introduces a novel recon-
177   struction method based on the implicit bias of gradient-based training methods and demonstrate that
178   it is generally possible to reconstruct a substantial fraction of the actual training samples from a
179   trained neural network, specifically focusing on binary MLP classifiers. Later (Buzaglo et al., 2023)
180   improve upon these results by showing that training data reconstruction is not only possible in the
181   multi-class setting but that the quality of the reconstructed samples is even higher than in the bi-
182   nary case. Also revealing that using weight decay during training can increase the susceptibility to
183
      reconstruction attacks.
184   The paper (Balle et al., 2022) addresses the issue of whether an informed adversary, who has knowl-
185   edge of all training data points except one, can successfully reconstruct the missing data point given
186   access to the trained machine learning model. The authors explore this question by introducing con-
187   crete reconstruction attacks on convex models like logistic regression with closed-form solutions.
188   For more complex models, such as neural networks, they develop a reconstructor network, which,
189
      given the model weights, can recover the target data point. Subsequenlty (Wang et al., 2023) in-
      vestigates how model gradients can leak sensitive information about training data, posing serious
190
      privacy concerns. The authors claim that even without explicitly training the model or memorizing
191
      the data, it is possible to fully reconstruct training samples by gradient query at a randomly chosen
192   parameter value. Under mild assumptions, they demonstrate the reconstruction of training data for
193   both shallow and deep neural networks across a variety of activation functions.
194
195
      In this paper, we explore the intersection of network inversion and training data reconstruction.
      Our approach to network inversion aims to strike a balance between computational efficiency and
196
      the diversity of generated inputs by using a carefully conditioned generator trained to learn the
197
      data distribution in the input space of a trained neural network. The conditioning information is
198   encoded into vectors in a concealed manner to enhance the diversity of the generated inputs by
199   avoiding easy shortcut solutions. This diversity is further enhanced through the application of heavy
200   dropout during the generation process, the minimization of cosine similarity and encouragement of
201   orthogonality between a batch of the features of the generated images.
202
      While network inversion may occasionally produce training-like samples, we encourage this process
203
      by exploiting key properties of the classifier with respect to its training data. The classifier tends to
204   be more confident in predicting in-distribution training samples than random, out-of-distribution
205   samples, and it exhibits greater robustness to perturbations around the training data. Furthermore,
206   the gradient of the loss with respect to the model’s weights is typically lower for training data, which
207   helps guide the generator toward reproducing these samples. Additionally, we incorporate prior
208   knowledge in the form of variational loss to create noise-free images and pixel constraint loss to keep
209   pixel values within the valid range, ensuring the generated images are both semantically and visually
210   aligned with the original training data. By leveraging these insights, we steer the inversion process to
211   reconstruct training-like data and extend prior work on training data reconstruction, which primarily
212
      focused on models with fully connected layers, to under-parametrized models with convolutional
      layers and standard activation functions, trained on larger datasets with regularisation techniques to
213
      prevent memorisation.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY & I MPLEMENTATION
217
218
      Our approach to Network Inversion and subsequent training data reconstruction uses a carefully con-
219
      ditioned generator that learns a diverse data distributions in the input space of the trained classifier
220   by simple modification of the training objectives.
221
222
      3.1     C LASSIFIER
223
224   In this paper inversion and reconstruction is performed on a classifier which includes convolution
225   and fully connected layers as appropriate to the classification task. We use standard non-linearity
226   layers like Leaky-ReLU (Xu et al., 2015) and Dropout layers (Srivastava et al., 2014) in the classifier
227   for regularisation purposes to discourage memorisation. The classification network is trained on a
228   particular dataset and then held in evaluation mode for the purpose of inversion and reconstruction.
229
230   3.2     G ENERATOR
231
232   The images in the input space of the classifier will be generated by an appropriately conditioned
233   generator. The generator builds up from a latent vector by up-convolution operations to generate the
234   image of the given size. While generators are conventionally conditioned on an embedding learnt of
235
      a label for generative modelling tasks, we given its simplicity, observe its ineffectiveness in network
      inversion and instead propose more intense conditioning mechanism using vectors and matrices.
236
237
238
      3.2.1    L ABEL C ONDITIONING
239   Label Conditioning of a generator is a simple approach to condition the generator on an embedding
240   learnt off of the labels each representative of the separate classes. The conditioning labels are then
241   used in the cross entropy loss function with the outputs of the classifier. While Label Conditioning
242   can be used for inversion, the inverted samples do not seem to have the diversity that is expected of
243   the inversion process due to the simplicity and varying confidence behind the same label.
244
245   3.2.2    V ECTOR C ONDITIONING
246
247   In order to achieve more diversity in the generated images, the conditioning mechanism of the
248
      generator is altered by encoding the label information into an N -dimensional vector for an N -class
      classification task. The vectors for this purpose are randomly generated from a normal distribution
249
      and then soft-maxed to represent an input conditioning distribution for the generated images. The
250
      argmax index of the soft-maxed vectors now serves as the de facto conditioning label, which can be
251   used in the cross-entropy loss function without being explicitly revealed to the generator.
252
253
      3.2.3    I NTERMEDIATE M ATRIX C ONDITIONING
254
255   Vector Conditioning allows for a encoding the label information into the vectors using the argmax
256   criteria. This can be further extended into Matrix Conditioning which apparently serves as a better
257   prior in case of generating images and allows for more ways to encode the label information for
258   a better capture of the diversity in the inversion process. In its simplest form we use a Hot Con-
259   ditioning Matrix in which an N XN dimensional matrix is defined such that all the elements in a
260
      given row and column (same index) across the matrix are set to one while the rest all entries are ze-
      roes. The index of the row or column set to 1 now serves as the label for the conditioning purposes.
261
      The conditioning matrix is concatenated with the latent vector intermediately after up-sampling it to
262
      N XN spatial dimensions, while the generation upto this point remains unconditioned.
263
264
      3.2.4    V ECTOR -M ATRIX C ONDITIONING
265
266   Since the generation is initially unconditioned in Intermediate Matrix Conditioning, we combine
267   both vector and matrix conditioning, in which vectors are used for early conditioning of the generator
268   upto N XN spatial dimensions followed by concatenation of the conditioning matrix for subsequent
269   generation. The argmax index of the vector, which is the same as the row or column index set to
      high in the matrix, now serves as the conditioning label.

      3.3.1    C ROSS E NTROPY
325
326   The key goal of the inversion process is to generate images with the desired labels and the same
327   can be easily achieved using cross entropy loss. In cases where the label information is encoded
328   into the vectors without being explicitly revealed to the generator, the encoded labels can be used
329   in the cross entropy loss function with the classifier outputs for the generated images in order to
330   train the generator. In contrast to the label conditioning, vector conditioning complicate the training
331   objectives to the extent that the generator does not immediately converge, instead the convergence
332
      occurs only when the generator figures out the encoded conditioning mechanism allowing for a
      better exploration of the input space of the classifier.
333
334
335   3.3.2    KL D IVERGENCE
336
      KL Divergence is used to train the generator to learn the data distribution in the input space of
337
      the classifier for different conditioning vectors. During training, the KL Divergence loss function
338
      measures and minimise the difference between the output distribution of the generated images, as
339   predicted by the classifier, and the conditioning distribution used to generate these images. This
340   divergence metric is crucial for aligning the generated image distributions with the intended condi-
341   tioning distribution.
342
343
      3.3.3    C OSINE S IMILARITY
344
345   To enhance the diversity of the generated images, we use cosine similarity to assesses and minimises
346   the angular distance between the features of a batch of generated images across the last fully con-
347   nected layers, promoting variability in the generated images. The combination of cosine similarity
348   with cross-entropy loss not only ensures that the generated images are classified correctly but also
349   enforces diversity among the images produced for each label.
350
351   3.3.4    F EATURE O RTHOGONALITY
352
353   In addition to the cosine similarity loss, we incorporate feature orthogonality as a regularization
354   term to further enhance the diversity of generated images by minimizing the deviation of the Gram
355   matrix of the features from the identity matrix. By ensuring that the features of generated images
356
      are orthogonal, we promote the generation of distinct and non-redundant representations for each
      conditioning label.
357
358
359   3.4     T RAINING -L IKE DATA R ECONSTRUCTION
360
361   While Network Inversion enables access to a diverse set of images in the input space of the model for
362   different classes, the inverted samples, given the vastness of the input space, are completely random.
363
      However, Network Inversion can be used for training data reconstruction as shown schematically in
      Figure 2 by exploiting key properties of the training data in relation to the classifier that guide the
364
      generator towards producing training-like data including model confidence, robustness to perturba-
365
      tions, and gradient behavior along with some prior knowledge about the training data.
366
367   In order to take model confidence into account, we use hot conditioning vectors in reconstruction in-
368   stead of soft conditioning vectors used in inversion, hoping to generate samples that are confidently
369
      labeled by the classifier. This encourages the generation of samples that elicit high-confidence pre-
      dictions from the model, aligning them more closely with the training set. Since the classifier is
370
      expected to handle perturbations around the training data effectively, the perturbed images should
371
      retain the same labels and also be confidently classified. To achieve this, we introduce an L∞ per-
372   turbation to the generated images and pass both the original and perturbed images represented by
373   dashed lines, through the classifier and use them in the loss evaluation. We also introduce a gra-
374   dient minimization loss to penalise the large gradients of the classifier’s output with respect to its
375   weights when processing the generated images ensuring that the generator produces samples that
376   have small gradient norm, a property expected of the training samples. Furthermore, we incorporate
377   prior knowledge through pixel constraint and variational losses to ensure that the generated images
      have valid pixel values and are noise-free ensuring visually realistic and smooth reconstructions.

      3.4.1   P IXEL L OSS

419   The Pixel Loss is used to ensure that the generated images have valid pixel values between 0 and 1.

424

      4     E XPERIMENTS & R ESULTS
433
434
      In this section, we present the experimental results obtained by applying our network inversion and
435
      reconstruction technique on the MNIST (Deng, 2012), FashionMNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), SVHN
436   and CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al.) datasets by training a generator to produce images that, when
437   passed through a classifier, elicit the desired labels. The classifier is initially normally trained on
438   a dataset and then held in evaluation for the purpose of inversion and reconstruction. The images
439   generated by the conditioned generator corresponding to the latent and the conditioning vectors are
440   then passed through the classifier.
441
      The classifier is a simple multi-layer convolutional neural network consisting of convolutional lay-
442   ers, dropout layers, batch normalization, and leaky-relu activation followed by fully connected layers
443   and softmax for classification. While the generator is based on Vector-Matrix Conditioning in which
444   the class labels are encoded into random softmaxed vectors concatenated with the latent vector fol-
445   lowed by multiple layers of transposed convolutions, batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015)
446   and dropout layers (Srivastava et al., 2014) to encourage diversity in the generated images. Once
447   the vectors are upsampled to N XN spatial dimensions they are concatenated with a conditioning
448   matrix for subsequent generation upto the required image size of 28X28 or 32X32.

518   5   C ONCLUSION & F UTURE W ORK
519
520   In this paper, we propose Training-Like Data Reconstruction (TLDR), a novel approach for re-
521   constructing training-like data using Network Inversion from convolutional neural network (CNN)
522   based machine learning models. We begin by introducing a comprehensive network inversion tech-
523   nique using a conditioned generator trained to learn the input space associated with different classes
524   within the classifier. To ensure the diversity and accuracy of the generated samples, we employed a
525
      combination of loss functions, including cross-entropy, KL divergence, cosine similarity, and feature
      orthogonality. By exploiting key properties of the classifier in relation to its training data—such as
526
      model confidence, robustness to perturbations, and gradient behavior we effectively encouraged the
527
      reconstruction of training-like data. Extensive experiments on standard datasets demonstrated that
528   machine learning models remain vulnerable to data reconstruction attacks, emphasizing the need
529   to reassess privacy assumptions in model sharing practices, especially when dealing with sensitive
530   data.
531
      As part of the future work, we plan to extend the TLDR approach to more complex architectures,
532
      such as transformer models, attention-based layers, and hybrid architectures that combine CNNs
533
      with attention mechanisms, to understand privacy vulnerabilities in more advanced neural networks.
534   Additionally, we intend to extend this work to models trained on larger, high-resolution image
535   datasets to evaluate privacy risks in more complex real world scenarios. Further improving the qual-
536   ity of reconstructed samples by leveraging the implicit bias of gradient-based optimization, which
537   tends to memorize a subset of training samples near decision boundaries, will also be explored.
538   Lastly, it would be of interest to evaluate the potential for learning generative models in coopera-
539   tion with classifiers through network inversion guided by successive weight updates in the classifier
      during the training process.
