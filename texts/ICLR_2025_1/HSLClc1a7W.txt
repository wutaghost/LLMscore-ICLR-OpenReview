009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning models often perform well on tabular data by optimizing av-
012            erage prediction accuracy. However, they may underperform on specific subsets
013            due to inherent biases and spurious correlations in the training data, such as as-
014            sociations with non-causal features like demographic information. These biases
015            lead to critical robustness issues as models may inherit or amplify them, resulting
016            in poor performance where such misleading correlations do not hold. Existing
017            mitigation methods have significant limitations: some require prior group labels,
018
               which are often unavailable, while others focus solely on the conditional distri-
               bution P (Y |X), upweighting misclassified samples without effectively balancing
019
               the overall data distribution P (X). To address these shortcomings, we propose
020
               a latent score-based reweighting framework. It leverages score-based models to
021            capture the joint data distribution P (X, Y ) without relying on additional prior
022            information. By estimating sample density through the similarity of score vec-
023            tors with neighboring data points, our method identifies underrepresented regions
024            and upweights samples accordingly. This approach directly tackles inherent data
025            imbalances, enhancing robustness by ensuring a more uniform dataset representa-
026            tion. Experiments on various tabular datasets under distribution shifts demonstrate
027            that our method effectively improves performance on imbalanced data.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Machine learning applied to tabular data has achieved significant success across various practical
033
      domains (Liu et al., 2024). While models trained using empirical risk minimization (ERM) often
      perform well on average, achieving low test errors overall, they can still exhibit high error rates on
034
      specific subsets of data (Vapnik, 1999). This inconsistency highlights a critical robustness issue, pri-
035
      marily stemming from inherent biases in the data. For example, training data may contain spurious
036   correlations where target labels are statistically linked to non-causal features like demographic in-
037   formation. As a result, models trained on such biased data may inherit or even amplify these biases,
038   leading to poor performance on data subsets where these correlations do not apply.
039
      To tackle this issue, researchers have proposed various methods to mitigate biases in the training
040
      data. Some approaches utilize additional prior information, such as group labels, to reduce the im-
041
      pact of spurious correlations by resampling or adding regularization terms (Arjovsky et al., 2019;
042   Sagawa* et al., 2020). Unfortunately, in many practical situations, such prior information is incom-
043   plete or unavailable, limiting these methods’ usefulness. To overcome this, other methods focus on
044   automatically generating proxy information for debiasing (Nam et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021a; Qiu
045   et al., 2023). A common strategy involves pre-training a classification model on the training data
046   and then upweighting samples that the model classifies incorrectly. Since these misclassifications
047   are often determined by a sample’s proximity to the model’s decision boundary, we refer to these
048   as boundary-based methods. While these methods can enhance robustness by focusing on under-
049   represented training samples near the classification boundary, their effectiveness is constrained as
050
      they may fail to achieve a globally balanced distribution of training data.

      To more effectively address inherent data imbalances, we propose leveraging score-based models,
088   also known as diffusion models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020). These models can cap-
089   ture the joint data distribution P (X, Y ), providing a powerful tool for modeling the underlying data
090   structure. Our key idea is to use a score-based model to estimate the density of samples in the dataset.
091   By identifying low-density regions—areas where data is underrepresented—we can upweight sam-
092   ples from these regions, ensuring a more balanced and unbiased representation during training. This
093   approach moves beyond boundary-based methods by directly targeting data imbalance, aiming to
094   improve performance across diverse data subsets.
095   However, directly using density estimates from the score-based model poses challenges due to po-
096   tential extreme values, leading to “density explosions” for certain samples. These extremes can
097   overemphasize a few extremely high-density points, making it difficult to distinguish between other
098   high- and low-density samples. To overcome this, we propose using a proxy for density based on an
099   important observation. We demonstrate this observation using data from a mixture of two Gaussians,

137   2   R ELATED W ORKS
138
139   Achieving robustness with prior information. Commonly, researchers aim to train robust models
140   towards distribution shift. Some works train models with the help of given or self-generated domain
141
      labels (Sagawa* et al., 2020; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Sun & Saenko, 2016; Liu et al., 2021b;c; Tong
      et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b). Generally they expect the model’s performance in the worst
142
      domain acceptable. Therefore, they may add regularizer into training loss when treating domain
143
      labels as extra supervised information. Predefining the form of prior data distribution is also a
144   common approach (Shen et al., 2020; Duchi & Namkoong, 2021; Shen et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2024).
145   Among them, stable learning is an effective approach which pursues feature independence under
146   certain assumptions. The details are listed in Section A.9.
147
      Achieving robustness without prior information. Apart from introducing the prior information,
148
      some other methods turn to use the performance of models to guide the final training process (Liu
149
      et al., 2021a; Nam et al., 2020; Levy et al., 2020; Qiu et al., 2023). They typically contain two
150   stages. In the first stage, they identify some biased samples which will make ERM-based models
151   make false predictions. In the second stage, they assign greater weights to these samples to develop
152   a more robust model. However, we notice that few of the following methods model the joint data
153   distribution, which is not fit for seeking overall robustness. The requirement of some prior-based
154   methods is also rather strict and can not generalize to realistic scenarios.
155
      Generative Models for Robustness. Generative models can produce novel and diverse data, en-
156   riching training datasets and leading to models with a deeper understanding of semantic content.
157   Consequently, several studies have leveraged generative models to enhance robustness, primarily
158   through data augmentation techniques (Li et al., 2021; Choi et al., 2021; Ilse et al., 2020; Dendorfer
159   et al., 2021; Oberdiek et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; 2024a). These approaches have demonstrated
160   promising performance improvements. In contrast to these methods, we introduce a novel approach
161   that utilizes generative models to estimate the density of the data distribution. By assigning weights
      to data samples based on this density estimation during training, we enhance the model’s robustness.

      3     M ETHOD
163
164
      We present an unbiased learning framework that operates without prior information or pre-trained
165   classification boundaries. The model is designed to provide robust predictions across a range of
166   biased covariates. To address distributional shifts, we employ score-based methods to faithfully
167   capture the joint distribution. The framework follows three steps: (1) training diffusion models on
168   latent representations to model the data distribution, (2) sampling several timesteps and estimating
169   scores to align the probability density of training data, and (3) reweighting samples based on data
170   density to ensure a balanced distribution. The detailed procedure is provided in Algorithm 1.

      At this stage, our goal is to estimate the probability density of each sample using the score models
      trained in Section 3.2. We first address the limitations of computing exact log-likelihood via the
239
      probability flow ordinary differential equation (ODE) proposed in (Song et al., 2021). Based on the
240
      insights from Figure 2, we introduce an approach to estimate the relative probability density through
241   a similarity measure. The difference of score similarities acts as a proxy, preserving the relative
242   magnitudes of probability densities among samples and offering a controllable numerical range at
243   the model level. This makes it well-suited for computing weights, as described in Section 3.4.

      To comprehensively validate our method, we selected six diverse datasets that exhibit various types

335

336

338         banking institution. The task is to predict if the client will subscribe a term deposit.

344
345       • Taxi (Navas, 2018): This dataset collects some information about the pickup and dropoff of
346
            taxi rides. The task is to predict whether the total ride duration time exceeds 30 minutes. We
            choose the data collected in Mexican City.
347
348       • US-Wide ACS PUMS Data (Ding et al., 2021): This large dataset contains individual records
349         from US Census sources. We choose the task where the outcome is whether an individual’s
350         income exceeds 50k. The performance is validated separately on three randomly chosen states.

      Specifically, for the Taxi and ACS datasets, we followed the preprocessing guidelines outlined in the

355   4.2    E VALUATION M ETRICS
356
      Our objective is to ensure that the model consistently makes robust predictions across all non-causal
357
      covariates, rather than focusing on a single one. For each dataset, we select several deterministic
358   non-causal attributes based on prior knowledge—for example, sex and race in the ACS income
359   dataset. We then record the worst-case prediction results for each feature and compute the average
360   of the worst-group accuracies across these features. Details of the selected features are provided in
361   Appendix A.5. Additionally, we track the mean accuracy for these features. We aim for our model to
362   enhance the average worst-group performance without significantly compromising mean accuracy.

364   4.3    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
365
366   We conducted each experiment three times with different random seeds and report the mean results

368   prediction model consists of two components: a Variational AutoEncoder (VAE) for generating
369   latent representations, and a MultiLayer Perceptron (MLP) for classification. Both our method and
370
      the baseline models utilize the same architecture. For training the VAE, we followed the default

                                                                                  2

      t follows a log-normal distribution, resulting in ln (σ(t)) ∼ N (Pmean , Pstd ). The Pmean and Pstd
      are set to −1.2 and 1.2, respectively. When computing aggregated similarity in Eq. 11, we choose

      0.002 and 80 as the minimum and maximum values of t and select T timesteps with equal intervals

      4.4     C OMPARED BASELINES
392
393   As referred in Section 4.2, our goal is to enhance overall robustness, with the accuracy on the worst
394   group serving as our main evaluation metric. Therefore, we compare our method with several ro-
395   bust machine learning techniques, including ERM (Vapnik, 1999), CVaR-DRO (Levy et al., 2020),
396   χ2 -DRO (Levy et al., 2020), KL-DRO (Duchi & Namkoong, 2021), JTT (Liu et al., 2021a), EIIL
397   (Creager et al., 2021), FAM (Petzka et al., 2021; Zou et al., 2024) and SRDO (Shen et al., 2020).
398   Among these, CVaR-DRO, χ2 -DRO, KL-DRO, JTT, and SRDO employ reweighting processes sim-
399   ilar to ours, while EIIL and FAM enhance robustness by adding regularization terms.

403   4.5.1    ROBUSTNESS ACROSS VARIOUS D ISTRIBUTION S HIFTS
404
405   We first demonstrate the effectiveness of our method under various types of distribution shifts in
406
      Table 1. Our method consistently achieves the highest worst-case accuracy across all five datasets,
      with an average improvement of at least 3% in worst-case accuracy over all baseline methods. Ad-
407
      ditionally, it attains the best mean accuracy on two of the five datasets, highlighting its ability to
408
      enhance robustness without a significant reduction in overall prediction accuracy.
409
410   In contrast, DRO-based methods like KL-DRO perform inconsistently on our datasets. For instance,
411   while KL-DRO achieves the second-best accuracy on the Shoppers dataset, its performance dete-
412
      riorates significantly on Default and Taxi. This inconsistency stems from KL-DRO’s reliance on
      expanding the search space without adequately modeling the underlying data distribution.
413
414   Another popular invariant learning-based method, EIIL (Creager et al., 2021), achieves high mean
415   accuracy. However, its worst-case accuracy is relatively disappointing compared to other methods.
416   EIIL divides samples into different groups based on generated environment labels, which creates
417   implicit boundaries based on data’s inherent characteristics. While this boundary-based approach
418
      may improve overall accuracy by exploiting data biases, it undermines the balance and robustness
      required to handle sensitive non-causal attributes.
419
420   Compared to the above methods which rely on either prior information or boundaries, our method
421   could achieve consistent and stable robustness on these datasets. The results demonstrate the benefit
422   of modeling relative probability density as referred in Section 1.

455   4.6     V ISUALIZATION OF S CORE - SIMILARITY- BASED W EIGHTS
456
457   In this section, we provide the visualization results on a synthetic dataset to directly explain the
458   reweighting process of our methods. In addition, we will explain why we use our aggregated sim-
459   ilarity difference measure in Eq. 12 instead of running a sampler based on the probability flow
460   ordinary differential equation that allow for exact likelihood computation proposed by Song et al..
461   Synthetic Data Generation. Our imbalanced synthetic training data has been illustrated in Fig-
462   ure 1a. The data have two features, x0 and x1 . The perfect classification boundary is a sine curve. We
463   introduce the bias on training data by sampling more points in regions where x0 ∈ [ π4 , 3π     5π 7π
                                                                                                 4 ]∪[ 4 , 4 ].
464   We expect the reweighting process could make the whole data distribution fairly balanced.
465
      Exact Likelihood Computation by Probability Flow ODE. As referred in Section 3.3, we can
466   compute the exact log-likelihood through a probability flow ODE in Eq. 10. Following Song et al.,
467   we apply the Skilling-Hutchinson trace estimator (Skilling, 1989; Hutchinson, 1990) to estimate
468   ∇ · f̃θ (x(t), t) and finally achieves the log-likelihood.
469
470   Extreme Value Problem Brought by Exact Likelihood. The log-likelihood of p(x) represents
471
      the estimated probability density at the feature level, making it useful for guiding the reweighting
      process. We compute the mean of the estimated probabilities across all features to determine the
472
      final sample-level probability density. In Figure 3a, color depth represents the log-likelihood of the
473
      predicted probability density. Points with higher density are shaded closer to violet, while those with
474   lower density appear more blue. Interestingly, not all points in high-probability regions are assigned
475   warm colors. In fact, most points are blue, similar to those in low-density regions. Only a few
476   points located at cluster centers exhibit significantly higher log-likelihoods. Reweighting based on
477   these estimates could lead to disproportionate emphasis on a small number of extreme high-density
478   points, potentially obscuring the distinction between other high- and low-probability regions.
479
      Density-Aware Weights Computed from Our Similarity-based Measure. We then visualize the
480   unnormalized score similarity in Figure 3b. Points with lower error, corresponding to higher relative
481   probability density, are shaded in blue and are expected to be downweighted. The visualization
482   clearly distinguishes these points, with two regions of relatively high probability density appearing
483   in blue. In contrast, points with truly low probability are represented by higher aggregated error,
484   shown in purple, and can be easily upweighted. Our similarity measure not only differentiates
485   density clearly but also maintains a consistent and stable numerical range, making it suitable for
      sample reweighting.

511   4.7           A BLATION S TUDIES
512
513   We conduct the sensitivity analysis in this section. As referred in Algorithm 1, our method has three
514
      hyperparameters. T denotes the number of selected timesteps for computing aggregation similarity.
      τ controls the strength of reweighting based on SimDiff(·). K represents the number of repeated
515
      sampling iterations for computing scores. The discussion on K is deferred to Appendix A.4, as it
516
      does not functionally impact the score computation.
517
518   The ablation study results of T on Adult are shown in Table 3. There are two main observations:
519
        • The mean accuracy shows minimal variation with changes in T .
520
521     • The worst-case accuracy improves as T increases. This is because T indirectly represents the
522       number of aggregated noisy points in the sampled neighborhood. A larger T leads to a more
523
          accurate computation of aggregated similarity in Eq. 11. We set the default value of T to 10,
          balancing fast computation with optimal performance.
524
525   In Figure 4, we show the classification results by using different τ . Our model performs consistently
526   well with all the values. The worst accuracies under different τ all surpass the baseline methods in
527   Table 1, which proves that our method does not heavily rely on the values of hyperparameters.

      5         C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we tackle the challenge of improving model robustness, measured across all non-
532
      causal covariates rather than focusing on a single attribute. We observe that previous methods are
533
      limited by their inability to model the original joint data distribution and apply effective balancing
534   strategies. To overcome these limitations, we propose using score-based models to capture the latent
535   data distribution. Specifically, we estimate scores at several fixed timesteps and use their similarity
536   to model the relative probability density of each sample. A score-based reweighting strategy is
537   then employed to train a robust classification model. Our approach requires no prior information
538   during training and ensures that the reweighting process aligns with the original data distribution.
539   Experiments on seven datasets demonstrate that our method effectively balances the original training
      data globally and achieves robust performance under distribution shifts.

843
      Our method use score-based model to model the original data distribution, serving a purpose similar
844
      to traditional density estimation methods such as Kernel Density Estimation (KDE). Traditional den-
845
      sity estimation methods, such as KDE, often require a large amount of background data. However,
846   thanks to the advantages of diffusion models, our score-based density estimation is not sensitive to
847   sample size, as demonstrated in our subsequent experimental results.
848
849
      In this section, we examine the impact of training samples’ size on our score-based proxy. We
      create subsets of varying sizes from the original training dataset and use these subsets to train score
850
      models. The final classification model is trained on the weights from new score models but tested on
851
      the original test data. We denote R as the ratio of the subset size relative to the original dataset. The
852   experimental results are listed in Table 9. It demonstrates that the weights generated from our score-
853   based proxy is insensitive to the sample size. The robustness of our method arises from score model’s
854   ability to construct the latent score field. As Kadkhodaie et al. (2024) stated, neural networks can
855   memorize the score field even when the number of training samples is finite. This property ensures
856   that our score-based weights faithfully approximate the original probability density, enabling robust
857   classification model training even with reduced subsets (R = 0.5 and R = 0.8). However, when
858   R = 0.2, the number of training samples becomes insufficient to construct an accurate score field,
859   which makes the estimated weights less accurate. However, even when we used only one-fifth of the
860
      data for training, the performance of our model still outperformed the results of the other baselines
      using the full dataset in Table 1.
861

954   A.9    T HE COMPARISON WITH STABLE LEARNING
955
956
      In this section, we provide a comprehensive comparison between our method and stable learning.
      Stable learning aims to decorrelate features to achieve a uniform and balanced data distribution,
957
      which is similar to our approach. However, our method offers three significant advantages over
958
      stable learning, as detailed below:
959
960    • Ability to handle potential Y -shift problems: Our method use a similarity difference measure
961      to address implicit Y -shift as discussed in Section 3.3. In contrast, stable learning focuses solely
962      on the decorrelation of covariates without differentiating the information carried by labels y.
963    • Balanced Distribution via Original Distribution Modeling vs. Feature Independence: Our
964      method obtains a balanced distribution by modeling the original distribution while stable learn-
965      ing achieves balance through feature independence. The reweighting process in stable learning
966      relies on feature decorrelation under a linear assumption. However, it is important to note that a
967      balanced distribution does not equate to feature independence, and feature decorrelation
968      does not necessarily achieve balance, particularly in non-linear cases.
969      Consider an example where feature decorrelation fails at the sample level but our method suc-
970      ceeds. Suppose there are two features x0 and x1 , with samples distributed such that 0 < x0 < 1
971      and x0 < x1 < x0 + 1. Suppose that original data distribution is imbalanced, e.g., samples with
         x0 > 0.5 all share a same higher density than those with x0 < 0.5. Under this circumstance,


                                                           18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
          our method can transform the original data distribution p(x0 , x1 ) into a uniform data distribution
973       U (x0 , x1 ) at the sample level easily. This is achievable because the score model captures and
974       recovers the original data distribution p(x0 , x1 ).
975
          In contrast, stable learning, which enforces feature decorrelation to achieve independence, cannot
976
          produce a uniform balanced distribution at the sample level while maintaining independence
977       between x0 and x1 . The geometry of the sample space—a parallelogram region—dictates that
978       x0 and x1 cannot be independent while maintaining a uniform sample-level distribution, i.e.,
979       U (x0 , x1 ) ̸= p(x0 ) ∩ p(x1 ). Thus, stable learning fails to balance the data distribution at the
980       sample level while simultaneously decorrelating features.
981       Decorrelating features to achieve feature independence is a good idea, but it is insufficient to
982       ensure a balanced dataset without the essential assumptions. In contrast, our method does not
983       depend on the presence or absence of feature correlations. Instead, it estimates the implicit
984       score field to model the original distribution, enabling a robust balancing operation based on the
985       estimated distribution.
986
        • Enhanced Experimental Outcomes Relative to Stable Learning: To facilitate a quantitative
987
          comparison between our method and stable learning, we conducted experiments in Tables 1 and
988
          2. Our approach shows a minimum improvement of 5% in the worst group accuracy compared
989       to the SRDO baseline across both tables. The superiority of our score-based reweighting over
990       stable learning stems from the accuracy of the weights derived from our score model com-
991       pared to the predicted probabilities generated by stable learning predictors. Stable learning
992       methods typically train a predictor to estimate probabilities, which involves the generation of
993       synthetic samples. For instance, SRDO (Shen et al., 2020) employs empty vectors to create
994       samples, whereas StableNet (Zhang et al., 2021) uses a Random Fourier Transformation. The
995       quality of these synthetic samples crucially affects the training process of the predictor, thereby
996       making the estimated probabilities potentially unreliable. In contrast, the score in our method
997
          represents the gradient of the log-likelihood of the original distribution p(x). This ensures that
          our reweighting process remains rigorously faithful to the actual data distribution.
998
999
       In a word, our method does not rely on any assumptions about the original data distribution. It
1000
       utilizes the score, i.e., the gradient of estimated log-likelihood of original data distribution, to per-
1001   form sample reweighting, which is flexible and easy to conduct. The additional experiments further
1002   confirm the effectiveness of our method.

       method, we can achieve a balance between both optimization objectives. Specifically, we defined

1083   parameter α controls the the influence of our weights in the optimization process. As α increases, the
1084   optimization objective aligns more closely with our score-based balancing strategy, while a lower α
1085   gives greater weight to ERM. By varying α, we can compare model performance and gain insights
