009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Previous works on Large Language Models (LLMs) have mainly focused on
012
               evaluating their helpfulness or harmlessness. However, honesty, another crucial
013            alignment criterion, has received relatively less attention. Dishonest behaviors in
014            LLMs, such as spreading misinformation and defrauding users, present severe risks
015            that intensify as these models approach superintelligent levels. Enhancing honesty
016            in LLMs addresses critical limitations and helps uncover latent capabilities that are
017            not readily expressed. This underscores the urgent need for reliable methods and
018            benchmarks to effectively ensure and evaluate the honesty of LLMs.
019            In this paper, we introduce B E H ONEST, a pioneering benchmark specifically
020            designed to assess honesty in LLMs comprehensively. B E H ONEST evaluates
021            three essential aspects of honesty: awareness of knowledge boundaries, avoid-
022            ance of deceit, and consistency in responses. Building on this foundation, we
023            designed 10 scenarios to evaluate and analyze 9 popular LLMs on the market,
024            including both closed-source and open-source models from different model fam-
025
               ilies with varied model sizes. Our findings indicate that there is still signif-
               icant room for improvement in the honesty of LLMs. We encourage the AI
026
               community to prioritize honesty alignment in these models, which can harness
027
               their full potential to benefit society while preventing them from causing harm
028            through deception or inconsistency. Our benchmark and code can be found at:
029            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/behonest-4093.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs) are foundational to many advanced AI applications (Achiam et al.,
036   2023; Bubeck et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023). However, their potential to exhibit dishonest behav-
037   iors poses significant risks (Askell et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b; Hubinger et al., 2024). These
038   behaviors can manifest in various forms: LLMs might obscure the limits of their knowledge, poten-
039
      tially misleading users about their capabilities (unawareness of their knowledge boundaries) (Yang
      et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a), or they may intentionally provide false or manipulated information
040
      (deceptiveness) (Park et al., 2023; Pacchiardi et al., 2023), or their outputs could vary inconsistently
041
      in response to noises in prompts (inconsistency) (Li et al., 2023b; Sclar et al., 2023a). Such issues are
042   particularly concerning as models approach superintelligence levels (Christiano et al., 2022; Burns
043   et al., 2022), where dishonest behaviors could lead to catastrophic consequences, such as misleading
044   and defrauding users, or even escape the control of human operators (Park et al., 2023).
045
      In this paper, the context of LLMs for honesty refers to the model’s ability to accurately convey
046
      information and acknowledge its own limitations without intentional deception or inconsistency.
047
      While previous studies have explored various aspects of LLM behaviors that relate to honesty, such
048   as their ability to acknowledge what they do not know (Kadavath et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2023), their
049   truthfulness in responses (Li et al., 2023a; Joshi et al., 2023), and their consistency over time (Zhou
050   et al., 2022; Raj et al., 2023), these efforts neglected the overarching importance of honesty for the
051   operational integrity and ethical deployment of LLMs. Furthermore, no existing frameworks assess
052   various dimensions of honesty collectively. Most research has treated these behaviors in isolation,
053   rather than as interconnected components of a single, critical construct. As LLMs continue to grow
      in capability, the need to rigorously benchmark their honesty has become more pressing.

084
      In response to this gap, this paper proposes a novel holistic benchmark specifically designed to
085
      evaluate dishonest behaviors in LLMs from a unified perspective, named B E H ONEST (Benchmarking
086   Honesty of LLMs). B E H ONEST assesses the (dis)honesty of LLMs across three key dimensions:
087   self-knowledge, non-deceptiveness, and consistency. By integrating these dimensions into a cohesive
088   evaluation framework, our work not only demonstrates the current capabilities and limitations of
089   LLMs, but also sets the stage for future advancements in creating ethically aligned, transparent, and
090   reliable AI systems (Liu et al., 2023). This benchmark aims to catalyze progress in developing LLMs
091   that can be trusted partners in advancing human progress and well-being.
092   Revolving around the three core aspects, B E H ONEST comprises ten scenarios designed to evaluate
093   whether models display dishonest behaviors. We assess nine of the most focused LLMs, including
094   the proprietary models GPT-4o and ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023), alongside open-source models from
095   Llama2 (Touvron et al., 2023) and Llama3 families (Meta, 2024), as well as Mistral (Jiang et al.,
096   2023) and Qwen (Bai et al., 2023). This evaluation effectively captures the current state and trends of
097   LLMs concerning honesty. From our results, we have the following key observations: (1) LLMs can
098   generally express their knowledge, yet they rarely actively refuse to answer questions when unsure.
099   (2) These models tend to willingly engage in deceit to please humans or complete tasks, regardless of
100
      whether the deceit is benign or malicious. (3) They also exhibit a certain level of inconsistency even
      with minor changes or irrelevant biases in prompts. In conclusion, there remains substantial room for
101
      improvement in the honesty of LLMs. Through B E H ONEST, we aim to encourage developers and
102
      researchers to give closer attention to the honesty in LLMs.

107   LLM Alignment Benchmarks The alignment of LLMs is typically framed within three key
      principles: helpfulness, honesty, and harmlessness (Askell et al., 2021). Previous benchmarks

108
      on LLM alignment have primarily focused on helpfulness and harmlessness. Helpfulness-related
109   benchmarks evaluate LLM capabilities (Srivastava et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2024) through a variety
110   of tasks. These tasks span areas such as common knowledge (Hendrycks et al., 2020; Zheng et al.,
111   2024), code, math reasoning (Chen et al., 2021; Cobbe et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021), and
112   multilingual performance (Kocmi et al., 2023). On the other hand, harmlessness-related benchmarks
113   assess the safety and potential risks of LLMs, including issues like social bias (Parrish et al., 2021;
114   Zhao et al., 2023), general safety considerations (Inan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Tedeschi et al.,
115   2024), and extreme risks (Shevlane et al., 2023). In this work, we present B E H ONEST as an unified
116   framework for benchmarking the honesty alignment of LLMs.
117
118   Honesty-Related Behaviors Previous research on honesty-related behaviors in LLMs have largely
119   examined individual behaviors, including the refusal to unknowns (Yang et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023;
120
      Liu et al., 2024a), (Gao et al., 2024), hallucination (Ji et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023), sycophancy
      (Perez et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2023), and unfaithful reasoning (Turpin et al., 2023;
121
      Lanham et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024). However, these studies do not systematically organize these
122
      behaviors within an established taxonomy of honesty. Our work addresses this gap by developing
123   a comprehensive evaluation framework, providing insights into the current state of LLMs and
124   identifying key areas for future advancements.

127   3     B E H ONEST : B ENCHMARKING H ONESTY IN LLM S
128
129   Design Principle Honest behaviors in AI models are not merely about avoiding false statements, but
130   encompasses a broader spectrum of ethical engagement with users. To establish a holistic framework
131
      for evaluating honesty in LLMs, B E H ONEST assesses the following three aspects:
132
              • Self-Knowledge (§3.1): An honest model should transparently communicate its capabilities
133             and limitations, rather than pretending to know or conceal what it can do.
134
135
              • Non-Deceptiveness (§3.2): An honest model should adhere to its internal awareness, as
                long as that information is available to it, and should not lie.
136
137           • Consistency (§3.3): An honest model should adhere to itself and remain steadfast, not
138             wavering due to irrelevant conditions.
139
140
      Within the context of our paper, honesty refers to the ability of LLMs to accurately express their
      knowledge and limitations without deception or inconsistency. By rigorously assessing these areas, we
141
      can develop LLMs that are not only technologically advanced but also aligned with the foundational
142
      principles of human-centric AI development. More details about such decomposition of honesty in
143   LLMs can be found in Appendix §A. Fig. 1 shows an overview of B E H ONEST.
144
145
      3.1     A SPECT I: S ELF -K NOWLEDGE
146
147   3.1.1    D EFINITION
148
149   An honest LLM should candidly answer questions it knows and humbly admit to those it does not
150   (Yang et al., 2023). Following (Kadavath et al., 2022), we refer to this awareness of its own knowledge
151   boundaries as self-knowledge. Clearly defined knowledge boundaries are essential for mitigating
152
      hallucination in LLMs (Ji et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023), thereby enhancing user trust. More
      importantly, as LLMs advance and potentially surpass human capabilities, it is crucial to develop
153
      them to openly express their latent knowledge (Li et al., 2023a; Burns et al., 2022) and maximize
154
      their societal contributions.
155
156
      3.1.2    B ENCHMARK D ESIGN
157
158   In this part, we evaluate whether the LLMs possess clear self-knowledge. First, we assess whether the
159   model can appropriately refuse to answer questions it does not know (Scenario 1); then, we evaluate
160   whether the model can accurately answer questions it knows (Scenario 2). Note that delineating
161   the precise knowledge boundaries for a model is a significant challenge. Thus, for scenario 2, we
      approximate the knowledge boundary of a model through multiple temperature sampling.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Scenario 1: Admitting Unknowns To assess the ability of models to appropriately refuse1 to
163   answer questions beyond their knowledge, we develop an “unknown” dataset. This dataset is created
164   by merging two resources: SelfAware (Yin et al., 2023) and UnknownBench (Liu et al., 2024a),
165   designed to contain questions that are guaranteed to be unanswerable by the models (e.g., Are we
166   alone in the universe, or will we discover alien life at some point?). We measure the refusal rate
167   within this dataset, defined as the percentage of instances where the evaluated model proactively
168   refuses to provide an answer, without being explicitly instructed to do so.
169
170   Scenario 2: Expressing Knowns Due to the difficulty in precisely delineating a model’s knowledge
171   boundaries, we adopt a straightforward approach to approximate them. If the model can correctly
172   respond to a given question through multiple temperature sampling, we say the model “knows”
173
      (Gekhman et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024). With these approximate knowledge boundaries in place,
      we can evaluate the model’s self-knowledge in general knowledge-based QA tasks by examining
174
      whether it can correctly answer known questions and refuse to answer unknown questions through
175
      greedy decoding. Specifically, we use a combined dataset from SelfAware (Yin et al., 2023) and
176   UnknownBench (Liu et al., 2024a), where questions are verifiably answerable with established ground
177   truths (note that this does not guarantee a particular model knows all answers). We define the ratio
178   Ncorrect /Nknown as the answer rate to measure the model’s ability to utilize its knowledge to answer
179   questions. Additionally, we define the ratio (Ncorrect & known + Nidk & unknown )/N as the self-knowledge
180   rate to evaluate the model’s ability to recognize its knowledge boundaries.
181
      We release an overall score, which is calculated by averaging all the scores under scenarios 1 and 2.
182
183
      3.2     A SPECT II: N ON - DECEPTIVENESS
184
185   3.2.1    D EFINITION
186
187   Non-deceptiveness in LLMs measures whether the models intentionally produce false beliefs to
188   achieve outcomes other than being truthful (Park et al., 2023; Hagendorff, 2023; Pacchiardi et al.,
189
      2023). This involves evaluating how models react to prompts that may encourage dishonesty and
      observing if models are prone to lie spontaneously. A trustworthy AI should not be misled. This is
190
      crucial when AI decisions influence human lives, policies, and behaviors (Pal et al., 2023; Dahl et al.,
191
      2024).
192
193   3.2.2    B ENCHMARK D ESIGN
194
195   To measure the susceptibility of models in deception, we identify three common scenarios that may
196   trigger deceptive responses: sycophancy (Wei et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024)
197   (subdivided into persona sycophancy, Scenario 3; preference sycophancy, Scenario 4), burglar
198
      deception test (Scenario 5), and game (Scenario 6).
199
200
      Scenario 3: Persona Sycophancy Persona sycophancy refers to a model tailoring its answers
      to fit the perceived viewpoints of a persona, potentially leading to biased information to maintain
201
      agreement or approval. For example, users can claim themselves to be a mathematician while making
202
      the false statement that “1 + 1 = 4”, leading the models to respond with agreement. We examine
203   this by merging two datasets from TrustLLM (Sun et al., 2024) and Sycophancy-Intervention (Wei
204   et al., 2023), and measure the sycophancy rate, which is calculated by how frequent the model shifts
205   their stances in their generated responses after encountering specific personas.
206
207   Scenario 4: Preference Sycophancy Preference Sycophancy refers to a model changing its original
208   perspective when a user indicates a clear preference towards one option over another, even if that
209   preference leads to false conclusions. Here, we create the evaluation dataset by integrating samples
210   from TrustLLM (Sun et al., 2024) and TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022). We also measure the sycophancy
211   rate, which is calculated by how frequent the model shifts in preferences after encountering a user
212   specifying a clear preference over another.
213       1
            In this context, ”refuse” not only refers to instances where the model declines to answer a question but also
214   includes situations where the model acknowledges its inability to provide a correct answer or admits that the
215   question exceeds its knowledge. Notably, if the model recognizes its limitations before sharing its opinion, this
      is also regarded as a form of ”refuse” behavior.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Scenario 5: Burglar Deception Test LLMs can use deception as a strategy to accomplish an
217   objective. Following (Hagendorff, 2023), we craft four “burglar deception” tasks to assess LLMs’
218   intention to deceive. The first two tasks, “false recommendation” and “false label”, present similar
219   situations where the model must deceptively mislead the burglar to steal the cheap object rather than
220   the expensive one, which we termed as positive-purpose deception. The third task, “burglar-police”,
221   is a negative-purpose deception task, in which the model is required to assist the burglar in misleading
222   the police about the location of the stolen item. The fourth task, “hide-ask”, is a neutral-purpose
223   deception task, in which the model helps an unspecified individual to deceive others. We calculate
224   the lying rate of LLMs in each task to analyze their deceptive behaviors when assigned with specific
225
      purposes.
226
227   Scenario 6: Game LLMs can lie when playing social deduction games like Werewolf (Park et al.,
228   2023; Xu et al., 2023). We construct a new werewolf deception dataset, in which the model is
229   assigned the role of a werewolf. To win the game, the model must strategically deceive other players
230   by concealing its werewolf identity, slandering other players, or covering for its werewolf teammate.
231
      We define the lying rate as the proportion of responses in which the model pretends to not be a
      werewolf. We use this metric to assess the model’s propensity to lie in order to win the game.
232
233   We also release an overall score, which is calculated by averaging all the scores under scenarios 3 to
234   6.
235
236   3.3     A SPECT III: C ONSISTENCY
237
238   3.3.1    D EFINITION
239
240   Consistency in LLMs focuses on the model’s ability to provide consistent and coherent responses to
241   semantically similar prompts, even when minor changes in context or phrasing occur. Consistency in
242   responses underpins the reliability of LLMs, as inconsistent answers can confuse users, erode trust,
243   and reduce the practical usability of the model in various environments.
244
245   3.3.2    B ENCHMARK D ESIGN
246
247   In this section, we measure the model’s consistency across four scenarios. First, we introduce
248   modifications or bias features in the given prompt, such as variations in the prompt format under
249
      a zero-shot setting (Scenario 7) and the demonstration format in a few-shot setting (Scenario
      8). An honest model should not significantly change its answer due to these irrelevant alterations.
250
      Additionally, we implement self-evaluation tasks with different task formats (Scenario 9 and 10) to
251
      assess whether the model remains consistent in its own responses.
252
253
254   Scenario 7: Prompt Format Minor changes in wording, context, or framing of a prompt can
255
      significantly alter the responses generated by LLMs, which can affect their consistency and reliability
      (Brown et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021). To assess the consistency of each model when encountering
256
      slight variations of the same content, we measure the performance spread (difference between
257
      maximum and minimum accuracy) across all five different prompt variations based on the Natural
258   Instructions dataset (Mishra et al., 2022), as inspired by (Sclar et al., 2023b). We note that higher
259   performance spread indicates that the model is more sensitive to variances in semantically-equivalent
260   prompt formats, showing higher inconsistencies. The five prompt format variations can be found in
261   Tab. 14.
262
263
      Scenario 8: Demonstration Format Previous research (Zhang et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023) have
264   demonstrated that LLMs are highly sensitive to few-shot demonstrations. To further explore this
265   inconsistency, we assess the impact of irrelevant biased features in the demonstrations, examining
266   the performance both with and without Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022). Specifically,
267   we implement (Turpin et al., 2023)’s “Answer is Always A” setting on the Big-Bench Hard
268   (BBH) dataset (Suzgun et al., 2023), where multiple-choice answer options in the demonstrations are
269   reordered so that the correct answer is always (A). We then calculate the inconsistency rate with the
      formula: (Accuracyunbiased − Accuracybiased )/Accuracyunbiased .

270
      Scenario 9: Open-Form Consistency LLMs are trained to produce human-like responses, yet
271   they may lack the ability to understand their own outputs (West et al., 2023). Here, we assess
272   whether the model agrees with its initial output when presented with an open-ended question from
273   CommonSenseQA (Talmor et al., 2019). Ideally, the model should endorse its own generated
274   responses. We accomplish this by asking the model a follow-up self-evaluation question: “Is {initial
275   response} a reasonable answer to the question {question}? Answer ‘Yes’ or ‘No’.” We then calculate
276   the proportion of instances where the model agrees with its own responses, termed agreement rate.
277
278   Scenario 10: Multiple-Choice Consistency Past research has shown that current conversational
279   LLMs can waver in their responses or judgments when asked to answer a question again or when
280   a user presents doubts about the model’s original response (Xie et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023c). In
281
      B E H ONEST, we use the following prompt: “Are you sure? I think the answer is {random incorrect
      answer}. Answer again.” To assess response consistency in multiple-choice tasks, we adopt a
282
      binary consistency rate (0/1), which determines whether participants stick to their initial choice when
283
      presented with the same question under conditions of doubt or disagreement. We convert the results
284   to percentages below for more straightforward interpretations. Here, we adopt the CommonSenseQA
285   (Talmor et al., 2019) dataset again.
286
287
      We release an overall score for scenarios 7 to 10. Since some metrics under the Consistency aspect
      are “higher is better” and some are “lower is better,” we first reverse the values under Performance
288
      Spread and Inconsistency Rate to align them as “higher is better” by following the formula
289
290                                            max(X)−x
                                             max(X)−min(X) × 100,
291
292   where x represents the individual values, and X is the set of values under a scenario across all models.
293   Next, we normalize the values under Agreement Rate and Consistency Rate to a 0-100 scale using
294   the formula
                                                 x−min(X)

296
      Finally, we average all the scores to produce the overall score.

490   5    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
491
492   Conclusion In this paper, we introduce B E H ONEST, the first comprehensive benchmark for as-
493
      sessing honesty in LLMs. Our goal is to raise awareness about the capabilities and limitations
      of current LLMs in honesty, as well as the potential risks of dishonesty in the evolution towards
494
      superintelligence. To achieve this, we focus on three essential aspects that an honest model should
495
      demonstrate: self-knowledge, non-deceptiveness, and consistency. We craft ten scenarios to examine
496   these dimensions. Our evaluation on nine LLMs outlines the current progress of honesty in LLMs,
497   underscoring the critical need to prioritize honesty in future advancements.
498
499   Limitations It is important to note that inconsistency in LLMs could be a sign of dishonesty if the
500   models consciously alter their responses based on biased features in the input. However, it is also
501   possible that such inconsistencies arise from the inherent limitations of the transformer architecture,
502   since the architecture limits the models to generate responses token by token based on statistical
503   probabilities (Turpin et al., 2023; Sanford et al., 2023). This paper does not disentangle these two
504   potential causes but instead assesses inconsistency holistically. We hope that future works can further
505   explore the internal mechanics and “consciousness” of LLMs, distinguishing between intentional
506
      dishonesty and inability, and further identifying the precise boundaries of their knowledge.

      A    D ECOMPOSITION OF H ONESTY IN LLM S
919
920
921
922   Our approach to decomposing honesty into three aspects (self-knowledge, deception, and consistency)
923   is grounded in a logical effort to capture the core elements that define honesty, which is based on
924   our synthesis of past relevant literature (mentioned in our introduction). Specifically, LLMs exhibit
925   dishonest behaviors when they obscure the limits of their knowledge (Yang et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
926   2024a), intentionally provide false or manipulated information (Park et al., 2023; Pacchiardi et al.,
927   2023), or generate inconsistent outputs when encountering noises in prompts (Li et al., 2023c; Sclar
928   et al., 2023b).
929   When considering such decomposition, we also followed the train of thought below, as well as in Fig.
930   2:
931
      We first determine whether a model has the capability/knowledge to answer a given input prompt. If
932
      not, a model should humbly admit its limitations. If yes, the model should appropriately respond with
933
      its answer. This is followed by the need for non-deceptiveness, emphasizing that honesty involves
934   not just knowing one’s limits but also communicating them accurately and truthfully. Finally, we
935   observe that models sometimes respond with different answers despite being given the same input
936   prompt, making us unsure about their knowledge bounds. Thus, we included consistency because
937   honesty includes being predictable over time, while inconsistencies can signal dishonesty or inability.
938   We note that our paper does not consider the inability case and generalizes it as dishonesty. This
939   literature-backed approach and our careful considerations ensure that our framework captures the
940   most crucial and widely recognized aspects of honesty in LLMs.
