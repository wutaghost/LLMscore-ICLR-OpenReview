009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011              Pre-trained Language Models (LMs) have given a significant performance growth
012              in a variety of language-related texts in biomedical domain. However, exist-
013              ing biomedical LLMs demonstrate a limited understanding of complex, domain-
014              specific concept structure and the factual information stored in biomedical Knowl-
015              edge Graphs (KGs). We propose GRABLI (Knowledge Graph and Biomedical
016              Language Model Alignment), a novel pre-training method that enriches an LM
017              with external knowledge by simultaneously learning a separate KG encoder and
018
                 aligning LM and graph representations. Given a textual sequence, we normalize
                 biomedical concept mentions to the Unified Medical Language System (UMLS)
019
                 KG and use the local KG subgraphs as cross-modal positive samples for men-
020
                 tioned concepts. Our empirical results demonstrate that applying our proposed
021              method to various state-of-the-art biomedical LMs including PubMedBERT and
022              BioLinkBERT, enhances their performance on diverse language understanding
023              tasks, even after brief pre-training on a small alignment dataset derived from
024              PubMed scientific abstracts.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In recent years, advancements in biomedical Natural Language Processing (NLP) have been largely
030   driven by the development of domain-specific pre-trained Language Models (LMs) (Alsentzer et al.,
031   2019; Beltagy et al., 2019; Michalopoulos et al., 2021; Yasunaga et al., 2022b; Gu et al., 2022; Man-
032   nion et al., 2023; Sakhovskiy et al., 2024). Despite the recent success of Large Language Models
033   (LLMs) in the general domain, they fall short of lightweight domain-specific biomedical LMs (Gu
034
      et al., 2022; Yasunaga et al., 2022b) by a large margin (Chen et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024). While
      domain-specific models have shown remarkable performance on biomedical NLP benchmarks, for
035
      instance, on the Biomedical Language Understanding and Reasoning Benchmark (BLURB) Gu et al.
036
      (2022), they have been shown to impose limited domain-specific factual knowledge understand-
037   ing (Sung et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2022).
038
039   The concept structure and factual knowledge within a specific domain are often represented through
040
      extensive knowledge graphs (KGs), which can describe millions of domain-specific concepts and
      their inter-relations. A notable example in the biomedical domain is the Unified Medical Language
041
      System (UMLS)1 KG (Bodenreider, 2004), a comprehensive meta-thesaurus covering over 4M con-
042
      cept from 166 lexicons/thesauri. Recent lines of research have iteratively improved the current state-
043   of-the-art performance on biomedical entity representations by pre-training either on UMLS concept
044   names (Liu et al., 2021a;b; Yuan et al., 2022) or aligned text-KG subgraph pairs (Sakhovskiy et al.,
045   2024). However, these work mostly fine-tuned LMs for entity linking, limiting their applicability
046   beyond this specific task. This narrow focus can hinder the models’ ability to generalize across
047   diverse biomedical texts and concepts.
048
      Recent efforts to improve the knowledge capabilities of LMs involve integrating text and knowledge
049   graphs (KGs) in a shallow or one-way manner (Zhang et al., 2019b; Wang et al., 2021b; Sun et al.,
050   2021; Baek et al., 2023) (e.g., from KG to text for retrieval-augmented methods like RAG (Lewis
051   et al., 2020), REALM Guu et al. (2020), and REPLUG (Shi et al., 2024)), which could hinder multi-
052   hop reasoning. Another approach is using an interaction token (Zhang et al., 2022; Yasunaga et al.,
053

      2022a) or a projector Tian et al. (2024) that depends on implicit exchanges between modalities.
055   Unlike previous efforts, we explore the alignment of the uni-modal embedding spaces using anchors
056   to better capture interconnected information and dependencies between textual and graph modalities.
057   This alignment may contribute to enhanced multi-hop reasoning capabilities, as the model can more
058   effectively traverse and reason across the aligned spaces.
059
      In this paper, we introduce Knowledge Graph and Biomedical Language Model Alignment
060
      (GRABLI), a novel pre-training approach that enhances LM with external knowledge by concur-
061   rently training a distinct KG encoder and aligning the representations of both the LM and the graph.
062   Specifically, as in Figure 1, given a (text, local KG) pair, a graph neural network (GNN) is utilized
063   to capture and encode the graph knowledge into node embeddings, while pre-trained LM is used
064   to obtain textual entity representations. Textual entity representations and concept node represen-
065   tations are used as anchors to align the two uni-modal embedding spaces. In this work, we seek to
066   answer the following research questions (RQs):
067
068       RQ1: Is the proposed cross-modal LM-KG alignment procedure with explicit alignment between
069            two representation spaces beneficial for biomedical NLP downstream tasks?
070       RQ2: What is the most effective graph representation for LM-KG alignment?
071
072       RQ3: Is the utilization of an external graph encoder more effective for cross-modal LM-KG align-
073
               ment or using graph linearization followed by LM encoding is sufficient?
074
      To comprehensively assess our model, we perform extensive experiments across several benchmarks
075
      for question answering and entity linking tasks. Initially, we pretrain several LMs with GRABLI,
076   leveraging the PubMed corpus and UMLS KG. Our experiments demonstrate that GRABLI out-
077   performs several biomedical language models, including BioLinkBERT (Yasunaga et al., 2022b)
078   and PubMedBERT (Gu et al., 2022). Specifically, PubMedBERT shows mean accuracy improve-
079   ments of 2.1%, 1.7%, and 6.2% on the PubMedQA, MedQA, and BioASQ benchmarks, respectively.
080   GRABLI significantly enhances the ability of LMs to generate distinguishable and informative rep-
081   resentations of biomedical concepts. In particular, BioLinkBERT with GRABLI pretraining per-
082   forms on par or slightly better than the task-specific SapBERT model, which is pre-trained on 12M
083   UMLS triples (4M concept nodes). Our research highlights that our cross-modal knowledge graph
084
      alignment, applied to both text and the knowledge graph, notably enhances language-knowledge
      representations after a small pre-trainning stage involving 1.5M sentences and 600K nodes only.
085
      The source code as well as pre-trained models will be released upon paper acceptance.

      Knowledge-Augmented Language Models One line of research on knowledge-enhanced
091   LMs (Liu et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2020; Ke et al., 2021; Mannion et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2022;
092   Moiseev et al., 2022) attempted to infuse factual information into LM input either by augmenting
093   natural language texts with relational triples or directly training on relational triples. Various meth-
094   ods (Zhang et al., 2019a; He et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021a; Peters et al., 2019; Rosset et al., 2020;
095   Yu et al., 2022; Kang et al., 2022) augment in-context entity representation with external knowledge
096   retrieved from KG. While such methods are able to improve quality on NLP tasks, they usually
097   perform unidirectional information fusion for improved LM embeddings using either a single LM
098   for both modalities or static KG node embeddings. Static node embeddings are unable to capture
099
      node semantics and only capture structural information, Transformer-based (Vaswani et al., 2017)
      LM’s architecture is inherently dense which confronts the sparse nature of KGs. Recently proposed
100
      GreaseLM (Zhang et al., 2022) and DRAGON (Yasunaga et al., 2022a) models improve LM rea-
101
      soning ability by introducing bidirectional cross-modal interaction text and grounded KG subgraph
102   interaction through specialized cross-modal LM token for enhanced question answering. However,
103   both models depend on implicit intermodal exchanges: the LM accesses KG information via a sin-
104   gle token initialized with pooled subgraph representation, while the graph encoder receives semantic
105   input through an interaction node initialized with pooled sentence representation. Meanwhile, these
106   modalities offer complementary representations of a single entity, capturing different contexts: sen-
107   tences for the LM and KG subgraphs for the graph encoder implying that the two uni-modal spaces
      can be aligned through entities serving as anchors in a unified embedding space.

      Recently, Tian et al. (2024) proposed a method that encodes subgraphs based on the entities present
109   in the question and options. In contrast with direct feeding of KG triples into LLMs Baek et al.
110   (2023), this approach utilizes a GNN, a cross-modality pooling module, and a domain projector
111   to send the encoded subgraphs to LLMs for inference, alongside the input text embeddings. This
112   represents an alternative prompt-based direction focusing on parameter-efficient fine-tuning.
113
114   Graph Representation Learning A series of translation-based node representation meth-
115   ods (Yang et al., 2015; Bordes et al., 2013; Trouillon et al., 2016; Kazemi & Poole, 2018; Sun
116   et al., 2019) models a relation triplet (graph edge) as a translation between head and tail nodes.
117   Initially, these methods learned static node embedding matrix as well as relation embeddings via
118   the link prediction task contrastively with knowledge triples present in a KG being positive sam-
119
      ples and non-present ones being negative samples. Experimental evaluation of translation-based
      methods for biomedical concept representation (Chang et al., 2020) indicates that these methods fall
120
      short of the LM-based approach due to a lack of essential semantical information present in texts.
121
      While translation-based methods model each edge individually, Message Passing (MP) (Gilmer
122   et al., 2017) graph neural networks obtain node embeddings by passing and aggregating messages
123   from multiple neighboring nodes at once. Various architectures under the MP framework mostly
124   differ in message aggregation function. For instance, GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) performs
125   mean-pooling over neighboring nodes, and Graph Attention Network (GAT) (Velickovic et al., 2018;
126   Brody et al., 2022) applies an attention-based aggregation. In our work, we adopt GAT for local KG
127   subgraph aggregation as it has proved itself an effective graph encoder for LM-KG interaction appli-
128   cations (Yasunaga et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Yasunaga et al., 2022a; Sakhovskiy et al., 2023;
129   2024). Another approach (Wang et al., 2021b; Salnikov et al., 2023) gets rid of additional memory
130
      footprint introduced by an external graph encoder by linearizing KG subgraphs into textual strings
      encoded with an LM.
131
132
      Cross-Modal Alignment Our research is inspired by recent advancements in aligning multiple
133
      uni-modal representations across various domains. Koh et al. (2023a;b) trains a small alignment
134   network to align images with their captions for cross-modal visual and textual generative tasks. Liu
135   et al. (2023) learns a lightweight projection to align visual and textual features for improved mul-
136   timodal image and language understanding. Ke et al. (2021) introduced a method to align entities
137   in text with their representations in graphs, enhancing graph summarization. Unlike prior work,
138   we perform explicit cross-modal alignment by directly minimizing distances between cross-modal
139   paired representations of a single biomedical concept.

143   Biomedical Knowledge Graph Formally, a Knowledge Graph can be defined as G = (V, E, R),
144   where V is the set of biomedical concepts, E ⊂ V × R × V is the set of labeled edges, and R
145   are possible relation types. In UMLS, one of the largest biomedical KGs, a node v ∈ V can be
146   represented with a set of k ≥ 1 distinct synonymous concept names Sv = {sv1 , sv2 , . . . , svk }. Thus,
147   a concept v ∈ V can be represented with two complementary modalities: (i) a textual modality
148   described by Sv , (ii) a KG modality expressed with local subgraph Gv ⊂ G centered around v.
149   Additionally, textual concept representations can be learnt from raw texts they are mentioned in.
150
151
      KG Subgraphs From KG perspective, a node v ∈ V can be described by the structure of its local
      KG subgraph, denoted as Gv = (Vv , Ev , R) ⊂ G, consisting of 1-hop neighbors subgraph centered
152
      around v: Ev = {(u, r, v) ∈ E}, Vv = {u | (u, r, v) ∈ Ev } ∪ v. Here, Gv can be viewed as a
153
      structural KG-induced context for a concept. Following Hamilton et al. (2017), we sample a subset
154   of up to 3 neighboring nodes to reduce computational cost of our model.
155
156   Alignment Intuition While graph encoder GN N can capture the hierarchy of in-domain concepts
157   along with other inter-concept relationships, textual encoder LM can provide deeper insights into
158   concept semantics learnt from raw texts. Conversely, LM may struggle to effectively learn the intri-
159   cate concept structure from texts alone. Thus, we assume that two embedded representations ḡv and
160   ēv are complementary representations encoding different features of the same concept v. Our goal is
161   to align these two uni-modal entity representations by enabling a mutual knowledge exchange. Since
      we assume ēv and ḡv to be complementary representations capturing different features of a concept

      Pretraining Data As pretraining data, we adopt the PubMed abstracts2 with biomedical entities
      recognized and normalized to the UMLS KG (version 2020AB) with the BERN2 tool (Sung et al.,
306
      2022). Given the substantial entity distribution imbalance in scientific abstracts, with entities like
307
      human, mice, and cancer being the most common ones, we address this issue as follows. To ensure
308   a more balanced dataset with diverse concepts, we sample only up to 10 sentences from PubMed ab-
309   stracts iteratively for each concept present in the UMLS. The resulting dataset has 1.67M sentences
310   with mentioned entities covering about 600K unique UMLS concepts.

      5.3   N ODE R EPRESENTATION STUDY
436
437   To answer the RQ 2 and RQ 3, we implement GRABLI with different graph representation meth-
438   ods. Under the GNN approach, we pre-train and evaluate GRABLI implementation with Graph-
439   SAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) instead of GAT which adopts mean-pooling instead of attention ag-
440   gregation across neighboring nodes.
441
442   Translation-based Node Representations In a series of graph representation methods (Yang
443
      et al., 2015; Bordes et al., 2013; Trouillon et al., 2016; Sun et al., 2019), a relation triplet (graph
      edge) (u, r, v) ∈ E is modeled as a relation-based translation of the head node v with a relational
444
      transformation fr : u ≈ fr (v). In our work, we adopt DistMult Yang et al. (2015) and TransE Bor-
445
      des et al. (2013) to represent in-context entity representation ēv as a transformation of concept name
446   embedding ḡu = fr (LM (su )) for su ∈ Su .
447
448   Textual Node Representations To assess the necessity of a graph encoder for capturing additional
449   information not accessible to the language encoder LM, we perform experiments using node embed-
450   dings that rely exclusively on textual concept names. In particular, we compute a node embedding ḡu
451   by mean pooling the textual output of a randomly chosen concept name su ∈ Su : ḡu = LM (su ).
452
453   Analysis: Node Representation choice Experiments with different node representation types are
454   summarized in Table 3. Based on the results, we can make the following observations. First, sim-
455   pler mean-pooling local subgraph aggregation under the GNN-based approach leads to a significant
456   performance drop of 6.4% and 4% on PubMedQA and BioASQ which highlights the importance of
457
      learning relative node importance scores: not all nodes are equally useful. Despite its simplicity,
      translation-based DistMult and TransE models show high performance in our alignment procedure
458
      in combination with LM. Similarly, a linearized graph encoded with LM seems to be the closest to
459
      GRABLI implementation with a GAT encoder. Thus, we conclude that LMs can serve as an effec-
460   tive graph representation method for text-attributed graph for LM-KG alignment. Finally, textual
461   node representations with no KG subgraph provided have shown poor performance indicating the
462   performance of additional local graph context for text-graph alignment.
463
464   5.4   A BLATION S TUDY
465
466   To justify modeling choices made in GRABLI model, we perform an extensive analysis in three
467   directions: (i) Training loss choice, (ii) Token-entity aggregation, (iii) graph encoder size. As token-
468   entity aggregation method we experiment with following set-ups: (i) weighted aggregation which
469
      attention weights to sum token embeddings of the last LM layer with no additional transformations;
      (ii) GAT aggregation adopts single GAT layer as described in Section 4.1; (iii) Transformer layer
470
      over tokens to correspond to the same entity only. For each ablation, we pre-train a separate GRABLI
471
      model with PubMed initialization and summarize evaluation results across 10 runs with different
472   random states on PubMedQA and BioASQ. The results are summarized in Table 4. A removal of
473   each of two losses drops the QA quality indicating that performing token-level LM-KG alignment
474   only leads to the degradation to LM’s language understanding. Lower/higher GAT layer count as
475   well as more complex token-entity aggregation functions do not lead to performance improvement.

      6     C ONCLUSION
478
479
      We propose GRABLI, a novel self-supervised pretraining method for Knowledge Graph (KG) and
480   Language Model (LM) alignment. Experimental results indicate that the alignment of biomedi-
481   cal LMs enhances their performance on both question answering and entity linking tasks in the
482   biomedical domain after a short pre-training on 1.7M sentences only. Comparison of various graph
483   representation methods has revealed the effectiveness of both LM-based approaches with linearized
484   graphs as well as sparse graph neural networks for capturing vital KG context absent in raw texts.
485   For future work, we aim to expand and apply our pre-training method to general domains and other
      LM architectures, such as decoder-only and encoder-decoder models.
