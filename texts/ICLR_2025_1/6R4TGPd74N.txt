009                                                  ABSTRACT
010
011            Large language model inference is both memory-intensive and time-consuming, often
012            requiring distributed algorithms to efficiently scale. Tensor parallelism (TP) is a
               common technique used in multi-gpu training and inference to partition computation
013
               across multiple devices, reducing memory load and computation time. However, such
014
               parallelism necessitates fast interconnects between the devices which has been a major
015            bottleneck and limits the gains obtained by scaling up the number of devices. We
016            introduce Ladder Residual, a simple architectural modification applicable to all residual-
017            based models that enable straightforward overlapping that effectively hides the latency
018            of communication. Our insight is that in addition to systems optimization, one can also
019            redesign the model architecture to decouple communication from computation. For a
020            Transformer model of 8B size, applying Ladder Residual to all its layers achieves 29%
021            end-to-end wall clock speed up at inference time with TP world size of 8 devices. We re-
022
               fer to such model as the Ladder Transformer. We train a 1B and 3B Ladder Transformer
               from scratch and observe comparable performance to a standard dense transformer
023
               baseline. We also conduct adaptation experiments for our approach and show that it’s
024
               possible to adapt parts of the Llama-3.1 8B model with minimal accuracy degradation
025            by only retraining for 3B tokens. To further push the performance frontier, we
026            propose another architectural modification which drops communications in the model,
027            unlocking fast LLM inference in settings devoid of NVLink or other fast interconnects.

029   1    INTRODUCTION
030
031   With the rapid scaling of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Smith et al., 2022; Workshop et al., 2023;
032
      Brown, 2020), the compute and memory requirements for training and inference have grown significantly.
      Tensor parallelism (TP) (Shoeybi et al., 2020) is a widely used model parallelism technique that partitions
033
      the weights and intermediate activations across multiple GPUs. In contrast to pipeline parallelism
034   (Narayanan et al., 2021) and data parallelism (Li et al., 2020), which rely on processing independent batches
035   of user requests on each device, tensor-parallel inference enables multiple devices to cooperate to process
036   a single batch of user requests at a time, therefore in theory allowing infinite scaling given a sufficient
037   number of processors. However, TP requires synchronizing the partitioned intermediate activations across
038   the GPUs. This synchronization is a blocking AllReduce operation on the activations across the GPUs
039   and is therefore bottlenecked by the network communication latency. Even for GPUs connected via fast
040   interconnects (like NVLink (NVIDIA Corporation, 2024)), the communication costs can account for 40%
041   of the latency at inference time when running llama 3 8B with batch size 4 and TP world size of 8.
042   Past works have attempted to overlap the communication latency of TP by overlapping computation
043   and communication. Chang et al. (2024) write fused kernels for AllGather followed by matmul and
044   matmul followed by ReduceScatter. They break down matmuls into tiles and try to hide the latency
045   of communicating a matmul tile with the computation of subsequent tiles. Jangda et al. (2022) propose
046
      CoCoNet, a domain-specific language to express distributed machine learning workloads. They propose
      to generate efficient GPU kernels for computation and communication using a custom compiler for
047
      the DSL. This approach has limited applicability with existing frameworks like PyTorch (Paszke et al.,
048   2019) and JAX (Frostig et al., 2018) since the user needs to be well-acquainted with the DSL to generate
049   efficient GPU kernels. Moreover, with the breakneck pace of accelerator and interconnect changes, these
050   low-level systems optimizations require a rewrite for every new generation of hardware. However, there
051   is a fundamental limit to how much communication latency can be reduced on such works which do not
052   change the underlying model architecture. Instead of pure hardware optimizations (e.g., larger NVLink
053   domain connecting 36 or 72 Blackwell GPUs) or pure low-level software optimization (e.g., rewriting
      all matmuls to overlap with communication), we explore model architectural changes that would enable

055   to apply in practice using a high level machine learning framework like PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019)
056   or JAX (Frostig et al., 2018) without writing any low-level device code.

      Communication is blocking because there is a sequential structure between communication and
080   computation in existing model designs: we wait for communication in order to prepare the correct input
081   for the next computation. In the prevalent residual-based architectures, the computation flow can be written
082   as xi+1 =hi+1(xi)+xi, where xi is the residual stream after layer i and hi+1 is the computation at layer
083   i+1. Notice that the communication of xi needs to be done before executing hi+1 if hi is partitioned
084   across devices. Liu et al. (2023b) found that activation changes slowly in Transformer, as the norm of
085   each update hi+1(xi) is small compared to the residual. Based on this observation, we hypothesize that
086   maintaining the regular residual connection is enough to restrict the representation shift, and we can feed
087
      each module a “stale” input to create overlapping opportunities.
088   We propose Ladder Residual, a simple change where we reroute the residual stream after layer i − 1
089   (instead of layer i) as input to layer i+1: xi+1 = hi+1(xi−1)+xi. With this design, the computation of
090   hi+1 is decoupled from the communication of xi, enabling straightforward overlapping to hide the latency
091   of communication. Figure 1 shows how Ladder Residual can be applied to the Transformer architecture. At
      inference time with TP world size of 8 (i.e., across 8 devices), running a Transformer with Ladder Residual
092
      can be around 30% faster over the standard Transformer. In Table 1, we provide the inference speedup
093
      on Transformers of different sizes. The proposed Ladder Residual method is also fully compatible with
094   Sequence Parallelism (SP) (Korthikanti et al., 2022) which helps in reducing the activation memory when
095   training large models with long context lengths. Our method obtains 5-7% training speedup when training
096   an 8B model with 8k context length on 64 H100s with 3D parallelism across the GPUs (Tensor Parallel,
097   Sequence Parallel and Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) (Zhao et al., 2023; Rajbhandari et al., 2020)),
098   but we decide to focus on the inference speed ups in this paper since training with just FSDP and SP and
099   is usually faster because weights can be pre-fetched and gradient synchronization using ReduceScatter
100   can be overlapped in FSDP making communications in FSDP non-blocking.
101   Because of the widespread use of Transformer (Vaswani, 2017) based Language models, we focus
102   on applying Ladder Residual on the Transformers in this paper, and we call the resulting model as
103   Ladder Transformer. We conduct experiments under two scenarios to verify if we can maintain the same
104   performance after applying Ladder Residual to a Transformer-based model:
105   • Pretraining from scratch: We train a 1B and a 3B parameter Ladder Transformer model with 100B
106     tokens on the FineWeb-edu dataset (Lozhkov et al., 2024) and compared it with the standard transformer
107     of the same size trained on the same amount of tokens. We find that the Ladder Transformer matches
        the performance of the standard Transformer model.

132
133   We provide some background of Tensor Parallelism (TP), where the communication happens in TP, and
134   how communication is a major latency bottleneck in standard Transformer.
135
      2.1   TENSOR PARALLELISM
136
137   Tensor parallelism (Shoeybi et al., 2020) is a commonly used technique in distributed training/inference.
138   It partitions weights and activations across devices and performs partial computations on each device.
139
      Consider a sequence of 2 linear layers with weight matrices A and B and input activation X that is running
140
      on 2 GPUs (TP world size     of  2), we split A along the output dimension into [A1,A2], and split B along
                                   B
141   the input dimension into 1 . Then the output of the sequence of the 2 linear layers can be computed
                                   B2
142
      as (XA)B =(XA1)B1 +(XA2)B2 and we effectively partition the computation on the two devices. The
143
      final summation requires an AllReduce operation to aggregate the partial sums on each device, which
144
      introduces communication overhead. The AllReduce overhead increases with increasing message size
145   and increasing number of devices participating in the AllReduce. A transformer layer consists of an
146   attention block and an MLP block: both can be considered as a sequence of two matrix multiplications and
147   therefore fit into the tensor parallelism paradigm described above. Thus each transformer layer contains
148   2 AllReduce operations: one for attention and another for MLP. Denoting the input to the ith block as
149   xi−1, the transformer can be viewed as the following sequential structure:
150
151
                                              x∗i =hi(xi−1)
152
153
                                               xi =AllReduce(x∗i )+xi−1
                                             ∗                                                                (1)
154                                         xi+1 =hi+1(xi)
155                                         xi+1 =AllReduce(x∗i+1)+xi
156
157   where the ∗ denotes a partial-sum that requires an AllReduce for the full output replicated across all
158   the GPUs. Note that the AllReduce operation is the identity function for a model running on 1 GPU.
159   A Transformer with N layers needs to perform the AllReduce 2N times and this can account for 40%
160   of the inference latency for a 8B model using TP world size of 8, even with NVLink interconnect. For
161   communication without NVLink support or inter-node communication, their latency can account for around
      85% of the end-to-end latency. Modern nodes contain GPUs connected via NVLink but usually have

      no more than 8 GPUs per node, due to limited PCIe lanes or power and cooling constraints in datacenters.
163   There is a steep falloff in communication bandwidth and latency when communication happens outside
164   a node either over InfiniBand or Ethernet thus making scaling TP practically infeasible outside a node.

      3.1   ARCHITECTURE DESCRIPTION
191
192   In Equation 1, the AllReduce operation is blocking the next block from execution since hi+1 requires
193
      xi as the input. Ladder Residual mitigates this problem by routing the xi−1 to block hi+1, effectively
      making the input of hi+1 independent of the output of the AllReduce, therefore allowing overlapping
194
      AllReduce(x∗i ) with hi+1.
195
196   Specifically, we change the computation flow of Equation 1 into:
197                                          x∗i =hi(xi−2)
198
                                              xi =AllReduce(x∗i )+xi−1
199                                         ∗                                                                (2)
                                           xi+1 =hi+1(xi−1)
200
201                                        xi+1 =AllReduce(x∗i+1)+xi
202   Note that the residual stream of each block still takes the output from the previous module as usual, this
203   ensures the module i is still able to process output from all previous i−2 modules.
204
205   3.2   INFERENCE IMPLEMENTATION
206   Ladder Residual Implementation: We present the Ladder Transformer’s layer’s pseudo-code in Algorithm
207   1. To implement the Ladder Transformer, following the description of Equation 2 we call AsyncAllReduce
208   for the Attention’s output. This returns a handle that can be used to synchronize the output to ensure that
209   the AsyncAllReduce has finished. It should be noted that NCCL collectives in PyTorch always run on a
210   different CUDA stream than the default compute stream used by PyTorch thus making them asynchronous.
211   As soon as the AsyncAllReduce is called, we synchronize by calling wait on the previous layer’s MLP’s
212   output and subsequently the CPU launches the kernels for MLPNorm and then MLP on the default compute
213
      stream and eventually calling the AsyncAllReduce for MLP. The handles for these NCCL operations
      are then passed onto the next layer which uses them for synchronization when needed.
214
215   Alignment with Real-World Scenarios: To evaluate the practical benefits of Ladder Residual, we
      integrated this mechanism into a standard Llama-like Transformer. Building upon gpt-fast (PyTorch

      Labs, 2024), we partition the weights of the attention and feedforward modules for tensor parallelism
217   to optimize inference speed. We use CUDA graphs (Coleman, 2020) to generate static computation graphs
218   for both the prefill and decode phase to reduce CPU kernel launch overheads which can be a big bottleneck
219   especially during the decode phase of Transformer inference. Additionally, we use FlashAttention (Dao,
220   2023) during prefill and FlashDecode (Dao et al., 2023) during the autoregressive decode phase of
221   generation for accelerating inference.

223   3.3     FASTER INFERENCE WITH LADDER RESIDUAL
224
      In this section, we benchmark Ladder Residual under various scenarios and show that across various model
225
      sizes, batch sizes, and varying TP world size, Ladder Residual can obtain considerable speedup over the
226   standard Transformer. We also compare against the parallel attention and mlp design in Chowdhery et al.
227   (2022); Wang & Komatsuzaki (2021), which effectively cuts half of the communication. We also consider
228   a scenario without NVLink, and show that our method can obtain more than 50% of the speedup when
229   high-speed interconnect is not available.
230
231   3.3.1    SETUP
232
233   We benchmark several algorithmic variants to evaluate their performance in large-scale language model
234
      inference. The candidates include:
235   • Standard Transformer: The standard transformer implementation.
236   • Parallel Attention and Mlp: Following the PaLM parallelization strategy Chowdhery et al. (2022); Wang
237     & Komatsuzaki (2021), we fuse the weights of the query, key, value, gate, and up projections into a
238     single matrix. The outputs are then split, and the attention and swiglu are performed in parallel. This
239     effectively cut half of the communication, with the extra benefit of being able to fuse attention and
240     mlp together. PaLM has shown its feasibility when trained from scratch, and we also experiment with
241     it in subsection 4.1.
242   • Ladder Residual: The architectural optimization we propose to overlap computation with communication
243     required for Tensor Parallelism.
244   • Communication-Free Upper Bound: An upper bound that removes all communication operations in
245     the model to represent the theoretical maximum speedup achievable.
246
      Note that different Transformer-based models usually have slightly different designs. The Transformer
247
      we used followed Llama-3’s design and we designed the width and depth to meet specific parameter
248   counts. The benchmarking results here won’t be too different when moving to a different design since
249   the communication patterns are mostly the same across various transformer variants.
250
      To simulate realistic inference scenarios, we select multiple experimental configurations. Users typically set
251
      prompt lengths not exceeding 1024 tokens and generate short outputs upto 32 new tokens while using an
252   LLM as a chat assistant. Therefore, we set the prompt length to 1024 and the generation length to 32 tokens.
253
      We vary the tensor parallel world sizes among 1, 2, 4, and 8, and batch fsizes among 1, 4, 16, and 64
254
      to understand performance under different generation settings. All models are benchmarked on 1, 2, 4
255
      or 8 NVIDIA H100 GPUs.
256
257
      To evaluate the impact of hardware communication capabilities, we adjust the NVLink settings using en-
      vironment variables. We enable NVIDIA SHARP (Graham et al., 2016) by setting NCCL NVLS ENABLE=1
258
      and disable NVLINK communication by setting NCCL P2P DISABLE=1. This allows us to assess the
259
      performance of different algorithms in varying communication environments. All of results has been
260   re-run for 3 times to ensure the results is stable and confident.

      4     EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
364   We empirically verify our assumption that applying Ladder Residual does not hurt the performance. We
365   show that Ladder Residual can be either used when training from scratch, or be applied to a pre-trained
366   model with hybrid adaptation, and in both cases, the performance is on par with the original architecture.
367
368
      4.1     TRAINING FROM SCRATCH
369   We train a 1B and 3B Ladder Transformer from scratch and compare its performance with an equally
370   sized standard Transformer model. All our models are trained on 100B tokens of FineWeb-edu dataset
371   (HuggingFaceFW, 2024) using the StarCoder tokenizer (Li et al., 2023b). We also compare our model
372   with the Parallel Attention/MLP architecture (Chowdhery et al., 2022; Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021)
      which parallelizes the computation of the attention and the MLP module. This effectively reduces the
373
      communication cost by 50% for the tensor parallel all-reduce in both the forward and backward computation.
374
375   4.1.1     EXPERIMENTAL DETAILS
376
377   We use DDP (Distributed Data Parallel) (Li et al., 2020) to train the 1B and HSDP (Hybrid Sharded Data
      Parallel) (Zhao et al., 2023; Rajbhandari et al., 2020) to train the 3B models. For HSDP, we shard the model


                                                                 7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      within 1 node (equipped with 8x H100 GPUs) and replicate the model outside the node. We use mixed preci-
379   sion training (Micikevicius et al., 2018) in BF16 (Kalamkar et al., 2019) with gradient accumulation and gra-
380   dient all-reduce/reduce-scatter in FP32 for training stability. We train all our models with 2048 context length
381   with a batch size of 4M tokens in a batch. The models are trained with cosine scheduler with a warmup of
382   8B tokens to a peak learning rate of 3×10−4. The learning rate is then decayed over 92B tokens to 3×10−5.
383   We use EleutherAI’s LM eval harness (Gao et al., 2024) to evaluate all our models on ARC (Clark et al.,

      5     ELIMINATING COMMUNICATION WITH DESYNCED RESIDUAL
450   We find that in settings where there is no NVLink connection, the latency for AllReduce dominates and its
451   not possible to hide the latency of the AllReduce completely with the computation of another block in the
452   Ladder Residual method. We thus propose an alternative method, Desynced Reisudal, that entirely drops
453
      the AllReduce communication and lets each device process its own activations independently. We call
      this architecture Desynced Residual because dropping the communication leads to desynchonization of the
454
      residual stream in the model which is re-synchronized at the next AllReduce operation. We experiment
455
      with 2 different Desync configurations: Desync Residual-2x and Desync Residual-4x. Desync Residual-nx
456   means that we only retain the last AllReduce operation in a group of n sequential AllReduce operations
457   i.e (n − 1) AllReduce operations are dropped from the model. While its possible to use an arbitrary
458   communication pattern, we find that dropping AllReduce for Attention yields a model with lower Wikitext
459   perplexity than dropping AllReduce for MLP while offering a good tradeoff between inference speed
460   and model accuracy. We compare the latency improvement of Desyncd-nx and Ladder Residual in Table 6.
461   While Desync-Residaul-2x performs slightly worse than Ladder Residaul, Desynced-Residaul-4x is able to
462   achieve a larger gain, especially under the no NVLink setup (39% for Desynced-Residual-4x compare with
463
      23% for Ladder Residual). The promising speedup shows the potential of Desync Residual as an alternative
      architecture to be further explored espeically in cases where communictaion can be very expensive.

      5.1      EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
466
467
      We run both 1B and 3B pretraining from scratch experiments for Desync Residual-2x and Desync
      Residual-4x models in the same setting as described in subsection 4.1. We find that the Desync Residual-4x
468
      model performs better than Desync Residual-2x model on 1B scale on Wikitext perplexity while being
469
      slightly worse on accuracy. While on the 3B scale, the Desync Residual-4x model is better on both
470   perplexity and average accuracy. This is quite surprising and demonstrates that its possible to train models
471   using the Desync Residual in a way to significantly reduce communication. For instance, the Desync
472   Residual-4x drops 75% communications with almost no drop in model performance.
473
      In our benchmarking results, we find that at a large batch sizes (64 in our experiments) with a TP world
474   size of 8, it is possible to achieve around 30% improvement in inference throughput over NVLink and
475   around 40% improvement without NVLink using the Desync Residual-4x architecture. We also observe
476   a significant improvement in the first token latency (prefill latency) using the Desync Residual-4x model
477   over Ladder Residual and Desync Residual-2x. The first token latency is especially important in scenarios
478   where the LLM might be used as a classifier for example or in cases where multiple calls to the LLM need
479   to be issues (agentic workflows for example). By manipulating where to communicate, Desync Residual
480   allows designing networks that can drop arbitrary percent of the communication, which allows flexible
481
      architecture design giving different needs. For setup with very slow communication (for example wireless
      communication), Desync Residual enables sacrificing a small amount of performance in exchange for speed.

509   more fine-grained operations in order to find computations with no dependency to overlap. Our work
510   doesn’t rely on such decompositions and therefore doesn’t require Sequence Parallelism to handle the

517
      Efficiency-aware architecture improvements Prior works have explored various alternative designs for
518   Transformer, for example parallel attention and mlp (Chowdhery et al., 2022; Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021),
519   linear attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020), Grouped Query Attention (Ainslie et al., 2023), Cross-Layer
520   Attention (Brandon et al., 2024) to improve the training and inference efficiency. Some of these variants
521   are more widely adopted than others, due to the degree of impact they have on performance and efficiency.
522   Past works have also considered adapting an existing checkpoint to these efficient variants. Ainslie et al.
523   (2023) extracted grouped-query attention from a multi-head attention model, and Wang et al. (2024)
524   converted a Llama model to a Mamba model. It retrained on 50B tokens to close the performance gap
525
      where our adaptation is much lighter (3B tokens), showing that the representation shift introduced by
      Ladder Residual is easier to recover. Wang et al. (2024) considered converting a Llama model to a Mamba
526
      model and used distillation to retrain the converted model. Such training paradigms that specifically tunes
527   the model to align the original model could further improve the Ladder Residual based models.

529   7    CONCLUSION
530
531   We introduce Ladder Residual and Desync Residual, architectural modifications that allow overlapping
532
      communication with computation or dropping it entirely when running Tensor Parallelism. When applying
      Ladder Residual to Llama-3.1 8B Instruct, we only need lightweight retraining to reach the same level of per-
533
      formance with the original model, showing its potential to be a plug-in for any pretrained Transformer and
534   we are able to obtain 14.5% end-to-end wall clock speed up at the inference time with TP world size of 8 and
535   batch size of 1. We also trained a 1B and 3B Ladder Transformer from scratch, and find that they are compa-
536   rable to the standard Transformer of the same size while achieving 25% speedup. On the other hand, Desync
537   Residual provides flexible removal of communication when designing an architecture to train from scratch,
538   which can be helpful in the case where the communication is too costly to overlap. Given that such a simple
539   architectural change can obviate the need for expensive interconnects while maintaining model quality, we
      hope that our methods will inspire even closer co-design between model architecture and inference systems.
