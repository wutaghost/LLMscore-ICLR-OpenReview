009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning machine representations with human understanding is key to improving
012            interpretability of machine learning (ML) models. When classifying a new image,
013            humans often explain their decisions by decomposing the image into concepts and
014            pointing to corresponding regions in familiar images. Current ML explanation
015            techniques typically either trace decision-making processes to reference prototypes,
016
               generate attribution maps highlighting feature importance, or incorporate inter-
               mediate bottlenecks designed to align with human-interpretable concepts. The
017
               proposed method, named COMiX, classifies an image by decomposing it into
018
               regions based on learned concepts and tracing each region to corresponding ones
019            in images from the training dataset, assuring that explanations fully represent the
020            actual decision-making process. We dissect the test image into selected internal
021            representations of a neural network to derive prototypical parts (primitives) and
022            match them with the corresponding primitives derived from the training data. In
023            a series of qualitative and quantitative experiments, we theoretically prove and
024            demonstrate that our method, in contrast to post hoc analysis, provides fidelity
025            of explanations and shows that the efficiency is competitive with other inherently
026            interpretable architectures. Notably, it shows substantial improvements in fidelity
027
               and sparsity metrics, including 48.82% improvement in the C-insertion score on
               the ImageNet dataset over the best state-of-the-art baseline.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Neural networks (NNs) have been successfully applied across various computer vision tasks, achieving
033   notable results in safety-critical domains such as medical image classification (Huang et al., 2023),
034   autonomous driving (Geiger et al., 2012), and robotics (Robinson et al., 2023) amongst others.
035   However, explaining their decisions remains an ongoing research challenge (Samek et al., 2021).
036   The two key factors in interpreting neural network decisions are: (1) representing the reasoning behind
037   the prediction in human-understandable terms and (2) ensuring that the explanations accurately reflect
038   the underlying computations of the neural network. Beyond their face value, such interpretations can
039   also help meet the legal requirements. The recently adopted EU AI Act (EUA, 2024) mandates that
040   individuals should fully understand high-risk AI systems, enabling them to monitor these systems
041   effectively, specifically requiring the ability to ‘correctly interpret the high-risk AI system’s output’.
042   Most existing explanation methods address this problem using attribution-based techniques, which
043   highlight the parts of the input that contribute to a particular decision (Selvaraju et al., 2017; Chattopad-
044   hay et al., 2018; Omeiza et al., 2019). However, these methods lack reliability as their explanations
045   have been shown to be sensitive to factors which do not contribute to the model prediction (Kin-
046   dermans et al., 2019). To address this issue, concept- and prototype-based explanations have been
047   proposed, which aim to link the decision to examples that illustrate the underlying concepts (Kim et al.
048   (2018); Ghorbani et al. (2019); Koh et al. (2020); Tan et al. (2024)). Nevertheless, such explanations
049   have also been demonstrated to be insufficient for human understanding as they do not point to the
050
      reasons why the input is linked to the associated concept prototypes (Kim et al., 2016).
051   Studies of human understanding show that concepts can be decomposed into smaller constituents
052   representing particular properties. These subconcepts can then be exemplified by the individual
053   instances called prototypes (Murphy, 2004). In this work, we propose a concept-based interpretable-
      by-design method, which highlights common class-defining features between the input image and the

087

089

097            We present the proof in Section 3.3

100

      2   R ELATED WORK
109
110
      Explainable and interpretable AI. The early methods for neural network post hoc explanations,
111   such as the work by Simonyan et al. (2013) and Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al. (2017)), were grounded
112   in the idea of differentiating through the model. Other important backpropagation-based models
113   include Bach et al. (2015); Sundararajan et al. (2017). Perturbation-based methods, such as Ribeiro
114   et al. (2016); Lundberg & Lee (2017); Petsiuk et al. (2018); Štrumbelj & Kononenko (2014), use
115   perturbations to figure out input features’ contributions. However, such a line of research is limited
116   in its ability to capture the true inner workings of the original model (Rudin, 2019). To address
117   this concern, a number of by-design interpretable machine learning models have been proposed,
118   presenting the interpretable architectures (Böhle et al. (2022; 2024)), concept-bottleneck models (Koh
119   et al. (2020); Shin et al. (2023); Schrodi et al. (2024); Losch et al. (2019); Qian et al. (2022)) and
120
      prototype-based interpretations (Chen et al., 2019; Donnelly et al., 2022; Angelov & Soares, 2020).
      Fel et al. (2023b) tackles a similar problem to the one in this paper: first, automatic extraction of
121
      concepts and then highlighting the similarities between such concepts and the testing image. However,
122
      the main conceptual difference between Fel et al. (2023b) and COMiX is that this work aims for
123   by-design explanation of the decision-making while Fel et al. (2023b) addresses the problem of post
124   hoc analysis. In contrast to these works, the described method is both inherently interpretable and
125   offers interpretation through the training data.
126
127   Concept discovery. Closely related to the studied problem interpretation is the challenge of concept
128   discovery, motivated by the neuroscience studies in human reasoning (Bruner et al., 1957). Kim
129   et al. (2018) proposed a paradigm of concept activation vectors. Another study by Ghorbani et al.
130   (2019) proposes extracting visual concepts through segmentation. Concept bottleneck models (Koh
131   et al., 2020; Shang et al., 2024; Sheth & Ebrahimi Kahou, 2023; Havasi et al., 2022) introduce
132
      constraints into training so that the classifier is limited to using human-understandable features.
      Similar to these models, COMiX also leverages concept discovery, where the concepts are individual
133
      interpretable classifier features. On the contrary, we do not constrain the classifier to learn the
134
      human-understandable features and instead project the learned features into human-understandable
135   space. In addition, COMiX traces these concepts back to the training data and provides inherent,
136   by-design, interpretations, which have not, to the best of our knowledge, provided in the existing
137   literature. ProtoPNet method (Chen et al. (2019)) is a well-known baseline for concept discovery
138   through patch prototypes. It has been further developed in a number of works such as Donnelly
139   et al. (2022); Ma et al. (2024); Sacha et al. (2023); Hase et al. (2019). Tan et al. (2024) propose to
140   combine post hoc explainability methods with transparent concept-based reasoning. Bontempelli
141   et al. (2022) analyses the problem of attainment of confounders within ProtoPNet and addresses it
142   with human-in-the-loop model debugging.
143
144   Evaluation of interpretability. Hesse et al. (2023) propose a synthetic dataset and benchmark for
145
      part-level analysis of explainable models for image classification. Fel et al. (2023a) propose a set of
      metrics for explainable AI which assesses the quality of attribution-based explanations. They use
146
      the Insertion and Deletion metrics from Petsiuk et al. (2018) for attribution assessment. Important
147
      desiderata for concept extraction include sparsity of the outputs: not only do these outputs need to
148   faithfully reflect the decision-making, but only a handful of concepts need to be activated for every
149   testing image. To measure this ability, we leverage the metrics from the sparsity literature. Diao
150   et al. (2022) propose a new PQ index metric, which measures the representation sparsity. One of the
151   aspects, however, is that most of these metrics target the problem of attribution-based explanations.
152   In our case, however, we combine concept-based and inherent attribution-based explanations, which
153   allows us to evaluate the results using both C-insertion and C-deletion as well as the sparsity of
154   concepts.

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES : B-Cos ARCHITECTURE
191
192   A B-cos encoder generates a reliable explanation of its computation. B-cos networks are neural
193   networks in which all the linear layers (along with activations) are replaced by B-cos layers. For
194   more details on the formulation and training of these networks, we refer the reader to Böhle et al.
195   (2022; 2024) and to Appendix A. Operation of a B-cos layer at a node for an input x and weights w
196   leading to the node is given by
                                                                B
197                   B-cos(x; w) = ∥x∥ · ∥w∥ · |cos(∠(x, w))| · sign (cos(∠(x, w))) ,                (1)
198
199   where B is a hyper-parameter that influences the extent to which alignment between x and w
200
      contributes to the magnitude of the output. Replacing linear layers with B-cos layers removes the
      need for other explicit non-linearity while training the network. Given an input, B-cos layer becomes
201
      a linear layer followed by a scalar multiplication (the cosine score: Equation 1). As each layer
202
      becomes a linear operation, the neural network collapses into a single linear transform that faithfully
203   summarises the entire model computations. Moreover, the B-cos layers introduce alignment pressure
204   on their weights during optimization. For the output of a node to be high, the input must align well
205   with the node’s incoming parameters, indicated by a high value of cos(∠(x, w)). In short, we choose
206   the B-cos network for two reasons: (a) B-cos has an input-dependent non-linearity which collapses
207   the encoder computations into a linear transformation for a test sample, and (b) the collapsed linear
208   operation (i.e., matrix) is aligned to the input sample when the output is high.
209   Given an input image x, (L + 1)-layer B-cos network collapses into a linear layer. This matrix is
210   aligned with the input if the output is high. The (L + 1)-layer transformation can be presented as a
211   shortcut representation

      4.1   DATASETS
364
365   Datasets We train and evaluate the presented model on a number of commonly-used computer
366   vision datasets. CIFAR-10&100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) contain generic natural images from 10 and
367   100 diverse classes respectively. CUB-200-2011 (Welinder et al., 2010) is a commonly used dataset
368   for evaluating interpretable vision models, which contains 200 fine-grained classes of birds. Stanford
369   cars dataset (Krause et al., 2013) contains 196 classes of cars. Oxford-IIIT Pets (Parkhi et al., 2012)
370   contains a fine-grained collection of images of 37 classes of cats and dogs. Finally, we present the
371
      results on ImageNet (ILSVRC 2012) (Russakovsky et al., 2015) which has 1000 diverse classes.
372   Baselines We compare COMiX to the following well-known baselines: (1) Standard architectures
373
      such as ResNet He et al. (2016), DenseNet Huang et al. (2017) and ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2021)
      (2) the B-Cos counterparts of the aforementioned architectures (Böhle et al., 2022; 2024) and (3)
374
      a number of interpetable and explainable ML methods including ProtoPNet (Chen et al., 2019),
375

507   4.4    PARAMETER SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS
508
509   In Figure 6 we compare the performance of the method depending upon the number K of nearest
510
      neighbours as well as the number of features M used for the prediction. This analysis shows that there
      is an inherent trade-off between the performance and the conciseness of explanation. In Appendix
511

512
      setting, instead of performing the predictions feature-by-feature as in Algorithm 1, Step 4, we show
513   the prediction performance using ℓ2 distances over the whole set of CDFs as common in the current
514   literature. This creates another trade-off: while it may increase the performance, the downside of
515   such an alternative approach would be that the explanations, and the decisions, would not follow
516   directly from the given features but from their combination.

519   5     C ONCLUSION
520
521
      COMiX demonstrates a novel form of interpretable machine learning, which performs decision-
522   making through the similarity of the concepts within the test image to the corresponding concepts
523   in the training set. We demonstrate that this allows both factual (Why did the model predict this
524   class?) and counterfactual analysis (How would the model explain the predictions if the alternative
525   class was predicted?). The experimental results show both a competitive accuracy of the method
526   and demonstrate, empirically and theoretically, that the method has favourable properties of fidelity,
527   necessity, sufficiency, and sparsity. Surprisingly, it also demonstrates impressive finetuning-free
528   k-NN generalisation to new datasets.
529   It is also worth noting that the training dataset D from Algorithm 1 can be, without any changes in
530   the method, be replaced with a trusted dataset, which can contain a private collection of data which
531   the predictor could relate the testing image to. It can be motivated by both lack of access to training
532   images or by lack of trust in the training data due to an inherent noise and can be especially useful in
533   safety-critical domains such as medical imagery.
534   While the current work only focuses on image classification, future work can expand this method to
535   the segmentation scenarios. To reflect upon this, we demonstrate, in Appendix G, the potential for
536   concept-based segmentation using COMiX. Such concept-based segmentation can enable human-
537   in-the-loop decision-making: a human can change class-defining features in the model by selecting
538   the corresponding segments. One can see the potential of the method to detect manipulated imaging
539   and adversarial attacks by highlighting the areas of forgery and comparing them with training-set
      examples. We outline the limitations and broader impacts in Appendices H and I respectively.

      A     B- COS N ETWORKS
811
812
      B-cos networks offer a novel approach to improving the interpretability of deep learning models
813
      by ensuring that input features align with the model’s weights throughout training. This innovation
814   arises from the realization that although deep neural networks excel in performance across various
815   tasks, their internal workings remain largely opaque and hard to interpret. Typically, deep models
816   rely on linear transformations coupled with non-linear activations, a design that contributes to their
817   "black box" nature.
818
      In contrast, B-cos networks replace traditional linear transformations with the B-cos transformation,
819
      which promotes alignment between inputs and weights. This transformation is defined as
820
821                               B-cos(x; w) = ∥w∥∥x∥ cosB (θ) · sign(cos(θ)),                         (13)
822
823   where θ denotes the angle between the input vector x and the weight vector w, and B is a hyperparam-
824
      eter that amplifies the model’s sensitivity to alignment. This transformation shifts the model’s focus
      from merely achieving high performance to fostering interpretability by emphasizing the relationship
825
      between the input data and model features.
826
827   The training of B-cos networks integrates this alignment directly into the optimization process. By
828   applying alignment pressure during weight adjustment, B-cos networks encourage the model to align
829   its weights with the most relevant input features, making this alignment a key objective rather than a
830
      byproduct of training, which is a departure from conventional methods focused solely on minimizing
      prediction error.
831
832   The integration of B-cos transformations into existing architectures is seamless since they can serve
833   as direct replacements for typical linear layers. This compatibility enables the application of B-cos
834   networks to a broad array of architectures such as VGG, ResNet, InceptionNet, DenseNet, and Vision
835   Transformers (ViTs) Böhle et al. (2024), without significant changes to their core structure. Empirical
836
      results demonstrate that this transformation maintains competitive performance on standard datasets
      like ImageNet while enhancing model interpretability.
837
838   During inference, a key advantage of B-cos networks becomes apparent. The sequence of B-cos
839   transformations throughout the network simplifies into a single linear operation, as the successive
840   alignment-focused layers collapse into a single transformation. Mathematically, this is expressed as:
841
                                       W1→L (x) = W1 × W2 × . . . × WL ,                                (14)
842
843   where W1→L (x) represents the effective weight matrix over all L layers, and W1 , W2 , . . . , WL are
844   the weight matrices of the individual B-cos layers. This reduces the network’s computation at test
845   time to:
846                                           y = W1→L (x) · x,                                        (15)
847
848   where y is the output. This reduction to a single matrix-vector multiplication significantly improves
849   both computational efficiency and transparency, offering a clear view of how input features affect the
850
      output. The network’s behavior, represented by θ1→L in the main text, becomes fully interpretable,
      as emphasized in Böhle et al. (2022).

       H    B ROADER IMPACTS
1189
1190
       While some of the existing post hoc explanation methods can explain the decision making, they do
1191   not follow the original decision making process Rudin (2019). This, therefore, cannot satisfy the
1192   current legal, ethical and policy-making needs. In contrast, by-design methods provide explanations
1193   which are causally linked with the decision making process. Such alternative is especially important
1194   for safety-critical applications, such as autonomous driving, robotics, medical imagery.
1195
       As finetuning-free learning was not considered the primary goal of this work, it was merely docu-
1196
       mented and not investigated further. It remains to be seen as to why COMiX results in surprisingly
1197   good finetuning-free performance.
