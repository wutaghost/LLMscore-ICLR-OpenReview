011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent studies highlight that the advancements in Large Language Models
014            (LLMs) have opened up exciting possibilities for scientific discovery, where
015            LLMs can assist researchers in generating novel hypotheses and ideas. In this
016
               work, we draw inspiration from Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and
               make the first effort to formalize the concept of zero-shot in-context adversarial
017
               learning and implement it through multi-LLM-agent interactions to improve the
018
               research ideation process. Our approach takes the best of two worlds: (1) by mak-
019            ing in-context learning adversarial, the utilization of an LLM’s vast parametric
020            knowledge can be optimized; and (2) by keeping adversarial learning in context,
021            we eliminate the need for bi-level optimization through additional model train-
022            ing. To evaluate the quality of the open-ended generation produced by LLMs,
023            we develop a relative quality ranking metric, designed to serve as a proxy for hu-
024            man evaluation when human assessments are impractical or costly. Our findings
025            demonstrate that zero-shot in-context adversarial learning significantly enhances
026            idea generation across two dimensions. Specifically, with GPT-4o, the novelty of
027
               generated ideas improved by 21%, and feasibility of the ideas saw an impressive
               increase of 322%. These results underscore the transformative potential of zero-
028
               shot in-context adversarial learning in driving innovation and creativity within the
029
               research process.

067   one hand, by leveraging adversarial learning, LLMs can more effectively utilize their parametric
068   knowledge to respond to user query. On the other hand, keeping adversarial learning within an

070   with bi-level optimization in adversarial training. We implement zero-shot in-context adversarial
071   learning through a multi-LLM-agent interaction system. To scale the evaluation of generated ideas,
072   we introduce a relative quality ranking metric, designed to approximate human-level evaluation in
073   a customizable and fair manner. We experimented with the state-of-the-art LLMs using zero-shot
074   in-context adversarial learning and measured the novelty and feasibility of the generated ideas using

076

089   2.1   T HEORETICAL F OUNDATIONS OF I N - CONTEXT L EARNING
090
      Due to the black-box nature of LLMs, researchers have been drawing analogies to explain why and
091   how in-context learning capabilities emerge in these models. Xie et al. (2021) argue that in-context
092   learning can be viewed as a form of implicit Bayesian inference, arising when the pretraining corpus
093   of LLMs contains documents with long-range coherence, forcing the model to infer latent concepts
094   to generate coherent text. Olsson et al. (2022) provide a compelling argument that “induction heads”
095   within transformer models play a crucial role in enabling in-context learning, as the model improves
096   its predictions by processing more tokens within a sequence. Similarly, Dai et al. (2022) propose
097   that the Transformer’s attention mechanism implicitly performs meta-optimization, akin to gradient
098   descent, where demonstrations create meta-gradients that fine-tune the model in context. Besides,
099
      TextGrad (Yuksekgonul et al., 2024) claims that the automatic differentiation can be performed via
      feedback for the generated answer provided by the LLMs.

      2.2   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS FOR S CIENTIFIC H YPOTHESIS G ENERATION

103   Going beyond literature-based discovery (LBD), which primarily focuses on predicting pairwise
104   relationships between discrete concepts (Wang et al., 2023c), recent research has started to explore
105   the potential of foundation models, especially Large Language Models (LLMs) for scientific ideation
106   (Si et al., 2024). For instance, the SciMON framework (Wang et al., 2023d) utilized historical
107   scientific literature to fine-tune LLMs for generating hypotheses. In the social sciences, MOOSE
      (Yang et al., 2023b) employed multi-level LLM self-feedback to improve the discovery of scientific

      hypotheses. Similarly, ResearchAgent (Baek et al., 2024) utilized LLMs to automatically generate
109   and refine research problems, methodologies, and experimental designs, starting from a core paper
110   and entity-centric knowledge graphs. Furthermore, (Zhou et al., 2024) proposed a prompting-based
111   approach that iteratively generates hypotheses using LLMs guided by training examples. Beyond
112   hypothesis generation, the Coscientist system described in (Boiko et al., 2023) equipped foundation

116   2.3   M ULTI -LLM-AGENT I NTERACTIONS FOR I MPROVING TASK P ERFORMANCE
117
      While promising, a single LLM often struggles to generate novel insights after establishing an initial
118
      stance, even when that stance is incorrect, and lacks the necessary feedback mechanisms for ratio-
119
      nal refinement (Bubeck et al., 2023). Recent studies (Huang et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023) have
120   further highlighted that this challenge cannot be easily addressed through self-correction mecha-
121   nisms(Madaan et al., 2024; Shinn et al., 2024). To overcome this limitation, recent works establish
122   multi-LLM-agent frameworks through discussion (Chen et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024), collabora-
123   tion (Chih-Yao Chen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023), and debate (Du et al., 2023; Liang et al.,
124   2023; Subramaniam et al., 2024) to incorporate both internal and external knowledge, thereby en-
125   hancing model ability such as improving consistency (Xiong et al., 2023), evaluation (Chan et al.,
126   2023; Wang et al., 2023a), and supervising other LLMs (Khan et al., 2024; Subramaniam et al.,
127   2024). For example, Liang et al. (2023) introduced the Multi-Agent Debate framework, in which
128
      multiple LLMs engage in argument exchanges, with a judge overseeing the debate to determine the
      final solution. Similarly, Lu et al. (2024) proposed the LLM discussion framework which enhances
129
      the creativity through divergent thinking in the discussion phase and reach conclusion in the con-
130
      vergence phrase. Recent research has explored multi-LLM-agent interaction in various contexts,
131   including scalable oversight (Kenton et al., 2024), translation (Liang et al., 2023), and knowledge
132   reasoning (Wang et al., 2023b; Ma et al., 2024). Building upon these frameworks, we theoretically
133   model the zero-shot in-context adversarial learning problem and extend it to the field of research
134   ideation.

138   In this section, we first present the theoretical framework for zero-shot in-context adversarial learn-
139   ing, aiming at optimizing the utilization of LLMs’ parametric knowledge to perform user-specified
140   tasks. In addition, we describe how this can be implemented through LLM-based agent interac-
141   tions to enhance the generation of research ideas. Following this, we introduce a relative quality
142   ranking-based metric designed to approximate human evaluation of the generated ideas.

144   3.1   ZERO - SHOT IN - CONTEXT ADVERSARIAL LEARNING FOR RESEARCH IDEA REFINEMENT
145
      The goal of zero-shot in-context adversarial learning is to optimize the utilization of LLMs parama-
146   tric knowledge such that LLMs can generate a suboptimal answer with limited context provided by
147   the user’s query. To achieve this goal, we begin our formulation with Assumption 1:
148
      Assumption 1. We assume that given any user query x, there exists a static optimal answer ẏ, al-
149
      though the LLM may not explicitly generate ẏ due to the discrete nature of its paramatric knowledge
150
      base {θ}.
151
152   The paramatric knowledge base {θ} of an LLM is obtained during the pre-training process of the
153   given LLM and plays a crucial role in answering any user’s query x. As the oracle answer ẏ may
154   not be directly achievable, the objective shifts to generating an approximation answer ŷ which is
155   sufficiently close to ẏ. Thus, we have Assumption 2:

157   {θ}, and ŷ lies in the neighborhood B of ẏ with radius ϵ, that is, ŷ ∈ Bϵ (ẏ), we posit that the
158   LLM has optimized the use of its parametric knowledge in response to the user’s query x, yeilding
159   suboptimal answer ŷ.

            E VALUATION
281   Although human judgment remains the gold standard for evaluating open-ended text generation,
282   the Natural Language Processing community has been actively developing scalable alternatives to
283   approximate human evaluation, as the labor involved in human evaluation is often costly and im-
284   practical in many cases. Recent studies have explored the use of LLMs as autoraters (Chiang & Lee,
285   2023; Liu et al., 2023; Bubeck et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024; Vu et al., 2024; Gu & Krenn, 2024).
286   These studies show that the correlation between human evaluators and LLM autoraters positions
287   LLMs as a promising alternative for large-scale assessments for open-ended generation. To auto-
288
      mate the evaluation of the generated ideas, we develop a relative ranking-based metric designed to
      assess idea quality in a fair and customizable manner. This metric can be customized to accommo-
289
      date various quality indicators, such as novelty, feasibility, or any other criteria specified by the user,
290
      as long as a target research idea and the context used to generate this target idea are available. The
291   target idea can either be generated by the user or selected from existing literature. Compared to the
292   winrate, our metric offers a more granular measurement (Zheng et al., 2023). Please refer to Section
293   A.3.1 in the Appendix for more discussions.
294
      For a given context used to generate a set of research ideas, we use GPT-4o to rank the quality of
295
      all the ideas (both generated ideas and the target idea) based on user-specified quality indicators,
296
      without revealing which idea is the target research idea. GPT-4o is prompted to assess the ideas
297   based on its understanding of quality indicators such as novelty and feasibility, and then rank them
298   accordingly. The prompt template used to achieve this is shown in Figure 12 in the Appendix. The
299   position of the target research idea within the ranked list of ideas reflects the quality of the generated
300   ideas with respect to the specified quality indicators. Intuitively, if the target idea ranks higher
301   on the list, this suggests that the generated ideas are of lower quality compared to the target idea.
302   Conversely, if the generated ideas rank higher than the target idea, it indicates that the generated
303   ideas may be of better quality. Given a target idea t and n generated ideas based on the given
304   context, let nt denote the rank of t among the target idea and the generated ideas. The relative
305
      quality ranking S of the generated ideas is computed as follows:
306
307

328   4.1   DATASET
329   To evaluate the effectiveness of zero-shot in-context adversarial learning in enhancing research idea
330   generation we constructed a dataset designed for this specific task. We gathered a set of “target
331   papers”; high-quality biomedical research papers published in 2024. We denote a target paper’s
332   research idea as ti , where i indexes the target paper out of the total m papers in our dataset. The
333   ideas of the target papers represent the “gold standard” for comparing the generated research ideas
334   through the relative quality ranking score.
335   In order to simulate the initial human query to the system we also collect the background information
336   that informed each paper’s research. This background information consists of the abstracts from the
337   reference papers cited by the target paper, which we represent as xi = {b1 , . . . , bki }, where ki is the
338   total number of references for the i-th paper.
339
      By linking the target papers to their reference papers, we build a comprehensive dataset that provides
340
      LLMs with the foundational context necessary to generate research ideas that stem from the same
341   background information from which the target papers were inspired. Formally, we represent the
342   dataset as {xi , ti }m
                           i=1 , where each data point consists of a target paper’s research idea ti and its
343   corresponding background information xi for m papers.
344
      We sourced target papers from top biomedical venues ranked by Google Scholar, using the Semantic
345
      Scholar API (Kinney et al., 2023). To ensure a high standard, we included papers from top-tier
346
      venues with at least one citation or those from other recognized venues that have garnered at least 20
347   citations. Duplicate entries were removed, and we only included papers that contained all essential
348   data fields, such as abstracts, to ensure dataset completeness. For our experiments, we gathered
349   m = 500 target papers and their corresponding background information.

351   4.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
352
      To evaluate the effectiveness of zero-shot in-context adversarial learning in improving research
353   ideation, we set up the proposer, reviewer and area chair agents to engage in structured interac-
354   tions through an agent discussion framework. This setup allows us to test whether the framework
355   enhances the novelty and feasibility of the generated research ideas.
356
      Agent Initialization. We initialize the agents with meta prompts (Liang et al. (2023)) to make sure
357
      each agent understands its role and which field it specializes in. The meta prompts specify the agent’s
358
      role—whether it is the proposer, reviewer, or area chair—detailing what the role entails, which
359   {research_area} the agent specializes in, and which {quality_indicator} (novelty or
360   feasibility) the agent is tasked with improving or judging. All the prompt templates along with
361   the algorithm for agent interactions are provided in the Appendix. This initialization underpins
362   the multi-agent system’s ability to enhance research ideation using zero-shot in-context adversarial
363   learning.
364
      Idea Initialization. To simulate how humans would query this system with some background con-
365   text to generate the initial research idea, we set the initial query to be a set of background information
366   xi = {b1 , . . . , bki } for a target idea ti . This background information is given to the proposer agent
367   to generate the initial research idea ŷ0 . The initial idea is then sent through our system to improve
368   the idea’s novelty or feasibility with zero-shot in-context adversarial learning.
369
      Baselines. To evaluate the effectiveness of our zero-shot in-context adversarial learning system,
370
      we compare its performance against two baselines: (1) the initial idea baseline and (2) the self-
371   reflection baseline. We measure improvement based on two key quality indicators—novelty and
372   feasibility—using the relative ranking quality score S described in Section 3.2.

987    Handling Potential Bias from GPT-4o as an Autorater.
988    Google researchers show that LLMs can be used as reliable autoraters, and GPT-4o is overall the best
989    off-the-shelf model in handling bias (Vu et al. (2024)). That’s why we use GPT-4o as the autorater
990    when evaluating research ideas. Furthermore, our relative quality metric does not prompt GPT-4o
991    to give an absolute score for the quality of the ideas, because it may be biased. Rather, we provide
992    a target idea to force GPT-4o to rank ideas based on a quality indicator specified by users, such as
993    novelty and feasibility. This enables GPT-4o to provide a more objective evaluation than asking for
994
       an absolute score.

       Comparison with Other Metrics. In open-ended generation tasks, winrate is a metric commonly
1015
       used to assess quality by determining the proportion of instances in which one model’s output is
1016   preferred over another’s in a binary comparison (Zheng et al., 2023). However, this approach re-
1017   duces nuanced evaluations to binary outcomes, which can lead to significant information loss in
1018   capturing the diversity and subtle differences between outputs. Our relative quality ranking offers a
1019   more granular approach by allowing for a graded comparison across multiple dimensions of quality.
1020   Instead of a binary decision boundary, this metric ranks outputs on a continuum, capturing more nu-
1021   anced differences in quality. This fine-grained assessment provides richer insights into the strengths
1022   and weaknesses of each model output, enhancing the accuracy of quality evaluations in open-ended
1023   generation tasks.

1066   In this section, we present two examples of how our method improves research ideation. One ex-
1067   ample focuses on improving the novelty of the research idea (Figure 14) and the other on feasibility
1068   (Figure 15). These examples show how zero-shot in-context adversarial learning with multi-LLM-
1069   agent interactions refines research ideas to improve novelty or feasibility.
1070
       Both examples begin with the proposer agent generating the same initial idea which hypothesizes
1071   that orexin levels may improve sleep quality and energy levels in adolescent athletes. When im-
1072   proving novelty, the reviewer agent suggests ways to make the idea more innovative. After seven
1073   iterations, the idea evolves to suggest that modulating orexin levels can enhance cognitive function
1074   and emotional resilience in adolescent athletes and proposes to use advanced statistical models to
1075   analyze the relationship between orexin, social jetlag, and cognitive/emotional outcomes to uncover
1076   insights that could redefine orexin’s role.
