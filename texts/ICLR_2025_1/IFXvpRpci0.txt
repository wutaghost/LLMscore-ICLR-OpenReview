009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Molecular dynamics simulations are crucial for understanding complex physical,
012            chemical, and biological processes at the atomic level. However, accurately cap-
013            turing interactions across multiple spatial and temporal scales remains a signif-
014            icant challenge. We present a novel framework that jointly models spatial and
015            temporal multiscale interactions in molecular dynamics. Our approach leverages
016            Graph Fourier Transforms to decompose molecular structures into different spatial
017            scales and employs Neural Ordinary Differential Equations to model the temporal
018
               dynamics at each scale. This unified framework explicitly links spatial structures
               with temporal evolution, enabling more accurate and comprehensive simulations
019
               of molecular systems. We evaluate our model on the MD17 and alanine dipeptide
020
               datasets, demonstrating consistent performance improvements over state-of-the-
021            art baselines across multiple molecules. Importantly, we introduce physics-based
022            evaluation metrics, including bond lengths, bond angles, and dihedral angles, to
023            assess the practical effectiveness of our model in preserving molecular geometry
024            over long time horizons. Ablation studies confirm the significant contributions of
025            both spatial and temporal multiscale modeling components. Our method advances
026            the simulation of complex molecular systems, potentially accelerating research in
027            computational chemistry, drug discovery, and materials science.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations are indispensable tools for investigating the behavior of
033
      molecular systems at the atomic level, offering profound insights into physical (Bear & Blaisten-
      Barojas, 1998), chemical (Wang et al., 2011), and biological (Salo-Ahen et al., 2020) processes.
034
      These simulations must capture interactions occurring across a wide range of spatial and temporal
035
      scales—from localized bond vibrations to long-range non-bonded interactions—posing significant
036   computational challenges. Accurately modeling these multi-scale interactions requires sophisticated
037   methods that can efficiently represent complex dependencies among atoms (Vakis et al., 2018).
038
039
      An intrinsic link exists between the spatial scales of molecular interactions and their correspond-
      ing temporal dynamics. To illustrate this connection, consider the vibrational spectrum of a water
040
      molecule, a well-studied system in molecular spectroscopy. In Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR)
041
      spectroscopy (Griffiths, 1983), different absorption peaks correspond to distinct vibrational modes,
042   each associated with specific spatial and temporal scales. As shown in Figure 1(a), high-frequency
043   absorption peaks arise from localized O-H stretching vibrations, involving interactions over short
044   spatial ranges and resulting in fast temporal dynamics. Conversely, low-frequency peaks correspond
045   to collective motions like hydrogen bonding and bending modes, which involve longer spatial ranges
046   and exhibit slower temporal dynamics. This spectrum exemplifies how localized spatial interactions
047   correspond to faster temporal dynamics, while extended spatial interactions lead to slower dynamics.
048   Existing methods have attempted to address multi-scale interactions in MD simulations but of-
049   ten focus on either spatial or temporal scales independently. Coarse-grained techniques (Joshi &
050   Deshmukh, 2021; Wang et al., 2019) reduce degrees of freedom to capture larger spatial scales but
051   may lose important high-frequency details inherent in localized interactions. Neural operator-based
052   methods like ITO (Schreiner et al., 2024), TimeWarp (Klein et al., 2024), and EGNO (Xu et al.,
053   2024) are effective in extracting different temporal scales of signals but do not explicitly model the
      varying spatial scales that give rise to these temporal dynamics. Similarly, graph neural ODEs such

      The recognition of the aforementioned challenge naturally raises a pivotal question:
086
087
                              How to simultaneously model spatial and temporal multi-scale interactions in molecular dynamics for
088                                                     comprehensive and accurate simulations?
089
090   In this work, we propose a novel approach that explicitly models the joint spatial and temporal
091   multiscale interactions in molecular systems. Inspired by molecular spectroscopy techniques like
092   FTIR, which reveal the dual connotations of frequencies in both spatial and temporal domains, we
093   leverage Graph Fourier Transformation to decompose molecular interactions into different spatial
094   scales. This transformation captures the high-frequency (localized) and low-frequency (extended)
095   spatial interactions, effectively encoding the spatial multiscale nature of the molecule.
096   To model the temporal dynamics associated with each spatial scale, we employ Neural Ordinary
097   Differential Equations (Neural ODEs) (Chen et al., 2018). The adaptive stepping nature of Neural
098   ODEs allows for flexible and efficient modeling of dynamics at different time scales, accommodating
099   both fast and slow temporal evolutions. By propagating the decomposed spatial scales through time
100   using Neural ODEs, we capture the temporal dynamics corresponding to each spatial scale. After
101   temporal propagation, we reconstruct the molecular state in the physical space by an inverse Graph
102   Fourier Transformation.
103   This joint modeling approach is motivated by the natural association between spatial and temporal
104   scales in molecular systems: localized spatial interactions (high-frequency spatial scales) correspond
105   to faster temporal dynamics, while extended spatial interactions (low-frequency spatial scales) cor-
106   respond to slower temporal dynamics. By explicitly linking these scales, our method provides a
107   comprehensive framework for capturing the complex interplay between spatial structures and tem-
      poral dynamics in MD simulations.

135   2.1   C OARSE -G RAINED M OLECULAR DYNAMICS
136
137   Coarse-grained molecular dynamics (CGMD) (Noid, 2013; Saunders & Voth, 2013) reduce com-
138   putational complexity by grouping atoms into larger units or “beads,” simplifying molecular repre-
139   sentations. This approach effectively captures large-scale molecular motions over extended periods,
140   facilitating the study of complex systems like protein folding and polymer dynamics. F3low (Li
141   et al., 2024) extends CG modeling to the SE(3) manifold using flow-matching for autoregressive
142
      sampling, enabling broader exploration of conformational space and improved insights into protein
      folding. Two for One (Arts et al., 2023) uses score-based generative models to learn CG force fields
143
      without force inputs, simplifying training and improving performance in protein simulations while
144
      preserving dynamics. However, CGMD primarily addresses spatial scaling and often sacrifices
145   fine-grained details inherent in localized interactions, such as bond vibrations and stretching. More-
146   over, it lacks explicit mechanisms for modeling temporal multi-scale dynamics, limiting its ability
147   to capture the interplay between fast and slow processes in molecular systems.
148
149
150   2.2   N EURAL O PERATOR M ODELS
151
152   Neural operator models (Kovachki et al., 2023) have emerged as powerful tools for learning map-
153
      pings between infinite-dimensional function spaces, showing promise in modeling complex dynamic
      processes. Conditional Diffusion Model (Hsu et al., 2024) introduces score dynamics (SD), which
154
      learns accelerated evolution operators from molecular simulations using diffusion models, enabling
155
      larger timesteps and faster simulations while maintaining accuracy. In molecular dynamics, meth-
156   ods like Implicit Transfer Operator (ITO) (Schreiner et al., 2024), Timewarp (Klein et al., 2024),
157   and Equivariant Graph Neural Operator (EGNO) (Xu et al., 2024) aim to capture temporal dynam-
158   ics over multiple scales. ITO and Timewarp employ coarse-graining and time-stepping techniques
159   to accelerate simulations but focus primarily on temporal scaling, potentially losing critical spatial
160   details. EGNO models temporal evolution using neural operators with SE(3) equivariance but does
161   not explicitly handle spatial multi-scale interactions. These methods often treat spatial and temporal
      scales separately, lacking a joint modeling framework that captures their intrinsic connection.

      2.3    G RAPH N EURAL ODE M ODELS
163
164   Graph Neural ODEs integrate Graph Neural Networks into continuous-time dynamics using Neu-
165   ral ODE frameworks (Chen et al., 2018), enabling the modeling of time-evolving graph-structured
166   data (Zang & Wang, 2020; Huang et al., 2023a;b). While effective in capturing temporal evolution
167   on graphs, these models typically rely on local message passing, which may not adequately represent
168   multi-scale spatial interactions present in molecular systems. Moreover, they often do not explicitly
169   link spatial scales with temporal dynamics, potentially overlooking the relationship between local-
170   ized spatial interactions and fast temporal dynamics, as well as extended spatial interactions and
171   slow dynamics.

      3     P ROBLEM D EFINITION
175
176   For the task of molecular dynamics, we aim to predict the future conformations of a molecular
177   system based on its initial structure. Let G t0 = (V, E) represent the molecular graph at the initial
178
      time t0 , where V = {v1 , v2 , . . . , vN } is the set of N atoms in the molecule, and E ⊆ V × V is the
      set of optional edges representing chemical bonds or interactions between atoms.
179
180   The position of each atom vi ∈ V at time t0 is given by its 3D coordinates xti0 ∈ R3 . Our objective is
181   to predict the atomic positions X(tk ) := {xt1k , . . . , xtNk } at future time points t1 , t2 , . . . , tK , where
182   t1 , t2 , . . . , tK are irregularly sampled on a continuous time scale. All data are parallel sequences
183   from molecular systems of the same type, meaning the data contain conformations sampled from
184   similar molecular systems.

190   interactions in molecular dynamics. Our approach integrates Equivariant Graph Neural Networks
191   (EGNNs) (Satorras et al., 2021) with Graph Fourier Transforms (GFTs) and Neural Ordinary Dif-
192

      B.2     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
662
663   Our model aims to jointly model spatial and temporal multiscale interactions in molecular dynamics
664   simulations. The primary components of the model include:
665
666   B.2.1    1. E NCODER (E QUIVARIANT G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORK )
667
              • Purpose: Encode the initial molecular structure and properties into latent representations.
668
669           • Input Features:
670               – Atomic Positions (x): 3D coordinates of atoms at each time step.
671               – Atomic Features (h): Scalars representing properties like atom types or initial em-
672                 beddings.
673           • Process: Utilize an Equivariant Graph Neural Network (EGNN) to encode spatial rela-
674             tionships while preserving equivariance under rotations and translations. Update h and x
675             based on neighboring information.
676
677   B.2.2    2. G RAPH F OURIER T RANSFORM (GFT)
678
              • Purpose: Decompose the molecular graph into different spatial frequency components to
679
                capture multiscale spatial interactions.
680
681           • Process: Apply GFT to the latent features h and positions x separately, transforming them
682
                into the spectral domain. The number of modes is specified by "num modes": 2,
                indicating that we capture two spatial frequency components.
683
684
      B.2.3    3. N EURAL O RDINARY D IFFERENTIAL E QUATIONS (N EURAL ODE S )
685
686           • Purpose: Model the temporal evolution of each frequency component obtained from the
687             GFT.
688           • Process: For each frequency mode, we define a Neural ODE that models the continuous-
689             time dynamics. The ODE solver used is "solver": "dopri5", with relative and
690             absolute tolerances specified by "rtol": 1e-3 and "atol": 1e-4, respectively.
691             Temporal embeddings are incorporated via "time emb dim": 32.
692
693   B.2.4    4. I NVERSE G RAPH F OURIER T RANSFORM (IGFT)
694           • Purpose: Transform the evolved frequency components back to the spatial domain to re-
695             construct the updated positions and features.
696
697   B.2.5    5. D ECODER (E QUIVARIANT G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORK )
698
699
              • Purpose: Refine the reconstructed features and produce the final predicted atomic posi-
                tions.
700
701           • Process: Apply an EGNN similar to the encoder to update h and x based on the evolved
                features.

      In this paper, we introduced a novel framework for modeling molecular dynamics by jointly cap-
759   turing spatial and temporal multiscale interactions. By leveraging the Graph Fourier Transform to
760   decompose molecular structures into different spatial scales and employing Neural Ordinary Differ-
761   ential Equations to model the temporal dynamics at each scale, our approach explicitly links spatial
762   structures with temporal evolution. Extensive experiments on the MD17 dataset demonstrated that
763   our model consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baseline methods across multiple molecules,
764   particularly under challenging conditions like irregular timestep sampling and long-term predic-
765   tion horizons. Ablation studies confirmed the significant contributions of both the spatial multiscale
766   modeling and the temporal multiscale modeling components. Our method provides a comprehensive
767
      and accurate representation of molecular behavior over time, advancing the simulation of complex
      molecular systems.
768
769   The proposed framework has the potential to significantly impact various domains by enhancing the
770   accuracy and efficiency of molecular simulations. In computational chemistry and drug discovery,
771   it can accelerate the understanding of molecular interactions and aid in the development of new
772   therapeutics. In materials science, it can assist in designing novel materials with desired properties
773
      by accurately modeling molecular dynamics at different scales. Additionally, the method’s com-
      putational efficiency may reduce resource requirements for large-scale simulations. However, it is
774
      important to consider potential limitations, such as the need for high-quality data and computational

      resources, as well as ethical considerations regarding the misuse of advanced simulation technolo-
