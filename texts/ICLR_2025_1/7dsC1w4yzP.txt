009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a novel approach to chemical foundation models, leveraging structured
012            state space sequence models (SSMs) to overcome the limitations of traditional
013            Transformer-based architectures. While Transformers have achieved state-of-the-
014            art results in chemical tasks such as property prediction and molecule generation,
015            their self-attention mechanism is constrained by its inability to model data outside
016
               of a finite context window and its quadratic scaling with respect to window length.
               In contrast, SSMs offer a promising alternative for sequence modeling, enabling the
017
               capture of complex patterns and dependencies in molecular structures. Our Mamba
018
               architecture, a simplified end-to-end SSM-based neural network, eliminates the
019            need for attention and MLP blocks, allowing for faster inference. We pre-train
020            Mamba on a large, curated dataset of 91 million SMILES samples (equivalent to 4
021            billion molecular tokens) sourced from PubChem, and evaluate its performance
022            on various benchmark datasets. Our experiments demonstrate the SSM’s capacity
023            to provide state-of-the-art results while maintaining fast inference, supporting
024            complex tasks such as molecular property prediction, classification, molecular
025            reconstruction, and synthesis yield prediction. This work advances the state-of-
026            the-art in AI methodology in chemical sciences, offering a promising direction for
027
               future research in molecular modeling and discovery.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large-scale pre-training methodologies for chemical language models (LMs) represent a significant
032   advancement in cheminformatics Sadybekov & Katritch (2023). These methodologies have shown
033   impressive results in challenging molecular tasks such as predicting properties and generating
034   molecules Ross et al. (2022). The success of these models can be attributed to their ability to learn
035   contextualized representations of input tokens through self-supervised learning on large unlabeled
036   corpora Bommasani et al. (2021).
037   Most chemical foundation models available are based on the Transformers architecture and its core
038   attention module Pesciullesi et al. (2020); Chithrananda et al. (2020); Janakarajan et al. (2023). The
039   efficacy of self-attention is attributed to its ability to route information densely within a context
040   window Vaswani et al. (2017), allowing it to model complex data Tay et al. (2022). However, this
041   property brings fundamental drawbacks as the inability to model anything outside of a finite window
042   and quadratic scaling with respect to the window length Lin et al. (2022). A substantial amount of
043   research has emerged on more efficient variants of attention to overcome these drawbacks Kotei &
044
      Thirunavukarasu (2023).
045   Structured state space sequence models (SSMs) have recently emerged as a promising class of archi-
046   tectures for sequence modeling Gu et al. (2021). These models can be interpreted as a combination
047   of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and convolutional neural networks (CNNs) Smith et al. (2022).
048   This class of models can be computed very efficiently as either a recurrence or convolution, with
049   linear or near-linear scaling in sequence length. Mamba is a simplified end-to-end SSM-based neural
050
      network architecture without attention or even MLP blocks Gu & Dao (2023). Mamba enjoys fast
      inference and linear scaling in sequence length Gu & Dao (2023).
051
052   In this study, we present a novel Mamba-based large foundation model, denoted as OSMI -SSM-336M .
053   Our OSMI -SSM-336M encoder-decoder foundation model was obtained using an efficient encoder
      SSM-based model aligned with an auto-encoder mechanism pre-trained on a large corpus of 91

135   Mamba models originates from a continuous-time system that maps an input function or sequence
136   x(t) ∈ RM to an output response signal y(t) ∈ RO through an implicit latent state h(t) ∈ RN which
137   can be mathematically formulated using the following ordinary differential equations.
138
                                                           h′ (t) = Ah(t) + Bx(t),
139                                                                                                                                                (1)
140                                                         y(t) = Ch(t) + Dx(t)
141
142   where A ∈ RN ×N and C ∈ RO×N control how the current state evolves over time and translates to
143   the output, B ∈ RN ×M and D ∈ RO×M depict how the input influences the state and the output,
144   respectively.
145   The tokens extracted from SMILES trough the SSM encoder are embedded in a 768-dimensional
146   space. The encoder-decoder layer is designed to process molecular token embeddings, represented as
147   x ∈ RT ×L , where T denotes the maximum number of tokens and L represents the embedding space
148   dimension. We limited T at 202 tokens, as 99.4% of molecules in the PubChem dataset contain fewer
149   tokens than this threshold Ross et al. (2022).
150   In encoder-only models, a mean pooling layer is typically employed to represent tokens as SMILES
151   in the latent space Bran & Schwaller (2023). However, this approach is limited by the lack of a
152   natural inversion process for the mean pooling operation. To overcome this limitation, we aim to
153   construct a latent space representation for SMILES by submersing the x in a latent space, denoted as
154   z, as described in Eq. 2.
155
156
                                          z = (LayerNorm (GELU (xW1 + b1 ))) W2 ,                                                                  (2)

      where z ∈ RL , W1 ∈ RL , b1 ∈ RL , W2 ∈ RL×L , with L denoting the latent space size (specifically,
159   L = 768). Subsequently, we can immerse z back by calculating Eq. 3.

165   A language layer (decoder) is used to process x̂, where it applies non-linearity and normalization,

      and projects the resulting vector into a set of logits over the vocabulary, which can then be used to
      predict the next token in the molecular Ferrando et al. (2023). This architecture serves as a tool for

      2.3   P RE - TRAINING STRATEGIES
170
171   Pre-training of OSMI -SSM-336M was performed for 130 epochs through the entire curated PubChem
172   dataset with a fixed learning rate of 3e-5 and a batch size of 128 molecules on a total of 24 NVIDIA
173   V100 (16G) GPUs parallelized into 4 nodes using DDP and torch run. It involves two distinct phases:
174   i) Learning of token embeddings through a masking process; ii) Subsequently, the token embeddings
175   are mapped into a common latent space that encapsulates the entire SMILES string. This latent space
176   not only facilitates the representation of the SMILES but also enables the reconstruction of both
177   individual tokens and complete SMILES strings. Consequently, the pre-training process involves
178   two separate loss functions: one for the token embeddings, which is based on the masking process,
179
      and another for the encoder-decoder layer, which focuses on the reconstruction of tokens. Two
      pre-training strategies are employed:
180
181          • In phase 1, the token encoder is initially pre-trained using 95% of the available samples,
182            while the remaining 5% is reserved for training the encoder-decoder layer. This partitioning
183            is necessary as the token embeddings may encounter convergence difficulties in the initial
184            epochs, which could adversely affect the training of the encoder-decoder layer.
185          • In phase 2, once the token embeddings layer has achieved convergence, the pre-training
186            process is expanded to utilize 100% of the available samples for both phases. This approach
187            leads to an enhancement in the performance of the encoder-decoder layer, particularly in
188            terms of token reconstruction.
189
190   For encoder pre-training we use the masked language model method defined in Devlin et al. (2019).
191
      Initially 15% of the tokens are selected for possible learning. From that selection, 80% of the tokens
      are randomly selected and replaced with the [MASK] token, 10% of the tokens are randomly selected
192
      to be replaced with a random token, while the remaining 10% of the tokens will be unchanged.
193
194   The adoption of different pre-training strategies has proven instrumental in enhancing the efficiency
195   of our model, as evidenced by improvements observed in the loss functions.

199   To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed Mamba-based model OSMI -SSM-336M , we conducted
200   experiments using a set of 11 datasets sourced from MoleculeNet Wu et al. (2018) as demonstrated
201   in Table 3. Specifically, we evaluated 6 datasets for classification task and 5 datasets for regression
202   tasks. To ensure an unbiased assessment, we maintained consistency with the original benchmark by
203   adopting identical train/validation/test splits for all tasks Wu et al. (2018). We also conducted the
204   experiments considered 10 different seeds for all the tests in other to guarantee the robustness of the
205   approach.
206   We also conducted high-throughput experiments on Pd-catalyzed Buchwald–Hartwig C–N cross-

208   experiments utilized three 1536-well plates, covering a matrix of 15 aryl and heteroaryl halides, four
209   Buchwald ligands, three bases, and 23 isoxazole additives, resulting in a total of 3,955 reactions. We
210   employed the same data splits as in Ahneman et al. (2018) to assess our model’s performance with

447   5    C ONCLUSION
448
449   This paper introduces OSMI -SSM-336M , a Mamba-based chemical foundation model pre-trained on
450   a curated dataset of 91 million SMILES samples from PubChem, encompassing 4 billion molecular
451   tokens. The model is designed to achieve high performance in evaluation metrics while decreasing
452   the inference time.
453
      The efficacy of OSMI -SSM-336M was rigorously assessed across a variety of tasks, including molecu-
454
      lar property classification and prediction. The model not only achieved state-of-the-art results but also
455   demonstrated significant efficiency improvements. Specifically, it was approximately 54% faster than
456   existing state-of-the-art Transformer-based approaches, reducing GPU usage by 6 hours and lowering
457   CO2 emissions by an average of 0.78 kg CO2 equivalent Lacoste et al. (2019) during the prediction
458   of HOMO-LUMO gaps for a dataset of 10 million randomly selected samples from PubChem.
459
      We also explored the model’s capabilities in predicting chemical reaction outcomes, such as reaction
460
      yields based on high-throughput screening data, a critical task in machine learning for chemistry.
461   The consistent performance of the Mamba-based model across both in-domain and out-of-domain
462   test sets underscores the effectiveness of pre-training on a diverse and comprehensive dataset. This
463   pre-training enables the model to adapt to a wide range of chemical environments and reaction
464   conditions. Our comparative analysis revealed that while traditional approaches, such as DFT-based
465   models, are grounded in quantum chemistry, they may not fully capture the complexity of reaction
466   mechanisms in practical scenarios. Similarly, transformer-based models like Yield-BERT and its
467   augmented variant, despite their strong performance, are outperformed by the Mamba-based model,
468   particularly in low-data regimes. This highlights the critical role that large-scale, diverse pre-training
469
      data plays in achieving superior results.
470   Finally, we conducted a comparative evaluation of the OSMI -SSM-336M model against several
471   baseline models for SMILES reconstruction and decoding. The model’s performance across diverse
472   metrics demonstrates the importance of leveraging large-scale dataset for pre-training, which can
473   lead to models that not only excel in generating high-quality molecules but also possess the flexibility
474   required to tackle complex challenges in computational chemistry and drug design.
