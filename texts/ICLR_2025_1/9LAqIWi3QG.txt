009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) provides a paradigm for
012                aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences. This involves the
013                initial training of a reward model based on pairwise human feedback. The reward
014                model is subsequently utilized in reinforcement learning to assess the scores of each
015                generated sentence as a whole, further guiding the optimization of LLMs. However,
016                current approaches have a significant shortcoming: They allocate a single, sparse,
017
                   and delayed reward to an entire sequence of output. This may overlook some
                   significant individual contributions of each token towards the desired outcome. To
018
                   overcome this limitation, our paper proposes a novel reward redistribution method
019
                   called R3HF, which facilitates a more fine-grained, token-level reward allocation.
020                Specifically, our method treats the reward prediction task of the reward model
021                as a regression problem. As a result, the redistributed rewards are computed by
022                evaluating the specific contribution of each token to the reward model’s output.
023                This detailed approach improves the model’s understanding of language nuances,
024                leading to more precise enhancements in its performance. Our method is crafted
025                to integrate seamlessly with most current techniques while incurring minimal
026                computational costs. Through comprehensive experiments across diverse datasets
027                and tasks, we have verified the effectiveness and superiority of our approach1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
      Large language models (LLMs) have showcased remarkable adaptabilities across various tasks, with
032
      applications spanning fields like psychology [12], education [49, 19], and medical support [46, 27].
033
      However, as LLMs become increasingly sophisticated, the complexity of their decision-making
034   processes and outputs also escalates, introducing potential risks such as the propagation of bias [14,
035   48], generation of misinformation [24, 29], and potential harm [16, 15]. This underscores the critical
036   need for effective alignment [35, 50, 26, 11] of LLMs. Such alignment aims to guide the models
037   to better comprehend and prioritize human preferences, ensuring their operations are in tune with
038   human values and ethics.
039
      Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) [10, 29, 5] is an advanced paradigm that
040   incorporates human feedback into LLM training. This approach typically unfolds in three primary
041   stages, which is shown in Figure 1 (left). The initial stage involves supervised fine-tuning (SFT)
042   applied to the target domain. Subsequently, the second stage develops and trains a reward model
043   on data that reflect human preferences. The final stage is dedicated to refining the language model
044   using reinforcement learning algorithms with the learned reward model. Though RLHF technology
045   has demonstrated its effectiveness in various scenarios, it also presents a significant drawback
046   that hampers the training efficiency of the model. Traditional reward models typically assess the
047   overall effectiveness of an entire generated sequence, assigning a score only after delivering the final
048
      token, with the other tokens receiving a score of zero. This reward structure, being both sparse and
      delayed, challenges the model in recognizing the impact of individual tokens. An intuitive example is
049
      illustrated in Figure 1(right). Consider a question-answering task with the prompt, “Was Walt Disney
050
      the original creator of Mickey Mouse? <end>” and the generated response, “Yes, Walter Elias Disney
051   was indeed the original creator of Mickey Mouse.” The reward model assigns a positive evaluation
052   score of 0.8. However, when treating the entire sentence as an episode, traditional methods only

082   Meanwhile, the initial tokens in a sequence can significantly influence the subsequent generation, a
083   nuance that current methodologies often struggle to accommodate effectively. In the example, the
084   word “Yes” is the most crucial token in the generated sequence that influences the overall score, yet it
085   receives a reward of zero. This highlights the urgent need for methodologies that better recognize and

087   To address this shortcoming, in this paper, we introduce a novel approach: Reward Redistribution for
088   enhancing Reinforcement learning from Human Feedback (R3HF). The foundational concept of
089   our method involves assigning individual credits to each token within the generated sentences, thus
090   providing a nuanced signal for optimization tailored to LLMs. As the example in Figure 1(right),
091   since “Yes” is the most crucial token according to the reward model, it receives the highest reward
092
      signal. Similarly, other tokens receive different rewards, which can be either positive or negative. The
      sum of the rewards for all tokens in the sequence is equivalent to the original overall reward score.
093
      To achieve this goal, our approach is operationalized through the framework of Sequence-Markov
094
      Decision process (SDPs) [4], wherein the allocation of rewards is not constrained by the Markov
095   property. Concretely, we conceptualize the reward model as a regression model, predicting each
096   sequence-wide return from the entire state-action sequence. By adopting this perspective, we allocate
097   to each token a portion of credit relative to its incremental impact on the reward model compared to
098   the preceding time-step. The credits can be inferred through temporally differentiated computation,
099   providing more granular guidance over the language generation process.
100
      Compared to state-of-the-art RLHF approaches, our R3HF offers the following advantages:
101
102   (1) Learning Efficiency. By providing token-level rewards, our method significantly enhances
103   learning by offering immediate and relevant information. This approach avoids the limitations of
104
      delayed rewards that may be less informative. Consequently, it facilitates more accurate fine-tuning
      of language models, leading to considerable advancements in language generation that are more
105
      closely aligned with human feedback.
106
107   (2) Independence from Human Labeling. The redistributed rewards do not depend on extensive
      human labeling of data. The reward model itself dynamically assigns value to each token based on

109   This feature facilitates the rapid incorporation of feedback without being bottlenecked by the pace of
110   human annotation, streamlining the training process.
111
112
      (3) Seamless Integration. Our method is designed for easy application across most mainstream
      RLHF paradigms, requiring only minimal modification that involves simple recomputation of rewards.
113
      This compatibility ensures that existing RLHF methods can be effortlessly enhanced with our token-

      level reward redistribution technique, boosting their effectiveness without necessitating extensive

      2.1   M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESS AND S EQUENCE -M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESSES
126
127   Natural language generation can be deemed as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) [33] which is
128                            △
      depicted as a tuple M = (S, A, R, P, γ, T ) with a finite vocabulary V. At the beginning of each
129
      episode, a prompt x is sampled and fed into the language model and is treated as the initial state
130   s0 ∈ S. At each time-step t < T , the language model acts as the policy π to choose an action at ∈ A
131   which means selecting a token from the vocabulary via π(at |st ), and then a new state is achieved
132   via the transition function P : S × A → S by adding the generated token to the previous state.
133   Meanwhile, a reward rt is gained via the reward function R : S × A → R. The goal of the policy
                                                                       PT
134   model is to maximize the expected accumulated return G(τ ) = t=0 γ t R(st , at ), where γ ∈ [0, 1)
135   represents the discount factor.
136
      In this paper, policy optimization follows a Sequence-Markov Decision Process (SDP) [4], where
137
      both the policy and the transition probabilities are Markov, but the reward function is not required
138   to be Markov. As claimed by Arjona-Medina et al. [4], return-equivalent SDPs possess identical
139   optimal policies. This implies that we can redistribute the reward gained at the end of the generation
140   sequence to optimize the policy model.

      3.2   R EWARD R EDISTRIBUTION FOR C REDIT A SSIGNMENT
190
191   We seek to perform credit assignment by allocating the earned reward (or penalty) across the sequence
192   of actions, thereby providing a more granular and immediate feedback mechanism. Taking a cue
193   from Arjona-Medina et al. [4], reward redistribution is realized within the sequence difference
194   penalties (SDPs). They posit that: (1) two SDPs are return-equivalent if they differ only in their
195   reward distribution and have the same expected return, and (2) return-equivalent SDPs share the
196   same optimal policy. Considering these properties, our remaining task is to devise an algorithm for
197
      constructing modified rewards r̃tRM that reflect the contributions of each token at every time-step,
      ensuring that the sum of the rewards equals rTRM .
198
199   Recalling the training process of the RL phase, rewards are generated using the last hidden state
200   with a logit head. This functions as a regression model that naturally predicts the score at the final
201   time-step. Consequently, there is no need to retrain or modify the reward model. Instead, we can
202   utilize the existing model to obtain all hidden states and predict scores at each time-step via the logit
203
      head. The redistributed rewards can then be computed using a time-difference approach, reflecting
      the incremental contribution of each time-step.
204

      3.4   C ONNECTION TO D IRECT P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION (DPO)
246
      Another recently popular algorithm DPO [35], eliminates the need for explicit reward modeling
247   and has gained widespread use due to its simplicity and effectiveness. Indeed, our method shares
248   the same optimal policy with DPO, as the summation of our redistributed rewards falls within the
249   same equivalence class as the traditional reward function. Interestingly, we also find that DPO can
250   implicitly perform any type of reward redistribution (credit assignment), which may be one of the
251   reasons for its effectiveness. We will provide a detailed analysis of this observation in the Appendix.
252
253   3.5   D ISCUSSION ABOUT C ONCURRENT W ORK
254
255   In parallel to our research, several studies have explored token-level rewards in RLHF [45, 51].
256   Xia et al. [45] extended DPO [35] by estimating the conditionally optimal policy directly from
257   model responses, enabling more granular and flexible policy shaping. Meanwhile, Zhong et al. [51]
258   calculated token-level rewards using a policy trained by DPO and then applied these rewards to
259   perform PPO. Unlike Xia et al. [45], our method employs a reinforced-style optimization approach [2],
260
      which, although more computationally intensive, provides stability on out-of-distribution (OOD) data.
      In contrast to Zhong et al. [51], our approach eliminates the need for an additional training phase for
261
      the reward model. We directly obtain token-level rewards from the original reward model by reusing
262
      its logit head, making the method simple, cost-effective, and efficient.

      Large Language Models. LLMs [22, 1, 39, 40, 3] have recently made significant strides in advancing
498
      the field of natural language processing, showcasing remarkable capabilities in language generation
499   and understanding. As these models have grown in scale, their proficiency in executing a range of
500   complex tasks—such as machine translation [47], summarization [37], and question answering [20,
501   43]—has increased, often achieving near human-like performance, especially when fine-tuned on
502   domain-specific datasets. Given their potential, the research community continues to push not only
503   for an expansion in the size of these LLMs but also for improvements in their efficiency, adaptability,
504   and alignment with human values. The integration of RLHF is one strategy aimed at refining the
505   behavior of LLMs by leveraging reward models that encapsulate human preferences [53, 5, 29, 37],
506   thereby enhancing the overall utility and safety of these powerful tools in real-world applications.
507   RLHF and Fine-grained Rewards. RLHF aims to refine the policy of language models to generate
508   content that aligns with human preferences. This approach has been explored to enhance capabilities
509   in various natural language processing applications, such as text summarization [37, 35], instruction
510   following [29], question answering [44], and reducing harmful outputs [38, 11]. Typically, these
511   studies involve gathering human evaluations of pairs of model-generated outputs based on one or
512   more desired traits. The goal is to train a reward model that can assess the overall quality of generated
513
      content during the reinforcement learning phase. However, such a reward signal in reinforcement
      learning is often sparse and delayed, making it challenging to determine the success of a segment
514
      or a token. Wu et al. [44] propose fine-grained RLHF, where the reward model can provide dense
515
      rewards for small text segments. This approach, however, depends on fine-grained human-labeled
516   datasets, which are labor-intensive to create. In this paper, we propose a method that automatically
517   redistributes the rewards to each token, thereby offering better guidance for optimizing LLMs.
518
519   6                   C ONCLUSION
520
521   This paper aims to explore methods for enhancing the performance of language models in RLHF
522   through fine-grained rewards, without relying on human labor. We introduce a reward redistribution
523   method named R3HF. Specifically, we conceptualize the reward model as a regression model and
524   calculate the token-level rewards in a time-difference manner, interpreting these rewards as the contri-
525
      butions of each token to the final output of the reward models. Our proposed reward redistribution
      is capable of eliciting the same optimal policy as traditional methods do, while also mitigating the
526
      issues of sparse and delayed rewards in certain contexts. Additionally, reward redistribution boasts
527
      scalability and can be seamlessly incorporated into most mainstream PPO-based methodologies.
528   In our empirical assessments, we test our method across different scenarios and with a variety of
529   methods. The results demonstrate both the effectiveness and superiority of our approach.
530
531
      Limitations and Future Work. This paper acknowledges several limitations. Firstly, it does
      not include human evaluation, opting instead for assessments via the advanced LLMs, GPT-4 [1].
532
      Although previous studies [35, 11] have shown that GPT-4’s judgments are consistent with those of
533
      humans, making it a reliable stand-in for human evaluation, this approach still represents a departure
534   from direct human feedback. Additionally, this research is confined to conducting a single round
535   of training for each task. While multi-round training is widely recognized and has demonstrated
536   effectiveness across various tasks [38, 11, 25], it was not employed in this study, as the primary
537   objective was to evaluate the effectiveness of reward redistribution. In future work, we aim to explore
538   reward redistribution in settings that involve multi-round training and deploy a broader range of LLMs.
539   Furthermore, we intend to extend our method to include large models with additional modalities to
      assess its versatility.

794   Traditional RLHF applies reinforcement learning at a specific token-level Markov Decision Process
795   (MDP) but often encounters issues with sparse and delayed rewards. Enlisting human efforts to label
796   high-quality data with fine-grained rewards is a usual and effective approach [44]. There is also a
797   growing interest in Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [35] , a method that has gained attention
798   due to its simplicity and absence of reward modeling requirements. Rafailov et al. [34] further posits
799   that DPO can be interpreted as a bandit problem where the model’s entire response is considered a
800   single option, and it can also learn per-token credit assignment.

887   reward model, we devised preference pairs that juxtapose rank one responses with those randomly
888   chosen from lower rankings.

893   assigning likert scale scores to assess the quality of individual summaries. We utilized the “axis”
894   part of the TL;DR dataset for the supervised fine-tuning and for applying reinforcement learning.
895   Conversely, the “comparisons” part was harnessed to train the reward model.

      learning processes within the scope of our harmfulness mitigation&helpfulness enhancement task.
