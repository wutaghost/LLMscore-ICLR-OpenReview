011                                             A BSTRACT
012
013           Drawing inspiration from the vulnerability of the initial feed-forward phase of bi-
014           ological perception in humans and primates to adversarial attacks, we propose a
015           novel defense strategy named Error Correcting by Agreement Checking (ECAC).
016
              This strategy is designed to mitigate realistic black-box threats where attackers
              don’t have full access to the model. We exploit the fact that natural and adversar-
017
              ially trained models rely on distinct feature sets for classification. Notably, nat-
018
              urally trained models retain commendable accuracy against adversarial examples
019           generated using adversarially trained models. Leveraging this disparity, ECAC
020           moves the input toward the prediction of the naturally trained model unless it leads
021           to disagreement in prediction between the two models, before making the predic-
022           tion. This simple error correction mechanism is highly effective against leading
023           SQA (Score-based Query Attacks) black-box attacks as well as decision-based
024           and transfer-based black-box attacks. We also verify that, unlike other black-box
025           defense, ECAC maintains significant robustness even when adversary has full ac-
026           cess to the model. We demonstrate its effectiveness through comprehensive exper-
027
              iments across various datasets (CIFAR and ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet
              as well as ViT).

109   it as ypredi = arg maxc fc (xi , θ ). In this text, we refer to naturally trained model as weakM as they

120   2.2   A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS AND DEFENSES
121
      Due to space constraints, we present the prominent adversarial attacks and defense methods in Ap-
122
      pendix D. We want to point out that most black-box defenses do not consider decision-based and
123
      transfer attacks. We believe this gives a false sense of security, as an adversary can easily bypass
124   such defenses by training a surrogate model. Szegedy et al. (2014); Sitawarin et al. (2024) further
125   underscore the relevance of defenses against transfer attacks.
126
127
      Related work: ECAC (ours) is an adaptive defense. Defenses that alter the input or model parame-
      ters or both during defense can be categorized as adaptive defense. ECAC alters the input by nudging
128
      it toward the expected correct class. Several existing works also nudge the input as our method, al-
129
      though for different reasons. Wu et al. (2021) nudges the input such that the cross-entropy summed
130   over all classes is maximized. However, as noted by Croce et al. (2022), this may often reduce the
131   accuracy when the input is near the decision boundary. Shi et al. (2021) nudges the input using self-
132   supervised signals while we leverage the disagreement between the strongM and weakM , i.e., we
133   use the signal from weakM as well. Tao et al. (2022) and Li et al. (2023) use input nudging during
134   the training time (and are not adaptive defenses).
135
      Qin et al. (2021) showed that simply adding Gaussian noise, of sufficient magnitude, provides non-
136   trivial accuracy for several SQA attacks. This defense is popularly known as RND. Chen et al.
137   (2022) have devised an adaptive method, dubbed AAA, most similar in spirit to ours, for defense
138   against black-box SQA. Instead of outputting true logits, they post-process it such that SQA locally
139   observes an increase in logits when it really decreases. Thus they are able to successfully mislead
140   the black-box SQA attackers. However, their method is limited to defense against SQA and does not
141   increase the correct decision area around an input, thus it remains exposed to the transfer attacks.

      3.1   M OTIVATION FROM BIOLOGICAL PERCEPTION : NEED FOR ERROR CORRECTION
146
147   Despite significant research in making neural network models robust, their robustness remains deci-
148   sively inferior to biological perception. While adversarially trained models seem to extract human-
149   interpretable features (Zhang & Zhu, 2019), it has been shown that even adversarially trained models
150   are brittle to real-world transformations like rotation and translation (Engstrom et al., 2019). Thus,
151   we ponder:
152
       Can feed-forward neural networks be adversarially robust, or are they intrinsically vulnerable?
153
154
155   Elsayed et al. (2018) showed that if adversarially crafted images are shown for a very brief moment
156   (about 71ms), they can even fool humans. However, humans easily predict the true class with ample
157   time (∼ 2 seconds). They attributed it to the top-down and lateral connections in human brains.
158   Guo et al. (2022) showed that artificial neural networks might already be more robust in represent-
159   ing the input than biological ones. They compared the IT layer in monkeys with the feature layer
160   of ResNet-50 and observed that adversarially trained ResNet-50’s encoding is less sensitive to ad-
161   versarial perturbation. Further, the attack on the biological system was not done using a white-box
      but a weaker black-box transfer attack, indicating that they are even more sensitive to the adversar-


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      ial attack. However, biological brains do not seem to be fooled by adversarial attacks. The paper
163   attributes this to some error-correcting mechanism of the brain not fully understood.
164
165   Inspired by the above observations, we propose a simple way to correct the input using backward
166
      propagation, described in detail in the following section. On the surface, it may crudely look similar
      to the suggested top-down mechanism of the neocortex (Hawkins & Sandra, 2004), but biological
167
      perception is much more sophisticated, and the comparison will not be apt.

170   3.1.1    O UR I NSIGHT
171
      While making a prediction, if an oracle could point us toward the correct class, we could nudge the
172
      input toward that class, thereby undoing the adversarial perturbations. We realize we could leverage
173   the disagreement between the strongM (adversarially trained model) and weakM (naturally trained
174   model) predictions as an oracle.
175
176
      As shown in Table 1, transferring an attack from an adversarially trained model to a naturally trained
      one is difficult. This is because the two models use different features. Naturally trained models
177
      often rely on smaller magnitude features (like texture) that are abundantly present in an image. This
178
      was demonstrated by Zhang & Zhu (2019), where they showed that if an image is divided into
179   tiles, and the tiles are shuffled, naturally trained models still manage to predict the true class, while
180   adversarially trained models fail. However, texture-like features are easier to remove by adversarial
181   attacks. Without adversarial training, the model has no incentive to learn better features (Tsipras
182   et al., 2018). On the contrary, adversarially trained models learn to use shape-aware features and
183   thus become indifferent toward the texture. We found that these two models can be combined to get
184   an overall more robust model.
185   We work with the assumption that when the two models agree, their agreed prediction tends to be
186   correct. This is based on the intuition that the models are trained to make the correct predictions
187   and the chance of both strongM and weakM predicting the same wrong class is low. We experimen-
188   tally validated this assumption for CIFAR-10 using the ResNet-18 model. We found that when an
189   adversary example is both crafted and nudged using jointM, then nudging towards the correct class
190   increases the agreement between the two models’ predictions from 34.98% to 49.20%, while if they
191   are nudged towards a random class, the agreement decreases to 16.99%. Further analysis of how and
192   when this assumption holds is given in Appendix A in the supplement. Based on this assumption,
193
      we design our defense as explained next.

297   3.4   D ESIGN C HOICES
298
299
      Given the observation that the two models use different sets of features, and only strongM is adver-
300   sarially robust, an intuitive design choice could be to nudge the input only when the models start
301   disagreeing on their prediction. This way, only strongM is exposed to the attacker till ECAC makes
302   the correct prediction. This approach boosts robustness but is nearly 10% less effective against sev-
303   eral SQA attacks, unless we fix the perturbation continuously. Therefore, we chose to continuously
304   correct the input and discard the nudging if it doesn’t lead to agreement.
305
      We always use strongM to output the logits. This way weakM confidence is kept hidden from the
306   attacker. Further, since it is much easier to fool the weakM model, if the two models disagree on their
307   prediction, we rely on the prediction made by strongM. We observe that if we use weakM instead
308   of strongM to output the logits, than the Square attack accuracy, for 100 iterations, on the first 1K
309   testset samples of CIFAR-10 drops from 83.50% to 71.80%.
310
      We also needed to select an appropriate model for nudging the inputs. To ensure the input remains
311
      within the correctly classified region for both the models (when it is being attacked), we nudge using
312   both in parallel. Specifically, we use jointM, which combines weakM and strongM, to perform the
313   nudging.
314
315
      To nudge the input, values for two parameters are needed: a) n steps: the number of PGD steps
      to nudge the input toward a class, and b) s size: the size of each such step. n steps × s size
316                                                                                                      8
      determines the amount of nudging. Since strongM is trained with a perturbation budget of ϵ = 255
317
      and for weakM, this value is 0, the right amount of nudging should be somewhere in between, i.e.,
318   around ϵ/2. We observed that the effect of doing multiple small steps could be approximated with
319   a single large step. Thus we set the value of n steps to 1 (and remove it as a parameter). Further,
320   as shown in the table 7 in section 4.3.1, we observed that the value of s size leads to a tradeoff
321   between transfer and black-box attacks. By experimenting on CIFAR-10 for ResNet-18, we freeze
322   on a value of 0.02 for s size. Thus we have s size = δ. For a slightly higher value, ECAC exhibits
323   slightly lower accuracy for transfer attacks but slightly higher accuracies for black-box attacks and
      vice versa.

507   4.4     L IMITATIONS
508
509   Our defense is limited to black-box setting, which includes decision based as well as transfer attacks.
510   Thus, the method is useful in many realistic scenarios where attackers lack complete access to
511
      deployed models. Further the model retains significant robustness even udner white-box attack.
512   Another weakness of the ECAC model is that it takes more time to make predictions than a simple
513   feed-forward model. A simple ResNet-18 model takes ≈ 1.6 seconds to classify the entire CIFAR-
514   10 test set, with a batch size of 500 using an RTX-2080 Ti graphics card, whereas ECAC takes ≈ 8.3
515   seconds. This is because it needs to make an extra forward and an extra backward pass. However, as
516   observed, the feed-forward part of biological perception also seems vulnerable to adversarial attacks
517
      and needs significantly more time to make a correct prediction. We believe that this weakness could
      be manageable for many safety-critical applications.
518
519
520   5     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
521
522   In this work, inspired by biological perception, we argue for the need to go beyond simple feed-
523   forward networks for adversarial robustness. We introduce a novel approach called ECAC, which
524   combines naturally and adversarially trained models to enhance robustness. ECAC uses the dis-
525   agreement between the two models as an oracle to nudge the input toward the correct class, simu-
526   lating an error-correction mechanism. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt in this
527   direction. We find that this technique significantly boosts black-box robustness, making it appealing
528   for real-world applications where attackers typically have limited access to deployed models.
529   This work highlights the potential of error-correction mechanisms to enhance adversarial robustness,
530   suggesting promising future research directions. One idea is to use a generative model to create an
531   image and then compare its similarity to the original image using, for instance, ℓ2 distance. This
532   approach, however, requires generating high-quality images, which can be challenging.

729   18 model, we found that when an adversary is both crafted and nudged using both strongM and
730   weakM in parallel (i.e., jointM), then nudging towards the correct class increases the agreement

      D    A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS AND DEFENSES
970
971
      We discuss the existing literature on Adversarial attacks and defense in this section.


                                                        18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Adversarial attacks: Popular white-box attacks include FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2014), PGD
973    (and targeted-PGD) (Kurakin et al., 2018; Madry et al., 2018), and its variants like AutoPGD
974    (APGD) (Croce & Hein, 2020b), FAB (Croce & Hein, 2020a), and C&W (Carlini & Wagner, 2017).
975    These are the strongest attacks as the adversary has complete access to the model, including its
976    parameters.
977
       In the real-world settings, attackers only have limited access to the model. If adversaries have access
978
       to the model’s confidence for each of the predicted class, they can progressively search for a better
979    adversary, called Score-based Query Attacks (SQA). Square attack (Andriushchenko et al., 2020) is
980    arguably the most popular attack of this kind. Other popular SQA attacks are Bandit (Ilyas et al.,
981    2018b), SimBA (Simple Black Box attack) (Guo et al., 2019), ZOO (Chen et al., 2017), SignHunter
982    (Al-Dujaili & O’Reilly, 2019), NES (Ilyas et al., 2018a) and others (Cheng et al., 2019), (Papernot
983    et al., 2017).
984
       In hard-label black-box attacks, also known as decision attacks, an attacker only knows the final
985    class predicted by the model. SPSA (Uesato et al., 2018), HopSkipJump (Chen et al., 2020), RayS
986    (Chen & Gu, 2020), and a few other (Ma et al., 2021; Shukla et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2018; Brendel
987    et al., 2018) attacks have been developed for this setting.
988
       In transfer attack setting, an attacker has access to a surrogate model and craft adversarial attacks
989
       using white-box attacks on the surrogate model. Due to transferability of the adversarial examples
990
       (Szegedy et al., 2014), such attacks often transfer to the adversary. Transfer attacks, when coupled
991    with above mentioned decision attacks can significantly reduce the number of queries needed by
992    the decision attacks. Sitawarin et al. (2024) further underscores the relevance of defenses against
993    transfer attacks. These attacks are most successful if surrogate model is trained on the same data
994    and has same architecture.
995
       Adversarial defense: Defenses based on Adversarial Training (AT), which incorporates adversarial
996
       samples during training, have been most successful so far. SAT (Madry et al., 2018) and TRADES
997    (Zhang et al., 2019) are the most popular defenses and several other defenses extend these methods
998    in some way: e.g. MART (Wang et al., 2019) GAIRAT (Zhang et al., 2020), HE (Pang et al., 2020;
999    Fakorede et al., 2023), MAIL (Liu et al., 2021), AWP (Wu et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2022). For an
1000   exhaustive survey of the field kindly refer to (Akhtar et al., 2021).
