011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We develop a novel data-driven framework as an alternative to dynamic flux bal-
014            ance analysis, bypassing the demand for deep domain knowledge and manual ef-
015
               forts to formulate the optimization problem. The proposed framework is end-to-
               end, which trains a structured neural ODE process (SNODEP) model to estimate
016
               flux and balance samples using gene-expression time-series data. SNODEP is de-
017
               signed to circumvent the limitations of the standard neural ODE process model,
018            including restricting the latent and decoder sampling distributions to be normal
019            and lacking structure between context points for calculating the latent, thus more
020            suitable for modeling the underlying dynamics of a metabolic system. Through
021            comprehensive experiments (156 in total), we demonstrate that SNODEP not only
022            predicts the unseen time points of real-world gene-expression data and the flux
023            and balance estimates well but can even generalize to more challenging unseen
024            knockout configurations and irregular data sampling scenarios, all essential for
025            metabolic pathway analysis. We hope our work can serve as a catalyst for build-
026
               ing more scalable and powerful models for genome-scale metabolic analysis.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      A distinctive characteristic of deep neural networks is their capability to implicitly learn complicated
031   features and dynamics from data, significantly saving human effort in composing those handcrafted
032   features and devising complex models. Therefore, there has been a growing interest in using them
033   in a variety of scientific contexts, such as quantum chemistry (von Glehn et al., 2022), tokamak
034   controller design (Degrave et al., 2022), climate sciences (Lam et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2023),
035   molecule generation (Hoogeboom et al., 2022) and drug discovery (Askr et al., 2023), to name a
036   few. For drug discovery problems in particular, it is essential to answer the questions of where and
037   how the drug should be targeted. The machine learning community has attracted increased atten-
038   tion in molecular design to address the latter question (Luo et al., 2022; Corso et al., 2022). On
039
      the other hand, metabolic pathway analysis techniques, such as flux balance analysis (FBA) (Orth
      et al., 2010) and dynamic FBA (Mahadevan et al., 2002), have been shown highly effective in find-
040
      ing drug targets (Sen & Orešič, 2023). These methods are widely used to study the effect of drugs
041
      or environmental stress simulated by gene knockouts on unwanted cells, such as cancer cells, by
042   curbing their metabolism (Raškevičius et al., 2018). Nevertheless, several key parameters, includ-
043   ing the optimization objective and constraints for the reaction flux in their linear programming (LP)
044   formulation, must be determined using domain expertise for each case, largely limiting their gener-
045   ality and scalability. In this work, we aim to develop scalable data-driven methods that can directly
046   predict the behavior of metabolic systems with time-varying flux, thus avoiding the manual effort
047   required to build FBA models.
048   More specifically, we achieve this by leveraging single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) time-
049   series data (Chen et al., 2019) and using single-cell flux estimation analysis (scFEA) technique from
050   Alghamdi et al. (2021) to estimate flux and balance of the metabolic system, because scRNA-seq
051   can churn out data in bulk, and getting time-series single-cell gene-expression data is much less
052   labor intensive than getting actual flux-balance time-series data. The challenge, however, lies in that
053   gene expression trajectories for individual cells cannot be tracked over time since cells die once their
      gene expression is read. Instead, we only have gene expression samples from different cells at each

054
      timestep, which can be viewed as samples from a time-varying distribution resembling a random
055   process. In fact, it’s well known that gene transcription is stochastic, especially when considered at
056   the single cell level (Thattai & Van Oudenaarden, 2001). Thus, the amounts of molecules produced,
057   or the chemical concentration, from a collection of cells can be considered to be sampled from some
058   distribution, with the amounts of mRNA molecules showing a Poisson-like behavior in a steady state
059   as shown in Thattai & Van Oudenaarden (2001).
060
      Since the time-varying metabolic concentrations are known to follow a non-linear ordinary differ-
061   ential equation (ODE), we propose a novel Structured Neural ODE Process (SNODEP) architecture
062   that is built on top of the standard neural ODE processes (Norcliffe et al., 2021) to predict the under-
063   lying dynamics of the metabolic system. We note that standard neural ODE processes have several
064   design choices that might not help to model the ODE dynamics in our case, like lack of structure in
065   the encoder to get the latent distribution from the context points and the use of Gaussian parametric
066   family for latent posterior and decoder distributions. Consequently, we design the architecture of
067   SNODEP to bypass these shortcomings, showing improved performance in tasks such as predicting
068   gene-expression distributions on unseen timesteps, predicting metabolic-flux and metabolic-balance
069
      distribution on unseen timesteps, and predicting the corresponding distributions for gene-knockout
      cases, considering both regularly and irregularly sampled data, all for several metabolic pathways.
070
071
      Contributions. We formulate the prediction problem of metabolic flux and balance as a stochastic
072
      neural processing task, where the goal is to learn the underlying dynamics by predicting their time-
073   varying distributions under different configurations (Section 2). We propose an end-to-end training
074   framework, which first defines the intermediary steps required to estimate metabolic flux and bal-
075   ance from scRNA-seq data and then learns a novel SNODEP model that can predict the unseen time
076   points of flux and balance and their dynamics under gene-knockout configurations (Section 3.2).
077   The proposed SNODEP architecture is designed by addressing a few limitations of the standard ar-
078   chitecture of neural ODE processes (Section 3.1); thus, it is more suitable to model the time-varying
079   distributions from metabolic systems. Comprehensive experiments on real-world datasets and vari-
080   ous metabolic pathways demonstrate that SNODEP is highly effective in modeling the dynamics of
081
      gene expressions and predicting metabolic flux and balance, consistently outperforming alternative
      models such as standard neural ODE processes (Sections 4.2-4.4). We also showcase the superiority
082
      of SNODEP under gene-knockout variations and scenarios with irregularly sampled data (Section
083
      4.5), suggesting its versatility and strong potential in solving challenges in biomedical domains.

086   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
087
088
      Metabolic Pathway Analysis. Genome-scale metabolic models (GSMMs) have proven to be pow-
089
      erful tools in the design of therapeutic treatments. For instance, Raškevičius et al. (2018) employed
090   GSMMs to identify therapeutic windows for cancer treatment, while Larsson et al. (2020) used them
091   to simulate gene knockouts in a Glioblastoma cancer cell model, identifying potential therapeutic
092   targets and predicting side effects in healthy brain tissue. Despite their importance, GSMMs are
093   time-consuming and require significant domain expertise to build. Recent studies have explored
094   integrating machine learning techniques with GSMMs, as reviewed in Sahu et al. (2021). From a
095   dynamical standpoint, Costello & Martin (2018) framed pathway dynamics prediction as a machine
096   learning problem, using XGBoost models to predict such dynamics, but their framework is not end-
097   to-end. More recently, Aghaee et al. (2024) introduced a graph neural network model to simulate
098
      the dynamic behavior of metabolites in oxidative stress pathways in bacterial cell cultures for syn-
      thetic data. In addition, RNA velocity (La Manno et al., 2018) estimates the time derivative of gene
099
      expressions but needs spliced and unspliced mRNA counts, usually not reported in the experiments.
100
      Similarly, Klumpe et al. (2023) investigated single-cell time series prediction, albeit also using syn-
101   thetic data with no specific focus on metabolic pathways. To the best of our knowledge, our work is
102   the first to comprehensively study the dynamically varying flux and balance of metabolic pathways
103   derived from real-world single-cell gene expression time-series data.
104
105   Neural ODE. The neural ODE family of models has shown strong capabilities in modeling dynamic
106   systems, particularly when the underlying dynamics are known to follow an ODE (Rubanova et al.,
107   2019). While latent neural ODEs have been applied to interpolation and extrapolation tasks, they
      are not suitable for modeling random processes. In contrast, neural processes (NP) (Garnelo et al.,

130   2   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
131
132   Classical methods like DFBA estimate time-varying metabolic flux and balance by solving an op-
133
      timization problem to maximize the biomass at each timestep (see Appendix B.1 for more details).
      Our work proposes to directly train a model on scFEA-estimated flux-balance values until a certain
134
      timestep and then predict the distributions of gene expression, flux, and balance in future timesteps,
135
      expecting that the trained model will learn the underlying dynamics. Figure 1 illustrates the overview
136   of our pipeline. Due to page limits, we defer more details on scFEA proposed by Alghamdi et al.
137   (2021) to Appendix B.2. Below, we provide detailed descriptions of our problem setup. The key
138   notations and their descriptions are provided in Appendix A.
139
140   Predicting Gene Expression, Flux and Balance. Suppose we have a gene count matrix of dimen-
141   sion K × N , where N is the total number of cells and K is the total number of genes, with gene
142   counts measured at each regular timestep t and total V timesteps. Let Bt be the index
                                                                                        P set repre-
143   senting the cells whose gene counts ∈ RK are observed at time t. Then, we have t |Bt | = N ,
144   indicating that all N cells get their expressions counted over various timesteps.
145   For a metabolic pathway, we only extract the relevant d genes from the total set of K genes. Let
146   gi,t ∈ Rd be the gene-expression array for cell i ∈ Bt at time t, and Gt ∈ Rd×|Bt | be the corre-
147   sponding matrix. For a certain metabolic pathway with u modules and v metabolites and each batch
148   Bt of cells at time t, we estimate the flux mf and balance mb using the scFEA framework detailed
149   in Appendix B.2. Specifically, we define:
150
151          • Stf : {gi,t }i∈Bt → {mfi,t }i∈Bt as the mapping that estimates the flux mfi,t ∈ Ru for each
152            cell i based on its gene expression. Let Mft ∈ Ru×|Bt | be the matrix of the flux samples.
153          • Stb : {gi,t }i∈Bt → {mbi,t }i∈Bt as the analogous mapping for estimating the metabolic
154
               balance mbi,t ∈ Rv . Let Mbt ∈ Rv×|Bt | be the matrix of the balance samples.
155
156   We note that scFEA was originally developed for static-FBA, but since the static-DFBA formulation
157   (Equation 4) can be interpreted as solving the static-FBA for different timesteps, we use scFEA to
158   estimate flux-balance values for different timesteps.
159
160   Gene-knockout. Gene knockout is a way to understand how a gene influences the metabolic net-
161   work, for example, in understanding how essential genes in pathogens affect metabolic pathways
      to design drugs to inhibit those pathways (Larsson et al., 2020); it’s also widely used in synthetic


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      biology Dalvie et al. (2021). In the gene-knockout simulations in FBA models, the constraints of
163   the reaction fluxes affected by essential genes are usually modified (Maranas & Zomorrodi, 2016).
164   In contrast, we train a model on certain gene-knockout configurations and then predict the distribu-
165   tion on unseen configurations and timesteps. To simulate gene-knockout conditions, we randomly
166   sample S subsets of k most expressed genes, set the gene-expression of genes from those subsets
167   to zero (See Algorithm 1 for more details), and estimate the flux-balance values again based on the
168   scFEA techniques. For s ∈ {1, 2, . . . , S}, we analogously define {m̃fi,t }s,i∈Bt and {m̃bi,t }s,i∈Bt
169   as gene-knockout flux and balance estimates, respectively, where we use M̃fs,t ∈ Ru×|Bs,t | and
170
      M̃bs,t ∈ Rv×|Bs,t | to denote the corresponding matrix of samples.
171
172   Essentially, our framework assumes that metabolic flux and balance from scRNA-seq data can be es-
173   timated using scFEA techniques, that knocking out a subset of genes does not change the expression
174   levels of the rest of the genes, and that gene essentiality and gene expression levels are correlated.
175

      Learning Objective. For each timestep t ∈ {t1 , t2 , . . . , tV }, we collect samples of gene ex-
177   pression {gi,t }i∈Bt , flux {mfi,t }i∈Bt and balance {mbi,t }i∈Bt and their gene-knockout samples
178   {{m̃fi,t }j,i∈Bt , {m̃bi,t }j,i∈Bt } with cells Bt using previous steps. We assume these samples are drawn
179   from some underlying distributions corresponding to gene expression G(θg (t)), flux M f (θf (t)),
180   balance M b (θb (t)) and their gene knockout versions {M̃ f (θf (t)), M̃ b (θb (t))}, respectively. The
181   goal is to learn a model F : t → Y (θt ) that can predict the underlying dynamics of time-varying dis-
182   tributions, which depend on some latent distribution L. In our setup, F is considered as an encoder-
183   decoder neural network, with a different network for each distribution in {G, M f , M b , M̃ f , M̃ b }.
184
      Let C < T < V be the length of context, target, and total available time points, respectively. Given a
185
      distribution Y , let yi ∼ Y (θt ) for any i ∈ {1, . . . , V }. When we say yi ∼ Y (θt ), it means a random
186   sample from the set {yi,t }i∈Bt . During training, our model’s encoder takes as input the context data,
187   which includes samples from context points C = {(t1 , y1 ), . . . , (tC , yC )}. The decoder then predicts
188   samples from the target points T = {(t1 , y1 ), . . . , (tT , yT )}. During inference, the model is used to
189   predict every timestep available, including hitherto unseen timesteps V = {(t1 , y1 ), . . . , (tV , yV )}.
190   In the following discussions, we denote IC = {1, . . . , C} and IT = {1, . . . , T } for simplicity.

      In this work, we have shown how to get the time-varying metabolic flux of a system using genomics
479
      data rather than metabolomics data, which is much harder to procure. Through our framework, we
480   intend to use the learned dynamics to generate quantities from future time steps and unseen gene-
481   knockout configurations without any particular domain expertise. Nevertheless, we want to point out
482   that our results with respect to flux and balance and their corresponding gene-knockout results are
483   on data estimated via scFEA. Ideally, we would’ve preferred a gene-expression time-series that was
484   sampled keeping metabolic pathways in mind, meaning time-series for normal conditions and sev-
485   eral metabolic stresses, along with ground-truth metabolic flux and balance measurement. Such an
      experiment should also have the alternative DFBA formulation available so that we can benchmark

777
778   B.1   F LUX BALANCE A NALYSIS
779
780
      Flux balance analysis (FBA) is by far the most widely used computational method for analyzing
      stoichiometric-based genome-scale metabolic models. In this section, we introduce the optimization
781
      formulation of FBA and the meaning of the terms in the formulation. We refer readers to Maranas
782
      & Zomorrodi (2016) for a more detailed description.
783
784   Static FBA. In its most general form, FBA is formulated as an LP problem (Dantzig, 2002) max-
785   imizing or minimizing a linear combination of reaction fluxes subject to the conservation of mass,
786   thermodynamic, and capacity constraints. The most widely used objective function in the FBA of
787   metabolic networks is the maximization of the biomass reaction flux built upon the assumption that
788   a cell is striving to maximally allocate all its available resources towards growth or maximizing the
789   biomass, which is a predefined reaction that includes all the components required for cell growth
790   like amino acids, nucleotides, lipids, and cofactors, etc. The formulation is as follows:
791                                                         X
792                                 maximize z =                    cj vj
793                                                    j∈{biomass}

797
798   Equation 3 assumes that the time constants for metabolic reactions are very small, hence a pseudo
799   steady state constraint S · v = 0. Here, z is the combination of reaction fluxes involved in the
800   biomass, vj is the j-th reaction flux, I is the set of all reactants, J is the set of the reactions, Sij
801   is the stoichiometric coefficient of reactant i in reaction j. LBj and UBj are the lower and upper
802   bounds on the rate of flux for reaction j, which depend on several factors such as reversibility based
803   on Gibbs Free Energy (∆G), type of reaction, etc. These constraint values typically are hard to
804   determine since they need to be meticulously determined experimentally for each case.
805   Depending on the use case, the constraints and the objective change. For example, in the case of
806   simulating gene knockouts, we set vj = 0, ∀j ∈ J KO , and gauge the gene essentiality via the GPR
807   relationships (see Figure 7), by targeting the biomass to be less than a threshold (no cell growth)
808   when we want to kill cells, e.g., cancer cells. In metabolic engineering, we want overproduction of
809   a target metabolic so our objective changes accordingly, and we can try different substrates, growth
      conditions, and gene knockout/knockin combinations towards overproduction.

      Dynamic FBA. There are, however, processes where the time constants of the reactions are higher,
811   like in the case of transcriptional regulation (minutes) or cellular growth (several minutes or hours)
812   (Maranas & Zomorrodi, 2016). In such cases, the steady-state assumption S · v = 0 isn’t valid.
813   Mahadevan et al. (2002) forego this steady-state assumption and study dynamic flux balance analysis

886   D    E FFECT OF VARYING CONTEXT LENGTH
887
888   To perform our experiments in Section 4, we need to know how many context-target timesteps our
889   model needs to be able to predict the remaining time steps properly. For this, we used the gene-
890   expression data of the M171 pathway since it is the largest, and for a context length C, our extra
891   target length is C//2. In Figure 8, we see that our model is able to learn optimally after C = 6
892   context steps or C + C//2 = 9 total steps. With little context, the model essentially learns next-
893   step prediction (e.g., for C = 2 or 3, C//2 = 1), which does not perform well. This experiment
894   thus validates that with enough context, our model can learn the latent dynamics and can be used to
895
      generate data from future unseen timesteps.
