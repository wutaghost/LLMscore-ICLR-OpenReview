011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models (DMs) have demonstrated exceptional performance across vari-
014            ous generative tasks, yet they also face significant security and privacy concerns,
015            such as Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs), where adversaries attempt to de-
016
               termine whether specific images were part of the DM’s training set. These threats
               present serious risks, particularly as pre-trained DMs are increasingly accessi-
017
               ble online. To address these privacy concerns, we begin by investigating how
018
               fine-tuning DMs on a manipulated self-synthesized dataset affects their genera-
019            tive privacy risks, and have the following observations: (1) DMs fine-tuned solely
020            on self-synthesized clean images are more vulnerable to privacy attacks (2) DMs
021            fine-tuned on perturbed self-synthesized images become more robust against pri-
022            vacy attacks but exhibit degraded image generation quality. Based on the observa-
023            tions, we propose MixSyn, a simple and effective framework designed to mitigate
024            privacy risks by fine-tuning DMs on a mixed self-synthesized dataset, which is a
025            mixture of clean and perturbed synthetic images. Extensive experimental results
026            demonstrate that our method significantly mitigates the generative privacy risks of
027
               DMs while preserving their original image generation quality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Text-to-Image Diffusion Models (DMs), such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), DALL-E
033
      3 (Ramesh et al., 2022), and Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022), have demonstrated outstanding perfor-
      mance in generating high-quality images. With the rapid advancements in DMs, their usage has
034
      significantly expanded across individuals and organizations for various applications. For instance,
035
      Stable Diffusion v1.4 has been downloaded more than 8 million times from the Huggingface repos-
036   itory, while Midjourney currently serves over a million users (Fatunde & Tse, 2022).
037
038   While diffusion models (DMs) demonstrate impressive capabilities in generating high-quality im-
039
      ages, their deployment raises serious privacy concerns and copyright issues. Recent studies (Duan
      et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023; Abascal et al., 2023) have identified that DMs are vulnerable to
040
      privacy leakage, particularly through Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs) (Shokri et al., 2016).
041
      MIAs attempt to determine whether a given data sample was part of the model’s training set (mem-
042   ber sample) or came from the hold-out set (non-member sample). When MIAs target DMs, they
043   can potentially expose sensitive or private images used during pretraining, such as personal profile
044   photos, medical images, or proprietary data from commercial entities. As artists increasingly form
045   unions to combat the unauthorized use of their works by commercial generative models, the need
046   for thorough security assessments and risk evaluations prior to the public release of these models
047   becomes ever more pressing. Unfortunately, to make things worse, recent work S2L (Li et al., 2024)
048   reveal that fine-tuning DMs on clean self-synthesized images could further amplify the power of
049   MIA and make DMs more vulnerable to privacy attacks. Their findings show that the privacy risk
050
      with diffusion models is even more severe than previously recognized, and it is even more challeng-
      ing to develop a robust defense framework against privacy attacks.
051
052   To address the above concerns, we aim to develop a defense framework that protects DMs from these
053   privacy attacks. Our key motivation is that fine-tuning DMs on a manipulated self-synthesized
      dataset could affect their privacy risks. Despite the finding that fine-tuning DMs on clean self-

      Privacy attacks against generative models. The privacy risks associated with large generative
      models have become a significant concern, primarily due to their reliance on vast collections of web
137
      images for training, which may inadvertently include sensitive information. Recent studies have
138
      demonstrated that diffusion models are particularly susceptible to Membership Inference Attacks
139   (MIAs) (Shokri et al., 2017). In such attacks, an adversary aims to determine whether a given data
140   sample was included in the model’s training set (i.e., a member) or as part of the hold-out set (i.e., a
141   non-member). For example, Hu & Pang (2023) employs the loss function LDM to infer the mem-
142   bership status of input samples. Similarly, Wu et al. (2022) explores this vulnerability under the
143   assumption of distinct distributions for member and non-member samples, thereby simplifying the
144   inference task. Furthermore, Carlini et al. (2022) highlights that the privacy risks for diffusion mod-
145   els are substantially greater compared to GAN-based models. More recently, SecMI (Duan et al.,
146   2023) has been introduced as a query-based MIA that assesses membership by analyzing the con-
147
      sistency of posterior estimates during the forward process at each timestep. Additionally, Proximal
      Initialization Attack (PIA) (Kong et al., 2023) offers a more efficient query-based MIA, leveraging
148
      the ground truth trajectory initialized at t = 0 and the predicted point to infer membership. These
149
      advancements underscore the pressing need for improved privacy protections in diffusion models,
150   as these attacks pose significant concerns to the society.

      4     M ETHOD
359
360
      The motivation behind our proposed framework, MixSyn, is to mitigate the increased vulnerability
361   to Membership Inference Attacks (MIA) that arise when diffusion models (DMs) are fine-tuned on
362   clean, self-synthesized datasets. From our earlier observations in Table 1 and Table 2, DMs fine-
363   tuned on clean datasets exhibit higher risks to MIAs, while fine-tuning on fully perturbed datasets
364   significantly reduces privacy risks but at the cost of image quality. Our approach aims to strike a
365   balance by mixing clean and perturbed datasets, preserving image quality while mitigating privacy
366   vulnerabilities.
367
368   4.1   M IXUP -I AND M IXUP -P
369
370
      We propose two novel methods, Mixup-I and Mixup-P, to combine clean and perturbed images in
      a way that minimizes privacy risks while maintaining the generative quality of DMs:
371
372          • Mixup-I (Image-based Mixing): This method applies perturbations at the image level. In
373            this approach, a fixed ratio of images in the dataset is perturbed, while the remaining images
374            are left unaltered. This introduces randomness in terms of which images are perturbed,
375            making it harder for MIAs to infer training data while preserving the quality of the non-
376            perturbed images. For instance, with a mixup ratio of 0.5, half of the images in the dataset
377            will be perturbed, reducing overall privacy risks without heavily distorting the generated
               image set.

410

414
      SecMI drops from 0.715 (pre-trained) to 0.552 (DreamBooth), highlighting Mixup-I’s effectiveness

420   privacy risks, while higher ratios enhance privacy protection at the cost of image quality. A mixup
421   ratio of 0.5 offers an optimal balance, significantly reducing the AUC for SecMI while preserving
422   image quality close to the pre-trained model.

      In this paper, We empirically examine the effect of self-synthesized data fine-tuning on DMs regard-
479
      ing their privacy risks. We observe that DMs fine-tuned solely on self-synthesized clean images are
480   more vulnerable to privacy attacks, whereas DMs fine-tuned on perturbed self-synthesized images
481   become more robust against such attacks but exhibit degraded image generation quality. Based on
482   the observation, we propose MixSyn, a simple and effective framework designed to mitigate pri-
483   vacy risks by fine-tuning DMs on a mixed self-synthesized dataset. Extensive experimental results
484   demonstrate that our method significantly reduces the efficacy of privacy attacks on DMs while pre-
485   serving the original image generation quality. We believe our work takes a further step to the privacy
      protection of DMs.

568
569   Threat Model The threat model assumes the presence of an adversary A who interacts with a pre-
570   trained diffusion model G designed for text-to-image synthesis, with the objective of extracting
571   private information embedded within its training dataset D.
572
      Victim Model Conditional diffusion models G for text-to-image synthesis are gaining increasing
573
      popularity due to the accessibility of semantic text inputs, which allow individuals without spe-
574
      cialized expertise to generate complex visual content easily. The details of the victim model is
575   elaborated in Appendix.
576
577   Adversary Goals. The adversary uses the target prompts {pz } as input, aiming to extract private
578
      information linked to the target domains Dz from the pre-training set D of G. We consider two
      primary attack objectives in the privacy literature: 182 Membership Inference: Given an image xi ,
579
      the adversary seeks to determine whether xi is part of the training set D. Membership leakage
580
      can theoretically correspond to generic privacy leakage under the framework of Differential Privacy
581   Zanella-Béguelin et al. (2023). In certain scenarios, Membership Inference Attacks (MIA) can
582   directly result in a privacy breach. For example, a patient’s clinical record could be used to train
583   a disease-associated model. 183 Data Extraction: The adversary aims to retrieve training images
584   from G within a targeted domain Dz associated with a prompt pz .
585
      Adversary Capabilities. We assume the attacker has the ability to manipulate the dataset used for
586
      fine-tuning the diffusion model. This assumption can hold in two situations: First, if the diffusion
587   model is publicly available, attackers can perform any operations on the model, including arbitrary
588   fine-tuning. Second, there is a growing trend where many model vendors keep the model parameters
589   confidential but permit users to upload data for fine-tuning. For instance, OpenAI allows fine-tuning
590   of DALL-E models via an API2 .
