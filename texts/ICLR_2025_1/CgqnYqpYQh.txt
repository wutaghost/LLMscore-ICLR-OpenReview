009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                With the rise and ubiquity of larger deep learning models, the need for high-quality
012                compression techniques has been growing in order to deploy these models widely.
013                The sheer parameter count of some models makes it difficult to fit them into the
014                memory constraints of different hardware. In this work, we present a novel ap-
015                proach to model compression by merging similar parameter groups within a model,
016                rather than pruning away less important parameters. Specifically, we propose a
017                straightforward method for selecting, aligning, and merging separate feed-forward
018                sublayers in Transformer models, and test our method on a language modeling task,
019
                   image classification, and machine translation. With our method, we demonstrate
                   performance comparable to the original models across our three diverse tasks while
020
                   combining more than a third of model feed-forward sublayers. For instance, we can
021
                   remove over 21% of total parameters from a Vision Transformer, while maintaining
022                99% of its original performance. Additionally, we observe that some feed-forward
023                sublayers often exhibit regions of high similarity between their activations, which
024                may help explain their surprising mergeability. 1

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Recent advances in deep learning have been marked by a stark increase in model size in order to
030   achieve state-of-the-art performance. With this trend towards increasing model parameter count
031   to improve results, there is a need for more high-quality compression techniques that balance
032   compression effectiveness with model capability. These techniques can help facilitate the use of these
033   models across a variety of inference settings, depending on hardware availability.
034
      Much of the prior work in model compression has built upon on distillation, quantization, and
035   pruning techniques (Hinton et al., 2015; Fiesler et al., 1990; LeCun et al., 1989). Regarding pruning,
036   prior work has introduced many approaches that identify regions of prunable parameters that can
037   be removed from the model without drastically changing performance. These techniques can target
038   individual neurons or general regions of a model—like attention heads, blocks from layers, or
039   even entire layers. (Voita et al., 2019; Lagunas et al., 2021; Sajjad et al., 2023). However, while
040   “unimportant” features have been important to identify for pruning techniques, we can also exploit the
041   notion of “redundant” features as a compression target. There has been far less focus on compression
042   methods that target potentially redundant features within a model.
043   When targeting redundant features for compression, we can turn to merging sets of similar parameters
044   rather than pruning them. Relatedly, a line of recent work has explored merging parameters from
045   two or more separate models in order to combine their functionalities into a single model (Goddard
046   et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a). In our case, we can imagine extending parameter merging to merge
047   sublayers within one model, rather than separate models, in order to achieve model compression.
048   To this end, we propose a novel compression method that aligns and merges several feed-forward
049   sublayers within Transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017). We target feed-forward sublayers
050   in particular due to their large parameter count and easy mergeability. With a small amount of
051   recovery fine-tuning, our models quickly regain competitive performance at reduced parameter counts.
052   Through our testing, we find that these groups of feed-forward sublayers are notably compressible

098   2.1       W EIGHT SHARING FOR SMALLER MODELS
099
100   Prior work on weight sharing has largely focused on training models from scratch with specific sharing
101

      count, but more importantly to provide important gradient sharing patterns for better generalization
102
      for many language tasks (Press & Wolf, 2017; Inan et al., 2017). In the case of non-embedding
103
      Transformer layers, prior work has explored numerous weight tying patterns for training new models
104   (Dehghani et al., 2019; Reid et al., 2021; Takase & Kiyono, 2023). Liu et al. (2024) use heavy weight
105   sharing at initialization between Transformer layers to achieve state-of-the-art sub-billion parameter
106   language models. Pires et al. (2023) specifically tie feed-forward sublayers at initialization and train

113   2.2     P RUNING AND REDUNDANCY
114
115   Prior work has explored different aspects of redundancy patterns between adjacent Transformer
116
      components, and suggested several techniques to reduce or exploit this phenomenon. Dalvi et al.
      (2020) use CKA to track layer redundancy in BERT and XLNet and correlation clustering to find
117
      redundant sets of neurons. Using the discovered clusters, they remove redundant neurons for fewer
118
      total parameters. Men et al. (2024); Gromov et al. (2024) show that by removing entire Transformer
119   layers in decoder-only models, very deep language models can achieve inference speedups without
120   sacrificing major performance. Li et al. (2024) propose a compression method applicable to sparsely-
121   activated mixture-of-expert models (SMoEs) that similarly draws from model merging work to
122   compress experts in large SMoE models. Our method extends a similar approach to a much wider set
123   of compressible models.

      3     M ERGING F EED -F ORWARD S UBLAYERS
137
138   In this section, we discuss choosing feed-forward sublayers as our merging target, review necessary
139   background for permutation-based neuron alignment, and then describe our compression method.
140
141
      3.1     F EED - FORWARD SUBLAYERS AS A MERGING TARGET
142
143   We focus our interest on Transformer feed-forward (FF) sublayers for several reasons. The first,
144   and most simple, is that these sublayers generally constitute around two-thirds of non-embedding
145   parameters Transformer encoder or decoder models. Compressing these parameters can constitute
146   substantial overall savings in a model. Secondly, as we consider merging these parameters, we
147   note that the parameterization of FF sublayers is far simpler than the other major sub-block of a
148   Transformer layer, namely multi-headed attention (MHA). This structural simplicity makes it a good
149   candidate for merging-based approaches for compression.
150   Beyond practical considerations, prior work has also established several properties of Transformer FF
151   sublayers that make them good candidates for compression via merging. Prior work on FF sublayers
152   has shown that they can be very sparsely activated (Li et al., 2023), where non-zero percentages of
153   FF activations can be as low as 3-5%. Additionally, another work has demonstrated evidence that
154   combining LayerNorm and feed-forwards, in both Post- and Pre-LN architectures, results in some
155   weakening effects of the contextualization effects of FF sublayers (Kobayashi et al., 2024). The
156   authors allude to redundancy in the Transformer’s processing due to this interaction. Finally, Pires
157
      et al. (2023) train Transformer-based translation models with only one feed-forward sublayer in the
      encoder, tied across each layer. Their models, when trained with extended FF widths, can outperform
158
      base transformers at the same parameter budget.

      3.2      BACKGROUND ON PERMUTATION - BASED NEURON ALIGNMENT
163
164   We propose a merging technique that combines several similar sublayers into a single parameter
165   set. Our merging technique is inspired by prior work in permutation symmetries of neurons (Li
166   et al., 2015). This type of prior work has been widely used in studying convergent learning between
167   models, as well as performing model merging between two or more separate models (Tatro et al.,
168   2020; Entezari et al., 2022; Ainsworth et al., 2023).
169   Permutation-based neuron alignment techniques seek to find a superior ordering of neurons in one
170   layer in order to more closely match the ordering of neurons from another layer. Given two layers
171   with neurons we wish to align, we compute a forward pass through both of these layers using relevant
172   data in order to collect features. The layers are generally corresponding parameters from two different
173   models. We collect these sets of activations, Xα , Xβ ∈ Rn×d , from the output of the two parameter
174   sets, where n is the number of tokens or patches processed in the forward pass, and d is the feature
175   dimension.
176   To determine corresponding features from these activation sets, we compute cross-correlation C, in
177   line with prior work (Li et al., 2015). µ represents mean vectors, and σ standard deviation vectors.

192   3.3      C OMBINING FEED - FORWARD SUBLAYERS
193
194   Now, with the appropriate background, we describe our compression method. For our method, we
195   first assume that we have some predetermined number of feed-forward sublayers k that we want
196   to merge. This number can be inferred if given a goal overall parameter reduction ratio, or set
197   otherwise. In summary, our compression method aligns the ordering of the neurons between the two
198   feed-forward sublayers in order to merge them.
199   Given a window of k adjacent feed-forward sublayers, we compute a forward pass using a subset of
200   data in order to compute features for each feed-forward hidden state. In other words, for Transformer
201   FF sublayer xout = W out ϕ(W in xin + bin ) + bout , we obtain features just before the ϕ activation. We
202   consider only the neurons just after W in because prior work has shown that to reorder the input to
203   W in and output of W out requires permuting many additional weights due to the residual connections
204   in order to maintain functional equivalence (Verma & Elbayad, 2024). For each of the k feed-forward
205   sublayers, we collect features Xi ∈ Rn×d i ∈ [0, k − 1], where n is the number of tokens or patches
206
      processed, and d is the feed-forward dimension.4
207   We designate the first feed-forward sublayer of the set to be our “anchor”, and we compute the
208   permutation finding algorithm on each pair of feed-forwards where the first item is always the anchor.
209   In other words, for each sublayer i ∈ [1, k − 1], we have inputs X0 and Xi , and find πi using the
210   permutation finding algorithm from Section 3.2.
211   After converting function πi to its corresponding permutation matrix Pi , we can apply them to each
212   of the corresponding k − 1 feed-forward sublayers. We then average the transformed weight matrices,
213   and replace each of the k feed-forwards with their average, as in Equation sets 3 and 4. Finally, we tie
214   these weights so that in memory they appear as just one sublayer, effectively removing the parameters

      3.4    S ELECTING SUBLAYERS TO MERGE
225
226   In selecting the k adjacent feed-forward sublayers to merge, we take a sliding window approach. For
227   all starting layer indices from 0 to (Nlayers − 1) − k, we apply the method outlined in Section 3.3,
228   and evaluate the resulting compressed model on a validation set.
229
      In reality, although we propose to test each potential window, the cost of computing permutations
230
      and parameter arithmetic is low. The largest cost each iteration is computing features and testing
231   candidates. However, we only compute features once despite testing Nlayers − k models, because one
232   forward pass through the exemplar data is sufficient for creating all necessary correlation matrices.
233   We test these potential candidates and choose the one with best starting evaluation score. We note
234   that there may be other possible selection heuristics in this setting.
235
      Finally, we follow our merging procedure with recovery fine-tuning to quickly heal performance on

257   Transformer-based models. Specifically, we use GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019), the Vision Transformer

      4.2   I MAGE CLASSIFICATION WITH V I T
280
281   We use a vision transformer (ViT) for our image classification experiments, with resolution of
282   224x224, and patch size of 16x16.. ViT is a 12-layer Transformer Encoder architecture that is
283   pre-trained on ImageNet-21k, and subsequently fine-tuned on ImageNet-1k. ImageNet-1k is a
284   classification task where images belong to one of 1000 categories (Russakovsky et al., 2015). For
285   computing feed-forward features, we use 10k patches from the ImageNet-1k validation set. Evaluation
286   results are computed on original validation labels.
287   We fine-tune our ViT models on ImageNet-1k train for up to 50k steps with a batch size of 128, and

      4.3   M ACHINE TRANSLATION
291
292   For our experiments on machine translation, we use a Chinese-English model from the OPUS-MT
293   release (Tiedemann & Thottingal, 2020). It is a 12-layer encoder-decoder Transformer with cross-
294   attention. For computing feed-forward features, we use 10k tokens from the Tatoeba validation set,
295   counted on the source side (Tiedemann, 2020). For fine-tuning, we use the original training data
296   released by the Tatoeba translation challenge, sourced from OPUS (Tiedemann, 2012). We apply our
297   method to both the encoder and decoder separately, constituting two anchors. However, we search
298   windows in sync, meaning that the same window from the encoder and decoder are merged.
299   We fine-tune our translation models for up to 100k steps with a batch size of 64. We use sacrebleu
300   to compute BLEU scores for evaluation (Papineni et al., 2002; Post, 2018).
301
302   4.4   L AYER PRUNING BASELINE
303
304   Recent work on structured pruning of Transformers has been marked by a large number of methods
305   presenting ways to remove adjacent layers from models and then optionally fine-tune the compressed
306   model (Men et al., 2024; Gromov et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b). We focus on structured pruning
307   baselines as many unstructured pruning baselines do not necessarily lead to smaller memory require-
308   ments unless they achieve 1) high sparsity ratios and 2) use specialized sparse libraries to store sparse
309
      model weights. Our method, on the other hand, is realized memory-wise as a smaller model due to
      shared weights only being stored once.
310
311   Many layer-pruning methods rely on a similarity technique to choose a subset of adjacent and similar
312   layers to prune. However, in our baseline, we forgo any specific similarity techniques to choose
313   a subset, and instead choose the best possible subset, much like our own technique, via a sliding
314   window. After selecting the best window after evaluation, we then fine-tune the model with the
315
      same specifications as our method. In all, this encapsulates a strong, structured pruning baseline that
      generalizes many layer-pruning based techniques.

      5.4   A DDITIONAL COMPRESSION VIA QUANTIZATION
442
443   We are interested in seeing if our method works well in combination with other compression methods.
444   For example, quantization is an extremely effective method to reduce the memory footprint of a
445   model by reducing the numerical precision of the parameters. While our compression method focuses
446   on identifying redundancies to reduce the overall parameter count via parameter sharing, quantization
447   can help reduce the overall storage needed for a model, and still proves an extremely effective
448   compression technique. Therefore, we wish to ensure that our method performs orthogonally to
449
      state-of-the-art quantization, so that both methods may be used for additional storage savings.
450   We experiment with the LLM.int8() quantization method due to its effectiveness and widespread
451   adoption (Dettmers et al., 2022). In brief, this method extends absmax quantization, but retains 16-bit
452   precision for outlier values. We quantize our models after removing 1/3 of feed-forward sublayers,

469   5.5   S IMILARITY TRENDS ACROSS FEED - FORWARD SUBLAYERS
470
471   So far, we have shown that simply aligning, merging, and tying adjacent feed-forward sublayers is a
472   simple, yet effective technique for compressing Transformer models. Because of this, we look further
473   into the similarities between representations computed from different feed-forward sublayers. We are
474   interested in if these sublayers exhibit signs of redundancy, as eluded to in previous work (Pires et al.,
475   2023; Kobayashi et al., 2024).
476   To this end, we compare outputs between feed-forward sublayers within the same models. Across
477   our three tasks, we use 10k tokens or patches, depending on the model, from task validation sets
478   to compute a set of output states from all feed-forward sublayers. Then, we use Centered Kernel
479   Alignment (CKA) to compute their similarity. CKA is a state-of-the-art method for comparing the
480   similarity between neural network activations (Kornblith et al., 2019). We plot CKA similarity values
481   for all pairwise interactions between FF sublayers in all three of our model types, shown in Figure 5.
482   We notice that across all three model/task pairs, clear regions of high similarity can be observed. This
483   means that the outputs of these feed-forward sublayers are highly similar, despite being interleaved
484   with multi-headed attention sublayers. We note that similar behavior is not seen in attention sublayers,
485   as seen in Appendix C. While prior work has shown similarities between the outputs of adjacent

508   6                           C ONCLUSION
509
510   In this work, we propose a novel compression method that applies to Transformer-based models
511   via merging and tying adjacent sets of feed-forward blocks. Our method serves as an alternative to
512   existing compression approaches, and opens possibilities of future methods that examine the use of
513   parameter merging and weight tying as a post-training compression technique in deep learning. We
514   demonstrate our method’s extensibility by applying it to several types of Transformer-based models,
515   namely GPT-2, ViT, and an OPUS-MT translation model. Across these diverse tasks, we show that
516   our method can maintain almost full performance while removing 1/3 of feed-forward sublayers,
517   and maintains high performance even after removing 1/2 of all feed-forward sublayers. Finally, we
518
      explore the differences in representation similarity between feed-forward and attention sublayers, and
      find regions of high similarity between feed-forward sublayers (despite being separated by attention
519
      sublayers), which may be related to their surprising mergeability found in our experimentation.
