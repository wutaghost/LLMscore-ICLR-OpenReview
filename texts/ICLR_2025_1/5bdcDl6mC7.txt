009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have attained significant performance in image/video generation
012            and related tasks. However, while diffusion models excel in delivering excellent
013            results, they suffer from substantial computational complexity due to their large
014            volume of parameters. This poses a significant issue for deployment on mobile de-
015            vices and hampers the practical applications of diffusion models. In this work, we
016            propose a new post-training quantization approach designed to reduce the compu-
017            tation complexity and memory cost of diffusion models. As the distributions of
018
               the outputs of diffusion models differ significantly across timesteps, our approach
               first splits the timesteps into different groups and optimizes the quantization con-
019
               figuration of each group separately. We then formulate the quantization of each
020
               group as a rate-distortion optimization problem to minimize the output distortion
021            caused by quantization given the model size constraint. Because output distor-
022            tion is highly related to model accuracy, by minimizing the output distortion, our
023            approach is able to compress diffusion models to low bit widths without hurting
024            accuracy. Furthermore, our approach applies Taylor series expansion approxi-
025            mation and proposes an efficient method to find the optimal bit allocation across
026            layers with linear time complexity. Extensive experimentation over four datasets
027            including CIFAR-10, CelebaHQ, LSUN-Bedroom, and LSUN-Church validates
028            the effectiveness of our approach. Empirical results show that our approach ob-
029
               tains a notable improvement over state-of-the-art when the model is quantized to
               low bit widths.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Diffusion models ( Song & Ermon (2019); Ho et al. (2020); Song et al. (2020; 2021); Dhariwal &
035
      Nichol (2021); Ramesh et al. (2022)) have proven highly effective performance in producing images
036   and videos with both exceptional diversity and fidelity. Recent advancements have showcased their
037   superior results compared to state-of-the-art GAN models, which often struggle with unstable train-
038   ing. As a versatile class of generative models, diffusion models have exhibited their efficacy across
039   a spectrum of applications.
040
      However, the great performance of diffusion models is counterbalanced by their considerable com-
041
      putational consumption. State-of-the-art diffusion models typically demand billions of or even more
042   parameters to obtain a powerful generative neural network. The large volume of parameters, along
043   with its associated memory requirement and computational cost, poses significant challenges for the
044   deployment of diffusion models on mobile devices, especially on resource-constrained platforms
045   such as phones, drones, watches, and self-driving cars. Given the increasing demand for mobile vi-
046   sion applications relying on diffusion models as a cornerstone technique, it is important to compress
047   diffusion models to reduce the time latency and lower the energy consumption.
048   Quantization ( Han et al. (2015b); Zhu et al. (2017); Zhang et al. (2018); Hubara et al. (2016); Zhou
049   et al. (2017; 2016); Rastegari et al. (2016)) is one of the powerful methods for model compression.
050   Instead of utilizing the original 32-bit floating-point representation, parameters can be quantized
051   into lower bit-widths such as 8-bit or even less to reduce model size and computation. More im-
052   portantly, Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) eliminates the need of re-training, which makes quan-
053   tization more practical and can significantly save the cost, as re-training itself is very expensive and
      even not possible in scenarios where retraining data is not available. Although a lot of post-training


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      quantization methods have been proposed, most of them are developed for Convolutional Neural
055   Networks (CNNs) and Vision Transformers (ViTs). Given that diffusion models possess a distinct
056   network architecture with unique features, employing Post-Training Quantization methods designed
057   for CNNs and ViTs is not optimal, as they fail to capitalize on the nature of diffusion models. One
058   main feature of diffusion models is that diffusion models have many timesteps and the distributions
059   of layer outputs differ significantly across timesteps.
060
      Several works studying post-training quantization methods tailored for diffusion models have been
061   propossed, such as PTQ4DM Shang et al. (2023), Q-Diffusion Li et al. (2023a), PTQD He et al.
062   (2023), Q-DM Li et al. (2023b), APQ-DM Wang et al. (2024), and TFMQ-DM Huang et al. (2024).
063   However, one common issue in prior works is that they use equal bit width to quantize weights and
064   activations of all layers. Because parameters in different layers react very distinctively to quantiza-
065   tion, it is more reasonable to use mixed precision for quantization. Moreover, in prior works, the
066   output distortion caused by quantization is not minimized. As output distortion is highly related to
067   accuracy, large distortion may lead to serious accuracy loss.
068   In this paper, we propose a novel post-training quantization approach for diffusion models. Our
069   approach adopts mixed-precision quantization and uses different bit widths to quantize parameters
070   in different layers. As the distributions of activations differ significantly across timesteps, we splits
071   the timesteps into groups and optimizes the quantization configuration of each step separately. We
072   formulate the quantization of each timestep as a rate-distortion optimization problem to maximally
073   maintain the accuracy when quantized to low bit widths by minimizing the output distortion caused
074   by quantization. To solve the optimization problem efficiently, our approach applys first-order Tay-
075
      lor approximation where an important additivity property is observed, that is the output distortion
      caused by quantizing all layers equals the sum of output distortion due to the quantization of each
076
      single layer. Utilizing such additivity property, we propose an efficient optimization method with
077
      only linear time complexity to find the solution.
078
079   Extensive experiments have been conducted on four datasets to validate the effectiveness of our
080   approach. Our approach advances the state-of-the-art noticeably and can reduce the bit width to 6
081
      bits on diffusion models without compromising accuracy. The key contributions of this paper are
      summarized as follows:
082
083
             • We propose a novel post-training quantization approach for diffusion model compression.
084            First, our approach adopts mixed precision to quantize parameters in different layers. We
085            then split the timesteps into groups and optimize the quantization configuration of each
086            timestep separately. The quantization of each timestep is formulated as a rate-distortion
087            optimization problem, where output distortion caused by quantization is minimized. To
088            the best of our knowledge, this is the first work which explicitly proposes rate-distortion
089            optimization for the quantization of diffusion models.
090          • We propose a very efficient method to solve the optimization problem, where we apply first-
091            order Taylor approximation and observe an important additivity property. A mathematical
092            derivation is provided for the additivity property. By utilizing the additivity property, we
093            develop an efficient algorithm with only linear time complexity to find the solution.
094          • Extensive experiments have been conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness. Our approach
095            noticeably outperforms state-of-the-art over four benchmark datasets.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
098
099
      In this section, we discuss prior works relevant to our paper. We first review post-training quantiza-
100   tion methods designed for Convolutional Neural Networks, where most of post-training quantization
101   works lie in this category. We then discuss the post-training quantization works developed for diffu-
102   sion models. Our approach belongs to the second category.
103
104   2.1   P OST- TRAINING Q UANTIZATION FOR C ONVOLUTIONAL N EURAL N ETWORKS
105
106   Most of post-training quantization works belong to this category. Choukroun et al. devised an
107   approach that treats quantization as a Minimum Mean Squared Error (MMSE) problem Choukroun
      et al. (2019), allowing for low-bit precision inference without requiring retraining. Banner et al.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      introduced the first practical 4-bit post-training quantization method Banner et al. (2019) through
109   a combination of analytical clipping, per-channel bit allocation, and bias correction. Zhao et al.
110   improved post-training quantization by introducing Outlier Channel Splitting (OCS) Zhao et al.
111   (2019), a technique that duplicates channels containing outliers and reduces their values by half.
112   Nagel et al. introduced adaptive rounding as a refined weight rounding mechanism for post-training
113   quantization Nagel et al. (2020), dynamically adjusting to both data and task loss for enhanced
114   effectiveness. Additionally, Nagel et al. proposed data-free quantization Nagel et al. (2019) by
115   incorporating weight equalization and bias correction.
116   Several studies have also been conducted to achieve extremely low bit widths through post-training
117   quantization. Cai et al. (2020) introduced Zero-Shot Quantization (ZSQ) as a method for achieving
118   ultra-low-bit neural networks by distilling datasets based on statistics derived from batch normaliza-
119   tion layers. Fang et al. (2020) employed piecewise uniform quantization for weights, resulting in im-
120   proved accuracy on ImageNet using a 2-piece configuration. Li et al. (2023a) leveraged the Hessian
121   of cross-layer weights to establish block-wise layer dependencies when optimizing the quantizer.
122   Zhong et al. (2022) proposed an innovative fine-tuning loss that preserved multiclass discriminative
123
      qualities through the integration of synthetically generated data. Oh et al. (2022) utilized a logarith-
      mic quantization scheme, complemented by corresponding shifter arithmetic operations, to replace
124
      multipliers, thereby enabling higher arithmetic precision while maintaining low representative preci-
125
      sion. Lin et al. (2023) introduced a bit-shrinking algorithm aimed at gradually quantizing networks
126   to lower bit-widths, which helps flatten the loss landscape and mitigates the risk of encountering
127   suboptimal local minima during the fine-tuning process. Ma et al. (2023) employed orthogonal lin-
128   ear optimization to efficiently search bit-allocation in mixed-precision post-training quantization,
129   resulting in significant time savings.
130
      The above approaches mainly targeted the quantization of Convolutional Neural Networks. How-
131
      ever, directly applying them to quantize diffusion models may yield sub-optimal outcomes. This
132   is because Diffusion Models have a distinct network architecture with unique features, which is
133   different with Convolutional Neural Networks.
134
135
136   2.2   P OST- TRAINING Q UANTIZATION FOR D IFFUSION M ODELS
137
138   Several Post-training Quantization methods have been proposed recently for Diffusion Models. Li
139   et al. (2023a) proposed Q-Diffusion, which is a PTQ method tailored specifically to the distinctive
140   multi-timestep pipeline and the architecture of diffusion models. Their approach aims to compress
141
      the noise estimation network to expedite the generation process. They identified the primary chal-
      lenge of quantizing diffusion models with dynamic output distributions across multiple time steps
142
      and the bimodal activation distribution of shortcut layers. To tackle these challenges, they employed
143
      timestep-aware calibration and split shortcut quantization.
144
145   On the other hand, Shang et al. departed from traditional training-aware compression methods and
146   introduced a post-training quantization approach named PTQ4DM Shang et al. (2023) to accelerate
147
      diffusion models. They focused on developing a diffusion-model-specific PTQ method by exam-
      ining PTQ applied to diffusion models from three perspectives: quantized operations, calibration
148
      dataset, and calibration metric. Drawing insights from their comprehensive investigations, they
149
      synthesized and utilized several strategies to devise their method, particularly targeting the unique
150   multi-time-step structure of diffusion models. He et al. (2023) recognized that at each denoising
151   step, quantization noise causes deviations in the estimated mean and mismatches with the predeter-
152   mined variance schedule. To address this, PTQD is proposed to eliminate quantization noise through
153   the use of correlated noise and residual noise correction techniques. Li et al. (2023b) identified that
154   the bottlenecks in low-bit quantized diffusion models stem from significant distribution oscillations
155   in activations and quantization errors accumulate during the multi-step denoising process. To ad-
156   dress these issues, they propose Q-DM, an efficient low-bit quantized diffusion model that achieves
157   a high compression ratio while maintaining competitive performance in image generation tasks.
158   To address key limitations in conventional quantization frameworks by designing distribution-aware
159   quantization functions and optimizing calibration timesteps, Wang et al. (2024) propose an accurate
160   post-training quantization framework for diffusion models (APQ-DM). Significant improvement of
161   image generation performance has been achieved with minimal computational overhead. To miti-
      gate the inefficiencies of PTQ in diffusion models, which typically suffer from extended inference


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      times and high memory demands, Huang et al. (2024) proposed Temporal Feature Maintenance
163   Quantization (TFMQ-DM). This method enhanced compression efficiency and preserved temporal
164   information by focusing on time-step-specific features, achieving near full-precision model perfor-
165   mance under 4-bit weight quantization. Zhao et al. (2024) introduced MixDQ, a mixed-precision
166   quantization method that handles both the imbalance sensitivity and alignment degradation prob-
167   lems for diffusion quantization. Sui et al. (2024) developed a novel weight quantization method that
168   quantizes the UNet from Stable Diffusion v1.5 to 1.99 bits.

170   3     A PPROACH
171
172   In this section, we present our post-training quantization approach designed for diffusion models.
173   We first introduce the formulation of quantization in our approach where quantization is formulated
174   as a rate-distortion optimization problem. We then present the algorithm to solve the optimization
175   problem and discuss the time complexity of our method. As the distributions of activation are
176   different across the time steps, we thus divide the timesteps into groups and optimize the quantization
177   of each group separately. Then for each of the groups, we apply mixed precision and find the optimal
178   bit widths for the layers, given the fact that different layers react differently to quantization.
179

      process adds Gaussian noise to data x0 ∼ q(x0 ) for T times, resulting in noisy samples x0 , ..., xT :

345   4   E XPERIMENTS
346
347   Datasets and models. We perform image generation experiments using two widely-adopted diffu-
348   sion models: Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) Ho et al. (2020) and Denoising
349   Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM) Song et al. (2021). Both models train the image denoiser
350   over the course of 1,000 time steps. To evaluate the performance of these models, we utilize four
351
      well-established benchmark datasets. The first is CIFAR-10 (32×32) Krizhevsky (2009), which
      contains 50,000 training images across 10 classes. Next, we use the CelebA-HQ dataset Liu
352
      et al. (2015) at a resolution of 256×256, consisting of 30,000 high-quality celebrity face images.
353
      Additionally, we incorporate LSUN-Bedrooms and LSUN-Churches Fisher et al. (2015), both with
354   image resolutions of 256×256, each providing 50,000 training images. These datasets enable a
355   comprehensive evaluation of model performance across diverse image types, from natural scenes to
356   human faces.
357
358
      Quantization settings. Following the approach of previous work Huang et al. (2024), we apply
359
      channel-wise quantization to the model’s weights and layer-wise quantization to its activations.
360   These quantization strategies help reduce the model’s memory footprint and computational require-
361   ments while maintaining performance. Additionally, based on empirical findings from traditional
362   model quantization techniques Han et al. (2015a); Mohammad et al. (2016), we preserve the input
363   and output layers in full precision (FP) to prevent significant degradation in accuracy. Maintaining
364   these critical layers in FP ensures that the model retains high-quality data representation at the
365   beginning and end of the network, where precision is most impactful.
366
367   Evaluation metrics. For each experiment, we assess the performance of the diffusion models using
368   the Frechet Inception Distance (FID), a widely recognized metric for evaluating the quality of images
369   generated by generative models. FID measures the distance between the distribution of generated
370   images and that of real images (referred to as the ‘ground truth’), providing insight into both the
371   fidelity and diversity of the generated samples. Lower FID scores indicate closer alignment between
372   the generated and real images, signifying higher image quality.
373   In addition to FID, we also report the Inception Score (IS) as a complementary metric. Unlike FID,
374   IS evaluates the generated images based solely on their own distribution, without comparison to real
375   images. It assesses both the recognizability of the generated images and the diversity within the set,
376   making it a useful reference for understanding model performance in isolation. By using both FID
377   and IS, we provide a comprehensive evaluation of the generative capabilities. All experiments are
      conducted using a single NVIDIA-A100-SXM4 GPU and implemented in the PyTorch framework.

      5           C ONCLUSIONS
476
477   While diffusion models achieve impressive performance in image and video generation, their prac-
478   tical application is hindered by high computational complexity and memory demands. To overcome
479   these limitations, we propose a post-training quantization approach that reduces both computational
480   and memory requirements. Our method groups timesteps and optimizes their quantization configu-
481   rations independently, effectively minimizing output distortion while preserving accuracy at lower
482
      bit widths.
483   We formulate the quantization of each group as a rate-distortion optimization problem and develop
484   an efficient algorithm to find the solution by apply first order Taylor approximation. Experiments
485   on various datasets show that our approach reduces the bit width to 5-6 bits while maintaining high
      accuracy, outperforming state-of-the-art methods.

949   The algorithm works as follows. Before optimization, we quantize weights and activations of each
950   layer with different bit widths and calculate the output distortion caused by quantization to generate
951   the rate-distortion curve for each layer’s weights and activations. After that, we assign a real value
952   to γ, and select the point with slope equal to γ on each curve. The selected points on all curves
953
      correspond to a group of solution for bit allocation. In practice, we explore multiple values for γ until
      the size of the quantized network meets constraint. We randomly select 50 images from ImageNet
954
      dataset to calculate output distortion caused by quantization. Assume that we have N curves and M
955
      points in each curve. The time complexity to find optimum bit allocation is O(K · M · N ), where
956   K is the total number of slope γ to be evaluated, M is the total number of bit widths, and N is the
957   total number of layers.
