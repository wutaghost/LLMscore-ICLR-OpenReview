009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Safe Reinforcement Learning (Safe RL) aims to ensure safety when an RL agent
012            conducts learning by interacting with real-world environments where improper
013            actions can induce high costs or lead to severe consequences. In this paper, we
014            propose a novel Safe Skill Planning (SSkP) approach to enhance effective safe RL
015            by exploiting auxiliary offline demonstration data. SSkP involves a two-stage pro-
016            cess. First, we employ PU learning to learn a skill risk predictor from the offline
017            demonstration data. Then, based on the learned skill risk predictor, we develop
018
               a novel risk planning process to enhance online safe RL and learn a risk-averse
               safe policy efficiently through interactions with the online RL environment, while
019
               simultaneously adapting the skill risk predictor to the environment. We conduct
020
               experiments in several benchmark robotic simulation environments. The experi-
021            mental results demonstrate that the proposed approach consistently outperforms
022            previous state-of-the-art safe RL methods.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Reinforcement Learning (RL) empowers the development of intelligent agents and the training of
028   decision systems, making it highly suitable for real-world applications. As RL continues to find
029   broader use in real-world scenarios, concerns regarding the safety of RL systems have become more
030   noticeable. These safety concerns have been particularly highlighted in human-centric domains,
031   such as autonomous driving (Wen et al., 2020), helicopter manipulation (Koppejan & Whiteson,
032   2011), and human-related robotic environments (Brunke et al., 2021), where significant risks can be
033
      associated with taking improper actions, leading to severe consequences.
034   Safe Reinforcement Learning (Safe RL) focuses on the development of RL systems while adhering
035   to predefined safety constraints (Garcıa & Fernández, 2015) and reducing the associated risk. In
036   Safe RL, in addition to optimizing a reward function (Sutton & Barto, 2018), an additional cost is
037   often assigned to evaluate the safety of actions taken by the RL agent; the RL agent aims to maxi-
038   mize the reward signal while ensuring a low cost (Altman, 1999; Hans et al., 2008). Conventional
039
      Safe RL methods aim to maximize cumulative rewards through interactions with online environ-
      ments (Achiam et al., 2017; Tessler et al., 2019; Thomas et al., 2021), which often incur nontrivial
040
      costs in the learning process. More recently, researchers have recognized the value of learning from
041
      offline data, a practice that avoids potential damage to online physical environments (Xu et al., 2022;
042   Liu et al., 2023). Reinforcement Learning from Demonstration (LfD) seeks to accelerate RL training
043   by initially pre-training the RL agent using an offline dataset of demonstrations, which has demon-
044   strated effective performance for standard RL tasks (Argall et al., 2009; Brys et al., 2015). Recent
045   research has started to exploit the potential of LfD in the context of Safe RL, aiming to incorporate
046   the safety-related information from the demonstration data to improve the training of safe policies
047   in online environments (Thananjeyan et al., 2021). Our research endeavors to further advance safe
048   RL in this intriguing direction.
049   In this paper, we introduce a novel Safe Skill Planning (SSkP) approach to enhance effective safe
050   online RL by exploiting the offline demonstration data. Skill learning is a commonly used technique
051   for LfD, allowing the RL agent to learn high-level representations of action sequences from offline
052   demonstrations (Pertsch et al., 2021). In SSkP, we first employ a skill model to capture the high
053   level behaviour patterns in the offline demonstrations as latent skills, and learn a skill risk predic-
      tor through Positive-Unlabeled (PU) learning on the demonstration data. The skill risk predictor

073   2   R ELATED W ORKS
074
075   Safe RL Safe Reinforcement Learning (Safe RL) is the study of optimizing decision-making for
076   RL systems while ensuring compliance with safety constraints. It aims to strike a balance between
077   exploration for learning and the avoidance of actions that could result in harmful or undesirable out-
078   comes (Garcıa & Fernández, 2015). Altman (1999) first introduced the formulation of Constrained
079   Markov Decision Processes (CMDPs) to frame the Safe RL problem. Subsequent research in (Hans
080   et al., 2008) introduced strict constraints that prohibit safety violations within a single exploration
081   trajectory. Thomas et al. (2021) developed a Safe Model-Based Policy Optimization (SMBPO)
082
      method, aiming to learn a precise transition model that prevents unsafe states during exploration
      by penalizing unsafe trajectories. Recent studies have highlighted the significance of incorporat-
083
      ing offline data into Safe RL. Xu et al. (2022) introduced Constrained Penalized Q-learning (CPQ),
084
      which employs a cost critic to learn constraint values during exploration. They further penalize the
085   Bellman operator in policy training to stop the update of the policy for potentially unsafe states. In
086   another endeavor, Thananjeyan et al. (2020) proposed the Safety Augmented Value Estimation from
087   Demonstrations (SAVED) approach, facilitating the learning of a safety density model from offline
088   demonstration data. They utilize the cross-entropy method (Botev et al., 2013) for planning safe
089   exploration, balancing task-driven exploration with cost-driven constrained exploration. Their more
090   recent work introduced a Recovery RL approach (Thananjeyan et al., 2021), learning a recovery
091   policy from offline demonstration data. This method ensures a recovery policy’s safety by leverag-
092   ing demonstration data, while also learning a recovery set to evaluate state safety. During online
093
      training, a task policy is learned when states are deemed safe, switching to the recovery policy when
      the RL agent encounters potentially unsafe situations.
094
095
096
      Skill-based RL Reinforcement Learning from Demonstration (LfD), also known as Imitation
      Learning, focuses on enhancing online RL training by leveraging an expert demonstration dataset
097
      (Argall et al., 2009; Brys et al., 2015). Thrun & Schwartz (1994) introduced skill learning to LfD,
098
      enabling RL agents to learn reusable high-level skills from action sequences within offline demon-
099   stration data. In more recent research, Pertsch et al. (2021) presented the SPiRL framework, which
100   leverages deep latent models to learn skill representations. The policy is trained using the skill
101   model in conjunction with a variant of Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) (Haarnoja et al., 2018) to accelerate
102   RL in downstream tasks. Furthermore, recent work has demonstrated the integration of skill learn-
103   ing into offline safe RL (Slack et al., 2022), which learns a safety variable posterior from offline
104   demonstration data and subsequently enhances online safe policy training.

139   3    P ROBLEM S ETTING
140
141   The safe RL problem is typically framed as a Constrained Markov Decision Process (CMDP) (Alt-
142   man, 1999), denoted as M = (S, A, T , R, C, γ), where S represents the state space, A is the action
143   space, T : S × A → S defines the transition dynamics, R : S × A → R is the reward function,
144   and γ ∈ (0, 1) is the discount factor. The additional cost function C : S × A → R is introduced to
145   account for safety violations during RL exploration. Hence an exploration trajectory within CMDP
146   can be expressed as τ = (s0 , a0 , r0 , c0 , . . . , st , at , rt , ct , . . . , s|τ |+1 ). We adopt the strict setting
147   that the safe RL agent will terminate a trajectory when encountering safety violation and inducing a
148
      nonzero cost (ct > 0) (Hans et al., 2008). The goal of safe RL is to efficiently learn a good policy π
      that maximizes expected discounted cumulative reward while incurring minimal costs.
149
150   To facilitate safe RL in online environments, we presume the availability of a small demonstration
151   dataset, denoted as Dd , which provides prior information regarding safety violations during explo-
152   ration: Dd = {. . . , (· · · , st , at , ct , · · · ), . . .}. The demonstration data can be gathered by either
153   human experts or a trained safe RL agent (Thananjeyan et al., 2021). A method that can effec-
154
      tively exploit such demonstration data is expected to accelerate safe RL in online environments with
      smaller costs.

167   4.1     S KILL R ISK P REDICTION FROM D EMONSTRATIONS
168
169   Conventional safe RL methods entail the learning of a safe policy through direct interaction with the
170   online environment, which often incur considerable costs in the exploration based learning process.
171   Learning from demonstration (LfD) offers a means to accelerate the online RL process and reduce
172   the cost by pre-training on an offline demonstration dataset. This pre-training phase is more effi-
173
      cient in terms of time and cost compared to the resource-intensive online environment. Skill-based
      learning stands as a prominent approach in LfD (Pertsch et al., 2021). It learns reusable skills as
174
      generalizable high level representations of action sequences from offline demonstrations, which can
175
      be used to guide the RL agent to explore in a safe manner for downstream online tasks. Inspired by
176   the principles of LfD, we aim to extract skill-based safety-related insights from the demonstration
177   dataset Dd , which can be utilized to assess the safety of reinforcement decisions and enhance the
178   ensuing online safe RL. In particular, we propose to learn a skill risk predictor Pζ (c|st , zt ) from the
179   demonstration data that can evaluate the safety of a skill-based decision, zt , on a given state st .
180
      To support skill-based learning, we first adopt the deep skill model from a previous work (Pertsch
181
      et al., 2021) to learn skills as latent representations of observed action sequences. This skill model
182   consists of three key components: a skill encoder network qµ (zt |at ), responsible for encoding an
183   action sequence at = {at , ..., at+H−1 } with length H into a high-level skill zt ; a skill decoder
184   network pν (at |zt ), which decodes the skill zt back into the action sequence at ; and a skill prior
185   network qψ (zt |st ), which generates the skill decision for a given state st . After being trained on the
186   demonstration data Dd , the components of the skill model can be deployed to facilitate subsequent
187   learning processes.
188
189   4.1.1    L EARNING S KILL R ISK P REDICTOR VIA PU L EARNING
190
      The demonstration data provides valuable insights for safe exploration of the environment. How-
191
      ever, estimating risk predictors for skill-based behaviors in the context of safe exploration poses a
192
      persistent challenge due to two primary reasons. First, the demonstration data, whether collected by
193   a human expert or a fully trained safe RL agent, often contain very limited actual examples of safety
194   violations, due to the finite trajectory lengths and limited skill horizons. Second, while a decision
195   made in a given state may not result in immediate safety violations, it could lead to a close prox-
196   imity to safety violations. Treating such decisions as strictly safe examples can be problematic. To
197   tackle these issues, we propose the utilization of Positive-Unlabeled (PU) learning, a technique that
198   can bypass the strict differentiation of safe decisions from unsafe ones and alleviate the scarcity of
199   unsafe examples.
200   Specifically, we collect the positive and unlabeled decision examples for PU learning as follows.
201   At a timestep t, if the current trajectory τ actually encounters a safety violation within the next H
202   steps when the RL agent is projected to select skill zt at state st , then we collect such state-skill pair
203   (st , zt ) as positive unsafe examples. Conversely, all other state-skill decision pairs that do not lead to
204   immediate risks are collected as unlabeled examples. For states near the termination of trajectories,
205   the corresponding action sequences have lengths that are insufficient (less than the horizon H) to
206   encode skills. We hence utilize the skill prior network qψ (zt |st ) from the skill model to produce the
207
      skill decision zt for each given state st in the demonstration data, instead of using the encoder.
208   Let Dp = (spt , ztp ) represent the set of positive examples of state-skill decision pairs, and Du =
209   (sut , ztu ) represent the unlabeled set. We learn the skill risk predictor Pζ (c|st , zt ) as a binary clas-
210   sifier parameterized with ζ, measuring the probability of selecting skill zt at state st leading to a
211   safety violation with risk c > 0. We compute the true positive loss on the PU training data as the
212   negative mean log-likelihood of the positive examples in Dp :

307   efficiently learn a skill-based safe policy network πθ (z|s) through iterative interactions with an

342   RL Environments We conducted experiments with four benchmark robotic simulation environ-
343   ments, namely, Ant, Cheetah, Hopper, and Humanoid, utilizing a customized variant of the MuJoCo
344   physics simulator (Todorov et al., 2012) as introduced in (Thomas et al., 2021). In these environ-
345   ments, the RL agent halts upon encountering a safety violation. In the Ant and Hopper environments,
346   a safety violation occurs when the robot topples over. In the Cheetah environment, a violation takes
347   place when the robot’s head hits the ground. In the Humanoid environment, the human-like robot
348   violates the safety constraint when its head falls to the ground. Figure 2 presents some instances
349   of failure in these environments. The RL agent is trained to maximize cumulative rewards while
350
      adhering to the safety constraint.
351
352
      Comparison Methods We compare our proposed SSkP approach with three state-of-the-art safe
      RL methods: CPQ (Xu et al., 2022), SMBPO (Thomas et al., 2021), and Recovery RL (Thananjeyan
353
      et al., 2021). CPQ is a constraints penalized Q-learning method. It learns from offline demonstration
354
      data, and penalizes the Bellman operator during policy training when encountering unsafe states.
355   SMBPO is a model-based method that relies on an ensemble of Gaussian dynamics-based transition
356   models. It penalizes trajectories that lead to unsafe conditions and avoids unsafe states under specific
357   assumptions. Recovery RL first learns a recovery policy from the offline demonstration data with
358   the objective of minimizing safety violations. During online training, the agent takes actions to
359   maximize the reward signal in safe situations and falls back on the recovery policy to reduce safety
360   violations if necessary.
361
362   Implementation Details A fixed horizon length H = 10 for skill action sequences is used in the
363   experiments. The dimension of the skill vectors is set as 10. The PU risk predictor, skill decoder,
364   and policy network employ standard MLP architectures, while the skill prior incorporates an MLP
365   with a Gaussian output layer. The skill encoder utilizes an LSTM with linear output. Following
366
      prior work on PU learning (Xu & Denil, 2021), the slack variable ξ is set to 0. For risk planning,

458   5.3   A BLATION S TUDY
459
      The main contribution of the proposed SSkP approach lies in devising two novel components: the
460
      risk planning component and the skill risk predictor. We conducted an ablation study to investigate
461
      their impact on the performance of SSkP.
462
463   The risk planning component in SSkP iteratively improves the safety of skills by leveraging the skill
464   risk predictor, aiming to generate and deploy the most effective safe skill decision. To investigate
465
      the extent to which the proposed risk planning process enhances safe policy learning performance,
      we introduced an alternative naive planning baseline as a comparison. Naive planning samples Ns
466
      skills using the current safe policy πθ (·|st ) at the given state st , evaluates them using the current
467
      skill risk predictor, and selects the best skill with the lowest predicted risk in a single iteration. We
468   denote the variant of SSkP with naive planning instead of the proposed risk planning as SSkP-NP.
469
470   The SSkP-NP variant nevertheless still leverages the skill risk predictor. To further investigate the
471
      impact of the skill risk predictor, we introduced another variant, SSkP-w/o-RP, which drops the skill
      risk predictor learning and deployment from both the offline and online learning stages. Conse-
472
      quently, risk planning that depends on skill risk assessment is also disabled in the online RL stage,
473
      while the skill decisions are produced directly by the skill policy function.
474
475   We compared the proposed full approach SSkP with the two variants, SSkP-NP and SSkP-w/o-
476   RP, in the Ant and Hopper environments, and the experimental results are presented in Figure 4.
477
      The curves in the figure reveal that our proposed SSkP with risk planning clearly outperforms the
      ablation variant SSkP-NP with naive planning in both environments. In the Hopper environment,
478
      SSkP-NP exhibits a very brief faster improvement during the early training stage but experiences a
479
      subsequent decline. Our proposed full approach SSkP produces a much better policy function that
480   achieves substantially much higher average episode reward than SSkP-NP with smaller cost—the
481   number of safety violations. This validates the contribution put forth by the proposed risk planning
482   process. We also note that by eliminating the skill risk predictor and consequently the entire risk
483   planning, the variant SSkP-w/o-RP, while still leveraging the offline demonstration data through
484   the skill model, experiences a substantial performance decline compared to SSkP-NP. The results
485   validate the significant contribution of the proposed skill risk prediction methodology, which is the
      foundation of the proposed safe RL method SSkP.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we introduced a Safe Skill Planning (SSkP) method to address the challenge of online
489   safe RL by effectively exploiting a prior demonstration dataset. First, we deployed a deep skill model
490   to extract safe behavior patterns from the demonstrations and proposed a novel skill risk predictor for
491   decision safety evaluation, which is trained through PU learning over the state-skill pairs. Second,
492   by leveraging the risk predictor, we devised a new and simple risk planning process to iteratively
493   identify reliable safe skill decisions in online RL environments and support online safe RL policy
494   learning. We compared the proposed method with several state-of-the-art safe RL methods in four
495   benchmark robotic simulation environments. The experimental results demonstrate that our method
496   yields notable improvements over previous online safe RL approaches.
