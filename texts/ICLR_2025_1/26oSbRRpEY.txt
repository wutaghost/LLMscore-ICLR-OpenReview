009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-video diffusion models enable the generation of high-quality videos that
012            follow text instructions, simplifying the process of producing diverse and individ-
013            ual content. Current methods excel in generating short videos (up to 16s), but
014            produce hard-cuts when naively extended to long video synthesis. To overcome
015            these limitations, we present StreamingT2V, an autoregressive method that gener-
016
               ates long videos of up to 2 minutes or longer with seamless transitions. The key
               components are: (i) a short-term memory block called conditional attention mod-
017
               ule (CAM), which conditions the current generation on the features extracted from
018
               the preceding chunk via an attentional mechanism, leading to consistent chunk
019            transitions, (ii) a long-term memory block called appearance preservation module
020            (APM), which extracts high-level scene and object features from the first video
021            chunk to prevent the model from forgetting the initial scene, and (iii) a random-
022            ized blending approach that allows for the autoregressive application of a video
023            enhancer on videos of indefinite length, ensuring consistency across chunks. Ex-
024            periments show that StreamingT2V produces high motion amount, while com-
025            peting methods suffer from video stagnation when applied naively in an autore-
026            gressive fashion. Thus, we propose with StreamingT2V a high-quality seamless
027
               text-to-long video generator, surpassing competitors in both consistency and mo-
               tion.

036   Singer et al. (2022); Girdhar et al. (2023); Blattmann et al. (2023a); Guo et al. (2023b); Li et al.
037   (2023); Zhang et al. (2023a); Guo et al. (2023a); Khachatryan et al. (2023); Villegas et al. (2022).

039   such as Ho et al. (2022b); Wang et al. (2023b); Blattmann et al. (2023b;a); Zhang et al. (2023a);
040   Zheng et al. (2024); PKU-Yuan-Lab & Tuzhan-AI (2024) are mostly focused on generating short
041   frame sequences (typically of 16, 24, or recently 384 frame-length). However, short videos genera-
042   tors are limited in real-world use-cases such as ad making, storytelling, etc.

124   Text-Guided Video Diffusion Models. Generating videos from text instructions using Diffusion
125   Models Ho et al. (2020); Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015) is a newly established and actively researched
126   field introduced by Video Diffusion Models (VDM) Ho et al. (2022b). The method can generate
127   only low-resolution videos (up to 128x128) with a maximum of 16 frames (without autoregression),
128   imposing significant limitations, while requiring massive training resources. Several methods thus
129
      employ video enhancement in the form of spatial/temporal upsampling Ho et al. (2022a); Singer
      et al. (2022); Ho et al. (2022b); Blattmann et al. (2023b), using cascades with up to 7 enhancer
130
      modules Ho et al. (2022a). While this leads to high-resolution and long videos, the generated content
131
      is still limited by the content depicted in the key frames.
132
133   Towards generating longer videos (i.e. more keyframes), Text-To-Video-Zero (T2V0) Khachatryan
134   et al. (2023) and ART-V Weng et al. (2023) utilize a text-to-image diffusion model. Therefore,
135
      they can generate only simple motions. T2V0 conditions on its first frame via cross-frame atten-
      tion and ART-V on an anchor frame. Due to the lack of global reasoning, it leads to unnatural or
136
      repetitive motions. MTVG Oh et al. (2023) transforms a text-to-video model into an autoregressive
137
      method through a training-free approach. As it uses strong consistency priors within and between
138   chunks, it results in very low motion amount, and mostly near-static background. FreeNoise Qiu
139   et al. (2024) samples a small set of noise vectors, re-uses them for the generation of all frames,
140   while temporal attention is performed on local windows. As temporal attention is invariant to such
141   frame shuffling, it leads to high similarity between frames, almost always static global motion and
142   near-constant videos. Gen-L Wang et al. (2023a) generates overlapping short videos and combines
143   them via temporal co-denoising, which can lead to quality degradation with video stagnation. Re-
144   cent transformed-based diffusion models Zheng et al. (2024); PKU-Yuan-Lab & Tuzhan-AI (2024)
145   operate in the latent space of a 3D autoencoder, enabling the generation of up to 384 frames. Despite
146
      extensive training, these models produce videos with limited motion, often resulting in near-constant
      videos.
147
148   Image-Guided Video Diffusion Models as Long Video Generators. Several works condition the
149   video generation by a driving image or video Xing et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023b); Blattmann et al.
150   (2023a); Guo et al. (2023a); Esser et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023c); Chen et al. (2023a); Zeng et al.
151   (2023); Dai et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024); Long et al. (2024); Ren et al. (2024). They can thus be
152
      turned into an autoregressive method by conditioning on the frame(s) of the preceding chunk.
153   VideoDrafter Long et al. (2024) uses a text-to-image diffusion model to obtain an anchor frame.
154   A video diffusion model is conditioned on the driving anchor to generate independently multiple
155   videos that share the same high-level context. However, this leads to drastic scene cuts as no consis-
156   tency among video chunks is enforced. StoryDiffusion Zhou et al. (2024) conditions on video frames
157   that have been linearly propagated from key frames, which leads to severe quality degradation. Sev-
158
      eral works Chen et al. (2023b); Zeng et al. (2023); Dai et al. (2023) concatenate the (encoded)
      conditionings (e.g. input frame(s)) with an additional mask (indicating the provided frame(s)) to the
159
      input of the video diffusion model.

      In this section, we introduce our method for high-resolution text-to-long video generation. We first
273   generate 256 × 256 resolution long videos (240 frames, or 1200 frames), then enhance them to
274   higher resolution (720 × 720). The overview of the whole pipeline is provided in Fig. 2. The long
275   video generation process comprises three stages: the Initialization Stage, where the first 16-frame
276   chunk is synthesized by a pre-trained text-to-video model (e.g. Modelscope Wang et al. (2023b)),
277   the Streaming T2V Stage where new content for subsequent frames is generated autoregressively.
278   To ensure seamless transitions between chunks, we introduce (see Fig. 3) our conditional attention
279   module (CAM), which utilizes short-term information from the last Fcond = 8 frames and our ap-
280   pearance preservation module (APM), which extracts long-term information from an anchor frame
281
      to maintain object appearance and scene details during the autoregressive process. After generat-
      ing a long video (e.g. 240, 1200 frames or more), the Streaming Refinement Stage enhances the
282
      video using a high-resolution text-to-short-video model (e.g. MS-Vid2Vid-XL Zhang et al. (2023c))
283
      autoregressively with our randomized blending approach for seamless chunk processing. This step
284   does not require additional training, making our approach cost-effective.

406    5.2        M ETRICS
407
408   For quantitative evaluation we employ metrics that measure temporal consistency, text-alignment,
409   and per-frame quality of our method.
410   For temporal consistency, we introduce SCuts, which counts the number of detected scene cuts in
411   a video using the AdaptiveDetector PyS with default parameters. In addition, we propose a new
412   metric called motion aware warp error (MAWE), which coherently assesses motion amount and
413   warp error, and yields a low value when a video exhibits both consistency and a substantial amount
414   of motion. To this end, we measure the motion amount using OFS (optical flow score), which
415   computes for a video the mean of the squared magnitudes of all optical flow vectors between any
416
      two consecutive frames. Furthermore, for a video V, we consider the mean warp error Lai et al.
      (2018) W (V), which measures the average squared L2 pixel distance from a frame to its warped
417
      subsequent frame, excluding occluded regions. Finally, MAWE is defined as:

426   (CLIP), which is applied to all frames of a video. CLIP computes for a video sequence the cosine

      6   C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we tackled the challenge of generating long videos from textual prompts. We ob-
532
      served that all existing methods produce long videos either with temporal inconsistencies or severe
533
      stagnation up to standstill. To overcome these limitations, we carefully analysed an autoregressive
534   pipeline build on top of a vanilla video diffusion model and proposed StreamingT2V, which in-
535   corporates short- and long-term dependency blocks to ensure smooth continuation of video chunks
536   with high motion amount while maintaining scene and object features. We proposed a randomized
537   blending approach that enables to use a video enhancer within the autoregressive process without
538   temporal inconsistencies. Experimental results demonstrate that StreamingT2V outperforms com-
539   petitors in terms of motion amount and temporal consistency, enabling the generation of long videos
      from text prompts without content stagnation.

902   details in Sec. D.1.1): (i) Connect the features of CAM with the skip-connection of the UNet via
903   zero convolution, followed by addition. We zero-pad the condition frame and concatenate it with

       D.2     A PPEARANCE P RESERVATION M ODULE
1246
1247
       We analyse the impact of utilizing long-term memory in the context of long video generation.
1248
1249   Fig. 13 and Fig. 14 show that long-term memory greatly helps keeping the object and scene features
1250   across autoregressive generations. Thanks to the usage of long-term information via our proposed
1251
       APM module, identity and scene features are preserved throughout the video. For instance, the face
       of the woman in Fig. 14 (including all its tiny details) are consistent1 across the video generation.
1252
       Also, the style of the jacket and the bag are correctly generated throughout the video. Without
1253
       having access to a long-term memory, these object and scene features are changing over time.
1254
1255   This is also supported quantitatively. We utilize a person re-identification score to measure feature
1256   preservation (definition in Sec. D.2.1), and obtain scores of 93.42 and 94.95 for Ours w/o APM,
1257
       and Ours, respectively. Our APM module thus improves the identity/appearance preservation. Also
       the scene information is better kept, as we observe an image distance score in terms of LPIPS
1258
       Zhang et al. (2018) of 0.192 and 0.151 for Ours w/o APM and Ours, respectively. We thus have an
1259
       improvement in terms of scene preservation of more than 20% when APM is used.

1319    E.1        T RAINING DETAILS
1320
1321   In order to train the APM module, we randomly sample an anchor frame out of the first 16 frames.
1322   For the conditioning and denoising, we use the frames 17 − 24 and 17 − 32, respectively. This
1323   aligns training with inference, where there is a large time gap between the conditional frames and
1324   the anchor frame. In addition, by randomly sampling an anchor frame, the model can leverage the
1325
       CLIP information only for the extraction of high-level semantic information, as we do not provide a
       frame index to the model.
