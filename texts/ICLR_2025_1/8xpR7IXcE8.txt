009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               We propose ClassroomKD, a novel multi-mentor knowledge distillation framework
012            inspired by classroom environments to enhance knowledge transfer between student
013            and multiple mentors. Unlike traditional methods that rely on fixed mentor-student
014            relationships, our framework dynamically selects and adapts the teaching strategies
015            of diverse mentors based on their effectiveness for each data sample. ClassroomKD
016            comprises two main modules: the Knowledge Filtering (KF) Module and the
017            Mentoring Module. The KF Module dynamically ranks mentors based on their
018            performance for each input, activating only high-quality mentors to minimize error
019            accumulation and prevent information loss. The Mentoring Module adjusts the
020
               distillation strategy by tuning each mentor’s influence according to the performance
               gap between the student and mentors, effectively modulating the learning pace.
021
               Extensive experiments on image classification (CIFAR-100 and ImageNet) and 2D
022
               human pose estimation (COCO Keypoints and MPII Human Pose) demonstrate that
023            ClassroomKD outperforms existing knowledge distillation methods for different
024            network architectures. Our results highlight that a dynamic and adaptive approach
025            to mentor selection and guidance leads to more effective knowledge transfer, paving
026            the way for enhanced model performance through distillation.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032
      Knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015) is a widely adopted model compression technique
      in deep learning, where a smaller, more efficient student model learns to replicate the behavior of a
033
      larger, more complex teacher model. While traditional KD methods typically employ a single teacher,
034
      multi-teacher (or multi-mentor) distillation has been proposed to further enhance performance by
035   leveraging an ensemble of teachers (You et al., 2017). This setup is expected to provide richer and
036   more diverse knowledge, improving the student’s generalization and robustness. We use the term
037   mentor to describe all networks involved in teaching the student, regardless of their size or role.
038
      Despite its potential benefits, multi-mentor distillation faces several significant challenges:
039
040   Large Capacity Gap: Employing multiple large mentors can create a substantial capacity gap
041   between the collective representation power of the mentors and that of the student. This gap can
042   hinder the student’s ability to effectively mimic the combined knowledge of the mentors, leading
043   to suboptimal learning outcomes. To bridge this gap, some works (Mirzadeh et al., 2019; Son
044
      et al., 2021) have introduced intermediate-sized mentors alongside a large teacher. However, smaller
      mentors may be less effective, potentially introducing additional errors into the student’s knowledge.
045
046   Error Accumulation: The lower performance of smaller mentors can contribute to cumulative errors
047   in the distillation process. This is particularly problematic in sequential distillation frameworks like
048   TAKD (Figure 1(b)), where each mentor teaches only the subsequent smaller model. Such setups
049   can lead to an "error avalanche," where inaccuracies from lower-performing mentors degrade the
050
      student’s performance (Son et al., 2021). Although DGKD (Figure 1(c)) attempts to mitigate this
      by allowing each mentor to teach all smaller models and randomly dropping some mentors during
051
      training, these strategies can result in valuable information loss and reduced learning efficiency.
052
053   Lack of Dynamic Adaptation: The performance gap between the student and its mentors is not
      static; it evolves throughout training. Current methods do not adequately address these dynamic

078   Observing that (1) a mentor’s performance varies across different data samples, (2) each mentor
079   possesses distinct teaching capabilities due to varying capacity gaps, and (3) the performance gap
080   evolves during training, we propose ClassroomKD (Figure 1(d)), a novel multi-mentor distillation
081   framework inspired by classroom dynamics (see Appendix E). Our method introduces two key
082
      modules (Figure 1(e)) designed to address the following questions:
083    Q1: Which mentors are effective teachers for a given data sample?
084
       We introduce the Knowledge Filtering Module to intelligently select mentors. This module
085    dynamically ranks all mentors based on their performance for each input, activating only those with
086    sufficient performance. A mentor is deemed effective and activated if its predictions are accurate
087    and more confident than the student’s. This minimizes error accumulation and information loss.
088
089    Q2: How much information should the student learn from each active mentor?
090    Our Mentoring Module addresses this by tuning the teaching strategy based on the performance
091    gap between the student and each active mentor. Specifically, we adjust each mentor’s distillation
092    temperature to control the teaching pace, allowing the student to appropriately weigh information
093    received from each mentor before integrating it into its own knowledge.
094
095   By addressing these questions iteratively, ClassroomKD ensures a continuously optimized learning
096   process that adapts to the student’s evolving capabilities. Our contributions are as follows:
097
098        1. ClassroomKD Framework: We introduce ClassroomKD, a novel multi-mentor distillation
099           framework to dynamically select effective mentors and adapt teaching strategies.
100        2. Knowledge Filtering Module: We develop a Knowledge Filtering Module to enhance
101           distillation quality by selectively activating high-performance mentors, thereby reducing
102           error accumulation and preventing information loss.
103        3. Mentoring Module: We create a Mentoring Module that dynamically adjusts teaching
104           strategies based on the performance gap between the student and each active mentor,
105           optimizing the knowledge transfer process.
106        4. Empirical Validation: Through extensive experiments on image classification (CIFAR-100
107           and ImageNet) and 2D human pose estimation (COCO Keypoints and MPII Human Pose),
              we demonstrate that ClassroomKD significantly outperforms state-of-the-art KD methods.

      2.1   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION A PPROACHES
111
112
      Knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015) is a widely adopted technique for compressing deep
113   neural networks, where a smaller student model learns from a larger teacher model by minimizing
114   the distance between their output probability distributions, or soft labels. Traditional KD methods
115   primarily focus on logit-based distillation, where the student learns directly from the teacher’s
116   output logits. Notable methods include PKT (Passalis & Tefas, 2018), which employs probabilistic
117   knowledge transfer, FT (Kim et al., 2018), which transfers factorized feature representations, and
118   AB (Heo et al., 2019), which leverages activation boundaries formed by hidden neurons.
119   Feature-based distillation methods transfer knowledge by aligning intermediate representations
120   between the teacher and student. FitNets (Adriana et al., 2015) introduced this approach using
121   intermediate feature maps for training. Later methods like AT (Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2016),
122   VID (Ahn et al., 2019), and CRD (Tian et al., 2020) enhance knowledge transfer by matching attention
123   maps, utilizing variational information distillation, and employing contrastive learning, respectively.
124
      Relation-based methods focus on preserving the structural relationships within the teacher’s feature
125   maps. RKD (Park et al., 2019) maintains data point structures through relational knowledge distilla-
126   tion, while SP (Tung & Mori, 2019) and SRRL (Yang et al., 2021) optimize for similarity-preserving
127   objectives. DIST (Huang et al., 2022) addresses large capacity gaps by applying a correlation-based
128   loss to maintain both inter-class and intra-class relationships, enhancing distillation efficiency.
129
      Recent approaches have explored more specialized distillation techniques. WSLD (Zhou et al., 2021)
130
      introduces weighted soft labels to balance bias-variance trade-offs, while One-to-All Spatial Matching
131   KD (Lin et al., 2022) focuses on spatial matching techniques. OFA (Hao et al., 2024) optimizes
132   feature-based KD by projecting features onto the logit space, significantly improving performance
133   for heterogeneous models. To enhance distillation effectiveness, several methods have incorporated
134   adaptive strategies. CTKD (Li et al., 2023) dynamically adjusts the temperature during training to
135   gradually increase learning difficulty, and DTKD (Wei & Bai, 2024) employs real-time temperature
136   scaling to improve knowledge transfer efficiency.
137
138   2.2   M ULTI -T EACHER K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
139
140   Multi-teacher distillation methods aim to further enhance student performance by leveraging an
141   ensemble of mentors (You et al., 2017).
142
      Online knowledge distillation has been particularly successful in this context. Deep Mutual Learning
143
      (DML) (Zhang et al., 2018) introduces a framework where multiple peer models learn from each
144   other simultaneously during training, fostering collaborative learning among smaller networks and
145   outperforming traditional one-way (offline) distillation. Other online methods include ONE (Zhu
146   et al., 2018), OKDDip (Chen et al., 2020), and FFM (Li et al., 2020), which often outperform offline
147   methods. Online distillation has also been extended to pose estimation tasks (Li et al., 2021b).
148   SHAKE (Li & Jin, 2022) proposed using proxy teachers with shadow heads to use the benefits of
149   online distillation in offline settings.
150   To address the capacity gap in multi-teacher setups, Teacher-Assistant KD (TAKD) (Mirzadeh
151   et al., 2019) employs intermediate-sized teacher assistants (TAs) to bridge the gap between the
152   largest teacher and the student. However, sequential distillation through TAs can result in an
153   "error avalanche," where errors propagate at each step, reducing final performance. Adaptive
154   Ensemble Knowledge Distillation (AEKD) (Du et al., 2020) mitigates this issue by using an adaptive
155   dynamic weighting strategy to reduce error propagation in the gradient space. Densely Guided KD
156   (DGKD) (Son et al., 2021) further improves upon these methods by guiding each TA with both larger
157   TAs and the main teacher, enabling a more gradual and effective transfer of knowledge. Additionally,
158
      DGKD introduces a strategy of randomly dropping mentors during training to expose the student to
      diverse learning sources, enhancing overall learning robustness.
159
160   While existing multi-teacher methods offer various mechanisms for knowledge distillation, they
161   still grapple with challenges such as managing the capacity gap, mitigating error accumulation, and
      adapting to dynamic mentor-student relationships.

186   3          M ETHODOLOGY
187
188
      ClassroomKD is a novel multi-mentor distillation framework inspired by real-world classroom
      environments. It is designed to address the challenges of large capacity gaps, error accumulation, and
189
      lack of dynamic adaptation. Our framework is illustrated in Figure 2.
190
191   Classroom Definition. A classroom comprises (1) a high-capacity teacher model, t, (2) a small
192   student model, s, and (3) n peer models of intermediate capacities, \mathbb {P}= \{p_i\}_{i=1}^n . We define
193   \mathbb {M}= \{t\} \cup \sP  as the set of pre-trained mentors that remain frozen during the student’s training process.
194
      At each training step, the student distills knowledge from a dynamically selected subset of mentors,
      called the active mentors (\mathbb {M}' \subseteq \sM ). The set of all classroom models is denoted \mathbb {C}= \{s\} \cup \sM . We
195
      use the Knowledge Filtering (KF) Module for intelligent mentor selection and the Mentoring Module
196
      to adjust the teaching pace based on the capacity gap of each mentor-student pair.
197
198
      3.1           K NOWLEDGE F ILTERING M ODULE
199
200   The KF Module is designed to intelligently select which mentors should contribute to the student’s
201   learning process for each data sample. This selective approach mitigates error accumulation and
202   prevents the student from learning from less effective mentors.
203
      Let \bm {x}= \{x_k\}_{k=1}^N be a batch of training data with size N , and \bm {y}= \{y_k\}_{k=1}^N be the ground-truth
204   labels. The batch inputs \bm {x} are forwarded through all classroom models to obtain the predicted logits
205   \protect \hat  {\vy }^m , which are then converted to probabilities with a softmax operation. We isolate the probability
206   assigned to the true class \bm {y} and compute a weighted average of the correct prediction probability
207   across the batch for each model. For all m \in \sC , this is defined as:
208                                                                \hat {\vy }^m &= m(\vx ) \\ \vp ^m &= \softmax (\hat {\vy }^m) \\ \vp ^m_{\text {gt}} &= \vp ^m[\vy ] \label {eq:rank_gt}\\ w^m &= \frac {1}{N} \sum _{k=1}^N \vp ^m_{\text {gt}}(x_k)
209

214
215   The weights w^m reflect the performance of model m on the current training batch. We use the
      computed weights as a proxy for mentor suitability in the distillation process and rank mentors based

      This ensures the student learns from high-quality sources by selecting mentors based on their perfor-
226   mance ranks. This selective approach prevents error accumulation as only mentors outperforming
227   the student can teach it, avoiding the propagation of errors from less effective mentors. Additionally,
228   it avoids information loss by consistently selecting the best-performing mentors, unlike random
229   mentor-dropping strategies (Son et al., 2021).

231   3.2    M ENTORING M ODULE
232
233   The Mentoring Module dynamically adjusts the influence of each active mentor based on the mentor-
234   student performance gap. This adaptive teaching strategy facilitates effective knowledge transfer
235   tailored to the student’s evolving ability to absorb information from each mentor.
236   The distillation loss minimizes KL divergence between the student and mentor’s output distributions:
237
238
                             \mathcal {L}_{distill}(P, Q; \tau ) = \tau ^2 \cdot \text {KL}\left (\softmax \left ({P}/{\tau }\right ) \,\|\, \softmax \left ({Q}/{\tau }\right )\right )                                                                (7)
239   where P and Q represent the logits from the mentor and student networks, respectively, and \tau  is a
240   temperature hyperparameter that smooths the probability distributions during the distillation process.
241
      The temperature \tau  controls the sharpness of the probability distributions, affecting the knowledge
242
      transfer from a mentor to the student. For each active mentor m \in \sM ' , we adjust the distillation
243   temperature \tau ^m based on the performance gap between the student and the mentor. The performance
244   gap is measured as the difference in their ranking scores:
245
246                                                                                                \label {eq:tau_t} \Delta r^m &= |r^m - r^s| / r^m \\ \tau ^m &= 1 + \Delta r^m \cdot \tau \label {eq:tau_adjustment}
247                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     (9)
248
      Here, \tau  is the base temperature, and \tau ^m increases with \Delta r^m , which represents the mentor-student
249   performance gap. A larger \Delta r^m results in a higher \tau ^m , smoothing the mentor’s output distribution.
250   This adjustment theoretically slows down the distillation process by softening the mentor’s predictions,
251   allowing the student to assimilate knowledge more gradually when the performance gap is large.
252   Conversely, the student receives sharper, more direct guidance when the gap is small.
253
      The total loss \protect \mathcal  {L} is computed by combining a task-specific loss Ltask with the weighted distillation
254
      losses from all active mentors:
255
256                                     \mathcal {L} = \alpha \mathcal {L}_{\mathrm {task}}(\hat {\vy }^s, \vy ) + \beta \sum _{m \in \sM '} \gamma ^m \mathcal {L}_{\mathrm {distill}}(\hat {\vy }^m,\hat {\vy }^s;\tau ^m) \label {eq:total_loss}    (10)
257
258   Here, \alpha = r^s represents the student’s self-confidence, which scales the task-specific loss. As the
259   student’s rank r^s improves, \alpha  increases, encouraging the student to rely more on its own predictions.
260   For each mentor m, \gamma ^m = r^m scales the corresponding distillation loss, where r^m is the mentor’s
261   rank relative to the student. \beta  is a hyperparameter to control the influence of distillation loss relative
262   to the task loss. This weighing, along with the mentor-specific temperature \tau ^m , ensures that higher-
263   performing mentors have a greater influence on the student’s learning, with each mentor distilling
264   knowledge at an appropriate rate based on the performance gap. We use Cross-Entropy Loss for
265   classification and MSE Loss for pose estimation tasks.
266   This promotes independent learning by increasing the student’s reliance on its own task performance
267   as its confidence grows. It also ensures that the student benefits from guidance based on the relative
268   performance of the active mentors, effectively balancing task-specific training with distillation from
269   the most suitable mentors. This dynamic and adaptive approach ensures optimized knowledge
      transfer, minimizes error accumulation, and enhances the overall performance of the student model.

528   5                  C ONCLUSION
529
530   We presented ClassroomKD, a novel knowledge distillation framework that mimics a classroom
531
      environment, where a student learns from a diverse set of mentors. By selectively integrating feedback
      through the Knowledge Filtering (KF) Module and dynamically adjusting teaching strategies with the
532
      Mentoring Module, ClassroomKD ensures effective knowledge transfer and mitigates the issues of
533
      error accumulation and capacity gap. Our approach significantly improves the student’s performance
534   in classification and pose estimation tasks, consistently outperforming traditional distillation methods.
535   Limitations. While we demonstrated the efficacy of ClassroomKD on image classification and
536   human pose estimation, its application to other domains and more complex tasks, such as object
537   detection and segmentation, presents a promising avenue for future work. Despite the improvements,
538   the framework introduces complexity, especially with respect to the mentor ranking and teaching
539   adjustments, which can require careful tuning. Future work will explore further optimizations and
      expand the framework’s utility to broader tasks.

      ClassroomKD shows strong potential in knowledge distillation, and one promising extension is its
759   application in dataset distillation, which can further broaden its impact across various tasks.
760
761   Dataset distillation aims to create small, synthetic datasets that enable neural networks to achieve
762
      comparable performance to those trained on the original, much larger datasets. This approach reduces
      computational costs and storage requirements while maintaining model generalization. By optimizing
763
      a small set of representative training samples, a distilled dataset S is generated such that a model
764
      trained on S performs well on the original dataset \protect \mathcal  {T} . In our experiments, we use FRePo (Zhou et al.,
765   2022) to create a distilled CIFAR-100 dataset, reducing each class to only 10 samples (Figure 7). Of
766   these, 7 images per class are used for training, while the remaining 3 are used for testing.

1268
1269              • In case (1), we notice two mentors with correct predictions, one above (green) and another
1270                below (gray) the student (blue). As we empirically show in Fig. 6 and Sec. F.2.1, the
1271                student probability for the correct class surpasses the weaker mentors as training progresses.
1272                Therefore, at any point in time, as long as the student prediction is not incorrect, it should
1273                only learn from those mentors who have a "sharper" peak than itself as the goal of
1274                knowledge distillation is to pull the student’s peak at the correct class upwards.
1275              • If we compare cases (1) and (2), despite both mentors predicting the correct class label
1276                with higher probability than the student, the red classifier is "sharper" than the green
1277                classifier. Hence, the student must give more importance to the feedback from the red
1278                classifier.
1279              • The ability of the student to approximate the distribution of a teacher is inversely related
1280                to the distance between the height of the student and the teacher’s peaks. As this distance
1281                is larger at the start of training and progressively decreases, the extent of softening the
1282                mentor distribution depends on the gap between the "sharpness" of the mentor and
1283                the student and varies with time.
