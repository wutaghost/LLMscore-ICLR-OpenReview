009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Combinatorial reasoning problems, particularly the notorious NP-hard tasks, re-
012            main a significant challenge for AI research. A common approach to addressing
013            them combines search with learned heuristics. Recent methods in this domain
014            utilize hierarchical planning, executing strategies based on subgoals. Our goal
015            is to advance research in this area and establish a solid conceptual and empirical
016            foundation. Specifically, we identify the following key obstacles, whose presence
017            favors the choice of hierarchical search methods: hard-to-learn value functions,
018            complex action spaces, presence of dead ends in the environment, or training data
019
               collected from diverse sources. Through in-depth empirical analysis, we establish
               that hierarchical search methods consistently outperform standard search methods
020
               across these dimensions, and we formulate insights for future research. On the prac-
021
               tical side, we also propose a consistent evaluation guidelines to enable meaningful
022            comparisons between methods and reassess the state-of-the-art algorithms.

      3    C OMBINATORIAL ENVIRONMENTS
083
084
      Our study targets solving combinatorial environments – domains in which the number of possible
085   configurations or decisions grows exponentially with the problem size, making them highly chal-
086   lenging to solve. This class includes several NP-hard problems, such as the Traveling Salesman
087   Problem (Applegate et al., 2006), the Rubik’s Cube (Singmaster, 1981), Sokoban (Culberson, 1997),
088   or solving non-linear inequalities (Sahni, 1974). To efficiently solve combinatorial problems an
089   algorithm should have the following key properties:
090
091         1. Learning from offline data. Since combinatorial reasoning environments are characterized
092            by a large space of possible configurations, learning without priors or handcrafted dense
093
               rewards is infeasible1 Thus, the algorithm has to be able to learn from additional offline data,
               such as demonstrations.
094
095         2. Combinatorial space abstraction. The space complexity significantly restricts the fraction
096            of observable states. As a result, it is unrealistic to expect repeated visits to nearby states, an
097            assumption that some approaches implicitly rely on.
098         3. Planning. The algorithm needs a planning module. In contrast, methods that don’t use search
099            and follow a single action trajectory are inherently limited by computational complexity,
100            since they can perform only a constant number of operations before choosing an action.
101            Solving NP-hard problems within a fixed computation budget is computationally infeasible
102
               (Bruck and Goodman, 1987).
103
      Many hierarchical methods have not been designed for combinatorial problems, so they fail to meet
104
      the listed conditions and cannot be expected to be efficient in these applications. For instance,

      4     S UBGOAL METHODS
115
      Subgoal methods, or hierarchical methods, are a family of algorithms designed to solve complex
116
      decision-making tasks by breaking down the overall objective into smaller, more manageable subgoals
117
      (Sutton et al., 1999). Instead of searching for a sequence of low-level actions that directly lead from
118   the initial state to the goal, the agent first identifies high-level intermediate targets – subgoals – that
119   guide the trajectory toward the final goal. The use of subgoals is widely considered as a method
120   that scales better to longer horizons (Chen et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2022), mitigates errors in value
121   approximations (Czechowski et al., 2021), and reduces overall complexity by decomposing the
122   problem into smaller subproblems (Sutton et al., 1999; Zawalski et al., 2023). The process of
123   searching involves the following components:
124
125          • Subgoal generator that, given a state within the search tree, outputs subgoals to be achieved.
126
               For instance, a subgoal may be a future state (Czechowski et al., 2021; Zawalski et al., 2023)
               or a class of desired outcomes (Jiang et al., 2019; Panov and Skrynnik, 2018). The generator
127
               is used by the planner to construct a search tree of subgoals.
128
129          • Low-level policy that determines a path of low-level actions between subgoals. For instance,
130
               it may be a trained goal-reaching policy (Czechowski et al., 2021; Zawalski et al., 2023), a
               local search (Czechowski et al., 2021; Kujanpää et al., 2023a), or a stored path from previous
131
               episodes (Eysenbach et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2022).
132
133          • Planner that determines the order in which the search tree nodes are expanded. Standard
134
               planning algorithms like BestFS (Czechowski et al., 2021), PHS (Kujanpää et al., 2023a), or
               their modified forms (Zawalski et al., 2023), are typically used.
135
136          • Value function that estimates the distance between the given state and the goal state. The
137            planner uses this information to select the next node to expand with the subgoal generator.
138            In some works it is also called heuristic value.
139
      In our experiments, we use kSubS Czechowski et al. (2021) and AdaSubS Zawalski et al. (2023) as
140
      subgoal methods well-suited for combinatorial problems, as they satisfy the conditions formulated
141
      in Section 3. We also experimented with HIPS and HIPS-ε (Kujanpää et al., 2023a;b), but these
142   methods generally fail to solve the problems within a reasonable computational budget. Therefore,
143   their results are omitted from the main text and discussed in see Appendix I.
144
145
      We compare the performance of the selected subgoal approaches against three popular low-level
      methods: BestFS, A*, and MCTS. To ensure a fair comparison and improve efficiency, we augment
146
      these algorithms by using a trained policy to select the top actions before each node expansion. We
147
      refer to them as ρ-BestFS, ρ-A*, and ρ-MCTS. A detailed description, analysis, and pseudocode for
148   each of these algorithms can be found in Appendix F. See also Appendix H for diagrams explaining
149   different search methods.

151   4.1   T RAINING C OMPONENTS
152
153   In our experiments, the models for both subgoal methods and low-level searches were trained
154   using imitation learning, following standard practice (Nair et al., 2018; Czechowski et al., 2021).
155   Specifically, we collected a dataset of approximately 500 000 trajectories for each environment.
156   Trajectories are sequences of consecutive states and actions leading to the goal state. We used various
157   methods of dataset collection, like hand-crafted algorithms, trained policies, reversed random shuffles,
158
      and others, which let us to study the influence of training data characteristics on the performance of
      search methods.
159
160   To ensure a fair comparison, all methods shared common components whenever applicable (e.g.,
161   each method uses the same value function). This allows us to focus on the differences between
      the search algorithms, rather than heuristic biases. No additional heuristics were used, ensuring

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      that performance differences arise solely from the algorithmic approaches. While hand-designed
163   heuristics often yield superior results in specific cases, our goal is to provide a broader understanding
164   of the strengths and limitations of different planning methods. Training components directly from
165   data allows us to draw conclusions that are more likely to generalize across diverse environments
166   compared to using hardcoded components.

      4.2     P ERFORMANCE M ETRIC
171   Our performance metric is the success rate, defined as the percentage of problem instances solved
172   within a given complete search budget. The complete search budget is the total number of visited
173   states in the search tree. In particular, for subgoal methods, the budget includes both the generated
174   subgoals and the states visited by the low-level policy used to connect these subgoals.
175
      By accounting for the total number of visited states, this metric provides a unified and fair comparison
176
      of search efficiency across different methods. We argue that reporting only the number of visited
177   subgoal nodes would unfairly favor subgoal methods (see Appendix I for details).

180   5     A NALYSIS
181
182   We investigate how environmental properties and training data influence the performance of hierar-
183   chical methods compared to low-level search approaches in combinatorial reasoning tasks. While
184
      previous works (Czechowski et al., 2021; Zawalski et al., 2023; Kujanpää et al., 2023a;b) show a
      considerable advantage of hierarchical methods, our experiments reveal that this advantage is not
185
      consistent across all scenarios (see Figures 4 or 5 for specific examples). Specifically, we answer the
186
      following research questions:
187
188         Q1. Is hierarchical search more effective than low-level search for solving combinatorial reason-
189             ing problems?
190
            Q2. What environmental properties and characteristics of the training data amplify performance
191             differences? When hierarchical search should be preferred over low-level search?
192
            Q3. What pitfalls should be avoided when interpreting experimental results?
193
194
      To address these questions, we conducted a wide range of experiments comparing subgoal and low-
195   level search algorithms across a variety of combinatorial reasoning tasks. Below, in each subsection
196   we summarize the key findings that reveal the most significant factors affecting performance, followed
197   by a brief discussion. For each finding, we link it to the relevant research questions. The extended
198   analysis of these factors can be found in Appendix B.
199
      We present our findings using the Rubik’s Cube, Sokoban, N-Puzzle, and Inequality Theorem Proving
200
      (INT) (Wu et al., 2021) environments. These classical benchmarks are widely used in planning
201   research (McAleer et al., 2019; Czechowski et al., 2021) and are known to be NP-hard (Demaine et al.,
202   2018; Culberson, 1997; Ratner and Warmuth, 1986). Detailed descriptions of these environments can
203   be found in Appendix A.
204
      All methods in our study were trained using imitation learning, with each approach sharing the
205
      same value function, as outlined in Section 4.1. In particular, no domain knowledge is used in
206
      any experiment. To ensure fair comparisons, we measured complete search budgets, in contrast to
207   counting only high-level search nodes, to avoid giving any unfair advantage to subgoal methods, as
208   discussed in Section 4.2 (which contributes to the research question Q3).

264

399   The primary difference between low-level methods and subgoal methods is that the former predicts
400   the next action, and the latter – the next state. In many environments, the action space is as simple as a
401   few bits, allowing for iterating over all possible actions, and sampling them. At the same time, states
402   may be considerably larger, up to the extreme of image observations. However, in some environments,
403   the action space is comparable to the state space, or even more complex.
404   Given a complex action space, in low-level methods, each node expansion involves executing many
405   similar actions, limiting their ability to efficiently search through the space. In contrast, subgoal
406   methods compute actions only to connect subgoals, which is a much simpler task. This targeted
407   approach reduces the negative impact of a large action space, allowing subgoal methods to maintain
408   their efficiency even as the action space grows (Q2).

410   where the action space was artificially inflated by giving the agent access to 100 copies of each action.

      Once an agent encounters a dead end, reaching the goal becomes impossible, leading to wasted
452
      computational effort. Our results, presented in Figure 13, show that subgoal methods tend to enter
453   dead ends less often than low-level methods. Using longer subgoals improves the ability to bypass
454   those areas.
455
456
      Among low-level methods, ρ-A* performs the best at minimizing dead ends rate, as its node selection
      regularizes values by depth in the search tree, preventing it from over-committing to dead ends.
457
      However, even ρ-A* is outperformed by subgoal methods, which rely on greedy value estimates and
458
      subgoals.
459
460   Deciding whether a state is a dead end can be NP-hard. Hence, it is much harder for the value function
461   to penalize dead ends compared to the policy, which only ranks the available actions and does not have
462
      to identify dead ends (Feng et al., 2022). Furthermore, demonstrations used for imitation learning
      lead to the goal state, hence they contain no dead ends. Therefore the value function trained this way
463
      is never directly instructed to penalize dead ends. At the same time, during training of the policy the
464
      actions leading to dead ends are never reinforced. Our experiments show that hierarchical search
465   relies much less on the value guidance compared to low-level search (Section 5.2), which further
466   supports our conclusions. For a more detailed analysis, see Appendix B.4.
467
468
            Takeaway Subgoal methods are more effective at avoiding dead ends compared to low-level
469
            search (Q2).

      5.5     S UBGOAL METHODS GENERALIZE OUT- OF - DISTRIBUTION
473
474   Planners that can generalize to out-of-distribution (OOD) instances are essential for robust decision-
475   making (Kirk et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2021). We tested two types of generalization in the Sokoban
476   environment: by significantly changing the layout of the board and by using extremely difficult
477   boards from the DeepMind dataset (Guez et al., 2018) (see Figure 14 for examples).
478
      In both cases, subgoal methods show better performance than low-level methods, with the gap
479
      increasing as the distribution shift become more visible (see Figures 15-16). However, we found
480   that kSubS, when using twice longer subgoals, collapses in OOD evaluations, despite outperforming
481   ρ-BestFS and other low-level methods on in-distribution tasks. As the subgoal distance increases,
482   predicting the distant future becomes more challenging, making it less likely for the generated
483   subgoals to be valid and reachable, especially in OOD tasks. In contrast, low-level methods avoid this
484   issue, as selecting an action from a limited set always results in a valid move. Thus, while subgoal
485   methods can be effective in OOD scenarios, excessively long subgoals can degrade performance
      (Q2).

      6    R ELATED W ORK
528
529   Solving Decision-Making Problems Decision-making problems are often framed as Markov
530   Decision Processes (MDPs) (Sutton et al., 1999), which can be solved using Reinforcement Learning
531
      (RL) algorithms like PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) or DQN (Mnih et al., 2015). These methods learn
      policies through interaction with the environment. An alternative to learning from trial and error is
532
      Imitation Learning (IL), training models directly from offline demonstrations. The availability of
533
      large-scale datasets (Walke et al., 2023; Collaboration et al., 2023; Grauman et al., 2022; Dosovitskiy
534   et al., 2017), make it applicable to the most complex domains like robotics (Mandlekar et al., 2018;
535   Edmonds et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2024), autonomous driving (Kelly et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022; Zhang
536   and Cho, 2017), and physics-based control (Kim et al., 2020; Fickinger et al., 2022). Key foundational
537   methods such as Behavioral Cloning (BC) (Sutton and Barto, 1998), Inverse Reinforcement Learning
538   (IRL) (Baker et al., 2009), or DAgger (Ross et al., 2011) have been instrumental in advancing IL
539   for complex environments where direct exploration is less practical. In this work, we use IL to train
      components for the search methods, such as the policy and value function.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      Subgoal Methods Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning methods tackle complex decision-making
541   tasks by breaking them into subgoals. HIRO (Nachum et al., 2018) reuses past data by goal relabeling.
542   HAC (Levy et al., 2019) builds a multi-layer hierarchy of policies trained with hindsight. Hierarchical
543   Diffuser (Chen et al., 2024) learns to predict future states with diffusion models. Graph-based
544   methods, such as SoRB (Eysenbach et al., 2019) or DHRL (Lee et al., 2022) build a high-level graph
545   of states, which then allow for efficient shortest path finding. GCP (Pertsch et al., 2020) learns to
546   predict middle states between two given observations. Algorithms such as HPG (Ghavamzadeh
547   and Mahadevan, 2003) or H-DDPG (Yang et al., 2018) extend the classical RL algorithms to the
548   hierarchical setting.
549   In the area of combinatorial reasoning, there has been growing interest in applying HRL techniques.
550   kSubS (Czechowski et al., 2021) introduces a hierarchical search algorithm that iteratively generates
551   subgoals to construct a search tree. Building on this, AdaSubS (Zawalski et al., 2023) incorporates
552   multiple subgoal generators, each trained to predict subgoals at different distances from the target,
553   allowing for dynamic adaptation of the planning horizon based on problem complexity. HIPS
554   (Kujanpää et al., 2023a) and HIPS-ε (Kujanpää et al., 2023b) perform search using subgoals generated
555
      by VQ-VAE models (van den Oord et al., 2017).
556
557
      Low-level Search Algorithms Traditional search algorithms like Best-First Search (BestFS), A*
      (Cormen et al., 2009; Russell and Norvig, 2009), and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Veness
558
      et al., 2009; James et al., 2017) have long been the foundation for solving complex decision-making
559
      problems. Recent advancements have improved these methods by integrating neural network-based
560   heuristics, improving their efficiency in large search spaces (Silver et al., 2018; Yonetani et al., 2021).
561   A variant of ρ-BestFS used in (Czechowski et al., 2021; Zawalski et al., 2023), leverage heuristics
562   learned through behavioral cloning to guide search. More recent algorithms, like PHS (Orseau and
563   Lelis, 2021) or LevinTS (Orseau et al., 2023), combine policy-driven and value-based approaches,
564   offering both theoretical guarantees and strong empirical performance. Additionally, PDDL planners
565   (Haslum et al., 2019) solve decision-making problems by using predefined action models and goals,
566   with domain-independent planners offering broad applicability, while domain-specific ones achieve
567   higher performance in specialized tasks.
568
569   Empirical Studies on Algorithmic Performance Our work aligns with recent empirical studies
570
      that investigate the conditions under which various algorithmic approaches excel. For instance,
      (Andrychowicz et al., 2020) investigates how specific design choices influence the performance of
571
      PPO, while other research compares offline reinforcement learning with behavioral cloning (Kumar
572
      et al., 2022) or explores design choices for language-conditioned robotic imitation learning (Mees
573   et al., 2022). In this paper, we focus on hierarchical search in combinatorial reasoning problems,
574   specifically studying the conditions where hierarchical methods outperform low-level planners. To
575   the best of our knowledge, this is the first systematic study of the relationship between hierarchical
576   and low-level search in this context.
577
578
      7    O PEN QUESTIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
579
580
      While we identified several features that facilitate the performance of subgoal methods, that list is
581
      not exhaustive. Thus, it is essential to study this topic further, expand the analysis to more subgoal-
582   based and low-level algorithms, and include even more types of environments. While most of our
583   takeaways were confirmed in multiple environments, extending the evaluation to more domains would
584   strengthen our conclusions. Additionally, our work provides mostly experimental validation of the
585   claims. Finding theoretical foundations for the observed properties, such as Theorem 1, would also
586   be a valuable direction.
587
      In our experiments, we focused on measuring the performance of the tested methods based on the
588   search tree size – an objective, algorithmic metric that is independent of the hardware or optimizations
589   used, can be measured precisely, and is fully reproducible, unlike the wall time. However, in many
590   practical applications computational complexity is also essential. We used the architectures proposed
591   by the authors, as our aim for each method was to optimize performance instead of time. To optimize
592   execution time, we can tune the number of parameters or use other architectures that are known to
593   work well for generating subgoals, such as VQ-VAEs (Kujanpää et al., 2023a), diffusions (Black
      et al., 2024), or MLPs (Park et al., 2023).

      8    C ONCLUSIONS
595
596
      We conducted a thorough comparison of hierarchical and low-level search methods for combinatorial
597
      reasoning tasks. Our experiments provides empirical and some theoretical evidence that hierarchical
598   approaches should be preferred in environments where value estimation is challenging and learned
599   estimates face significant uncertainty, particularly when learning from diverse suboptimal data.
600   Furthermore, subgoal methods demonstrate better scalability in complex action spaces and are more
601   effective at avoiding dead ends than low-level methods. Thus, in environments characterized by those
602   properties, it is advisable to consider subgoal methods as an alternative to low-level search. While
603   these properties are not sufficient conditions, they serve as useful indicators.
604   Based on our results, we propose guidelines for future research in this area. According to our
605   experiments, the best-performing low-level search was usually ρ-BestFS with a confidence threshold
606   (see Appendix F). Although it is rather sensitive to the threshold value, which has to be optimized
607   for each domain separately, we advocate using this simple method as a standard baseline for further
608   research in hierarchical search. Our guidelines are further discussed in Appendix J.
609
      Additionally, we identified easy-to-overlook mistakes in reporting the results that may lead to
610   misleading conclusions. Most importantly, the reported complete search budget of hierarchical
611   methods must include all the visited states and not only the high-level nodes as used in some prior
612   works.
613
614
615   9    B ROADER I MPACT
616
617   Our study has broader implications for other complex domains. For example, advancements in
618   robotics often face significant challenges due to limited data, leading many methods to rely on collec-
619
      tive datasets like Open X-Embodiment (Collaboration et al., 2023). As shown in our experiments,
      hierarchical search methods benefit substantially from training on diverse expert data (Section 5.1).
620
      Furthermore, the data bottleneck increases the need for the models to generalize to out-of-distribution
621
      scenes and tasks, which is also an advantage of hierarchical methods (Section 5.5). Finally, an
622   essential aspect of robotics involves preventing the robot from becoming stuck or losing a manipu-
623   lated object, events that can be seen as dead-end scenarios (Section 5.4). Successful applications of
624   hierarchical methods in robotics include models such as SuSIE (Black et al., 2024) and HIQL (Park
625   et al., 2023).
626
      Additionally, our experiments indicate that hierarchical methods scale well in long-horizon tasks,
627
      as evidenced by their performance in the N-Puzzle and the Rubik’s Cube (using Beginner-level
628   demonstrations), where the average sequence of steps often exceeds 200. Interestingly, while low-
629   level methods can still perform well in these scenarios, we observed that they tend to be much more
630   sensitive to hyperparameter tuning.
631
      It is important to note that we do not claim hierarchical methods are universally superior to low-level
632
      approaches in all complex domains. Instead, the properties highlighted in our analysis suggest cases
633
      where they should be considered.

       B.1   L EARNING FROM DIVERSE DATA SOURCES
1202
1203   Achieving superhuman performance in complex tasks, as demonstrated by AlphaGo Silver et al.
1204   (2016), often involves large-scale datasets of demonstrations obtained from agents with varying
1205   skill levels and strategies. However, this diversity introduces challenges such as inconsistencies in
1206   demonstrations and variations in quality (Fu et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Levine et al., 2020).
1207   Widely used datasets like D4RL (Fu et al., 2020), Open X-Embodiment (Collaboration et al., 2023),
1208   or Waymo Open Dataset (Sun et al., 2020) reflect this diversity, highlighting the need to address these
1209
       challenges effectively. We want to answer the question whether such setting is handled better by
       high-level or low-level search algorithms.
1210
1211   Experiment setup For this analysis, we focus on the Rubik’s cube environment. We collected a
1212   dataset of 500 000 trajectories, computed with four different solvers for the Rubik’s cube:
1213
1214          • Beginner – the simplest human-oriented solving algorithm. It aims to order the cube layer
1215
                by layer with a few primitive tactics. Because of that the solutions are structured, but also
                very long (typically between 150 and 200 moves).
1216
1217          • CFOP – an algorithm designed for speedcubers. It is based on the same principle as
1218
                Beginner, but employs many advanced tactics that make the solutions faster (typically about
                100 moves).
1219
1220          • Kociemba – a computational solver that finds near-optimal solutions (usually between 20
1221            and 40 moves) in short time. It is heavily optimized based on the algebraic properties of the
1222
                Rubik’s cube.
1223          • Random – solutions obtained by scrambling an ordered cube with random moves and
1224            reversing the trajectory.
1225
1226
       Figure 31 shows example solutions generated with each solver. Clearly, the algorithmic solvers
       (Beginner and CFOP) generate much longer solutions that the other methods. They are also more
1227
       structured, as they are based on building patterns. The computational solver Kociemba on the other
1228
       hand go directly towards the solution because its moves are carefully optimized to ensure maximal
1229   advantage. Because of that, this dataset represent a truly diverse set of demonstrations.
1230
1231   Results As shown in Figure 2, the subgoal methods outperform the low-level methods by a wide
1232
       margin. While ρ-BestFS is comparable on small budgets, it struggles with solving most of the
       instances. Also, it should be noted that the performance of the subgoal methods changes only slightly
1233
       compared to training on a single Random solver (Figure 4) while the low-level searches are heavily
1234
       affected.
1235
1236   Learned values To find the sources of that outcome, we checked the values learned by the heuristic
1237   function. Because of the diversity introduced by combining the experts, we should expect that the
1238
       estimates are subject to high uncertainty and possibly high variance.
1239   Figure 21 shows the distribution of the learned heuristic for random fully shuffled cubes. Although
1240   most instances can be solved optimally within 20-26 moves, the estimates range from 14 to 90
1241   steps. Furthermore, the distribution is clearly bimodal – one mode correspond to a typical length of
       Kociemba solution, the other to CFOP.

1267   the search. If a search algorithm aims to imitate Beginner or CFOP, it has to reach the layer pattern,
1268   characteristic of those solvers. However, the random states tend to have very low distance estimate,
1269   compared to the initial layer patterns. Because of that, for tens of steps the heuristic estimates would
1270   be actually increasing, making the reached states less and less probable to expand.
1271
       In practice, the low-level searches usually fail to cross this gap. On the other hand, the high-level
1272
       methods are partially guided by the subgoal generators that ignore the values. The value gap that
1273   spans across about 30 steps can be crossed with as few as 5 subgoals of length 6. Because of that
1274   both kSubS and AdaSubS can successfully leverage the schematic algorithmic solutions.

       To finally confirm that conclusion, we must answer the question whether the performance of low-level
       searches would increase if they could leverage the algorithmic solutions as well. For that purpose,
1277
       we trained the components for each method using data only from the Beginner solver. This way
1278
       we remove the challenge of noisy initial values. As shown in Figure 5, the low-level searches
1279   indeed perform much better. BestFS even matches the performance of AdaSubS. That confirms our
1280   observation that low-level searchas fail to utilize multimodal data because they rely too much on the
1281   value function and seek monotonic slopes.
1282
       At the same time we observe that since BestFS and AdaSubS show nearly identical performance
1283
       on Beginner solutions, it is questionable that hierarchical methods handle long-horizon tasks better,
1284
       which is a common belief (Nachum et al., 2018; Eysenbach et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2024).

1396   Observe that the search performed by low-level methods is guided mainly by the value function.
1397   Hence, if the computed estimates are subject to high variance, low-level search struggles to make
1398   any progress. On the other hand, the subgoal search is guided both by the value function and the
1399   subgoal generator. Both the subgoal generator and the conditional policy that connects subgoals do
1400
       not depend on the values. Hence, the value function is used only in the high-level nodes, which is
       only a fraction of the search tree.
1401
1402   An extreme case of that behavior is demonstrated by Adaptive Subgoal Search. Because in our
1403   configuration each generator outputs a single subgoal, the value is nearly not used at all for search.
       Only when the search is stuck, the secondary generators select the highest-ranked node to expand,

       B.3   C OMPLEX ACTION SPACES
1462
1463   In environments with large action spaces, search methods often struggle due to the exponential
1464   increase in the number of choices at each decision point (Sutton and Barto, 1998). This complex-
1465   ity makes it difficult to efficiently identify optimal actions, slowing down decision-making and
1466   exploration (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2016).
1467   The primary difference between low-level methods and subgoal methods is that the former predicts
1468   the next action, and the latter – the next state. In many environments, the action space is as simple
1469   as a few bits, allowing for iterating over all possible actions, and sampling them. At the same time,
1470   states may be considerably larger, up to the extreme of image observations. However, in some
1471   environments, the action space is comparable to the state space, or even more complex. A classic
1472   example is the AntMaze environment, in which actions are 8-dimensional, while the goal space is
1473   only 2-dimensional (Fu et al., 2020).
1474   Among the combinatorial reasoning environments we consider, INT has the most complex action
1475   space. In INT, actions correspond to proof steps and are represented as the chosen axiom, specification
1476   of its input entities, and the required premises (Wu et al., 2021). Thus, the complexity of the action
1477   is at least comparable to the states. Moreover, solving the INT inequalities is based on constant
1478   simplification of the given expression, so the state is getting even smaller with each step.
1479   Our experiments, shown in Figure 10, clearly confirm the advantage of using subgoal methods in the
1480   INT environment. To further verify the source of that advantage, we conducted another experiment, in
1481   a modified Rubik’s cube environment. Recall that the experiment presented in Section 5.1 shows that
1482   subgoals offer no significant advantage in the original Rubik’s cube (with a single data source). Now,
1483   we want to check whether the outcome would be different if the action space were more complex.
1484   For that purpose, we extended the action space 100 times. That is, the new action space consists of
1485   1200 possible moves to choose from – 100 copies of each original action.
1486   As shown in Figure 11, the subgoal methods are barely affected by the change, while the low-level
1487   searches are unable to exceed 20% success rate. That result confirms our proposition that when facing
1488   a complex action space, hierarchical methods offer considerably better performance.
1489
       According to our analysis, the primary issue with low-level searches in the augmented Rubik’s cube
1490
       is the lack of diversity of visited states. When for each state there are hundreds of actions that lead
1491   to a similar outcome, they are rated similarly by the policy. Hence, all the top actions essentially
1492   lead to the same outcome, which strongly limits the branching factor and trivializes the search trees.
1493   On the other hand, subgoal methods are not affected because subgoal generation does not depend
1494   on the action space. The conditional policy that connects the generated subgoals does not build a
1495   search tree, but always follows the single best action. Because of that, subgoal methods maintain
1496   their performance, even though the action space is much more complex.
1497   It is also important to note that even though some state spaces may seem complex, the underlying
1498   manifold of possible configurations is in fact low-dimensional. For instance, we use 12x12 Sokoban
1499   boards, where each square is encoded as one-hot of 7 possible items, so technically the state space is
1500   1008-dimensional, while there are only 4 actions. However, in practice the subgoal is defined by the
1501   positions of agent and boxes, which is at most 10-dimensional, hence rather simple to generate.

       F.4    K S UB S AND A DA S UB S
2161
2162   Overview AdaSubS is a hierarchical search algorithm designed to solve combinatorial problems by
2163   operating on high-level nodes, which represent multiple steps rather than single actions. It employs
2164   multiple generators Gk1 , Gk2 , . . . , Gkm to generate subsequent subgoals, a value function V to estimate
2165   the distance from a given state to the solution, and a conditional low-level policy P to execute a series
2166   of actions leading from one subgoal to the next. kSubS is a special case of AdaSubS, where only
2167   a single generator is used. These methods are introduced and studied in (Czechowski et al., 2021;
2168
       Zawalski et al., 2023).
2169
       Stages The method begins by adding m initial nodes (one per each generator) to a priority queue,
2170
       where each initial node i is assigned a priority (ki , V(s0 )). Here, ki is the length of the generator
2171
       used during the node’s expansion, and V(s0 ) estimates the distance (in low-level actions) between s0
2172   and the solution. The following steps are repeated until a solution is found or the budget is exhausted:
2173
2174           • Selection for expansion: The node ((k, V(s), s) with the highest priority is extracted from
2175             the queue. This priority structure ensures that the algorithm prioritizes expanding the longest
2176             subgoals whenever possible.
2177           • Generating subgoals: The current state s is passed to the selected generator Gk , which
2178             produces multiple subgoal propositions represented as states s∗1 , s∗2 , . . . , s∗p .
2179           • Verifying reachability: Since Gk can produce invalid or unreachable subgoals, each pro-
2180             posed subgoal must be verified. The conditional low-level policy P begins an iterative
2181             process, taking single steps from s towards the proposed subgoal s∗j . If s∗j is reached within
                 k steps, the subgoal is accepted, and new high-level nodes { (ki , V(s∗j )), s∗j }i∈{1...m} are

2182
2183             added to the priority queue as potential future subgoals to expand.
2184
       For a graphical overview of how AdaSubS works, see Appendix H.

       J     C OMMON PITFALLS IN HIERARCHICAL SEARCH EVALUATIONS
2431
2432   In this study, one of our primary goals is to identify common but often overlooked pitfalls in evaluating
2433   hierarchical search methods, which can lead to misleading conclusions. Based on our findings, we
2434   propose a set of guidelines that help ensure meaningful and consistent comparisons across different
2435   methods. We observed that the nature of hierarchical search makes it easy, whether intentionally
2436   or not, to present results in a way that favors certain methods, often without readers being aware.
2437   In this section, we present key insights on this issue, with an emphasis on the following evaluation
2438   guidelines:
2439
              • Report results using a complete search budget.
2440
2441          • Include ρ-BestFS with a confidence threshold as a baseline.
2442          • Ensure careful tuning of the confidence threshold.
2443          • Use up-to-date code for running experiments.
2444
2445   J.1   C OMPLETE SEARCH BUDGET
2446
2447   We define the performance metric in terms of success rate, which is the percentage of problem
2448   instances solved within a specified complete search budget. This budget refers to the total number of
2449   states visited during the search process. For hierarchical methods, this includes both the subgoals
2450   generated and the states visited by the low-level policies connecting those subgoals.
2451   Reporting the complete search budget is crucial, as opposed to the sparse search budget, which counts
2452   only the high-level nodes in the search tree. As discussed in Appendix I, Kujanpää et al. (2023a) rely
2453   on the sparse search budget for their evaluations. This creates a misleading impression that HIPS
2454   outperforms low-level baselines, while in reality, it requires significantly more computational effort
2455   to solve the same problems.
2456   To illustrate this issue, consider a simple environment where an agent must navigate a 100x100 empty
2457   room to reach a goal on the opposite side. In this case, a hierarchical method may require only a single
2458   subgoal – directly corresponding to the goal state – while a low-level method, even if following the
2459   optimal path, would require at least 100 steps. A sparse search budget would misleadingly indicate
2460   that the hierarchical method solves the task in one step, while the low-level approach requires 100
2461   steps, implying a 100x higher cost. However, both methods traverse the same path, making this
2462   comparison inaccurate. Using the complete search budget, both methods would be assigned the same
2463
       cost, providing a much more meaningful comparison.
2464   This issue arises in practical settings as well. Figure 44 compares subgoal methods and low-level
2465   BestFS on the Sokoban environment. The dashed line represents the same runs but evaluated with the
2466   sparse search budget instead of the complete search budget. For BestFS, both budget measures are
2467   equivalent. The figure clearly demonstrates that while kSubS and ρ-BestFS visit a similar number of
2468   states to solve an instance, the sparse search budget falsely amplifies the difference between the two
2469
       methods.

       J.2   BASELINES
2485
2486   A common evaluation practice in hierarchical search studies is to compare hierarchical methods
2487   against the search algorithm used as the planner (Czechowski et al., 2021; Zawalski et al., 2023;
2488   Kujanpää et al., 2023a;b). While this is generally a good approach, it is critical to ensure that baseline
2489   methods are properly tuned to allow for fair comparisons.
2490   Our study shows that the most effective low-level method is ρ-BestFS with a confidence threshold.
2491   This simple greedy search often performs significantly better than other low-level methods and,
2492   in some cases, is competitive with subgoal methods. However, if we were to follow prior works
2493   such as (Czechowski et al., 2021; Zawalski et al., 2023) and restrict our comparisons to variants of
2494   BestFS that select a fixed number of actions in each node expansion, without employing a confidence
2495   threshold (see Appendix F.1 for detailed definitions and analysis), we would artificially widen the gap
2496   between BestFS and subgoal methods. As noted in Appendix F.1, the performance of ρ-BestFS is
2497   highly sensitive to the confidence threshold, and proper tuning is essential. Nevertheless, we advocate
2498
       for using ρ-BestFS with a confidence threshold as a standard baseline in evaluations of hierarchical
       methods.

       L    P ROOF OF THE D ENSIFICATION OF THE ACTION SPACE T HEOREM
2647
2648   In Section 5.3, we showed experimentally that both in the mathematical INT environment and Rubik’s
2649   Cube with multiplied action space the advantage of subgoal methods is significant. We attributed
2650   those benefits to the ability of subgoal methods to use states as actions and the reduced diversity in
2651   low-level search. And indeed, we can prove in general that as the action space gets more complex,
2652   the diversity of top actions drops.
2653
       To give an illustrative example, in the Rubik’s Cube experiment, to model the increasingly complex
2654   action space, for an arbitrary state we can view the training data as a ground-truth density function f
2655   over an interval [0, 1], that is split evenly between the actions (i.e. into 12 intervals of length 1/12).
2656   Then, we can define arbitrarily dense action spaces An consisting of n points distributed evenly in
2657   the domain. For instance, A12 corresponds to the standard Rubik’s Cube action space, while A1200
2658   corresponds to the variant multiplied 100 times. Our theorem confirms that the actions selected by
2659   the policy gets less diverse as the complexity of the action space increases, up to the extreme of
2660   converging to a single point as n approaches infinity. In practice, it is even more general, since the
2661   data-driven action distribution f may also model smooth interpolation between actions.
2662   While this is rather intuitive when the learned distributions are perfect, it may seem that approximation
2663   errors, induced both by the limited training data and the policy network can actually improve diversity.
2664   We show that the result holds even in presence of arbitrarily large approximation errors, which is a
2665   bit counter-intuitive.
2666   Formally, the theorem is as follows:
