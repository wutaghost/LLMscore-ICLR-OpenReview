009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Designing proper experiments and intervening targets is a longstanding problem
012            in scientific or causal discovery. It is fundamentally impossible to identify the
013            underlying causal structure merely based on the observational data. Obtaining
014            interventional data, on the other hand, is crucial to causal discovery, yet it is usually
015            expensive or time-consuming to obtain sufficient interventional data to facilitate
016
               causal discovery. Previous approaches usually leverage uncertainty or gradient sig-
               nals to determine the intervention targets, and may suffer from the suboptimality. In
017
               this work, we investigate a different approach, whether we can leverage Large Lan-
018
               guage Models (LLMs) to assist with the intervention targeting in causal discovery
019            by making use of the rich world knowledge about the experimental design in LLM.
020            Specifically, we present Large Language Model Guided Intervention Targeting
021            (LeGIT), a robust framework that effectively incorporates LLMs to assist with the
022            intervention targeting in causal discovery. Surprisingly, across 4 different scales
023            of realistic benchmarks, LeGIT significantly outperforms previous approaches.
024            LeGIT opens up a new frontier for using LLMs in experimental design.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Science originates along with discovering new causal knowledge with interventional experiments
029   inspired by observations (Hanson, 1958; Kuhn & Hawkins, 1963). The art of finding causal relations
030   from different interventions is then summarized and improved with statistical methods (Pearl &
031   Mackenzie, 2018; Spirtes et al., 2000; 2010; Glymour et al., 2019). Identifying and utilizing
032   causal relations is essential to a variety of applications such as biology (Vowels et al., 2022) and
033   financial system (Dong et al., 2023). Despite the wide deployment of causal discovery methods,
034   identifying the underlying causal connections merely based on observational data is fundamentally
035   impossible (Spirtes et al., 2000). It usually requires additional interventional data obtained by
036   perturbing part of the causal system to overcome the limited identifiability issue (Spirtes et al., 2000).
037   Nevertheless, collecting interventional data is expensive and time-consuming, as it usually involves
038   a physical process of a real-world system (Cherry & Daley, 2012; Sunar et al., 2019). Conse-
039   quently, both the number of samples and intervention targets are significantly limited in real-world
040   experimental design (Murphy, 2006; Tong & Koller, 2001). Previous approaches usually leverage
041   uncertainty (Lindley, 1956) or information theoretic metric to maximize the utility of an experi-
042   ment (Tigas et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). Recently, leveraging gradient signals for intervention
043
      targeting has gained significant success (Olko et al., 2023), as it naturally fits into various gradient-
      based causal discovery methods (Lippe et al., 2022b). Despite some success, both uncertainty-based
044
      and gradient-based approaches may still suffer from suboptimality, as the estimation of the signals is
045
      usually noisy (Olko et al., 2023) and can easily mislead the intervention targeting.
046
047   The emergence of large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2022; Ouyang
048   et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Bubeck et al., 2023), provides an opportunity to
049
      incorporate world knowledge about experimental design into the intervention targeting process. It
      therefore raises an intriguing research question:
050
051              Can we incorporate the knowledge of LLMs to assist with intervention targeting?
052
053   In fact, early explorations with LLMs in multiple causal learning and reasoning tasks show that LLMs
      may have already captured a large amount of domain knowledge (Kiciman et al., 2023; Lampinen

105   Intervention/Experiment Design Scientific progress in causal discovery is often driven by inter-
106   ventional experiments inspired by observational insights (Hanson, 1958; Kuhn & Hawkins, 1963).
107   Traditional methods focused on designing effective experiments to establish causal links, while statis-
      tical approaches aimed to automate causal inference from observational data (Pearl & Mackenzie,


                                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2018; Spirtes et al., 2000; 2010; Vowels et al., 2022). However, observational data alone is insufficient
109   for identifying causal structures, and interventional data is costly to collect (Spirtes et al., 2000). To
110   address these challenges, several methods for optimal intervention design have been developed.
111
112
      Active Intervention Targeting (AIT) selects intervention targets using an F -test inspired criterion,
      evaluating discrepancies in interventional sample distributions from a posterior distribution of graphs
113
      (Scherrer et al., 2021). Causal Bayesian Experimental Design (CBED) uses Bayesian Optimal
114
      Experimental Design to select interventions that maximize mutual information (MI) between new data
115   and existing graph beliefs, with MI estimated via a BALD-like method (Tigas et al., 2022; Houlsby
116   et al., 2011). Gradient-based Intervention Targeting (GIT) Olko et al., 2023 leverages gradient
117   information to determine interventions that maximize impact on causal parameter updates, which
118   is particularly advantageous in low-data settings. Causal Active Learning for Optimal Intervention
119   Design Zhang et al., 2023b takes an active learning approach, using Bayesian updates to iteratively
120   choose interventions that most effectively reduce uncertainty in achieving a target outcome. In our
121   work, we explore leveraging these advanced intervention strategies within the framework of LLMs to
122   determine whether LLMs can effectively engage in experimental design for causal discovery, pushing
123
      the boundaries of what automated, data-driven causal inference can achieve.
124
125   Causal Discovery With LLMs Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) like
126   ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2022) have opened new opportunities in causal inference by incorporating domain
127   knowledge, common sense, and contextual reasoning into the causal discovery process (Kiciman
128   et al., 2023). LLMs have demonstrated capabilities across Pearl’s ladder of causation—association,
129   intervention, and counterfactuals—bridging gaps that traditional models have with high-level causal
130   reasoning. They have shown promising results in pairwise causal discovery tasks by utilizing semantic
131
      information not accessible through numerical data alone (Kiciman et al., 2023).
132   Despite these advances, challenges remain. LLMs can sometimes behave like “causal parrots”,
133   repeating learned associations without demonstrating true causal reasoning (Zečević et al., 2023).
134   Moreover, their performance varies significantly depending on task complexity, with limited success
135   in advanced causal reasoning such as full graph discovery and counterfactual analysis (Zhang et al.,
136   2023a; Jin et al., 2023b;a; Long et al., 2023c). Another promising line of work integrates LLMs with
137
      traditional causal discovery methods to leverage their complementary strengths (Long et al., 2023a;
      Abdulaal et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). This hybrid approach has shown improved performance in
138
      constructing causal graphs, benefiting from LLMs’ understanding of language context and traditional
139
      methods’ data-driven precision.
140
141   While these studies highlight the use of LLMs in causal analysis, the question of whether LLMs
142   can effectively contribute to experimental design in causal discovery remains largely unexplored.
143
      Designing experiments involves proposing interventions, predicting outcomes, and evaluating experi-
      mental strategies—tasks that require more than mere causal inference. This paper aims to fill this gap
144
      by exploring the potential of LLMs to assist in experimental design, evaluating their strengths and
145
      limitations in guiding causal experiments.

      3.2   O NLINE C AUSAL D ISCOVERY
184
185
      To identify the underlying ground truth DAG from the MEC, interventional data is widely incorporated
186
      into the causal discovery process (Tong & Koller, 2001; Hauser & Bühlmann, 2011; Ke et al., 2019).
187
      Hence, online causal discovery is proposed to overcome the issue (Ke et al., 2019; Olko et al., 2023).
188
189   As given in Algorithm 1, an online causal discovery procedure is built upon a causal discovery
190   algorithm A that is able to leverage both the observational data and interventional data to recover
191
      the underlying causal structure. More formally, the interventional data is usually obtained through
      single-node intervention on some causal variable Xi . The intervention will replace the generation
192
193
      process of Xi with a new distribution, for which we denote as Pb(Xi |P Ai ) (Pearl & Mackenzie,
      2018). Then, it yields an interventional distribution:

201   The online discovery will proceed by T rounds. At the beginning of the first round, an initial graph
202   model ϕ0 is fitted based on the observational data. Then, in the follow-up T rounds, an intervention
203   target I will be selected using some intervention targeting method. For each selected I, a batch of
204   samples will be obtained and be integrated into all interventional data to execute the causal discovery
205   algorithm A. After T rounds, the fitted DAG will be the final output.
206   Previous approaches may use different intervention targeting methods. For example, Scherrer et al.
207   (2021) propose Active Intervention Targeting (AIT) to select the desired intervention targets based
208   on the F -test. Tigas et al. (2022) approximate the posterior distribution over all possible DAGs and
209   leverage Bayesian Optimal Experimental Design to select the most informative intervention targets.
210   Different from the Bayesian approaches, Olko et al. (2023) propose Gradient-based Intervention
211   Targeting (GIT), which leverages the gradient signals from the gradient-based causal discovery
212   methods to estimate the utility of each intervention target via hallucinated gradients (Ash et al.,
213   2020). Due to the natural combination of the gradient-based causal discovery methods and the
214   GIT method, GIT achieves significant performance improvements over previous Bayesian-based
215   approaches. Therefore, in this work, our follow-up discussion will center on the gradient-based
      approaches, i.e., the GIT method and the ENCO causal discovery methods(Lippe et al., 2022b).

306   Warmup Stage Since at the very beginning of the online causal discovery, numerical-based
307   estimations are noisy and easily mislead the online causal discovery, we begin by prompting LLMs to
308
      relate the pre-trained knowledge, analyze the variable description, and suggest influential candidates.
      The prompt template is given in Fig. 3. The prompting will give the beginning list of intervention
309
      targets Dwarmup . From Dwarmup , we will select Twarmup variables to obtain a basic map of the
310
      underlying causal system.
311
312
313   Bootstrapped Warmup Stage Although the first warmup stage yields a basic structure of the
314
      underlying causal system, due to the intrinsic limitations of LLMs such as limited context length (Liu
      et al., 2023) and hallucination (Zhang et al., 2023c), LLMs may only focus on a subset of the variables
315
      and find the influential nodes therein. Nevertheless, when the number of causal variables is large,
316
      LLMs tend to give an incomplete set of influential nodes. Therefore, we further incorporate a second
317   warmup stage, to bootstrap the use of LLM’s world knowledge in early intervention targeting.
318
319   More concretely, we leverage the intermediate causal discovery results ϕTwarmup after the first
320
      Twarmup rounds, and examine the left variables that have not been involved in ϕTwarmup . Then,
      we further prompt LLMs to give more focus on the left set of variables and to find the influential
321
      variables that were missing in previous rounds.
322
323   In addition, since we have already obtained relatively high-quality intermediate causal discovery
      results, we can also incorporate the numerical-based methods to suggest a set of promising candidates

325   warmup rounds, we still encourage LLMs to determine the finalist.

328   clearer yet complicated causal graphs. Even for humans, it is hard to determine the best experimental

332   4.3   T HEORETICAL D ISCUSSION
333
334   After setting up the LeGIT algorithm, we now briefly discuss the convergence of LeGIT. Since
335   LeGIT ends up with a numerical-based methods to conclude the online causal discovery, intuitively,
336   given any numerical-based methods, such as GIT (Olko et al., 2023), and a useful online causal
337
      discovery algorithm, such as ENCO (Lippe et al., 2022b), LeGIT can converge. Nevertheless, due to
      a better warmup strategy in LeGIT, empirically, we find that LeGIT can converge to a better solution
338
      even when compared to the same numerical-based methods without LLMs involved.

      4.4   P RACTICAL D ISCUSSION
342   Following the practice in the literature, we mainly adopt GIT as the numerical-based method M, and
343   ENCO as the gradient-based causal discovery method. Nevertheless, as also suggested in GIT (Olko
344   et al., 2023), ENCO can also be switched to other gradient-based methods. In addition, LeGIT is also
345   compatible with other numerical-based approaches.

527   6    C ONCLUSIONS
528
529   In this work, we investigated how to incorporate LLMs into the intervention targeting in experimental
530   design for causal discovery. We introduced a novel framework called LeGIT, that combines the best
531
      of previous numerical-based approaches and the rich world knowledge in LLMs. Specifically, LeGIT
      leverages LLMs to warm up the online causal discovery procedure by identifying the influential root
532
      cause variables to begin the intervention. After setting up a relatively clear picture of the underlying
533
      causal graph, LeGIT then integrates the numerical-based methods to continue to select the intervention
534   targets. Empirically, we verified the effectiveness of LeGIT that leveraging LLMs to warm up the
535   online causal discovery can achieve the state-of-the-art performance across 4 different scale of
536   realistic causal discovery benchmarks. Future studies can be established by a further investigation of
537   various approaches to integrate world knowledge in LLMs for causal discovery.

1309      5. **<bronc>**: Bronchitis, especially chronic bronchitis, can be caused by smoking
