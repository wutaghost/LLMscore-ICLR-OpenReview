009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, it was proved that group equivariance emerges in ensembles of neural
012            networks as the result of full augmentation in the limit of infinitely wide neural
013            networks (neural tangent kernel limit). In this paper, we extend this result signif-
014            icantly. We provide a proof that this emergence does not depend on the neural
015            tangent kernel limit at all. We also consider stochastic settings, and furthermore
016            general architectures. For the latter, we provide a simple sufficient condition on
017            the relation between the architecture and the action of the group for our results to
018
               hold. We validate our findings through simple numeric experiments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
021
022
      Consider a learning task with an inherent symmetry. As an illustrative example, we can think of
023
      classifying images of, say, apples and pears. The classification should of course not change when
024   the image is rotated, meaning that the learning task is invariant to rotations. If we are segmenting
025   the image, the apple segmentation mask should instead rotate with the image – such tasks are called
026   equivariant. An a-priori known symmetry like this is a strong inductive bias, that should be used to
027   improve performance of the model.
028
      There are essentially two ’meta-approaches’ to do the latter. The first one is to use data augmentation
029
      to generate new data that respects the symmetry – in our example, to add all rotated versions of
030   the training images to the dataset. This is versatile, simple and effective. However, there are of
031   course no guarantees that the model after training exactly obeys the symmetry, in particular on
032   out-of distribution data. To solve this problem, one can use the second meta-approach, namely
033   to use a model that inherently respects the symmetry. Over the last half decade or so, an entire
034   framework concerning achieving this for neural network has emerged under the name of Geometric
035   Deep Learning (Bronstein et al., 2021).
036   The relations between these two approaches are, from a theoretical point of view, still unclear: there
037   is yet to emerge a definitive answer to the question if and when the augmentation strategy is guar-
038   anteed to yield an equivariant model after training. Recently, an interesting discovery in this regard
039   was made in Gerken & Kessel (2024). In this paper, it was shown that ensembles of infinitely wide
040   neural networks (in the neural tangent kernel limit Jacot et al. (2018)) are equivariant in mean, even
041   though individual members of them are not. See Figure 1 for an illustration. Although this asymp-
042   totic result is already interesting, it has some drawbacks. The proofs rely heavily on the simplified
043   dynamics of the NTKs, and therefore do not work at all for network of finite width. Furthermore,
044
      the proof only works for finite groups, and is only formulated for the somewhat unrealistic gradient
      flow training.
045
046   The main message of this paper is that the phenomenon of ensembles of neural networks being
047   equivariant in mean is much more general than Gerken & Kessel (2024) suggests. Our analysis
048   reveals that the equivariance has nothing to do with the neural tangent kernel limit. Instead, if the
049   geometry of the neural network architecture in a sense is not inherently biased against equivariance
050
      (the technical condition is described in Definition 1), and the parameters of the ensemble are initial-
      ized in a symmetric way (which can be achieved by Gaussian initialization), the ensemble mean will
051
      automatically be equivariant at all points in the training.
052
053   Our proofs are non-asymptotic, work for any compact group, for stochastic gradient descent and
      random augmentation, and apply to a wide range of neural network architectures. Consequently, the

079   2   L ITERATURE REVIEW
080
081
082   To give a comprehensive overview of Geometric Deep Learning goes well beyond the scope of this
083   paper. We instead refer to the textbook (Bronstein et al., 2021).
084   The question of the effect of symmetries in the data on the training of neural networks has often
085   been understood as a comparison question: Is it better to train a non-equivariant architecture on
086   augmented (that is, artificially symmetric) data, or to use a manifestly equivariant architecture as
087   e.g. Cohen et al. (2019); Maron et al. (2019); Kondor & Trivedi (2018); Weiler & Cesa (2019);
088   Finzi et al. (2021); Fuchs et al. (2020)? Empirical comparisons between the two approaches as
089   part of experimental evaluations of manifestly equivariant architectures have been made more or
090   less since the invention of equivariant networks – more systematic investigations include Gandikota
091   et al. (2021); Gerken et al. (2022).
092   In Chen et al. (2020), a group theoretical framework for data augmentation was developed, which
093   has inspired many treatises of the question, including ours. Linear (i.e. kernel) models are studied in
094   Elesedy & Zaidi (2021); Mei et al. (2021); Dao et al. (2019). Lyle et al. (2019; 2020) study so-called
095   feature-averaged networks. So-called linear neural networks – i.e., neural networks with a linear
096   activation function – are studied in Chen & Zhu (2024). In these settings, equivalence between
097   augmenting the data and restricting the networks can be proven. The same has not been established
098
      for ’bona-fide’ neural networks with non-linear activation functions – they are studied Nordenfors
      et al. (2024), but there, only local guarantees are derived.
099
100   The work most tightly related to ours is Gerken & Kessel (2024). In there, it was realized that equiv-
101   ariance emerges from augmentation in ensembles, and not in individual networks. They provide
102   a formal explaination of this only in the so-called neural tangent kernel (NTK) limit (Jacot et al.,
103   2018). Importantly, the optimization dynamics of neural networks in the NTK-limit turn linear (Lee
104
      et al., 2019), so that that setting is considerably simpler than the one we consider here.
105   Equivariant flows have been studied before in the context of generative models (Köhler et al., 2020;
106   Katsman et al., 2021; Satorras et al., 2021). In this paper, we use results from Köhler et al. (2020)
107   in a different context, namely to study the flow of the parameters of the network under (stochastic)
      gradient flow (descent).

173   architecture. It also has a technical consequence that we will need: it lets us write the affine space
174   L = AL + TL, for an AL ∈ HG ∩ L, and TL the direction of L.

228   We define ensembles as in Gerken & Kessel (2024): For a distribution p on H, we consider the
229   distribution of parameters At implied by initalizing the parameters of a network according to p, and

234   function can be well approximated by drawing a sample mean, i.e. starting N neural networks
235   with i.i.d. initialisation (drawn according to p), training them, and then calculating the mean after t
236   points. This is the strategy we will follow in the experimental section. We will however only make
237   claims about the true mean, and leave the study of the effect of the approximation error to further

243   This section is devoted to proving the G-equivariance of ensembles (Theorem 4.2). Our result will
244   hold under a geometric condition on the space L. Let us present it and discuss it in some detail.
245
246   4.1   T HE G- INVARIANCE CONDITION
247
248
      Definition 1. We say that L is G-invariant if ρ(g)L ⊆ L for all g ∈ G.
249   It should be noted that the invariance of L depends on the representation ρ, and not just on G.
250   However, we find this terminology more intuitive, and we will make sure to point out the dependence
251   when it matters.
252
      From the view of the neural network architectures, G-invariance can be interpreted as saying that
253
      there is no inherent bias against being G-equivariant in the definition of your admissible layers. It
254
      has the for us important consequence, which we prove in Appendix A.
255
      Lemma 4.1. L is G-invariant if and only if ΠL is G-equivariant.
256
257   G-invariance is related to the so-called compatibility condition that was used in Nordenfors et al.
258   (2024) to prove statements about the stationary points of the augmented risk Raug : that the projec-
259   tion ΠL commutes with the orthogonal projection ΠG . In fact, the G-invariance was there argued
260   to be sufficient for the compatibility condition to hold. It was furthermore argued that G-invariance
261   is satisfied for essentially all commonly used architectures and reasonable representations, such as
262   fully connected ones with and without bias, RNNs, residual connections, etc. Let us here, again,
263   only discuss our running examples.
264   Example 4.1. In Example 3.1, it was argued that when using the canonical ρrot -representations on
265   all the intermediate layers, we have ρ(g)⟨ψ⟩ = ⟨ρ(g)ψ⟩. Consequently, a ⟨ψ⟩ ∈ Lsym will be
266
      mapped by ρ onto a tuple of other symmetric filters, and hence Lsym is G-invariant. By the same
      argument, Las is not.
267
268   As we previously remarked (Remark 1), there is no need to choose the ρrot -representations in the
269   intermediate layers. However, it turns out that there aren’t any representations on the intermediate
      spaces which makes Las G-invariant. The somewhat involved proof for this is given in Appendix C.

271
272   Let us now state our main result.
273   Theorem 4.2. If (i) the parameters have a G-invariant distribution at initialization, ρ(g)A0 ∼ A0
274   (ii) the affine space L of the architecture is G-invariant, then the ensemble model trained with either
275   gradient flow with full augmentation or SGD with random augmentation will for all times t be G-
276   equivariant.
277   Remark 2. It is not hard fulfill condition (i) if L is G-invariant – simply choose the c-parameter as
278   a standard Gaussian vector. Then, A = AL + Lc will also be a Gaussian vector on the space L,
279   invariant to all the unitary transformations ρ(g). A detailed argument is given in Appendix A.1.
280   Remark 3. As we remarked in Remark 1, the representations on the intermediate spaces Xi are
281   free to choose. One should hence really interpret condition (ii) in Theorem 4.2 as that there exist
282   intermediate representations so that L is G-invariant with respect to the corresponding ρ.

284
      case in fact is essentially the same, but needs a little more tools from probability theory, whence we

363   We use two metrics to evaluate the invariance of the trained models. First, similarly as in
364   Gerken & Kessel (2024), we calculate the average orbit same prediction (OSP). That is, for
365   each test image, we test how many of the transformed versions of it are given the same pre-
366   diction as the untransformed one by the ensemble model. OSP lies between 1 and |G|, where
367
      a value equal to the order of the group |G|. indicates perfect invariance. The second metric we cal-
      culate is the average symmetric KL-divergence DKL between the class probabilities predicted on an
368
      image with each of the transformed versions of it. Note that the class probabilities can vary quite
369
      a lot but still yield the same prediction, whence this is a finer measure of invariance than the OSP.
370   Both metrics are evaluated both on the MNIST test set, as well the (out-of-distribution) CIFAR-10
371   (Krizhevsky, 2009) test set (reshaped and converted to grayscale).

508   6   C ONCLUSION
509
510   The goal of this paper was to extend the results of Gerken & Kessel (2024), regarding G-equivariance
511   of ensembles of infinitely wide neural networks trained with gradient descent under full augmenta-
512   tion, to ensembles of finite–width neural networks trained with SGD and random augmentation.
513   We succeeded in our goal with Theorem 4.2, where we proved that, modulo an oft–satisfied G-
514   invariance condition on the network architecture, ensembles become G-equivariant when training
515   with SGD and random augmentation, assuming that the parameters of the network were distributed
516   G-invariantly to begin with (e.g., with Gaussian initialization). That is, we have shown that if
517   the goal is group equivariant model, it suffices to train a neural network ensemble model under
518   data augmentation. Our numerical experiments, while not conclusive, support these conclusions for
519
      sample mean ensembles as well.
520
521   Limitations Theorem 4.2 gives a guarantee of G-equivariance for ’infinitely large’ ensembles. In
522   practice the expected value in Equation 3 would need to be estimated with a finite ensemble mean.
523   Furthermore, the G-invariance condition under which the result holds is only a sufficient condition.
524   The same is true of the assumption that the initial distribution of parameters is G-invariant.
525
526   Future Work Interesting directions for future work include bounding the error when using sample
527   mean ensembles, as well as investigating the necessity of the G-invariance condition. In addition
528   to this, it is an interesting question whether or not the initial distribution of parameters needs to be
529   G-invariant or if the ensemble will still be ’pulled into’ a G-equivariant state by the augmentation
530
      process.

      A.2   A RESULT ABOUT THE GRADIENTS OF RANDOM FUNCTIONS
721   In preparation of our proof of the stochastical version of the main result, let us prove the following
722   very intuitive lemma about random functions: If two random functions, that almost surely are dif-
723   ferentiable, have the property that their pointwise distributions are equal, the same is true for their
724   gradients. For the proof, we will invoke the Cramér-Wold Theorem (Cramér & Wold, 1936).
725   Theorem A.4 (Cramér-Wold). A probability distribution p on Rd is uniquely determined by the set
726   of distributions p ◦ πv−1 on R, indexed by the unit vector v, where πv : Rd → R, x 7→ ⟨x, v⟩.

955   The architecture used for all three networks is the same with the exception of the support of the 3 × 3
956   filters. Namely, in the first layer we use a convolution with zero padding, with 1 channel in and 16
957   out, followed by an average pooling with a 2 × 2 window with a stride of 2, followed by a tanh
958   activation, followed by a layer normalization. The second layer is the same except the convolution
959   has 16 channels in instead of 1. The third layer is the same as the second, but without the average
960   pooling. The final layer flattens the image channels into a vector, followed by a linear transformation
961   into R10 .

963   the equivariance/invariance of the network in that case. Due to interpolation effects, it is not equiv-
964   ariant to C16 – adding to the assumptions of our theorem that are violated in that case.

      A prediction of the network is then the argmax of the output, which yields the predicted label as a
