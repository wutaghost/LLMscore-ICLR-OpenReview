009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models (DMs) have demonstrated powerful distributional modeling ca-
012            pabilities by matching the first-order score of diffused distributions. Recent ad-
013            vancements have explored incorporating the second-order Fisher information, de-
014            fined as the negative Hessian of log-density, into various downstream tasks and
015            theoretical analysis of DMs. However, current practices often overlook the inher-
016            ent structure of diffused distributions, accessing Fisher information via applying
017            auto-differentiation to the learned score network. This approach, while straight-
018
               forward, leaves theoretical properties unexplored and is time-consuming. In this
               paper, we derive the analytical formulation of Fisher information (AFI) by apply-
019
               ing consecutive differentials to the diffused distributions. As a result, AFI takes a
020
               gradient-free form of a weighted sum (or integral) of outer-products of the score
021            and initial data. Based on this formulation, we propose two algorithmic variants
022            of AFI for distinct scenarios. When evaluating the AFI’s trace, we introduce a
023            parameterized network to learn the trace. When AFI is applied as a linear opera-
024            tor, we present a training-free method that simplifies it into several inner-product
025            calculations. Furthermore, we provide theoretical guarantees for both algorithms
026            regarding convergence analysis and approximation error bounds. Additionally, we
027            leverage AFI to establish the first general theorem for the optimal transport prop-
028            erty of the diffusion ODE deduced map. Experiments in likelihood evaluation and
029
               adjoint optimization demonstrate the superior accuracy and reduced time-cost of
               the proposed algorithms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The emerging diffusion models (DMs) Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho et al. (2020); Song & Ermon
035
      (2019); Song et al. (2020), generating samples of data distribution from initial noise by learning a
036   reverse diffusion process, have been proven to be an effective technique for modeling data distribu-
037   tion, especially in generating high-quality images Nichol et al. (2022); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021a);
038   Saharia et al. (2022); Ramesh et al. (2022); Rombach et al. (2022); Ho et al. (2022). The training pro-
039   cess of DMs can be seen as employing a neural network to match the first-order score ∇x log qt (x)
040   of the diffused distributions at varying noise levels.
041
      Recently, there has been a growing trend to recognize the importance of the Fisher information in
042   DMs, defined as the negative Hessian of the diffused distributions’ log-density, −∇2x log qt (x). The
043   Fisher information provides valuable second-order information of DMs and plays a crucial role in
044   likelihood evaluation (Lu et al., 2022a; Zheng et al., 2023), adjoint optimization (Pan et al., 2023a;b;
045   Blasingame & Liu, 2024), and optimal transport analysis Zhang et al. (2024a).
046
      However, current practices (Sanchez et al., 2022; Song & Lai, 2024) typically overlook the inherent
047
      structure of diffused distributions, and access the Fisher information by applying auto-differentiation
048   to the score network. While this is a straightforward approach, it leads to time-consuming gradient
049   operations, even with the help of the Jacobian-vector-product (JVP) technique. Moreover, in the
050   likelihood evaluation task, the current JVP method still needs a quadratic order time complexity
051   concerning the dimension to calculate the trace of the Fisher information, rendering likelihood eval-
052   uation intractable for SD-level models. Additionally, due to a lack of comprehensive understanding
053   of Fisher Information, Zhang et al. (2024a) have to impose stringent assumptions to characterize the
      optimal transport property of the diffusion ODE deduced map.

054
      In this paper, we delve deeper into the inherent quadratic structure of diffused distributions and
055   derive the analytical form of Fisher information (AFI) by applying the consecutive partial differ-
056   ential chain rule to the marginal distributions. Notice that, while this inherent structure has been
057   consistently utilized in the learning process of the score network, it has often been overlooked when
058   accessing Fisher Information up until now. The main contributions of our paper are listed as follows:
059
060          • We develop the first analytical formulation of the Fisher Information (AFI) of diffused
061
               distributions, which is gradient-free. Initially, we show that the AFI manifests as a weighted
               summation of outer-products of the score and initial data when the initial distribution is a
062
               sum of Dirac. We then extend this result to an integral form in a more general setting.
063
               The AFI suggests a theoretical possibility of accessing Fisher information without needing
064            costly gradient calculations in practice.
065
             • Based on the AFI, we propose two algorithmic alternatives to the JVP, each tailored to dif-
066
               ferent types of Fisher Information access. For the evaluation of Fisher information’s trace,
067            we introduce a parameterized network to learn the trace, significantly reducing the time
068            complexity of trace evaluation from quadratic to linear w.r.t. the dimension. In scenarios
069            where Fisher Information is applied as a linear operator, we present a training-free method
070            that simplifies the complex linear transformation calculations into several simple inner-
071            product calculations. Furthermore, we provide theoretical guarantees for these algorithms,
072            including convergence analysis and approximation error bounds.
073          • Utilizing the analytical knowledge of the Fisher information, we establish the first theorem
074            that allows the general diffusion ODE deduced mapping to possess the optimal transport
075            property, eliminating the need for stringent assumptions.
076
      We evaluate our AFI algorithms on likelihood evaluation and adjoint optimization tasks. The empir-
077
      ical results demonstrate the enhanced accuracy and reduced time-cost of our AFI methods.

361   Experiments We trained two AFI-TM networks for the SD-1.5 and SD-2base pipeline on the
362   Laion2B-en dataset (Schuhmann et al., 2022), which contains 2.32 billion text-image pairs. Our
363   tθ follows the U-net structure, similar to stable diffusion models, but with an added MLP head to
364   produce a scalar-valued output. We utilize the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with
365   a learning rate of 1e-4. The training is executed across 8 Ascend 910B chips with a batch size of
366   384 and completes after 150K steps. In Figure 1, we demonstrate that the training loss of AFI-TM
367   nets converges smoothly, indicating the robustness of the AFI-TM training scheme in Algorithm 1.
368   In Figure 2, we evaluate the average NLL and Clip score of samples generated by various SD models,
369   using 10k randomly selected prompts from the COCO dataset Lin et al. (2014). A lower NLL
370   suggests more realistic data generation, while a higher Clip score indicates a better match between
371   the generated images and the input prompts. The results imply that the NLL and the Clip score form
372   a trade-off curve across different guidance scales. This phenomenon, previously hypothesized in
373   theory (Wu et al., 2024), is now confirmed in the SD models, thanks to the effective NLL evaluation
374
      via the AFI-TM method.

499   There is an increasing trend towards analyzing the probability modeling capabilities of DMs by
500
      interpreting them from an optimal transport perspective (Albergo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024).
      The foundational concepts of optimal transport can be found in Appendix A.9. One of the central
501
      questions is whether the map deduced by the PF-ODE could represent an optimal transport. If so,
502
      how should we design the noise schedule, or what conditions should the data distribution meet? For
503   a specific noise schedule where f (t) ≡ 0, Zhang et al. (2024a) demonstrates that having all data
504   points lying on a single line is a sufficient condition for the map to represent an optimal transport.

531   7    C ONCLUSIONS
532
      This paper introduced the Analytical Fisher Information (AFI), an analytical formulation that al-
533
      lows for more efficient and theoretical exploration of Fisher Information of diffused distribution.
534   Practically, we have proposed two algorithmic variants of AFI for different scenarios: AFI trace
535   matching (AFI-TM) and AFI endpoint approximation (AFI-EA). Both methods are gradient-free,
536   theoretically guaranteed for approximation error bounds and convergence properties, and offer im-
537   proved accuracy and reduced time-cost compared to the traditional JVP method. Theoretically, we
538   have established the first general theorem for the PF-ODE map to be optimal transport. This work
539   not only improves the efficiency of Fisher Information evaluation but also widens our understanding
      of the diffused distributions. Please refer to further discussions in appendix C.

1136   The optimal transport is the general problem of moving one distribution of mass to another as
1137   efficiently as possible. The optimal transport problem can be formulated in two primary ways,
1138   namely the Monge formulation (Monge, 1781) and the Kantorovich formulation (Kantorovich,
1139   1960). Suppose there are two probability measures µ and ν on (Rn , B), and a cost function
1140   c : Rn × Rn → [0, +∞]. The Monge problem is

1147   as x, to a single point T(x). In contrast, Kantorovich provided a more general formulation, referred

1360   C.4   T HE RELATION OF AFI TO C OVARIANCE IN DM S
1361
1362   We note that there is a series of studies aiming to learn the covariance of the reverse diffusion
1363   Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) (Bao et al., 2022b;a). In Bayesian statistics, Fisher informa-
1364
       tion is defined as the covariance of the score. These studies derive their formulation by analyzing the
       covariance of the score and obtaining the Fisher information in terms of the score. However, our AFI
1365
       is derived directly from the marginal distribution and is composed solely of the initial distribution
1366
       and noise schedule. Furthermore, our application is unique; we are the first to replace the use of
1367   JVP with AFI, while the focus of these studies is to enhance the performance of Diffusion Models
1368   (DMs) with analytical covariance.
1369
1370
       A very similar form of the Fisher information is proposed in Lemma 5 of Benton et al. (2024). The
       difference is that our Proposition 3 presents the specific form of Fisher information in terms of data
1371
       distribution, which is not included in Lemma 5 of Benton et al. (2024). This distinction is crucial
1372
       as it facilitates the derivation of our new algorithms. Also, we adopt the currently more commonly
1373   used αt , σt notation to represent the noise schedule. Instead, Benton et al. (2024) αt ≡ e−2t . We
1374   gave out this detailed formulation to avoid unnecessary misunderstandings in the development of
1375   the subsequent training scheme.

1378   C.5   C OMPARISON OF AFI-TM TO H UTCHINSON TRACE ESTIMATOR
1379
1380   We notice that the naive trace calculation of the JVP method can be accelerated by the Hutchinson
1381   trace estimator. We have not conducted experiments using the Hutchinson estimator, as it is a Monte-
1382   Carlo type estimation, unlike the direct evaluation methods like the full-JVP baseline and our AFI-
1383   TM. Furthermore, the JVP method under the help of the Hutchinson method and its variants is still
1384
       considerably more expensive than our method, and their applicability is also more restricted.
1385
1386          • The Hutchinson method is much more costly: To attain a relative error less than ϵ with a
                                                                     2(1− 38 ϵ) log δ1
1387            probability of 1 − δ, the Hutchinson method requires        ϵ2         samples Skorski (2021).
1388            Assuming that the goal is to obtain an estimation with a relative error of less than 10%
1389            with a probability exceeding 90%, a minimum of 351 NFEs is required. This equates to 1
1390            or 2 minutes on SD-v1.5 for a single trace estimation. For a complete NLL estimation of
1391
                an image with 20 steps, this would take 20 minutes, which is entirely impractical in any
                business context. In contrast, our AFI-TM only requires 1 NFE for a single trace estimation,
1392
                needing merely 10 seconds for the full NLL estimation of an image.
1393
1394
              • The application of the Hutchinson method is more restricted: Due to its Monte-Carlo
1395            characteristics, the Hutchinson method is more appropriate for contributing to the com-
1396            putation of certain training objectives as in Lu et al. (2022a), where the unbiased prop-
1397            erty is sufficient, and large variance may be absorbed into network training. However, the
1398            Hutchinson method may encounter difficulties with accurate per-sample trace estimation.
1399            Our method can accommodate both scenarios, including per-sample computation.
1400
1401   There are already attempts to use the Hutchinson method to expedite the JVP of trace estimation in
1402   diffusion models Lu et al. (2022a). Nonetheless, due to Hutchinson’s limitations, these practices are
1403   restricted to relatively small DMs (CIFAR-10 at most). We will add discussions on the Hutchinson
       method in our revision.
