011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013            A regime modelling framework can be employed to address the complexities of
014            financial markets. Under the framework, market periods are grouped into dis-
015            tinct regimes, each distinguished by similar statistical characteristics. Regimes in
016            financial markets are not directly observable but are often manifested in market
017            and macroeconomic variables. The objective of regime modelling is to accurately
018            identify the active regime from these variables at a point in time, a process known
019
               as regime identification. One way to enhance the accuracy of regime identification
               is to select features that are most responsible for statistical differences between
020
               regimes, a process known as feature selection. Models based on the Jump Model
021
               framework have recently been developed to address the joint problem of regime
022            identification and feature selection. In the following work, we propose a new set
023            of models called Regularised Jump Models that are founded upon the Jump Model
024            framework. These models perform feature selection that is more interpretable than
025            that from the Sparse Jump Model, a model proposed in the literature pertaining to
026            the Jump Model framework. Through a simulation experiment, we find evidence
027            that these new models outperform the Standard and Sparse Jump Models, both in
028            terms of regime identification and feature selection.

      4     S IMULATION S TUDY
396
      We conduct a simulation study to compare the accuracy of the Standard, Sparse and Regularised
397
      Jump Models, with respect to both state estimation and feature selection. In the simulation study,
398
      the true state sequence and array of relevant features are both known, which makes it possible to
399   evaluate the ability of each model to correctly identify the state sequence and set of relevant features.
400
401   We adopt the same data generating process as in (9). In their simulation study, the authors test
402
      the Standard and Sparse Jump Models against several popular regime-switching models such as
      the Hidden Markov Model (HMM) and its various extensions, and determine that these two Jump
403
      Models deliver superior performance.
404
405   Instead of duplicating the simulation study by testing the same regime-switching models, we test the
406   proposed Regularised Jump Models and compare their performances with the Standard and Sparse
407   Jump Models.

      Comparing Standard and the remaining models in Table 2, it is evident that feature selection im-
      proves accuracy of state estimation when the number of features P is increased. In Table 2, the BAC

      of the Standard Jump Model, a model that does not perform feature selection, generally decreases

      when P is increased. On the other hand, the BAC of the Sparse and Regularised Jump Models stay

      vance of the first fifteen features becomes more apparent and hence easier for the model to identify.
      This logic is reinforced by the results in Table 3; the feature selection performance of the models

      improves for higher values of µ.

      5   C ONCLUSION
453
454
      In the new class of models called Regularised Jump Models, the regularised K-means model pro-
455   posed in (11) has been adapted to the Jump Model Framework. These models perform joint state
456   and parameter estimation, and feature selection. The feature selection process performed by the
457   Regularised Jump Models is more direct and interpretable than that from the Sparse Jump Model,
458   which performs feature selection by proxy. These models were tested in a simulation experiment
459   and demonstrate evidence of outperformance over existing Jump Models.
460
      One avenue of future research is adapting the asymptotic properties of the Regularised K-Means
461   model proven in (11) to the Regularised Jump Models. Some of these properties include consistency
462   and strong consistency in terms of the Hausdorff distance (see (11) Theorem 6 and the proof in
463   Appendix A. A.3).
464
      We’ve proposed a new hyperparameter tuning method for Jump Models, based on the idea of cluster-
465
      ing stability. There is scope for future research on developing and testing alternative hyperparameter
466
      tuning methods for Jump Models.
