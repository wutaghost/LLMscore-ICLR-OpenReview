009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, LLMs such as the Llama and Qwen families have rapidly improved by
012            significantly scaling their training corpora, with smaller models trained on larger
013            datasets now approaching or surpassing the performance of previous-generation
014            larger models on public benchmarks. In this paper, we revisit the scaling effects
015            of LLMs, using the medical field as a case study, by carefully analyzing how
016
               training corpus size and parameter size affect model performance on problems
               of varying difficulty. To this end, we present MedResEval, a new benchmark
017
               built upon the MedQA dataset. It is designed to demand more complex reasoning
018
               and decision-making and more accurately reflect real-world medical scenarios.
019            Leveraging MedResEval, we investigate the scaling effects of training corpus and
020            model size in LLMs through a comprehensive analysis of several prominent LLM
021            families on medical reasoning tasks of varying complexity. The results reveal
022            that while smaller models like Llama 3 (8B) approach the performance of older,
023            larger models like Llama 2 (70B) on simple tasks like MedQA, they consistently
024            underperform on complex tasks requiring advanced reasoning. Furthermore, we
025            develop a difficulty-dependent scaling-law formula to characterize how LLMs’
026            performance varies with training data size at a fixed model parameter size. The
027
               formula reveals that reasoning error reduction rates are 1.3 times greater for large-
               scale LLMs (≈ 70B) compared to small-scale LLMs (≤10B) on simple tasks, and
028
               2 times greater on difficult reasoning tasks. Our study highlights that while both
029
               data and parameter scales enhance LLM performance, greater emphasis must be
030            placed on parameter scales, particularly for complex reasoning tasks. Only LLMs
031            with sufficiently large parameters can effectively tackle the complexities of real-
032            world medical scenarios.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large language model (LLM) technology has witnessed rapid advancement (Ouyang et al., 2022;
038   Achiam et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a) and shown potential in various do-
039
      mains. Scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020) highlight the critical role of training data size and model
      parameters in enhancing large language models (LLMs). Guided by scaling laws, mainstream LLMs
040
      families have been advancing new iterations by continuously scaling their training corpora. For ex-
041
      ample, the Llama family has evolved from the first generation, Llama 1, to the third generation,
042   Llama 3, by expanding the training corpus size by about 10 times. Another well-known open-source
043   LLM family Qwen has done something similar. Recently, LLMs have achieved significant advance-
044   ments by significantly scaling their training corpora. The latest small-scale LLMs (Dubey et al.,
045   2024; Hui et al., 2024) trained on larger datasets are now approaching or even surpassing the perfor-
046   mance of previous large-size LLMs across various public benchmarks. For example, on the famous
047   MMLU benchmark (Hendrycks et al.), the latest Llama3 (8B) (Dubey et al., 2024) trained with 15T
048   tokens has significantly outperformed Llama1 (65B) (Touvron et al., 2023a) trained with 1.4T to-
049   kens. Therefore, a critical question arises that requires serious consideration: is it sufficient to solely
050
      scale up the training corpus to develop strong small-scale LLMs with capabilities of addressing
      various general or field-specific real-world application needs?
051
052   In this study, we revisit the scaling effects of both training corpus size and model parameters, exam-
053   ining how they influence the capabilities of LLMs. Since the medical field is a critical application
      area for LLMs, we use it as a case study to investigate the scaling effects. To ensure consistency

109   ters that need to be determined by fitting. The authors also pointed out that, when the computation

      LLM Medical Evaluation Existing evaluations of LLM medical capabilities typically utilize the
      medical QA datasets, where the questions are sourced from medical exams (Jin et al., 2021; Pal
120
      et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2024), medical literature (Jin et al., 2019), and consumer health questions
121
      (Ben Abacha et al., 2017; Singhal et al., 2023). Other evaluation benchmarks (Zhu et al., 2023) also
122   utilize traditional NLP tasks such as NER, relation extraction to evaluate the medical capabilities of
123   LLMs. Recent studies (Nori et al., 2023; Singhal et al., 2023) indicate that several LLMs perform
124   notably on these medical benchmarks. For example, GPT-4 achieves an accuracy of 90.2 on the
125   medical exam benchmark MedQA, approaching the experts’ performance on this benchmark. In this
126   study, we create reasoning-complex tasks by reformulating the MedQA benchmark to investigate the
127   scaling effects of model parameters and training data size on medical reasoning capabilities.

180
181          • Available clues: Current medical benchmark questions often provide extra clues within
182            the context, which can aid LLMs in predicting the correct answer with tricks. For example,
183            in multiple-choice questions (MCQs), there is only one correct answer among the options,
184            allowing LLMs to reach the correct answer through the process of elimination rather than

186
               no option provided), leading to more challenging reasoning.
187          • Decision space: The decision space of existing evaluation tasks is quite restricted. For
188            example, the decision space of MCQs contains limited options (typically 4-6 options). In
189            contrast, the decision spaces for real-world tasks are typically much larger. For instance, in
190            clinical diagnosis, there may be hundreds of potential candidate diseases, requiring more
191
               complex reasoning to reach the correct diagnosis within a broader decision space.
192          • Reasoning steps: Solving problems in existing benchmarks often requires a single step of
193            decision-making (e.g., choosing an option, making a judgement). In contrast, real-world
194            tasks typically involve a more extended decision-making process. For instance, when man-
195            aging a patient’s care, a doctor first establishes a diagnosis and then prescribes treatment
196
               based on the diagnosis made in the previous step.

198   3.3   M EDICAL R EASONING C APABILITIES E VALUATION B ENCHMARK
199
200   Studying the scaling effects of LLMs in the medical domain requires further evaluating them on tasks
201   with reasoning complexity approaching the level of real-world clinical scenarios. Although some
202   existing medical datasets involve real clinical tasks, variations in the underlying medical knowl-
203   edge across these benchmarks may introduce bias in performance comparisons across tasks. To
204
      address this, we developed a new benchmark, MedResEval, by reformulating the well-known med-
      ical benchmark MedQA (Jin et al., 2021), which contains multiple-choice questions sourced from
205
      United States Medical Licensing Examination (USMLE). Tasks in MedResEval are designed to re-
206
      quire more complex reasoning and decision-making capabilities related to the key factors introduced
207   above, leading to more accurate reflection of the reasoning complexity involved in real medical tasks.
208   The generation process of this benchmark and our evaluation process are illustrated in Figure 2.
209
210   Evaluation Tasks Given the key factors of reasoning complexity, we first devise three types of
211   reformulated tasks that focus on reducing available clues, expanding decision space, and increasing
212   reasoning steps, respectively:
213
214          • Reducing Available Clues: We consider reducing the extra available clues provided in the
215            MCQ options by combining the question part of each MCQ sample with one of its options,
               resulting in a statement verification task. To verify the statements, LLMs must rely solely

      Although the above tasks evaluate LLMs’ medical reasoning capabilities from three separate as-
245
      pects, real medical tasks often require integrating multiple reasoning skills simultaneously. More-
246   over, considering that real-world medical context may contain noise and contradictions, the ability
247   to identify and revise contradictory information is crucial for real-world clinical tasks, such as diag-
248   nosis. Therefore, we design a more complex diagnosis-simulation task to evaluate LLMs’ ability
249   to apply multiple reasoning skills simultaneously. Figure 3 presents an example of this task. Specif-
250   ically, we perturb the key diagnostic information (gender, examination results, symptoms) in the
251   original diagnosis-type MCQs. Next, we have three experienced doctors annotate whether a contra-
252   diction exists in the perturbed questions. For each perturbed question, LLMs should first determine
253   if a contradiction exists. If so, they must revise it by choosing the appropriate revision option before
254
      providing the final diagnosis; otherwise, they directly answer the original question. We further ex-
      pand the decision space of this task by adding more candidate diseases and randomly removing the
255
      correct diagnosis from the options, requiring LLMs to select “None of the above” in such cases.

      4       E XPERIMENT S ETUP
271
272   Datasets We primarily conduct experiments on both the original MedQA benchmark and the pro-
273   posed MedResEval benchmark to compare the scaling effects of LLMs on tasks that require varying
274   levels of medical reasoning capabilities. However, given that the differences in results between
275   MedResEval and MedQA could also be due to simple generalization effects, it is important to in-
276   troduce an additional baseline task to eliminate the impact of generalization. On the other hand,
277   apart from reasoning capabilities, mastering the diverse semantic representations of the same medi-
278   cal concept is also crucial for addressing real-world medical tasks. Therefore, we construct another
279   semantic-varied task by replacing the medical terms in the original MedQA dataset with corre-
280
      sponding synonyms. By introducing this baseline task, we can eliminate the effects of simple gener-
      alization and further explore the differences in scaling effects across various dimensions of medical
281
      capabilities, including semantic understanding and reasoning capabilities. More details of dataset
282
      generation, including the statistics and examples of tasks, are provided in Appendix A.
283
284   Evaluation Setting For evaluation, we employ the 5-shot Chain-of-Thought (CoT) setting (Wei
285   et al., 2022) to maximize the evaluation of LLMs’ potential in the medical domain. Following
286   (Nori et al., 2023), we use GPT-4o to generate CoTs that are consistent with the ground truth labels.
287   Additionally, we apply the self-consistency approach (Wang et al., 2022) to enhance performance
288   and ensure evaluation stability. We used handcrafted regular expressions to parse the answers from
289   the LLM responses and found that they successfully extracted the answers most of the time. We
290   select accuracy as the performance metrics, and conduct the normalization mentioned in Equation
291   4. More details of evaluation (e.g., prompt formats, inference setting) are provided in Appendix B.

305   Qualitative Analysis We begin by comparing the scaling effects of LLMs on medical tasks with
306   varying levels of reasoning complexity. Specifically, we split the evaluation tasks into two groups:
307   (1) Reasoning-simple tasks, including the original MedQA task and the semantic-varied task;
308   (2) Reasoning-complex tasks, including the reducing-available-clues task, the expanding-decision-
309   space task, and the increasing-reasoning-steps task4 . Figure 4 presents the results of this analysis.
310
      We observed that for simple tasks, scaling model size and training data size by the similar fac-
      tor yields similar performance improvement, and those smaller models trained on much larger
311
      datasets have matched or even outperformed larger models trained on smaller datasets. For example,
312
      Llama3-8B (∼7B, 15T) performs comparably to Llama-65B (∼70B, 1.4T) on simple tasks (34.4%
313   vs. 31.6%), despite being 8x smaller but trained on 10x more data. However, for tasks that re-
314   quire more complex reasoning, scaling model size by the similar factor results in significantly
315   greater gains compared to scaling data by the similar factor. The experimental results show that
316   Llama models with ∼7B parameters consistently underperform compared to Llama-65B, even when
317   trained on 10x more data. A similar trend is also observed in the Qwen model family, with the latest
318   Qwen2.5-7B underperforming Qwen-72B by 10% on complex tasks. In general, as tasks require
319   more complex reasoning, the effect of scaling model size becomes much more pronounced than
320   scaling data size.

      Finally, we also investigate the impact of instruction tuning on downstream performance of LLMs
453
      with varied model sizes and training data sizes. Specifically, we evaluate four instruction-tuned
454   LLMs with varying model sizes and training data sizes, and compare them to their respective base
455   models. We also evaluate two flagship commercial LLMs (GPT-4o-mini and GPT-4o) for reference.
456   The experimental results are presented in Table 2, with detailed performance provided in Appendix
457   D. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of instruction tuning, as it largely im-
458   proves performance on complex tasks by > 10%. However, instruction tuning does not reduce
459   the performance gap between models with different parameter scales. In fact, for complex
460   tasks, the performance gap between 7B and 70B models widened by ∼5% after instruction tuning.
461   This further confirms the generalizability of our conclusions regarding parameter and training data
462

463
      GPT-4o significantly outperforms all of the evaluated LLMs across tasks of varying difficulty levels.
464
      This further underscores the importance of scaling model size for addressing complex real-world
465   problems. It is worth noting that a large gap (13%) still exists between GPT-4o’s performance on
466   simple and complex tasks, indicating that the current ability of LLMs to generalize medical knowl-
467   edge across different tasks requires further improvement.

      6     C ONCLUSION
471
472
473   Solving real-world clinical problems require LLMs to master advanced medical reasoning capabili-
474   ties. In this paper, we revisiting the scaling effects of LLMs on medical reasoning capabilities. We
475   focus on three key factors that affect the reasoning complexity, and devise a specific task that sim-
476   ulates a problem from a real medical scenarios. Based on the evaluation of 18 LLMs with varying
477
      model sizes and training data amounts, we find that though some latest 7B LLMs have achieved
      comparable or even superior performance than elder 70B LLMs on existing benchmarks, they per-
478
      form consistently worse than the latter on tasks involving more complex reasoning. Furthermore,
479
      our quantitative analysis demonstrate that scaling the model size always lead to a more pronounced
480   performance improvement than scaling data size, especially on reasoning-complex tasks. Further
481   investigation indicates that though instruction-tuning does improve LLMs’ reasoning capabilities to
482   some extent, the scaling effects of model size are still notable. Our study findings indicate that while
483   both data and parameter scales enhance LLM performance, greater emphasis must be placed on
484   model parameter scales, particularly for difficult reasoning tasks. LLMs with sufficiently large-scale
485   model size are essential for effectively addressing difficult reasoning tasks, such as some real-world
      medical problems encountered.

590   We construct MedResEval by reformulating the original MedQA dataset, as introduced in preceding
591   sections. MedQA is a large-scale medical exam dataset, containing medical exams sourced from
592   three different regions. In this study, we use the questions in the US subset, which are collected from
593   the United States Medical Licensing Examination (USMLE). Questions in MedQA are MCQs with
      five options, as shown in the top of Figure 8. Typically, a MedQA question consist of introduction of

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


594
      a patient’s condition, together with a corresponding question part regarding the diagnosis, treatment,
595   or other aspect of the patient. For task reformulation, we first use regular expressions to identify and
596   parse the question part to determine the type of question. Furthermore, we generate three types of
597   reasoning-complex questions as follows:
598
599          • reducing-available-clues questions: We combine the extracted question part of the MCQ
600            with a correct answer and a randomly selected incorrect answer, resulting in two state-
601            ments. To increase the reasoning difficulty, we further derive two additional statements by
602
               adding negation words. Finally, we concatenate these statements with the description of
               the patient’s condition.
603
604          • expanding-decision-space questions: We replace the correct option in the original MCQ
605            with a distractor generated by GPT-4 to create unanswerable questions. To ensure the
606
               accuracy of the generated questions, we had two experienced doctors on our team review
               the reformulated questions.
607
608          • increasing-reasoning-steps questions: We generate this type of question by providing a
609            possible answer and instructing LLMs to verify the given answer, correcting it if it is in-
610
               correct. For example, we might ask, “Alice chose answer [given answer]. Please verify if
               this is correct, and if not, provide the correct answer.” In our implementation, we generate
611
               two questions based on the original MCQ by providing a correct answer and a randomly
612
               selected incorrect answer.

615   parsable questions sourced from the MedQA test set, we generate 3,200 questions for the reducing-

      B    D ETAILS OF E VALUATION S ETTING
703
704
      We illustrate the prompt format used for evaluation, which combines five-shot Chain of Thought
705   (CoT) prompting with self-consistency reasoning to maximize the performance of large language
706   models (LLMs) on challenging reasoning tasks. Specifically, we concatenate five demonstrative
707   question examples with the test question, where each example consists of a question, a CoT, and the
708   corresponding answer. Using this constructed input sequence, we prompt the evaluated LLM five
709   times to obtain five different reasoning chains. We set the temperature to 0.8 and top p to 0.8 to
710   encourage more diverse responses. Finally, we use regular expressions to extract answers from the
711   responses and conduct a majority vote to determine the final prediction.
712   For the generation of CoT, we follow (Nori et al., 2023) by prompting GPT-4o to generate CoT
713   based on the demonstrative question using the following template: [Question]. Please provide a
714   complete chain-of-thought to answer the given question. We prompt the GPT-4o several times for
715   each questions, only keeping the CoTs that are consistent with the ground-truth label.
