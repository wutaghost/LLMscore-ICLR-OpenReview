009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Incorporating prior knowledge or specifications of input-output relationships into
012            machine learning models has gained significant attention, as it enhances general-
013            ization from limited data and leads to conforming outputs. However, most exist-
014            ing approaches use soft constraints by penalizing violations through regulariza-
015            tion, which offers no guarantee of constraint satisfaction—an essential require-
016
               ment in safety-critical applications. On the other hand, imposing hard constraints
               on neural networks may hinder their representational power, adversely affecting
017
               performance. To address this, we propose HardNet, a practical framework for
018            constructing neural networks that inherently satisfy hard constraints without sacri-
019            ficing model capacity. Specifically, we encode affine and convex hard constraints,
020            dependent on both inputs and outputs, by appending a differentiable projection
021            layer to the network’s output. This architecture allows unconstrained optimization
022            of the network parameters using standard algorithms while ensuring constraint
023            satisfaction by construction. Furthermore, we show that HardNet retains the uni-
024            versal approximation capabilities of neural networks. We demonstrate the versa-
025
               tility and effectiveness of HardNet across various applications: fitting functions
               under constraints, learning optimization solvers, optimizing control policies in
026
               safety-critical systems, and learning safe decision logic for aircraft systems.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      Neural networks are widely adopted for their generalization capabilities and their ability to model
031
      highly non-linear functions in high-dimensional spaces. With their increasing proliferation, it has
032   become more important to be able to impose constraints on neural networks in many applications.
033   By incorporating domain knowledge about input-output relationships into neural networks through
034   constraints, we can enhance their generalization abilities, particularly when the available data is
035   limited (Pathak et al., 2015; Oktay et al., 2017; Raissi et al., 2019). These constraints introduce
036   inductive biases that can guide the model’s learning process toward plausible solutions that adhere to
037   known properties of the problem domain, potentially reducing overfitting to limited data. As a result,
038
      neural networks can more effectively capture underlying patterns and make accurate predictions on
      unseen data, despite the scarcity of training examples.
039
040   Moreover, adherence to specific requirements is critical in many practical applications. For instance,
041   in robotics, this could translate to imposing collision avoidance or pose manifold constraints (Ding
042   & Fan, 2014; Wang & Yan, 2023; Ryu et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022). In geometric learning, this
043
      could mean imposing a manifold constraint (Lin & Zha, 2008; Simeonov et al., 2022). In financial
      risk-management scenarios, violating constraints on the solvency of the portfolio can lead to large
044
      fines (McNeil et al., 2015). By enforcing the neural network outputs to satisfy these non-negotiable
045   rules (i.e., hard constraints), we can make the models more reliable, interpretable, and aligned with
046   the underlying problem structure.
047
048
      However, introducing hard constraints can potentially limit a neural network’s expressive power. To
      illustrate this point, consider a constraint that requires the model’s output to be less than 1. One
049
      could simply restrict the model to always output a constant value less than 1, which ensures the
050   constraint satisfaction but obviously limits the model capacity drastically. This raises the question:
051
          Can we enforce hard constraints on neural networks without losing their expressive power?
052
053   The model capacity of neural networks is often explained through the universal approximation the-
      orem, which shows that a neural network can approximate any continuous function given a suffi-

      ciently wide/deep architecture. Demonstrating that this theorem still holds under hard constraints is
055   essential to understanding the trade-off between constraint satisfaction and model capacity.

057   Contributions We tackle the problem of enforcing hard constraints on neural networks by
058    • Presenting a practical framework called HardNet (short for hard-constrained neural net) for
059      constructing neural networks that satisfy input-dependent affine/convex constraints by construc-
060      tion. HardNet allows for unconstrained optimization of the networks’ parameters with standard
061      algorithms.
062    • Proving a universal approximation theorem for our method, showing that despite enforcing
063      the hard constraints, our construction retains the expressive power of neural networks.
064    • Demonstrating the utility of our method on a variety of scenarios where it’s critical to satisfy
065      hard constraints – learning optimization solvers, optimizing control policies in safety-critical
066
         systems, and learning safe decision logic for aircraft systems.
067    • Outlining a survey of the literature on constructing neural networks that satisfy hard constraints.

069   2   R ELATED W ORK
070
      Neural Networks with Soft Constraints Early directions focused on implementing data augmen-
071
      tation or domain randomization methods to structure the dataset to satisfy the necessary constraints
072   before training the neural network. However, this does not guarantee constraint satisfaction for
073   arbitrary inputs (especially those far from the training distribution), and the output often violates
074   the constraints marginally on in-distribution inputs as well. Other initial directions focused on in-
075   troducing the constraints as soft penalties (Márquez-Neila et al., 2017; Dener et al., 2020) to the
076   cost function of the neural network along with Lagrange multipliers as hyperparameters. Raissi
077   et al. (2019); Li et al. (2024) leveraged this idea in their work on physics-informed neural networks
078   (PINNs) to enforce that the output satisfies a given differential equation.
079   Neural Networks with Hard Constraints Some of the conventional neural network components
080   can already enforce specific types of hard constraints. For instance, sigmoids can impose lower and
081   upper bounds, softmax layers help enforce simplex constraints, and ReLU layers are projections
082   onto the positive orthant. The convolution layer in ConvNets encodes a translational equivariance
083   constraint which led to significant improvements in empirical performance. Learning new equivari-
084   ances and inductive biases that accelerate learning for specific tasks is an active area of research.
085   Recent work has explored new architectures to (asymptotically) impose various hard constraints by
086   either finding certain parameterizations of feasible sets or incorporating differentiable projection
087   layers into neural networks, as summarized in Table 1. Frerix et al. (2020) addressed homogeneous
088   linear inequality constraints by embedding a parameterization of the feasible set in a neural network
089   layer. Huang et al. (2021) and LinSATNet (Wang et al., 2023) introduced differentiable projection
090   methods that iteratively refine outputs to satisfy linear constraints. However, these iterative ap-
091
      proaches do not guarantee constraint satisfaction within a fixed number of iterations, limiting their
      reliability in practice. C-DGM (Stoian et al., 2024) enforce linear inequality constraints in genera-
092
      tive models for tabular data by incrementally adjusting each output component in a finite number of
093   iterations. However, its application to input-dependent constraints is limited as it cannot efficiently
094   handle batched data. When constraints are input-dependent, the method requires recomputing the
095   reduced constraint sets for each input, making it computationally prohibitive.
096
      Beyond the affine constraints, RAYEN (Tordesillas et al., 2023) and Konstantinov & Utkin (2023)
097
      enforce certain convex constraints by parameterizing the feasible set such that the neural network
098   output represents a translation from an interior point of the convex feasible region. However, these
099   methods are limited to constraints that depend solely on the output and not on the input. Extending
100   these methods to input-dependent constraints is challenging because it requires finding different
101   parameterizations for each input, such as determining a new interior point for every feasible set.
102
      Another line of work considers hard constraints that depend on both input and output. Balestriero
103   & LeCun (2023) proposed the POLICE framework for enforcing the output to be an affine function
104   of the input in certain regions of the input space by reformulating the neural networks as continuous
105   piecewise affine mappings. KKT-hPINN (Chen et al., 2024) enforces more general affine equality
106   constraints by projecting the output to the feasible set where the projection is computed using the
107   KKT conditions of the constraints. However, these affine equality constraints are too restrictive.

142   Formal Verification of Neural Networks Verifying whether a provided neural network (after
143   training) always satisfies a set of constraints for a certain set of inputs is a well-studied subject. Al-
      barghouthi et al. (2021) provide a comprehensive summary of the constraint-based and abstraction-
144
      based approaches to verification. Constraint-based verifiers are often both sound and complete but
145
      they have not scaled to practical neural networks, whereas abstraction-based techniques are approx-
146   imate verifiers which are sound but often incomplete (Brown et al., 2022; Tjeng et al., 2019; Liu
147   et al., 2021; Fazlyab et al., 2020; Qin et al., 2019; Ehlers, 2017). Other approaches have focused on
148   formally verified exploration and policy learning for reinforcement learning (Bastani et al., 2018;
149   Anderson et al., 2020). Contrary to formal verification methods, which take a pre-trained network
150   and verify that its output always satisfies the desired constraints, our method guarantees constraint
151   satisfaction by construction.

      3.2 U NIVERSAL A PPROXIMATION T HEOREM
170   The universal approximation property is a foundational concept in understanding the capabilities of
171   neural networks in various applications. Classical results reveal that shallow neural networks with
172
      arbitrary width can approximate any continuous function defined on a compact set as formalized in
      the following theorem (Cybenko, 1989; Hornik et al., 1989; Pinkus, 1999):
173
174   Theorem 3.1 (Universal Approximation Theorem for Shallow Networks). Let ρ : R → R be any
175
      continuous function and K ∈ R be a compact set. Then, depth-two neural networks with ρ activation
      function universally approximate C(K, R) if and only if ρ is nonpolynomial.
176
177   To further understand the success of deep learning, the universal approximation property for deep
178   and narrow neural networks has also been studied in the literature (Lu et al., 2017; Hanin & Sellke,
179   2017; Kidger & Lyons, 2020; Park et al., 2021). Interesting results show that a critical threshold
180   exists on the width of deep networks that attain the universal approximation property. For instance,
181   deep networks with ReLU activation function with a certain minimum width can approximate any
182   Lp function as described in the following theorem (Park et al., 2021, Thm. 1):
183   Theorem 3.2 (Universal Approximation Theorem for Deep Networks). For any p ∈ [1, ∞), w-
184   width neural networks with ReLU activation function universally approximate Lp (Rnin , Rnout ) if and
185   only if w ≥ max{nin + 1, nout }.
186
      Despite these powerful approximation guarantees, they fall short in scenarios where neural networks
187   are required to satisfy hard constraints, such as physical laws or safety requirements. These theo-
188   rems ensure that a neural network can approximate a target function arbitrarily closely but do not
189   guarantee that the approximation will adhere to necessary constraints. Consequently, even if the
190   target function inherently satisfies specific hard constraints, the neural network approximator might
191   violate them–especially in regions where the target function barely meets the constraints. This
192   shortcoming is particularly problematic for applications that demand strict compliance with non-
193   negotiable domain-specific rules. Therefore, ensuring that neural networks can both approximate
194
      target functions accurately and rigorously satisfy hard constraints remains a critical challenge for
      their deployment in practical applications.

      4   HardNet: H ARD -C ONSTRAINED N EURAL N ETWORKS
197
198   In this section, we present a practical framework HardNet for enforcing hard constraints on neural
199   networks while retaining their universal approximation properties. In a nutshell, for a parameter-
200
      ized (neural network) function fθ : X ⊂ Rnin → Rnout , we ensure the satisfaction of given con-
      straints by appending a differentiable projection layer P to fθ . This results in the projected function
201
      P(fθ ) : X → Rnout meeting the required constraints while allowing its output to be backpropagated
202
      through to train the model via gradient-based algorithms. Importantly, we show that the proposed
203   architecture has universal approximation guarantees, i.e., it universally approximates the class of
204   functions that satisfy the constraints.
205
      We begin with the simple intuitive case of a single affine constraint and then generalize the ap-
206
      proach in two directions. First, we propose HardNet-Aff that ensures compliance with multiple
207
      input-dependent affine constraints through a differentiable closed-form projection. Then, we present
208   HardNet-Cvx as a framework to satisfy general input-dependent convex constraints exploiting dif-
209   ferentiable convex optimization solvers.
210

319   following universal approximation theorem; see Appendix A.2 for a proof.
320   Theorem 4.5 (Universal Approximation Theorem with Affine Constraints). Consider input-
321   dependent constraints (4) that satisfy assumption 4.1. Suppose X ⊂ Rnin is compact, and
322   A(x), C(x) are continuous over X . For any p ∈ [1, ∞), let F = {f ∈ Lp (X , Rnout )|f satisfies (4)}.
323   Then, HardNet-Aff with w-width ReLU neural networks defined in (7) universally approximates F
      if w ≥ max{nin + 1, nout − neq }.

325   fθ such that ∥f − fθ ∥ is arbitrarily small, we can make P(fθ ) approach the target function f as
326   closely as desired. The existence of such an fθ is guaranteed by existing universal approximation
327   theorems. While we utilize Theorem 3.2 in this theorem, other universal approximation theorems
328   on plain neural networks, such as Theorem 3.1, can also be employed.

506   5.4   A IRBORNE C OLLISION AVOIDANCE S YSTEM (ACAS)
507
508
      In this example, we consider learning an airborne collision avoidance system called the Horizontal
      Collision Avoidance System (HCAS), which is a variant of the popular system ACAS Xu used by
509
      Katz et al. (2017), for which the labeled training dataset is publicly available (Julian & Kochenderfer,
510
      2019). The goal of the HCAS system is to recommend horizontal maneuvers—clear of conflict,
511   weak left, strong left, weak right, strong right—to aircrafts in order to stay safe and avoid collisions.
512   The system takes the state of the ownship and the relative state of the intruder airplane as inputs, and
513   outputs a score for each of the 5 possible horizontal maneuvers listed above. Traditionally, this was
514   accomplished using lookup tables but using neural networks has recently become customary.
515   In their original work, Katz et al. (2017) developed a method called Reluplex for formally verifying
516   that the deep neural networks used for generating the scores of the advisories always satisfy certain
517   hard constraints. This requires training a model and then verifying that it satisfies the constraints by
518   solving a satisfiability problem. In addition, the original work trains 45 different models for various
519   values of τ (the time to collision or loss of vertical separation) and pra (the previously recommended
520   advisory) in order to satisfy strict computational limits imposed by the inference hardware. In our
521   work, we train a single model that generalizes across various values of τ and pra.
522   In our implementation, we demonstrate a method for learning neural networks that output con-
523   strained airplane advisories by construction, rather than engaging in an iterative cycle of training a
524   neural network and separately verifying it until convergence. While some of the properties in the
525   original problem (Katz et al., 2017) are non-convex in nature, we pick all five of the properties that
526   can be encoded in a convex form. The results for this example are presented in Table 5. Additional
527
      details for the experiment can be found in Appendix A.6.

530
531
532   In this paper, we presented HardNet, a practical framework for constructing neural networks that
      inherently satisfy input-dependent affine/convex constraints. We proved that imposing these hard
533
      constraints does not limit the expressive power of these neural networks by providing universal
534
      approximation guarantees. We demonstrated the utility and versatility of our method across several
535   application scenarios, such as learning solvers for optimization problems, control policies for safety-
536   critical systems, and advisories for aircraft navigation systems. Using HardNet in other application
537   domains that benefit from incorporating domain-specific knowledge is a promising direction for
538   future work. Additionally, we aim to explore developing methods for performing fast projections
539   for problems with more general constraints. Lastly, extending our approach to support other forms
      of inductive biases, such as equivariances and invariances, would potentially be of great interest.

      projection layer P appended at the end, we propose a training scheme that can potentially result

       in better-optimized models. For the first k epochs of training, we disable the projection layer and
973    train the plain neural network fθ . Then, from the (k+1)-th epoch, we train on the projected model
974    P(fθ ). During the k epochs of warm start, the neural network fθ can be trained in a soft-constrained
975    manner by regularizing the violations of constraints. In this paper, we train the HardNet-Aff models
976    without the warm-start scheme for simplicity, except in Section 5.2 where we use the warm-start

978    necessary for cvxpylayers since it makes it easier for the convex program solver (SCS) to perform
979    the projection.

       A.9   R ELATED W ORK IN N EURO -S YMBOLIC AI
1081
1082   HardNet also aligns with the objectives of Neuro-symbolic AI, a field that has gained significant at-
1083   tention in recent years for its ability to integrate complex background knowledge into deep learning
1084   models. Unlike HardNet, which focuses on algebraic constraints, the neuro-symbolic AI literature
1085   primarily addresses logical constraints. A common approach in this field is to softly impose con-
1086   straints during training by introducing penalty terms into the loss function to discourage constraint
       violations (Xu et al., 2018; Fischer et al., 2019; Badreddine et al., 2022; Stoian et al., 2023). While
1087
       these methods are straightforward to implement, they do not guarantee constraint satisfaction. In
1088
       contrast, works such as Giunchiglia & Lukasiewicz (2020); Ahmed et al. (2022); Giunchiglia et al.
1089   (2024) ensure constraints are satisfied by embedding them into the predictive layer, thus guarantee-
1090   ing compliance by construction. Another line of research maps neural network outputs into logical
1091   predicates, ensuring constraint satisfaction through reasoning on these predicates (Manhaeve et al.,
1092   2018; Pryor et al., 2023; van Krieken et al., 2023).
