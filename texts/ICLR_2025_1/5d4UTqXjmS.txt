009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Cognitive flexibility has been extensively studied in human cognition but remains
012            relatively unexplored in the context of Visual Large Language Models (VLLMs).
013            This study assesses the cognitive flexibility of state-of-the-art VLLMs (GPT-4o,
014            Gemini-1.5 Pro, and Claude-3.5 Sonnet) using the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test
015            (WCST), a classic measure of set-shifting ability. Our results reveal that VLLMs
016
               achieve or surpass human-level set-shifting capabilities under chain-of-thought
               prompting with text-based inputs. However, their abilities are highly influenced by
017
               both input modality and prompting strategy. In addition, we find that through role-
018
               playing, VLLMs can simulate various functional deficits aligned with patients
019            having impairments in cognitive flexibility, suggesting that VLLMs may possess
020            a cognitive architecture, at least regarding the ability of set-shifting, similar to
021            the brain. This study reveals the fact that VLLMs have already approached the
022            human level on a key component underlying our higher cognition, and highlights
023            the potential to use them to emulate complex brain processes.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Cognitive flexibility, a key component of executive function, is fundamental to human adaptability
028
      and problem-solving (Dajani & Uddin, 2015; Uddin, 2021). This ability to shift between mental sets
029
      or strategies in response to changing environmental demands is crucial for everyday functioning and
030   has been extensively studied in cognitive psychology research (Ionescu, 2012). The prefrontal cortex
031   (PFC) is known to be central to this cognitive process(Spellman et al., 2021; Funahashi & Andreau,
032   2013), facilitating goal-directed behavior and controlled processing.
033
      Recent advancements in artificial intelligence (AI), particularly in visual large language models
034
      (VLLMs) (Chen et al., 2024a), have sparked a growing need to assess these systems’ cognitive abil-
035
      ities using paradigms analogous to human cognitive assessment (Momennejad et al., 2024; Chang
036   et al., 2024; Qu et al., 2024). State-of-the-art VLLMs such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), Gemini-1.5
037   Pro (Reid et al., 2024), and Claude-3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024) have demonstrated remarkable ca-
038   pabilities in processing and interpreting both textual and visual information, excelling in tasks that
039   demand complex reasoning and contextual understanding.
040
      Despite these achievements, the extent to which VLLMs exhibit cognitive flexibility, especially
041
      in tasks requiring set-shifting and adaptation to changing rules, remains largely unexplored. While
042   these models have demonstrated impressive performance across diverse tasks, their ability to flexibly
043   adapt to changing environmental demands has not been systematically evaluated. This gap in our
044   understanding is particularly significant given the increasing integration of VLLMs into complex,
045   dynamic real-world environments where adaptability is crucial.
046
      The Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST), developed in the 1940s (Berg, 1948) and refined over
047
      decades, has emerged as the gold standard for assessing cognitive flexibility in both clinical and
048   research settings (Miles et al., 2021; Greve, 2001). Originally developed to evaluate PFC function,
049   it requires participants to discover sorting rules based on feedback and then flexibly shift to new rules
050   when the criteria change (see Figure 1). The test’s sensitivity to PFC function has been consistently
051   demonstrated through lesion studies(Jodzio & Biechowska, 2010), neuroimaging research(Lie et al.,
052   2006), and clinical observations, cementing its status as a crucial tool in understanding the cognitive
053   flexibility. While other measures of cognitive flexibility exist, the WCST’s established validity make
      it a core benchmark for evaluating this fundamental cognitive capacity.

081   investigate how different input modalities (image-based vs. text-based) ,prompting strategy (direct
082   vs. chain-of-thought reasoning) and rule description specificity affect their performance. Addi-

090   2      R ELATED WORK
091
092   2.1      C OGNITIVE F LEXIBILITY AND A SSESSMENT M ETHODS
093
094
      Neuroimaging studies have consistently implicated the PFC in cognitive flexibility tasks. The dor-
095
      solateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) play critical roles in set-
096   shifting, with the DLPFC maintaining and updating task rules, and the ACC involved in conflict
097   monitoring and error detection (Kim et al., 2011). The fronto-parietal network, encompassing these
098   regions, dynamically reconfigures during flexibility-demanding tasks (Qiao et al., 2020). Cogni-
099   tive flexibility is closely interrelated with other executive functions: working memory maintains
100   task-relevant information and goals (Ionescu, 2012), while inhibitory control suppresses previous
101   cognitive sets when rules change (Diamond, 2013).
102   Several tasks have been developed to assess cognitive flexibility in humans, with the WCST being
103   a widely recognized measure of set-shifting ability (Eling et al., 2008). The WCST’s sensitivity to
104   PFC dysfunction has been extensively validated (Stuss & Levine, 2002). Complementary assess-
105   ments include the Dimensional Change Card Sort (DCCS) task for children (Zelazo, 2006) and the
106   computerized Intra-Extra Dimensional Set Shift (IED) subtest of the Cambridge Neuropsycholog-
107   ical Test Automated Battery (CANTAB) (Heinzel et al., 2010), offering targeted measures across
      different populations and modalities.

      2.2   M ULTIFACETED E VALUATION OF LLM S
109
110   Recent studies have employed diverse assessments to evaluate large language models (LLMs) and
111   VLLMs across various domains and tasks. Models like GPT-4 demonstrated human-level or su-
112   perior performance on most theory of mind tests (Strachan et al., 2024). Similarly, research on
113   human creativity found that ChatGPT-assisted ideas were more creative compared to those gener-
114   ated without LLM assistance (Lee & Chung, 2024). However, challenges persist in other areas.
115   The Test of Time (ToT) benchmark exposed difficulties with complex temporal reasoning tasks,
116   particularly those requiring multi-fact integration and intricate arithmetic operations (Fatemi et al.,
117   2024). Despite strong performance on high-level vision tasks, state-of-the-art VLLMs struggled
118
      with basic geometric tasks that are straightforward for humans (Rahmanzadehgervi et al., 2024). A
      neuropsychological investigation revealed a discontinuous profile in ChatGPT’s prefrontal function-
119
      ing, with performance ranging from superior to impaired across different cognitive tasks (Loconte
120
      et al., 2023). To address the multifaceted nature of artificial intelligence, researchers have proposed
121   new evaluation frameworks. A comprehensive framework for artificial general intelligence (AGI)
122   tests inspired by cognitive science emphasizes the need for multidimensional intelligence assess-
123   ment (Qu et al., 2024). Additionally, the concept of Turing Experiments (TEs) was introduced as
124   a method for evaluating LLMs’ ability to simulate human behavior in experimental settings (Aher
125   et al., 2023).
126
127
      2.3   P ROMPTING S TRATEGIES
128
129
      Prompting strategies significantly influence the performance of LLMs (Liu et al., 2023). The sim-
130
      plest approach, ”Straight-to-Answer” (STA), directly queries the model without additional context.
131   While effective for straightforward tasks, STA often falters on complex problems requiring multi-
132   step reasoning. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting encourages step-by-step reasoning(Wei et al.,
133   2022), substantially improving performance on complex reasoning tasks (Chu et al., 2023). Vari-
134   ations such as zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al., 2022) and self-consistency CoT (Wang et al., 2022)
135   have further refined this approach, adapting it to scenarios with limited or no task-specific exam-
136   ples. In multimodal contexts, visual CoT have extended these concepts to VLLMs (Chen et al.,
137   2024b), demonstrating the potential for improved reasoning in tasks that combine textual and vi-
138   sual information. Other task-specific strategies, such as least-to-most prompting address challenges
139
      of easy-to-hard generalization (Zhou et al., 2022a), while meta-prompting and automatic prompt
      engineering techniques aim to optimize the prompts themselves (Pryzant et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,
140
      2022b).

      3.1   M ODELS AND E XPERIMENTAL P ROCEDURE
147
      This study focuses on three state-of-the-art VLLMs: GPT-4o, Gemini-1.5 Pro, and Claude-3.5 Son-
148
      net. These models represent the current pinnacle of multimodal LLMs capabilities, demonstrating
149
      proficiency in processing both textual and visual inputs (see Appendix.A for details). We employs a
150   standard version of the WCST-64 (Greve, 2001) to assess the cognitive flexibility of VLLMs. Our
151   experimental design incorporates a 2x2 factorial structure, manipulating input modality (Visual In-
152   put (VI) / Textual Input (TI)) and prompting strategy (Straight to Answer (STA) / Chain of Thought
153   (CoT)) to comprehensively evaluate VLLMs performance. This design resulted in four experimental
154   conditions: STA-VI, STA-TI, CoT-VI, and CoT-TI. Each VLLM was tested independently across
155   all four conditions, with 10 repetitions per condition. The arrangement of stimulus cards and the
156   sequence of sorting rules were randomized for each repetition.
157   Additionally, we collected data from 30 cognitively healthy human participants (aged 20-35) as a
158   baseline for comparison. Human participants interacted with a web-based interface designed to
159   replicate the WCST experience while accommodating human response patterns (Figure B.3). The
160   interface presented cards sequentially and allowed participants to indicate their sorting choices via
161   button presses. The language used in instructions was carefully adapted to be more intuitive for
      human subjects while maintaining the essential structure of the task.

302   4.2   D ETAILED A NALYSIS BY E VALUATION M ETRIC
303
304   To offer a comprehensive assessment of the VLLMs’ performance on the WCST, we analyzed six
305   key metrics outlined in the previous section. Table 1 presents the mean scores and standard devi-
306   ations across all evaluation metrics for each VLLM and condition. This analysis reveals distinct
307   patterns in cognitive flexibility and set-shifting abilities among the models.
308   PE were most prevalent in the STA-TI condition for all models, with Claude-3.5 Sonnet showing
309   the highest number of errors in this condition (µ = 15.90, σ = 17.53). In the STA-VI condition,
310   PE were relatively low for all models, as they largely failed to follow the rules at all. However, the
311   transition to CoT conditions reduced PE, with Claude-3.5 Sonnet demonstrated the lowest number
312   of PE in the CoT-TI condition (µ = 6.30, σ = 0.82). This suggests that Claude-3.5 Sonnet may
313   surpass human performance in adapting to changing rules, especially when provided with explicit
314
      reasoning prompts and textual descriptions.
315   NPE showed a dramatic reduction from STA to CoT conditions across all models, with improve-
316   ments observed in the transition from VI to TI inputs. In STA conditions, NPE were extremely high,
317   indicating near-random performance. The near-elimination of NPE in CoT-TI (e.g., Claude-3.5 Son-
318   net: µ = 2.00, σ = 0.82) suggests that VLLMs can achieve a level of consistent rule application that
319   exceeds human performance. This suggests that explicit reasoning prompts enable VLLMs to main-
320
      tain a more consistent internal representation of the current sorting rule, reducing random errors.
321   All models required the fewest trials to complete the first category in the CoT-TI condition, with
322   Claude-3.5 Sonnet performing best (µ = 12.00, σ = 0.94), followed closely by GPT-4o (µ = 12.60,
323   σ = 2.46) and Gemini-1.5 Pro (µ = 13.30, σ = 1.95). Notably, Claude-3.5 Sonnet outperformed the
      human baseline (µ = 12.93, σ = 1.62).

      5   D ISCUSSION
459
460
      This study demonstrates that state-of-the-art VLLMs can achieve, and in some cases surpass, human-
461
      level cognitive flexibility as measured by the WCST, suggesting a potential for emulating and ex-
462   ceeding human set-shifting abilities in specific contexts. The observed performance gradient under-
463   scores the complex interplay between input modalities and prompting strategies. The performance
464   gap between VI and TI conditions indicates that current VLLMs may rely more heavily on language-
465   based reasoning pathways, even when processing visual information. Explicit reasoning prompts
466   enable VLLMs to maintain more stable internal representations of task rules.
467
      Our analysis of explicit rule exclusivity reveals a critical dependence of VLLMs on precise task
468   instructions. The significant performance decline observed when specific rule constraints were re-
469   moved highlights the models’ reliance on explicit information to guide their decision-making pro-
470   cesses. This finding suggests that VLLMs’ impressive performance in structured tasks may not
471   fully generalize to more ambiguous real-world scenarios without careful consideration of instruc-
472   tion design. The simulation of cognitive impairments through role-playing prompts demonstrates the
473   potential of VLLMs to model complex patterns of executive dysfunction. The distinct performance
474   profiles observed under simulated goal maintenance, inhibitory control, and adaptive updating im-
475   pairments closely mirror patterns seen in human neuropsychological research.
476   Future research should focus on elucidating the underlying mechanisms that enable VLLMs to per-
477   form set-shifting tasks and investigating the generalizability of these abilities to other domains of ex-
478   ecutive function. Additionally, the development of more sophisticated visual processing capabilities
479   and the integration of multimodal information processing warrant further exploration. The potential
480   of VLLMs to simulate specific patterns of cognitive impairment also opens up new possibilities for
481   creating realistic models of neuropsychological conditions, which could have applications in both
482
      clinical research and AI safety. By analyzing VLLMs’ internal representations during simulated im-
      pairments, we could potentially decode the computational principles underlying various cognitive
483
      functions, complementing traditional neuroscience methods.
484
485   In conclusion, this study provides insights into our understanding of cognitive flexibility in VLLMs,
      revealing capabilities that match or exceed human performance and important limitations that de-


                                                         9
486
      pend on task framing and input modalities. As these models continue to evolve, a deeper understand-
487   ing of their cognitive processes will be crucial for harnessing their potential while addressing their
488   constraints, ultimately leading to more adaptive and robust AI systems that can flexibly navigate
489   complex, real-world environments.

      6   L IMITATIONS
493
      This study’s findings are constrained by several factors. Our focus on a limited subset of current
494
      VLLMs may not generalize to the entire spectrum of models or future iterations. The role-playing
495
      approach to simulating cognitive impairments, while innovative, requires validation against clini-
496   cal populations to confirm its fidelity. The observed sensitivity to input modalities and instruction
497   specificity suggests potential variability in less structured scenarios. Our analysis of visual process-
498   ing was limited to feature recognition, neglecting deeper aspects of visual cognition. Additionally,
499   the study did not investigate the underlying mechanisms enabling VLLMs to perform set-shifting
500   tasks, constraining our understanding of how these abilities emerge from the models’ architecture
501   and training. Addressing these limitations in future research will be crucial for a more comprehen-
502   sive understanding of cognitive flexibility in VLLMs and their potential applications in complex
503   cognitive tasks.

697   B.1   D ETAILED PROMPTS
698
699   The WCST setup consists of four stimulus cards, each featuring unique combinations of color (red,
700   green, yellow, blue), shape (triangle, star, cross, circle), and number (one, two, three, four) of sym-
701   bols. A series of 64 response cards is used, each sharing properties with the stimulus cards but in
      different combinations. The sorting rules are based on three possible categories: color, shape, or


                                                         13
702
      number. In our implementation, each trial is presented to the VLLMs as a single image containing
703   two rows. The top row displays the four stimulus cards, while the bottom row shows one response
704   card. This format is consistent across all 64 trials, providing a standardized visual input for the mod-
705   els to process. For the text-based conditions (TI), detailed descriptions of these images are provided
706   instead.
707
      The test procedure begins with the VLLM being instructed to match each response card to one of
708
      the stimulus cards based on a rule that it must deduce from feedback. After each match attempt, the
709   VLLM receives feedback (correct or incorrect) without explicit mention of the current sorting rule.
710   The sorting rule changes after 10 consecutive correct matches, without notification to the VLLM.
711   The test concludes after all 64 cards have been presented.
712

713
      VI (Straight to Answer with Original Image input), STA-TI (Straight to Answer with Original Text
714
      description input), CoT-VI (Chain of Thought reasoning with Original Image input), and CoT-TI
715   (Chain of Thought reasoning with Original Text description input). Figure B.1 provides a visual
716   representation of the WCST procedure and sample stimuli used in our study.

718

      VLLMs were explicitly instructed to verbalize their reasoning process, including their observations,
719
      hypotheses about the current rule, and justification for their sorting decisions.

722   designed to closely mimic the experience of VLLMs while accommodating human interaction pat-
723   terns. It features a clear presentation of stimulus and response cards, along with intuitive controls
724
      for participants to indicate their sorting choices. The interface also provides immediate feedback on
      sorting decisions, mirroring the feedback mechanism used with VLLMs.
