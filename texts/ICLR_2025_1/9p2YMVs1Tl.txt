013
014
               Predict-and-search is increasingly becoming the predominant framework for solv-
015            ing Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) problems through the applica-
016            tion of ML algorithms. Traditionally, MILP problems are represented as bipartite
017            graphs, wherein nodes and edges encapsulate critical information pertaining to
018            the objectives and constraints. However, existing ML approaches have primarily
019            concentrated on extracting features from nodes while largely ignoring those as-
020            sociated with edges. To bridge this gap, we propose a novel framework named
021            SHARP which leverages a graph neural network SKEGAT that integrates both
022            node and edge features. Furthermore, we design an adaptive Regret-Greedy algo-
023
               rithm to break the barriers of the problem scale and hand-crafted tuning. Experi-
               ments across a variety of combinatorial optimization problems show that SHARP
024
               surpasses current SOTA algorithms, delivering notable enhancements in both so-
025
               lution accuracy and computational efficiency.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) has seen a surge of interest in many practical applica-
031   tions spanning from production planning (Pochet & Wolsey, 2006; Wu et al., 2013), supply chain
032   scheduling (Sawik, 2011), and resource allocation (Liu & Fan, 2018; Watson & Woodruff, 2011).
033
      As a discrete extension of linear programming, MILP often faces both integer and continuous vari-
      ables, thereby leading to the NP-hard complexity. As a well-established topic, the significant suc-
034
      cesses of traditional algorithms over the past decades, such as branch-and-bound (Land & Doig,
035
      2010; 1960) and cutting plane algorithm (Gomory, 1960), have made it possible to near-optimal
036   solutions for MILP. Besides, these algorithms consist of the core of commercial solvers, such as
037   Gurobi (Gurobi Optimization, 2022). However, these algorithms adhere to an iterative paradigm
038   which heavily lacks parallelism, often making them unsuitable for handling large-scale problems in
039   industrial scenarios.
040
      Recently, there has been significant interest in machine learning (ML) algorithms as a viable alter-
041
      native to solve MILP in a data-centric fashion. The potential advantage lies in developing faster
042   algorithms in practice by exploiting typical patterns found through the analysis of large training in-
043   stances. They could automatically discover variable assignment strategies to speed up the similar
044   instance from the same distribution. Specifically, the attempts by several well-studied algorithms to
045   solve MILPs are shifting towards two major categories: the former involves ML-based algorithms
046   learning branch and bound strategies for exact solving, while the latter adopts ML-based methods
047   to learn heuristic rules for approximate solving. We will discuss these methods respectively in Sec-
048   tion A.1 and Section A.2 in detail.
049   Most notably, the pioneering work in approximate solving involves the Neural Diving approach
050   (Nair et al., 2020), which formulates the MILP problem as a bipartite graph and treats the problem
051   solving as the prediction of integer variable assignments. However, previous studies have high-
052   lighted challenges associated with insufficient representation for MILP problems and the inaccuracy
053   of a single prediction from the model. To address the issue of inadequate representation, numer-
      ous strategies have been employed to compensate for this, such as generating higher-quality initial


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      solutions (Nair et al., 2020), enhancing representational capacity through additional modules (Nair
055   et al., 2020), and adopting more complicated models like SelectiveNet (Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2019).
056   To tackle the issue of inaccurate model predictions, the post-searching process is applied after the
057   Neural Diving method. For instance, a confidence threshold is used to control which variables to
058   assign. Building on the aforementioned successes, the Predict-and-Search framework (Han et al.,
059   2022) is proposed to consider feasibility by introducing a trust-region-like algorithm that allows
060   the algorithms to change certain fixed variables. However, the bipartite graph representation of the
061   MILP contains rich information about constraint coefficients and objective function coefficients,
062   which can potentially compromise solution quality. Previous works draws much attention on the
063
      node representation while ignoring the edge representation. Additionally, algorithms that directly
      predict variable assignments often struggle to satisfy the constraints, whereas most post-searching
064
      algorithms rely on the specific structure of the problem to determine the neighborhood search range,
065
      which limits their applicability beyond the initial designed scope and requires hand-crafted parame-
066   ter tuning.
067
068   To tackle above challenges, We propose a nevel framework named SHARP (Sinkhorn-regularized
069
      Edge Attention with Adaptive Regret-based Procedure). In terms of insufficient representation chal-
      lenge, we innovatively integrate EGAT (Gong & Cheng, 2019) with probability distribution learning
070
      to provide a finer-grained representation of nodes and edges. Besides, we use the Sinkhorn al-
071
      gorithm (Sinkhorn & Knopp, 1967) for feature normalization, which accelerates computation and
072   stabilizes the training process. In terms of prediction inaccuracy challenge, we propose a confidence
073   threshold-based greedy regret search method. Specifically, we fix variables greedily on the basis
074   of the variable assignment probability in proportion, and then allow for the adaptation of the last
075   variable assignment in a flexible manner. This strategy not only enhances solution feasibility and
076   improves solving accuracy but also captures subtle differences in problem structures, demonstrating
077   robust generalization capabilities.
078   We conducted comprehensive experiments on Combinatorial Auction (CA) and Item Placement (IP)
079   problems, evaluating SHARP against other methods using three metrics: Primal Gap (PG), Survival
080   Rate (SR), and Primal Integral (PI).
081
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
082
083
             • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose the EGAT with Sinkhorn normal-
084            ization, which more accurately captures node and edge information, thereby enhancing the
085            expressive power of the model and accelerating training while improving learning stability.
086
             • Our proposed adaptive variable assignment strategy enhances solution feasibility and over-
087
               comes the limitations of previous work that are bound by scale.
088
089          • The SHARP respectively achieves average 24.88% and 5.86% performance improvements
090
               to the modern solvers the Gurobi and SCIP on primal gaps, and exceed the performance of
               the SOTA ML-based algorithm by 17.19%.

097   A mixed integer linear programming (MILP) problem is a type of the combinatorial optimization
098   problem. In these problems, some or all of the variables are constrained to take integer values.
099   Formally, a mixed integer linear programming problem M can be defined as

104   be optimized. The vector c ∈ Rn is the objective coefficient vector, and A ∈ Rm×n and b ∈ Rm

      2.4      S INKHORN A LGORITHM
163
164   The Sinkhorn algorithm is commonly used to compute doubly stochastic matrices, which are non-
165   negative square matrices where both rows and columns sum to 1. (Sinkhorn & Knopp, 1967) stated
166   that a simple iterative method to approach the double stochastic matrix is to alternately rescale all
167   rows and all columns of A to sum to 1. Sinkhorn and Knopp presented this algorithm and analyzed
168   its convergence.
169   The Sinkhorn algorithm plays a crucial role in various fields such as machine learning, computer
170   vision, and natural language processing, and is frequently applied in graph matching (Wang et al.,
171   2019), image registration (Sander et al., 2022), and optimal transport problems (Groueix et al.,
172   2019). In terms of its functionality for computing doubly stochastic matrices, the Sinkhorn algorithm
173   can be used to accelerate matrix calculations and improve numerical stability.

282
      We introduce a novel confidence threshold-based regret greedy search method that leverages
283
      marginal probabilities as input. The core concept of this approach is to strategically fix the val-
284   ues of certain variables based on the marginal probabilities generated by a neural network. This
285   technique effectively reduces the problem sizes, consequently mitigating the exponential time com-
286   plexity bottleneck inherent in the solver. The comprehensive framework of the proposed method is
287   illustrated in Algorithm 2.
288

289
      with a value of 1 should, after neural network processing, exhibit marginal probabilities closer to 1,
290   and conversely, closer to 0. Leveraging this insight to reduce problem size and accelerate the solving
291   process, we employ a strategy to force certain binary variables to take specific values.

      5   C ONCLUSION
493
      This paper introduces SHARP, a novel framework that addresses the limitations of existing ap-
494
      proaches by integrating EGAT with Sinkhorn normalization to enhance the representation of both
495
      nodes and edges within MILP problems. This combination not only provides a richer and more
496   accurate representation of the problem space but also facilitates faster convergence and improved
497   stability during the training phase. Furthermore, the proposed adaptive variable assignment strat-
498   egy, which incorporates a confidence threshold-based greedy regret search method, significantly en-
499   hances the feasibility of the solutions generated by the model. However, the SHARP still has some
500   shortcomings, such as further integrating with the pos-search search algorithm to inversely optimize
501   the parameters. Nevertheless, the SHARP represents a significant step forward in the application of
502   machine learning techniques to MILP problems.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
703
704
      In general, ML-based algorithms typically follow two primary strategies: those that rely on branch
705   and bound algorithms for exact solutions, and those that use heuristic algorithms for approximate
706   solutions. Zhang et al. (2023) provides a comprehensive survey of solving MILP via machine learn-
707   ing.
708
709   A.1   B RANCH AND B OUND
710
711   As a famous topic in operation research, the branch and bound (BnB) algorithm (Land & Doig, 2010;
712   1960) aims to iteratively partition the solution space through branching and pruning until an exact
713   solution is built. These algorithms form the foundation of most modern MIP solvers (Gurobi Opti-
714   mization, 2022; Bolusani et al., 2024), typically alongside with cutting plane algorithms (Gomory,
715
      1960).
716   Most ML-based methods focus on locally improving the selection strategies within the BnB algo-
717   rithm, such as branching variable selection, node selection, and cutting plane selection. These meth-
718   ods frame the selection strategies as decision-making problems (Khalil et al., 2016; Balcan et al.,
719   2018) and develop heuristic selection rules by learning from extensive sets of training instances.
720   Branching variable selection has been drawn much attention due to its significant impact on BnB
721   performance. There has been developed various branching rules for variable selection, including
722   strong branching (SB) (Applegate et al., 1995), pseudo-cost (Bénichou et al., 1971), and hybrid
723   branching (Achterberg & Berthold, 2009). Recent approaches have employed imitation learning
724   (Ho & Ermon, 2016) to approximate these branching rules. Marcos Alvarez et al. (2014) and Khalil
725   et al. (2016) learned branching policies by imitating the strong branching rule. To mitigate the
726   complexity of feature calculation, Gasse et al. (2019) proposed encoding the MILP into a lossless
727
      bipartite graph, which serves as input to a graph convolutional network (GCN) (Kipf & Welling,
      2016). Building upon this bipartite graph model, Nair et al. (2020) employed a GCN to encode
728
      MIPs as bipartite graphs and train it to imitate an ADMM-based policy for branching. Gupta et al.
729
      (2020) introduced a hybrid architecture that combines GNN and MLP models. Deviating from the
730   bipartite representation, Ding et al. (2020) generated a tripartite graph from MIP formulation and
731   train a GCN for variable solution prediction. Additionally, Zarpellon et al. (2021) proposed a novel
732   neural network design that incorporates the global BnB tree state.
733
      Compared to the branching variable selection, other strategies receive less attention. He et al. (2014)
734
      employed imitation learning to train both node selection and node pruning policies, while Yilmaz &
735
      Yorke-Smith (2021) focused more on learning a policy to select only the most promising children of
736   a given node. And for cutting plane selection, Tang et al. (2020) introduced an MDP formulation and
737   trains a reinforcement learning agent using evolutionary strategies. Huang et al. (2022) proposed a
738   cut ranking method using neural networks in a multiple instance learning setting.
739
740   A.2   ML- BASED A PPROXIMATE MILP S OLVING
741
742   While BnB can solve MILP instances exactly, its NP-hard nature makes it impractical for large-
743   scale problems. Therefor, researchers have explored heuristic-based methods to find approximate
744   solutions efficiently.
745   One promising direction in this field is the application of machine learning to generate initial so-
746   lutions or partial assignments for MILP problems. Nair et al. (2020) introduced Neural Diving, a
747   learning-based approach that uses GCN to generate promising partial assignments for integer vari-
748   ables. They then trained an additional network called SelectiveNet (Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2019)
749   to determine which variable assignments to keep. Yoon (2022) simplified this framework by in-
750   troducing a confidence threshold concept. Building on these efforts, Han et al. (2022) proposed a
751   Predict-and-Search framework, incorporating a trust region-like algorithm to enhance feasibility by
752
      allowing the solver to modify certain fixed variables. In our paper, we primarily follow this line of
      research due to its promising potential.
753
754   Inspired by the success of Neural Diving, researchers have applied similar machine learning tech-
755   niques to enhance traditional heuristics for MILPs, particularly Large Neighborhood Search (LNS)
      and Feasibility Pump (FP). LNS iteratively explores neighborhoods of the current solution, while


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      FP alternates between finding rounded solutions to the continuous relaxation and nearby feasible in-
757   teger solutions. The effectiveness of these methods depends crucially on aspects like initial solution
758   generation and search strategy. For LNS, Song et al. (2020) leveraged the ideas from Neural Div-
759   ing and used imitation and reinforcement learning for neighborhood selection. Similarly, Sonnerat
760   et al. (2021) adapted the neural diving concept for both initial solution and neighborhood selection
761   in LNS. In the FP domain, Qi et al. (2021) employed reinforcement learning to guide non-integer
762   solution selection during the algorithm’s execution, further demonstrating the potential of machine
763   learning in improving MILP heuristics.
764   Recent studies have increasingly concentrated on developing predictive models to intelligently se-
765   lect and utilize existing MILP solvers or methodologies for optimal performance. For instance, Ding
766   et al. (2020) developed a prediction-based method to choose between heuristic algorithms and ex-
767   act branching approaches for specific variables. Similarly, Grover et al. (2018) proposed an online
768   learning framework using reinforcement learning to select predefined heuristics in CPLEX based on
769   instance features. Taking a data-driven approach, Xavier et al. (2021) trained a k-Nearest Neighbors
770   (KNN) model to predict redundant constraints, good initial feasible solutions, and promising affine
771
      subspaces for optimal solutions. Moreover, the concept of early stopping, widely employed in hy-
      perparameter optimization (Makarova et al., 2022), exhibits substantial promise for MILP solvers
772
      by predicting the optimal termination point of the BnB process.
