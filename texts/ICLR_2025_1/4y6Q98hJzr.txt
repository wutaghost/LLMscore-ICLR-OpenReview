009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Adapting Large Language Models (LLMs) to specialized domains like medicine
012            and law through domain continual pre-training has become the cutting-edge
013            method. However, contrary to our expectations of immediate gains, we’ve un-
014            covered a surprising phenomenon: a temporary performance drop at the start of
015            the process, followed by a performance recovery phrase. This drop is not only
016
               unexpected but remarkably consistent across different model sizes and domains,
               such as medical and law. To gain a deeper understanding of this issue, we in-
017
               troduce the concept of stability gap—borrowed from visual models dealing with
018
               new class classifications—to explain this initial drop in LLM performance. Based
019            on this concept, we hypothesize that the initial performance drop arises from in-
020            stability in the model’s general abilities, which we further validated through our
021            experiments. We further reveal that this initial instability is intricately tied to
022            training settings that involve distribution shifts. To address this initial instabil-
023            ity and enhance LLM performance within a fixed compute budget, we propose
024            one training strategy that reduces the instability by increasing the epoch number,
025            along with two data sampling strategies focused on data quality and corpus dis-
026            tribution. We conduct various experiments on Llama-family models to validate
027
               the effectiveness of our strategies in both medical and legal continual pre-training
               and instruction tuning. For example, our strategies improve the average medical
028
               task performance of the OpenLlama-3B model from 36.2% to 40.7% with only
029
               40% of the original training budget and enhance the average general task perfor-
030            mance without causing forgetting. Furthermore, we apply our strategies to contin-
031            ually pre-train and instruction-tune the Llama-3-8B model. The resulting model,
032            Llama-3-Physician, achieves the best medical performance among current open-
033            source models and performs comparably to or even better than GPT-4 on several
034            medical benchmarks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Continual pre-training is an important approach for LLMs to improve their performance in target
      domains (Huang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2023c), learn new topics and lan-
040
      guages (Jiang et al., 2024; Gupta et al., 2023), and even boost their general capabilities (Ibrahim
041
      et al., 2024). While extensive research has focused on understanding LLM mechanisms during pre-
042   training from scratch (Biderman et al., 2023a; Xue et al., 2024), far less attention has been given
043   to how LLMs behave during continual pre-training (Que et al., 2024). This gap in the literature is
044   particularly striking given the importance of continual pre-training in adapting models to new do-
045   mains and evolving knowledge. In this paper, we report a surprising phenomenon observed during
046   continual pre-training: rather than an immediate improvement, LLM performance on target domain
047   tasks initially declines in the early stages of training. Only after further training, when more data
048   is incorporated, does performance recover and eventually surpass that of the original model. We
049   consistently observe this performance pattern—a V-shaped curve—across various model scales and
050
      target domains, including both medical and legal fields. This counterintuitive finding challenges
      common assumptions about continual pre-training, where improvement is typically expected at ini-
051
      tial.
052
053   To explore the underlying mechanisms of this phenomenon, we draw inspiration from the concept
      of the stability gap (De Lange et al., 2022; Caccia et al., 2021), originally introduced in the context


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      of vision models in continual learning. The stability gap describes how a model’s performance on
055   previously learned tasks initially degrades when learning new tasks, before gradually recovering as
056   it adapts. Previous research attributes this initial drop to an imbalance between the model’s stability
057   gradient—its ability to maintain performance on prior tasks—and its plasticity gradient—the capac-
058   ity to adapt to new ones. Early in training, the model’s plasticity gradient dominates, leading to a
059   temporary performance decline. As training progresses, the stability gradient strengthens, allowing
060   performance to recover.
061   Applying this framework to LLMs, we hypothesize that the initial performance drop in continual
062   pre-training stems from a similarly insufficient stability gradient to preserve the model’s general
063   capabilities (e.g., instruction-following skills). Over time, as the plasticity gradient diminishes and
064   the stability gradient rises, task performance rebounds. Supporting this hypothesis, we observe
065   a similar V-shaped pattern in general-domain tasks, where initial declines give way to recovery.
066   Further analysis of weight updates throughout the training process provides additional evidence for
067   this interpretation.
068   But how can we harness it to optimize continual pre-training? Given a fixed computing budget,
069   we know that the stability gap causes inefficiency in continual pre-training as it delays perfor-
070   mance improvement. To address this, we propose three efficient continual pre-training strategies:

103   2   R ELATED WORK
104
105   Large language Models such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), Gemini (Team), and Llama (Touvron
106   et al., 2023a)), have billions of parameters and show strong performance on various basic natural
107   language tasks (Qin et al., 2023), human examination (Hendrycks et al., 2020b; Zhong et al., 2023),
      and agent-related tasks (Guo et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). Their success attracts


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      researchers to analyze LLMs’ learning properties during the pre-training process (Kaplan et al.,
109   2020; Biderman et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024a). Kaplan et al. (2020) finds the pre-training
110   scaling rule for model size and dataset size and then Hoffmann et al. (2022) proposes the Chinchilla
111   rule that claims the equal importance of the model size and the number of training tokens. Sorscher
112   et al. (2022) further claims that pruning low-quality data can improve the above neural scaling
113   laws. However, high-quality training tokens are limited and may be run out soon (Villalobos et al.,
114   2022). Thus, some researchers try to maximize the utilization of the existing corpus by training it
115   for multiple epochs (Muennighoff et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024). But they observe the performance
116   degradation (Hernandez et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2023; Hoffmann et al., 2022) after training 4 epochs.
117
118   Continual pre-training gradually becomes necessary for LLMs to expand their basic ability (Wu
119   et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2024; Zhuang et al., 2024), avoid outdated information (Jiang et al., 2024),
120   and become the domain expert (Huang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2023c; Nguyen
121   et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Yıldız et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024a). The domain corpus for con-
122   tinual pre-training can be collected by n-gram models (Muennighoff et al., 2024), heuristic rules
123
      designed by human experts (Chen et al., 2023c; Zhang et al., 2024c) or automatically identified by a
      LLM (Zhang et al., 2024c). For the continual pre-training techniques. Ke et al. (2023; 2022) focused
124
      on adding masks or adjusting the architecture of small Language models like RoBERT to protect
125
      the learned general knowledge. However, these techniques result in huge computational consump-
126   tion for LLMs. Recent studies (Gupta et al., 2023) show that learning rate re-warming can improve
127   LLMs’ downstream task performance and a stability gap appears when replaying the previous data.
128   Ibrahim et al. (2024) further claims that learning rate re-warming, re-decaying, and replay can make
129   the continual pre-training performance match the performance of fully re-training when continually
130   pre-training the English LLM on the German corpus. Other continual pre-training method studies
131   focus on selecting useful tokens (Lin et al., 2024), expanding MOE architecture (Chen et al., 2023a),
132   and knowledge distillation (Jin et al., 2021b).
133
134   Continual learning and the Stability Gap Continual learning aims to design methods that can
135   learn new knowledge without the catastrophic forgetting of previously learned knowledge (Kirk-
136   patrick et al., 2017; Van de Ven et al., 2022). To mitigate the forgetting problem when learning a new
137   task, replaying previous tasks’ data (Rolnick et al., 2019; Buzzega et al., 2020; Prabhu et al., 2020;
138   Buzzega et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2022) becomes the main approach. De Lange et al. (2022); Caccia
139   et al. (2021) further find that, although they conduct the replay approach, the vision model still first
140
      loses its performance stability in previous classification tasks ( the performance drops abruptly) and
      then gradually recovers. They call it the stability gap phenomenon. Different from them, we focus
141
      on the continual pre-training of the LLM and observe that both the LLM’s domain task performance
142
      and general ability suffer from the stability gap.

201   3.2     S TABILITY G AP : A CONCEPTUAL EXPLANATION FOR THE INITIAL PERFORMANCE DROP
202           AND THEN FOLLOWING RECOVERY.
203
204   The Stability Gap refers to the initial decline in a vision model’s performance on previous tasks
205   while learning a new task, followed by a subsequent improvement, even when data from the ear-
206   lier tasks is replayed. Lange et al. (2022) explains this by disentangling the model gradient G into
207   α-weighted plasticity and stability components: G = αGplasticity + (1 − α)Gstability , where Gplasticity
208
      focuses on learning the new task by minimizing its data loss, while Gstability seeks to maintain perfor-
      mance on previous tasks by keeping the loss of replay data low. They attribute the initial performance
209
      drop to the plasticity gradient exceeding the stability gradient to reduce new task loss, resulting in a
210
      failure to maintain performance on previous tasks. As performance declines, the stability gradient
211   strengthens, leading to a balance between gradients and eventual performance recovery.
212
213   Explanation of our observations Directly applying the concept of the stability gap to explain our
214   phenomenon is not feasible, as we do not replay the pretraining corpus. However, during domain-

255   4   E FFICIENT CONTINUAL PRETRAINING STRATEGIES FOR MITIGATING THE
256       STABILITY GAP
257
258   In this section, we propose three efficient continual pre-training strategies for reducing the above
259   stability gap problem. The training process and details follow those in the above section.
260
261   Strategy I: Continually pre-train the LLM on a corpus subset across multiple epochs rather
262   than the entire large corpus for a single epoch. The key insight is that a larger corpus demands
263   a high plasticity gradient for a longer period. In contrast, pretraining the LLM on a properly sized
264   random subset of the corpus across multiple epochs reduces the need for sustained high plasticity
265   after the first epoch and accelerates the rise of the stability gradient. In Figure 4(a), we observe
266   that this strategy leads to faster performance recovery. The LLM achieves peak performance at the
267   fourth epoch, consistent with previous studies (Xue et al., 2024).
268
269   Strategy II: Continually pre-train the LLM on the corpus subset with the highest quality. The
      performance of domain tasks also depends on the learned domain knowledge. Therefore, collecting

296
      mixture rate is a vital factor for the pre-training performance of large language models (LLMs) (Xie

297
      data’s mixture rate to construct the continual pretraining training subset, aiming to reduce the distri-
298
      bution gap and stabilize the instruction-following ability of the LLM during continual pre-training.
299   Specifically, for the OpenLlama model, we follow the Llama mixture rate (Touvron et al., 2023a) to
300   collect 5 billion tokens initially. We then replace the CC and C4 data (82% of the 5 billion tokens)
301   with medical tokens sampled from the highest quality 5 billion medical tokens (HQ-5b). There are
302   two ways to sample these medical tokens. The first method randomly samples the medical tokens
303   once to construct a fixed training corpus. We call this “rate-fixed-data-fixed”. The second method
304   randomly samples the medical tokens from the HQ-5b tokens for each epoch. We call this “rate-
305   fixed-data-dynamic”.
306   From Figure 4(b), we observe that the second method achieves a higher peak performance as it offers
307   a better trade-off between recovering performance and learning domain knowledge. Additionally,
308   our strategies further improve the average performance on general commonsense tasks, as shown
309   in Figure 4(c), and reduce the medical perplexity and the rate of relative weight update, as detailed
310   in Appendix D. We also investigate the effectiveness of our three strategies in the general continual
311   pre-training setting in Appendix E.

356
357   Impact of learning rate and training subset size To analyze the impact of training factors such
358   as learning rate and training subset size, we conducted a series of experiments, with details provided
359   in Appendix G. Our findings show that a learning rate that is too high leads to significant drops
360   in generalization ability, while a rate too low hampers the acquisition of new domain knowledge.
361   Additionally, using a subset that is too large (e.g., 10 billion tokens) introduces a stability gap and
362   slows performance. Conversely, a smaller subset yields better initial performance but leads to rapid
363
      overfitting in later epochs. Finally, we validate the optimal hyperparameter configuration for our
      experiments.

478   6    C ONCLUSION
479
480   Our paper explores the behavior of LLMs when continually pre-training them on a new domain’s
481   corpus and observes the stability gap, a phenomenon marked by a significant initial performance
482   drop followed by a slow recovery. We explain it from the view of plasticity and stability gradients
483   and then propose three strategies that effectively improve the LLM’s domain performance and reduce
484   computational costs by reducing the stability gap. Furthermore, we deploy our strategies on the
485   newest Llama-3-8B model, which achieves the strongest performance among open-source baselines
      of similar model scales and outperforms the closed-source GPT-3.5 model.

      Limitations and Potential impacts Ideally, knowing the pre-training data mixture could maxi-
487   mize the outcome of our method, but most strong open-source LLMs didn’t provide their training
488   data mixture. Our Llama-3-8B experiment shows we can still improve significantly in this scenario.
489   Due to limitations in computing resources, we plan to verify our conclusions and strategies on larger
490   LLMs in the future. Our strategies are designed to address the machine learning problem of the
491   stability gap, and we do not see any potential risks. The datasets and base models used in this paper
492   will be open-sourced. Although we do not consider our model to be ready for real-world medical
493   use in its current form, we are releasing it to the research community to promote work on large
494   language models for the medical domain and the safety of language models in medical applications.

867   For OpenLLaMa-3B, TinyLLaMa-1B, and Pythia-410m, we download them from their official web-
868
      site. For OpenLLaMa-3B and TinyLLaMa-1B LLMs, we continually pre-train them with the 50 bil-
      lion medical tokens we constructed in Sec. 4 for one epoch. For the high-quality medical reference
869
      file, we use the dataset ’wiki medical terms’ downloaded from the huggingface. For the high-quality
870
      legal reference file, we use the dataset ’Caselaw Access Project’ downloaded from the huggingface.
871   For the Pythia-410m LLM, we continually pre-train it with the 100 billion tokens randomly sampled
872   from the 2021-2022 subset of the RefinedWeb dataset. We consider this subset as the Pile dataset
873   only contains data before the year 2021 and then the tokens sampled from the 2021-2022 subset are
874   unseen for the Pythia-410m model. The pre-training code is based on the transformers. The task is
875   to predict the next token with a context size of 2048. The training is executed using 192 V100 GPUs.
876   We employ the AdamW optimizer with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95, a weight decay of 0.01, and a learning
877   rate of 3e-4. We use a cosine learning rate scheduler with a 0.1 warmup ratio for gradual adaptation
878   to training complexity and bf16 precision for computational efficiency. Gradient accumulation is
879
      set to 4 steps, and each training batch contains about 340 million tokens. We also add support for
      FlashAttention-2 (Dao, 2023) for more efficient inference and long-context decoding.
880
881   When deploying our strategies into the continual pretraining process, we use the same learning rate
882   schedule as the one used for pretraining. For baselines, we follow their setups in their official papers.

925
      MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022) is a large-scale and comprehensive dataset for multichoice (four-
      option) medical question answering. It is derived from real-world medical entrance exam questions
926
      (Indian AIIMS and NEET-PG) and consists of over 194,000 high-quality medical questions. These
927
      questions cover 2,400 healthcare topics and 21 medical subjects, exhibiting a wide range of topical
928   diversity. The average token length is 12.77.
929
930   MedQA (Jin et al., 2021a)is a multichoice question-answering dataset collected from the profes-
931
      sional medical board exam, the United States Medical License Exams (USMLE). It comprises
      12,723 questions sourced from a comprehensive collection of 18 English medical textbooks that
932
      have been extensively utilized by medical students and USMLE candidates. Questions in MedQA
933
      cover a wide range of topics in clinical medicine, necessitating responses with professional exper-
934   tise and complex multi-hop reasoning across multiple pieces of evidence. The average question and
935   option length is 116.6 and 3.5, respectively.
936
937
      MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020b) is a comprehensive multi-task language understanding test dataset
      that encompasses 57 tasks across various domains such as mathematics, history, computer science,
938
      law, etc. In our experiments, we specifically focus on a subset of medical reasoning-related tasks
939
      including clinical knowledge, college medicine, medical genetics, and professional medicine.
940
941   PubMedQA (Jin et al., 2019) is a biomedical question and answering dataset derived from PubMed
942   abstracts. It contains 1k expert annotated multi-choice question-and-answer samples based on
943
      211.3k PubMed articles. The task of PubMedQA is to provide answers to research questions with
      yes/no/maybe responses based on the corresponding abstracts. The average question and context
944
      length is 14.4 and 238.9, respectively.
945
946   HOC (Baker et al., 2016) is a classification task to decide the Hallmarks of Cancer (HOC) taxonomy
947   of the article based on its abstract. The input is an abstract text. There are 10 topics you will need
948   to decide whether the article is related to. Topics: sustaining proliferative signaling, evading growth
949
      suppressors, resisting cell death, enabling replicative immortality, inducing angiogenesis, activat-
      ing invasion and metastasis, genomic instability and mutation, tumor promoting inflammation, and
950
      cellular energetics, and avoiding immune destruction.
951
952   DDI 2023 (Segura-Bedmar et al., 2013) is a task to predict the relationship between the given head
953   entity labeled as @DRUG1andtailentitylabeledas@DRU G2 within a given sentence, this relation
954   which must be in (‘mechanism’, ‘effect’, ‘advice’, ‘int’, ’none’). mechanism: this type is used to
955
      annotate drug-drug interactions that are described by their pharmacokinetic mechanism. effect: this
      type is used to annotate drug-drug interactions describing an effect or a pharmacodynamic mech-
956
      anism. advice: this type is used when a recommendation or advice regarding a drug interaction is
957
      given. int: this type is used when a drug-drug interaction appears in the text without providing any
958   additional information. none: there are no drug-drug interactions.
959
960   BioNLI (Bastan et al., 2022) is a task to classify the relationship between the given medical premise
961
      and hypothesis into one of the following labels: entailment, contradiction, or neutral. This dataset
      contains abstracts from biomedical literature and mechanistic premises generated with nine different
962
      strategies.
963
964   MIMIC-CXR (Johnson et al., 2019) is a generation task that derives the impression from findings
965   in the radiology report.

998    consists of 70k multiple choice questions about grounded situations: each question comes from one
999    of two domains – activitynet or wikihow – with four answer choices about what might happen next
1000   in the scene. The correct answer is the (real) sentence for the next event; the three incorrect answers
1001   are adversarially generated and human-verified, so as to fool machines but not humans.
1002   OpenBookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018) is a new kind of question-answering dataset modeled after
1003   open-book exams for assessing human understanding of a subject. It consists of 5,957 multiple-
1004   choice elementary-level science questions (4,957 train, 500 dev, 500 test), which probe the under-
1005   standing of a small “book” of 1,326 core science facts and the application of these facts to novel
1006   situations.
1007   PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020) dataset introduces the task of physical commonsense reasoning and a cor-
1008   responding benchmark dataset Physical Interaction: Question Answering or PIQA. Physical com-
1009   monsense knowledge is a major challenge on the road to true AI-completeness, including robots that
1010   interact with the world and understand natural language. PIQA focuses on everyday situations with
1011   a preference for atypical solutions.

1014   which are designed for middle school and high school students. The dataset can serve as the training
1015   and test sets for machine comprehension.

1019   evidence for the correct answer is provided.

1155   0.9, β2 = 0.95, a weight decay of 0.01, and a learning rate of 3e-5. We use a cosine learning rate
1156   scheduler with a 0.1 warmup ratio for gradual adaptation to training complexity and bf16 precision
1157   for computational efficiency. Gradient accumulation is set to 12 steps, and each training batch
1158   contains about 340 million tokens. We also add support for FlashAttention-2 (Dao, 2023) for more
1159   efficient inference and long-context decoding.
