011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Predicting retrosynthesis routes is a fundamental challenge in chemistry, involv-
014            ing the design of a sequence of chemical reactions to synthesize a target molecule
015            from commercially available starting materials. With a rapidly growing interest in
016
               using large language models for planning, this work introduces an LLM-powered
               framework for multi-step retrosynthesis. Our framework employs molecular-
017
               similarity-based retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to generate an initial ret-
018
               rosynthesis route, which is then iteratively refined through expert feedback. The
019            use of molecular-similarity-based RAG improves reaction round-trip validity from
020            24.42% to 51.64% compared to GPT-4 with representative routes. With further
021            refinement, the validity increases to 89.81%, resulting in an overall route validity
022            of 79.5% with a perfect query success rate, comparable to traditional methods.
023            Our framework offers a flexible, customizable approach to retrosynthesis, and we
024            present a comprehensive analysis of the generated routes along with promising
025            future research directions in LLM-driven multi-step retrosynthesis.
026
027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028   In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in plan-
029   ning tasks across various domains, including robotics, entertainment, and scientific problem-solving
030   (Hu et al., 2024; Prasad et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023; Zhang & Lu, 2024; Tan et al., 2024; Zhou
031   et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024a; Shinn et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Trinh et al., 2024).
032   These approaches leverage LLMs with advanced architectures pretrained on vast datasets, employ
033   well-structured prompts and external expert tools, and integrate them into carefully designed sys-
034   tem pipelines to generate coherent plans and strategies. In many cases, LLMs have achieved results
035   comparable to, or even surpassing, traditional methods.
036   Finding a synthesis route for a given molecule is a pivotal challenge in chemistry with earliest efforts
037   traces back to the 1960s(Corey, 1967). Existing approaches address this from a retrosynthesis per-
038   spective by framing the problem as a navigating task through an AND-OR tree rooted on the target
039   molecule node, as illustrated in Figure A1, a process known as retrosynthesis planning, which will
040   be discussed in detail in later sections. Similar to planning in other domains, retrosynthesis requires
041   both high-level reasoning to guide the overall process and a deep understanding of chemistry to en-
042
      sure that each step involves feasible actions. Given the demonstrated success of LLMs in various
      fields, a compelling question arises: Can LLMs be effectively applied to discover retrosynthesis
043
      pathways for molecules?
044
045   In this work, we introduce a novel methodology for retrosynthesis route generation, where the
046   entire route is generated and refined holistically, rather than being iteratively expanded step-by-
047   step as in traditional approaches. This approach leverages the inherent ability of LLMs to gen-
048
      erate complex sequences, allowing for more flexibility and creativity in the initial prediction with
      guidance from reference routes provided by the molecular-similarity-based RAG module. By in-
049
      corporating RAG, our method guides LLMs to reference retrosynthesis routes from structurally
050
      similar molecules, providing a more informed basis for initial predictions. This is particularly
051   important, as structurally similar molecules often exhibit analogous reactivity patterns, making
052   them valuable references. We propose an iterative refinement framework, ensuring that the gen-
053   erated route can be adjusted and optimized as a whole. The iterative refinement process is
      driven by expert models, which offer targeted feedback to improve the accuracy and feasibility.

104   2.2   R ETROSYNTHESIS P LANNING
105
106   A retrosynthesis route Rt is a sequence of chemical reactions [r1 , r2 , . . . , rn ] designed to synthesize
107   a target molecule t. Each reaction must involve chemically feasible molecules, denoted as M ,
      and all intermediate materials used in the synthesis, denoted as I, must be synthesizable from a


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      set of commercially available building-block materials, denoted as B. Given the vast size of B
109   (23.1M molecules in eMolecules1 as of 2019), forward reasoning from B to t is inefficient. A
110   more commonly adopted methodology is planning-based, which begins with decomposing t with
111   a predicted reaction r1 to form I0 , where I0 is the set or reactants suggested by r1 . At the ith
112   step, planning-based approaches select molecules m ∈ Ii to decompose with predicted single-step
113   retrosynthesis reaction ri , thereby expanding both the set of reactions R and the set of intermediates
114   I. This iterative process stops when all intermediates i ∈ I are decomposed to molecules present in
115   B. This process is typically abstracted as navigating through an AND-OR tree, comprising selection,
116   expansion, and update phases, as illustrated in Figure A1.
117   In the planning-based paradigm, explicit value functions fV : M 7→ R, which map an intermediate
118   molecule to a score, are necessary to select the appropriate m ∈ I for further decomposition.
119
120   2.3       R ETHINKING M ULTI -S TEP R ETROSYNTHESIS
121
122   Our vision is to generate complete retrosynthesis routes, Rt , in a single pass, without relying on
123   the iterative selection-expansion phases typical of traditional methods. Although generated Rt may
124   not represent the final route, it can be iteratively refined based on user preferences or expert knowl-
125   edge as holistically, with the assistance of LLMs. This process allows for flexible adjustments, as
126
      modifications can be applied to a complete route rather than to partial sequences. In line with Strieth-
      Kalthoff et al. (2024)’s vision of utilising both experts knowledge and data-intensive models, this
127
      approach fosters a more dynamic, user-driven process while improving the reliability and quality of
128
      the retrosynthesis route by enabling direct user feedback and simplifying optimization.

      Pipeline Building on our vision of holistic retrosynthesis route generation, we propose a retrosyn-
133
      thesis route generation framework that employs molecular-similarity-based RAG to generate an ini-
134   tial, possibly flawed retrosynthesis route Rt0 . This route is then iteratively refined using feedback
135   from expert models and local knowledge databases to form the final prediction Rt . The overall
136   pipeline is illustrated in Figure 2, with pseudocode provided in Algorithm 1. Our approach com-
137   prises four key components: a molecular-fingerprint-based RAG module, an LLM-backed formatter
138   module, an expert-powered feedback module, and a local knowledge database module. These com-
139   ponents will be discussed in detail in the subsequent sections.
140
141   Molecular-similarity-based Retrieval-Augmented Generation Unlike RAG applications in the
142
      natural language domain where the similarity metric is calculated on the embedding of texts, we
      utilize the similarity of molecular fingerprints to find similar molecules and provide their synthe-
143
      sis routes to the LLM for an initial generation. More specifically, a vector database that uses the
144
      molecular fingerprint as embedding and Tanimoto similarity as a similarity metric is applied for the
145   retrieval. We begin by filtering out routes associated with the top five most similar molecules, each
146   having a Tanimoto similarity greater than 0.5 in the database; these are designated as the retrieved
147   routes. If no similar routes are found, we instead rely on representative routes. To identify these,
148   we calculate the route fingerprint as the sum of the reaction fingerprints, following the method pro-
149   posed by Schwaller et al. (2021). We then apply the Density-based spatial clustering of applications
150   with noise (DBSCAN) clustering algorithm (Ester et al., 1996) to these route fingerprints, gener-
151   ating five distinct reaction clusters. Within each cluster, the route corresponding to the molecule
152   nearest to the cluster centroid is selected as the representative route. Since directly restricting the
153
      output format may lead to performance degradation as suggested by Tam et al. (2024), we provide
      those reference routes as textual descriptions in the prompt as shown in Figure 3c. The textual de-
154
      scription is generated using a rule-based method, highlighting route connectivity information. A
155
      Chain-of-Thought(CoT)-like (Wei et al., 2023) prompt with reference routes and specific instruc-
156   tions to generate textual descriptions of routes is fed into the LLM to guide the generation, the
157   prompt template is included in Table A3.

220   Feedback from Experts The synthesis tree is evaluated by a series of expert models for feedback,
221   ranging from molecule-wise to reaction-wise, and finally route-wise. Specifically, LLM-generated
222   routes are first analyzed for SMILES validity and node availability. This is done using RDKit and
223   cross-referencing with the molecule-level availability database. Any invalid SMILES or unavailable
224   leaf nodes are flagged in the feedback. Next, forward reaction prediction models are applied to
225
      assess whether the proposed reactants can synthesize the target products. If the predicted reactions
      are infeasible, reaction-level database and retrosynthesis prediction models are employed to gener-
226
      ate suggestions for refining the LLM-generated routes. Route-wise feedback focuses on identifying
227
      disconnected synthesis pathways or incorrect final products. For forward reaction prediction, we
228   utilize MolecularTransformer (Schwaller et al., 2019), a template-free approach, and LocalTrans-
229   form (Chen & Jung, 2022), a template-free approach. Retrosynthesis predictions are performed
230   using LocalRetro (Chen & Jung, 2021), a template-based framework, and a one-step retrosynthesis
231   MLP model described in (Chen et al., 2020). Finally, a rule-based system integrates input from
232   local expert models to address various types of errors, as outlined in Table A2. Note that those ex-
233   pert models, while performing well on individual datasets, often fail to cope with out-of-distribution
234   datasets as pointed out by Yu et al. (2024b). Hence, we also build a web-based interface to take
235   advice from human experts as illustrated in Figure A3 While the framework has the capability to
236
      incorporate feedback from human experts, this feature has not been tested in our experiments.

      Local Knowledge Databases Following the feedback module, the local knowledge databases
238
      are organized into three levels of granularity: molecule level, reaction level, and route level. The
239   molecule-level database contains 23 million commercially available molecules. The reaction-level
240   database is initialized with all retrosynthesis reactions from the training dataset and is expanded us-
241   ing the results from retrosynthesis prediction models. The route database starts with all routes and
242   sub-routes from the training dataset and is further expanded by incorporating LLM-generated routes
243   or sub-routes once they have passed all expert scrutinies. Sub-routes refer to the synthesis pathways
244   of intermediate products.

250   • Dataset We perform experiments on retro* dataset introduced by Chen et al. (2020) and evaluate

253   • Baseline We select Retro* (Chen et al., 2020) and EG-MCTS (Hong et al., 2023) as our baselines.
254     We limit the number of expansions for baseline methods to 5 as only 5 suggested reactions are
255     given to LLMs and the iteration budget is set to 500 as in (Chen et al., 2020). We also fine-

257
        fine-tuning approach, more details on the fine-tuning process can be found in Table A6.
258   • Metrics We employ a diverse set of both text-based and chemistry-based metrics to evaluate the
259     quality of the generated retrosynthesis routes, considering their similarity to reference routes as
260     well as their chemical feasibility, including: 1) Query Success Rate, the percentage of instances
261     where a route is successfully generated for a target molecule, regardless of quality; 2) ROUGE
262
        (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation), introduced by Lin (2004), measures the
        overlap of textual elements between generated and reference outputs. Specifically, we report the
263
        ROUGE-1 score between the string representation of the ground truth retrosynthesis tree and the
264
        generated retrosynthesis tree, both formatted in JSON, as illustrated in Fig. 3d. 3) BLEU (Bilin-
265     gual Evaluation Understudy), proposed by Papineni et al. (2002), evaluates the precision of n-gram
266     overlaps between the generated and reference outputs. Similar to ROUGE, we calculate the BLEU
267     score using the string representations of the retrosynthesis trees. 4) Exact Match evaluates the per-
268     centage of instances where the generated retrosynthesis tree is identical to the reference tree. To

      5.1    R ETROSYNTHESIS P LANNING
464
465   In the early days, from the proposal of retrosynthesis by Corey (1967) in the 1960s to the first
466   decade of this century, chemists relied heavily on their expertise and rule-based methods to navigate
467   the complex space of chemical transformations. This approach, while effective for certain prob-
468   lems, was limited by the scope and scalability of human intuition and manually curated rules. The
469   landscape began to shift with the pioneering work of Segler et al. (2018), who introduced Monte
470   Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) augmented with neural networks. This method represented a significant
471
      breakthrough, using machine learning to predict reaction outcomes and guide the search process,
      marking one of the first instances where computational intelligence illuminated retrosynthetic plan-
472
      ning. Building on this foundation, several advanced algorithms have since been developed to explore
473
      the vast space of chemical transformations further. Hong et al. (2023) further improves the efficiency
474   and performance of MCTS by replacing rollouts with an experience-guided neural network. Retro*
475   (Chen et al., 2020) and RetroPrimeWang et al. (2021) employ a trained value function to steer the
476   expansion with A* algorithm (Hart et al., 1968). Schwaller et al. (2020) employs a hyper-graph
477   exploration strategy backed by forward reaction prediction models and synthesis difficulty scores.
478   Reinforcement learning-based methods, such as those proposed by Yuan et al. (2024); Yu et al.
479   (2022); Schreck et al. (2019), update the scoring function during self-play, allowing the model to
480   learn and improve its decision-making by simulating retrosynthetic pathways. updates the score
481   function during self-play. Yu et al. (2022) applied a goal-driven actor-critic reinforcement-learning
482
      agent to guide the expansion and Yuan et al. (2024) utilized a critic model of route quality based
      on yields. Aligned with previous efforts in route-aware retrosynthesis planning, Liu et al. (2023b)
483
      represented the current synthetic route as graphs to facilitate single-step retrosynthesis predictions.
484
485   Despite significant advancements in machine learning-based retrosynthesis planning, leading com-
      mercial software like SYNTHIA™ (formerly Chematica (Grzybowski et al., 2018)) continues to


                                                                     9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      integrate expert-encoded chemical rules with sophisticated algorithms. This approach underscores
487   the importance of combining human expertise with data-driven models to enhance the accuracy and
488   reliability of synthetic pathway predictions. As highlighted by Strieth-Kalthoff et al. (2024), the
489   future of retrosynthesis planning lies in the tight cooperation between data-intensive models and
490   human expertise, ensuring that computational predictions are both feasible and scalable.
491
492
493
      5.2   LLM FOR P LANNING
494
495
496
      Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized the way complex planning
      processes are approached on diverse tasks from internet browsing to robotics (Hu et al., 2024; Prasad
497
      et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023; Zhang & Lu, 2024; Tan et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024a;
498
      Shinn et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Trinh et al., 2024).
499
500   One diversion between LLMs and traditional planners is that LLMs may produce plans that include
501   impossible actions. To ground LLMs to the feasible action spaces, previous approaches provide
502
      available actions in different granularity as external tools to agent-like planners (Prasad et al., 2024;
      Hu et al., 2024) or as functions to code-style planner Sun et al. (2023); Zhang & Lu (2024). Some
503
      approaches (Liu et al., 2023a) also use LLMs as a format converter to translate actual problem text
504
      into sequences compatible with traditional planners and use them to solve the planning problem.
505
506   Another important designing factor in LLM planning is the utilization of feedback provided by
507   the underlying systems. Methods like Chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2023), Least-to-most (Zhou
508
      et al., 2023) generate plans in a single pass without taking any feedback. ReAct (Yao et al., 2023)
      decouples one planning step into a dedicated reasoning stage and an acting stage, where observations
509
      in previous steps are utilized in the reasoning stage of the current step. Adaplanner (Sun et al., 2023)
510
      further refines the entire plan with feedback on the execution results of the current plan from LLMs.
511
512   Albeit the rapid development of LLMs in the field of planning, Kambhampati et al. (2024) advo-
513   cated that LLMs themselves cannot perform planning due to the underlying n-gram-like generation
514
      mechanism. Instead, Kambhampati et al. (2024) suggested that LLMs can be utilized in the overall
      planning process as auxiliary parts like format translators, summarizers whereas the soundness of the
515
      entire planning pipeline is bounded by the experts (human beings or models) involved. Our method
516
      aligns with this philosophy where the local experts back the soundness of generated retrosynthesis
517   routes, and also with the philosophy suggested by Strieth-Kalthoff et al. (2024).
518

521   6     C ONCLUSION & F UTURE D IRECTIONS
522
523   In this work, we have introduced a new methodology for retrosynthesis route generation and pro-
524   posed a novel framework for leveraging LLMs with RAG and iterative refinement through expert
525   feedback. Our approach demonstrates the ability of LLMs to successfully generate retrosynthesis
526   routes with high query success rates and competitive route quality compared to traditional methods.
527   Notably, the iterative refinement process enhances the feasibility of generated routes, addressing the
528   challenges associated with round-trip validity and retrieval quality.
529   Despite promising results, challenges remain in balancing LLM generalization with domain-specific
530   chemical knowledge. Expanding the route database and enhancing feedback mechanisms could
531   improve performance and enable advanced expert systems with human-in-the-loop strategies. Our
532   experiments also indicate that instruction-based fine-tuning struggles to fully capture retrosynthesis
533   complexities, highlighting the need for improved tokenization or training objectives. Additionally,
534   the scalability of our approach is limited by latency of token generation. While we avoided sampling
535   for reproducibility, future work could explore sampling multiple routes in a single pass to address
536
      this limitation.
537   Overall, our findings suggest that LLMs hold significant potential for automating complex retrosyn-
538   thesis tasks, paving the way for more efficient and scalable approaches in chemical synthesis plan-
539   ning. Future directions will explore the integration of dynamic knowledge updates and optimization
      of LLM prompt structures to further enhance performance in out-of-distribution scenarios.

           A.6   C ASE A NALYSIS
1019
1020
1021       We present three case studies: where the LLM generates a round-trip valid route while Retro* fails
1022
           to (1) generate a valid route or (2) even provide a prediction, and (3) where Retro* generates a
           round-trip valid route while the LLM fails to generate a valid route, as illustrated in Figure A2.
1023
1024       In the first case, Retro* may fail to generate a round-trip valid route due to an invalid reaction in one
1025       step of the retrosynthesis process, as shown in Figure A2a. In contrast, the LLM corrects its initial
           prediction in the first trial and successfully generates a fully valid round-trip route.


                                                              19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       It is also possible for Retro* to exhaust its entire iteration budget (500 iterations), as demonstrated
1027   in Figure A2b. In this case, Retro* spends excessive resources on subroutes that lead to dead ends,
1028   such as ‘ClP(Cl)(Cl)(Cl)Cl’ and ‘O=S(Cl)Cl’, for which no expert can provide any valid single-step
1029   retrosynthesis suggestion. However, the LLM overcomes this challenge by leveraging expert advice
1030   to generate a valid route. Upon manually examining the suggestions that contributed to the valid
1031   route, we found that all of them came from the same single retrosynthesis model used by Retro*.
1032   This suggests that the LLM can effectively select a single-step model leading to overall success,
1033   even without relying on an explicit value function.
1034   The LLM may also encounter dead ends, as shown in Figure A2c, where several attempts are made
1035   to synthesize ‘CCOC(=O)c1c(O)c2cc(Br)c(C)c(C)c2oc1=O’. However, it is worth noting that, com-
1036   pared to the 500 iteration budget used by Retro*, we only utilized 5 iterations to refine the route.
1037   With additional iterations, the issue may potentially be resolved.

1190   We extract synthesis routes from the Pistachio dataset after applying rule-based filtering to remove
1191   reactions that are either incomplete or uninformative. Following the approach of Chen et al. (2020),
1192   we construct the training dataset of routes using reactions from the training set, the testing dataset
1193   of routes using reactions from both the training and testing sets, and the validation dataset of routes
1194   using reactions from both the training and validation sets. We further remove duplicate routes in

1197
       filtering includes removing non-necessary reactants from the reactions (reactants with molecular
1198

1200   may not perform well on the new route dataset. We present the results on the Pistachio dataset in

1202   BLEU score. Upon careful examination, the low BLEU score is attributed to hallucinations by the
1203   LLM, which generate unwanted lengthy sequences for certain molecules. Additionally, our method
1204   achieves a higher exact match rate, primarily due to the overlapping reactions used in constructing
1205   different dataset splits. Lastly, we observe a comparable route validity between our approach and
