009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Navigation is a dynamic process that involves learning how to represent the en-
012                vironment, along with positions in and trajectories through it. Spatial navigation
013                skills vary significantly among individual humans. But what exactly differentiates
014                a good navigator from a bad one, or an easy-to-navigate path from a hard one,
015                is not well understood. Several studies have analysed exploration and naviga-
016                tion behaviour using static quantitative measures, like counts of positions visited
017                or distance travelled. These static measures, however, are inherently limited in
018                their ability to describe dynamic behaviors, providing a coarse quantification of
019
                   the navigation process. To fill this gap, we introduce the Flow Tree, a novel data
                   structure, which quantifies the dynamics of a group of trajectories through time.
020
                   This is a discrete adaptation of the Reeb graph, a mathematical structure from
021
                   topology, computed from multiple trajectories (from different people or the same
022                person over time). Each divergence in trajectory is captured as a node, encoding
023                the variability of the collection of trajectories. A Flow Tree encodes how diffi-
024                cult it will be to navigate a certain path for a group of humans. We apply the
025                Flow Tree to a behavioural dataset of 100 humans exploring and then navigating
026                a small, closed-form maze in virtual reality. In this paper we (1) describe what a
027                Flow Tree is and how to calculate it, (2) show that Flow Trees can be used to pre-
028                dict path difficulty more effectively than static metrics, and (3) demonstrate that a
029                trajectory through the Flow Tree is predictive of that individual’s success. We (4)
030
                   introduce a hypothesis testing framework over Flow Trees to quantitatively differ-
                   entiate between the strategies of the best navigators from those of worst. Thus, we
031
                   show that Flow Trees are a powerful tool to analyse dynamic trajectory data.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
036
037
      Navigation refers to the set of essential processes through which an individual adjusts one’s position
      and orientation in space, familiarises one’s self with new environments, and plans routes to specific
038
      destinations in familiar settings. Spatial navigation is a vital skill, as becoming disoriented or lost
039
      can lead to profound consequences.
040
041   At its core navigation is how agents move through spaces, both physical and abstract. It is a dynamic
042   process that involves working out where one is, deciding where one wants to go, and figuring out
043
      how to get from the one place to the other. This dynamic process relies on cognitive and neural
      representations of the environment, positions within it, and potential routes. However, in humans,
044
      navigation abilities vary wildly. What is it about the underlying representations that leads to such
045
      different outcomes?
046
047   Despite its importance, we know little about the underlying representations supporting navigation
048   and how they are acquired. Are they spatial grids, graphs, sequences of landmarks, or something
049
      else? Most current models rely on static representations (such as path averages or counts), yet real-
      world navigation involves a series of decisions made dynamically over time.
050
051   Flow Trees quantify the dynamics and variability of multiple trajectories over time, capturing diver-
052   gences to assess the difficulty of navigating a path for both individual and groups of humans.

074
      In navigation research, the main limitation is not the quantity of data, but our ability to extract
075
      meaningful insights from it. Tools like DeepLabCut allow researchers to precisely identify ani-
076
      mals’ behavioral positions (Mathis et al., 2018), while virtual reality technologies have expanded
077   our ability to gather rich navigation data for humans.
078
079   Most existing models rely on static representations, such as trajectory lengths (Richter & Duckham,
080
      2008) or heat maps (Coutrot et al., 2022; Brunec et al., 2023), which provide only a coarse and
      imprecise description of the highly dynamic process. As datasets become more complex, composed
081
      of much more behavioural data over much long periods of time, like in Ward et al. (2024), new
082
      analytical tools are required to capture the complexity of the dynamic process. Flow Trees provide a
083   complementary approach to testing scientific hypotheses in behavioural neuroscience, because they
084   encode some of the spatio-temporal complexity of navigation.
085
086
      In this paper, we (1) introduce the Flow Tree, a novel, dynamic way to analyse navigation. This data
      structure captures the complexity of a path by encoding the uniformity of group navigation decisions
087
      over time. We also show that this structure has predictive power, for both (2) the difficulty of a path
088
      for a group of individuals and (3) predicting an individual’s success from their trajectory through the
089   Flow Tree. Finally, we (4) propose and apply a hypothesis-testing scheme that uses Flow Trees to
090   quantitatively compare different people, paths, and strategies. This allows us to move beyond static
091   models and analyse the fluid, decision-making process of navigation more effectively.

096   2.1   M AZE NAVIGATION
097
098   This paper applies Flow Trees to behavioural analysis of navigation through discrete mazes. Brunec
099
      et al. (2023) collected a dataset of human navigation in a virtual-reality maze to explore how explo-
      ration behaviours affect cognitive map formation. They found that spending time in well-connected
100
      areas was predictive of success, but overall coverage was not. Gagnon et al. (2016) conducted a
101
      study where participants must navigate a virtual world to find a fixed set of objects as quickly as
102   possible. They found that performance was linked to spatial memory ability. In a follow-up study
103   Gagnon et al. (2018) found gender differences in free exploration in terms of navigational efficiency
104   and location visit counts. Ward et al. (2024) looked at another virtual navigation task, split into
105   exploration and then test phases. They investigated how 11 different exploration metrics, along
106   with survey data, might affect how accurate and efficient individuals are in the test phase. Dynamic
107   frameworks like the Flow Tree provide an additional strategy to encode and analyse behaviour over
      time.

      2.2    N EUROSCIENCE AND NAVIGATION
109
110   Research into the neural basis of navigation seeks to understand how the brain creates and then uses
111   spatial representations, like cognitive maps (Whittington et al., 2022), in order to guide behaviour.
112   Navigation behaviour is supported by a distributed neural network, which integrates spatial, sensory
113   and motor information (Taube, 2007). The dynamic nature of navigation—learning new spaces, re-
114   quiring a continuous feedback loop to factor in environmental changes, and accounting for both past
115   and future states—makes neural representation incredibly complex. In order to fully understand how
116
      neural systems produce behaviour, we must find a way to link dynamic models of neural systems
      with models of behaviour.
117
118
119   2.3    R EEB G RAPHS
120
      Navigation behaviours cannot be adequately explained with Euclidean distances and angles alone
121
      (Warren, 2019). Cognitive maps may be best modelled as labelled graphs, with local and approxi-
122
      mate metric information (Chrastil & Warren, 2014; Warren et al., 2017).
123
124   The Flow Tree is a directional variant of another type of graph, a Reeb graph. This is a data structure
125   in topology that represents how the level sets of a scalar function over a manifold evolve (Reeb,
126
      1946). The nodes of a Reeb graph correspond to places where components of the level set split or
      merge, and its edges correspond to the continuous components between those points. These have
127
      been applied to shape analysis (Biasotti et al., 2008) and data skeletonisation (Ge et al., 2011).
128
      Recently, Reeb-graph-based methods have also been applied to analyse spatiotemporal data, such as
129   motion tracking and trajectory analysis. For example, Zhang et al. (2024) proposed a Reeb-graph
130   based model to identify anomalies in a dataset of human GPS trajectories. Similarly, Flow Trees
131   track how groups of trajectories evolve over time, but they only encode when groups split, ignoring
132   when they merge again. That there are no cycles helps to encode time in the tree, where moving
133   down the tree from root to leaves means moving forward in time.

      3     M ETHODS
136
137
      We introduce the Flow Tree to study navigation data (see Figure 2). This is computed from collection
138
      of a group of people’s attempts to navigate from a starting location to a common goal. Once built, we
139   use Flow Trees to extract new insights from navigation data, about the difficulty of a navigation task
140   for the group, as well as an individual’s relative performance. To this aim, we introduce exploratory
141   and inferential statistics, along with machine learning approaches, for Flow Trees. We consider
142   three complementary perspectives: extracting features from the trees, embedding the trees in a latent
143   space, and studying the shapes of the trees.

      We compute a Flow Tree for a certain navigation goal or path (i.e., to get from point A to B) by
163   looking at a collection of attempts, or trajectories. This Flow Tree represents how well a group
164   sticks together in space through time, as they navigate, because it contains information about a
165   group’s cohesiveness and therefore the path’s difficulty. An easy-to-navigate path creates a simpler
166   Flow Tree, because any given group makes more similar decisions, as individuals make fewer errors.
167   A Flow Tree is an attributed tree.
168
      Definition 1 (Graphs and Trees). A graph is an ordered pair G = (V, E) where V , a set of nodes
169   and E ⊆ {{x, y} | x, y ∈ V and x ̸= y}, a set of edges, which are unordered pairs of nodes. A tree
170   T = (VT , ET , r) is a graph in which any two nodes are connected by exactly one path, and where
171   one node r is designated as the root. The edges of the tree are then assigned a natural orientation,
172   going away from the root r. An attributed tree is a tree T with an attribute map a : ET 7→ R
173   which assigns an attribute a(e) to each edge e ∈ ET , for example, an edge weight. A tree can be
174   equivalently represented by its adjacency matrix, defined as the square VT × VT matrix A such that
175   the element Aij is a(e) when there is an edge from vertex i to vertex j, and zero otherwise.
176
177
      To compute this (pseudocode in Appendix C, Algorithm 1), we select a start and end node (e.g.,
      A and B in Figure 2). Then we collect a group of people’s trajectories as they attempt to navigate
178
      from start to end. The start node must be the same for every trajectory in the collection, whereas the
179
      trajectories’ end nodes can vary. Because all trajectories start in the same place, the group starts as
180   one at the root node of the Flow Tree. From here, a Flow Tree node is added at points when the group
181   splits (i.e., when people in the group make different decisions). In this example, the second tree node
182   would happen at the third step in the trajectory, when the first three people (red, orange, yellow) go
183   from H to S and the other two (green, blue) go from H to Q. The Flow Tree is labelled with the last
184   point at which the group’s decision was together (i.e., H). A leaf node is added when a trajectory or
185   group of trajectories ends. The edges of the tree are weighted by the fraction of the original group of
186   people who belong to the group making that decision / passing along that trajectory. This means the
187   edge from A to H has a weight of 1, the edge from H to D a weight of 53 and from H to Q a weight
188   of 25 .
189   Definition 2 (Flow Tree). Let be given a graph G = (V, E) representing a navigation map where
190   intersections are nodes in V and corridors are edges in E. Take A, B ∈ V two nodes in the map
191   and consider M trajectories going from A to B. The Flow Tree is defined as the attributed tree
192   T = (VT , ET , r, a) with root r = A, node set VT ⊂ V and edge set ET built according to the
193   procedure described above, and attribute map a : ET 7→ R assigning the number of trajectories
194   going through each edge eT ∈ ET .
195
196   Because Flow Trees are only concerned with group splittings, they are less sensitive to path length
197
      and net group size (i.e., related more to the topology). (Another collection of paths that would
      generate an equivalent Flow Tree to {ABDG, ABCI, ABCI} would be {AXYZBDG, AXYZBCI,
198
      AXYZBCI}.) If the path is very easy and the group’s navigation decisions are uniform the Flow
199
      Tree will be very simple. If the path is complicated, or the group makes random decisions, the
200   Flow Tree will be complex. Longer paths do offer more of a chance for a group to fracture, and a
201   smaller group will give a coarser Flow Tree and therefore a less statistically robust estimate of the
202   path complexity, but the high-level pattern should be the same.
203
      Navigating a path involves making a series of decisions over time, moving in a direction in both
204
      space and time. The tree structure of Flow Trees encodes the dynamics inherent in this. Each depth
205
      level in the Flow Tree is a new level in time. Decisions made earlier in the navigation are encoded
206   closer to the root Flow Tree node. Navigation can involve loops in space (particularly if one is lost),
207   but Flow Trees permit no cycles. Creating a Flow Tree, therefore, effectively unrolls the trajectories
208   to create branches flowing from root to leaves. Static spatial representations, like heat maps, might
209   show uniformity of group navigation in space, but it collapses over the time dimension, effectively
210   ignoring the sequential nature of the navigation process. Critically, Flow Trees allow us to consider
211   the temporal dynamics of group travel.

214
215   Computing a Flow Tree involves iterating over a group trajectories, each of which goes through a
      sequence of nodes.

      Proposition 1 (Time and Space Complexity). Consider a Flow Tree built from M trajectories, the
217   longest of which traverses N nodes.
218
219           • The worst-case time complexity of the tree generation is O(N ∗ M ).
220
221           • The worst-case space complexity of tree is O(M ).
222
      For our recursive implementation, the worst-case time complexity is O(N ∗ M ). To compute the
223
      Flow Tree we loop over each node in the trajectory, so up to depth N . At each step, we loop through
224
      each trajectory to check whether all decisions taken at that node are identical or not. Using hashing
225   this takes O(M ). Creating a node and edge in the Flow tree is O(1). When the group splits, we
226   still iterate through each node (just in separate groups); it might be one has M − x and the other x
227   trajectories, which will give O(M − x + x) = O(M ) for each of the N steps.
228
      The worst-case for space complexity is when the group bifurcates at each of the N steps in the M
229
      trajectories. The tree will start with 1 root node and end up with M different leaf nodes. To make
230
      this simpler, let’s assume M is a power of 2 and N = log2 M (longer trajectories will not matter for
231   space, as it is P
                      differing decisions among people that create Flow Tree nodes). The total number of
232   nodes then is 0≤n≤N M/2n = 1 + 2 + 4 + ... + M             M

      3.2     U SING F LOW T REES TO ANSWER SCIENTIFIC QUESTIONS
235
236   Flow Trees are powerful tools for analysing navigation data. They can be used to better understand
237   and quantify why some paths are more difficult than others, dynamically predict an individual’s
238   success, and quantify different group and exploration dynamics. In this subsection we describe
239   three different ways to extract information from them: features, embeddings, and shapes.
240
241   3.2.1    A NALYSE TREE FEATURES
242
243   As a first pass, we can use standard tree features to quantify them for purposes of statistics and
244   machine learning. The features that we consider are:
245
              • Number of Nodes and Edges: Nodes and edges are highly correlated. A higher num-
246
                ber of nodes indicates more frequent divergence in the group’s trajectories, as each node
247
                represents a decision point where trajectories split.
248
249           • Tree Depth: The maximum number of decision points along any trajectory, correspond-
250
                ing to the longest path from start to end. This is the maximum amount of times along
                a trajectory that different decisions were made, bounded above by the longest trajectory.
251
                A smaller depth compared to the ideal path length suggests consensus on certain correct
252
                decisions, indicating easier sections of the path.
253
254           • Average Node Degree: The average number of branches (edges) per node, measuring the
255             level of divergence at each decision point. A higher average node degree indicates more
256
                possible choices at each split, signaling greater variation in the group’s trajectory decisions.
                This depends on the maze’s structure (or the degree of the nodes in the maze).
257
258           • Diameter: The longest distance between two leaf nodes, representing the maximum di-
259             vergence between any two individuals’ trajectories. A larger diameter suggests a greater
260             spread of possible paths and perhaps more challenging navigation.
261           • Number of Leaves The total number of leaf nodes. Fewer leaves mean there are more
262             people who take the same exact trajectory as others.
263
              • Average Path Length The average distance from the root node to a leaf node, indicating
264             the typical number of decisions made before reaching an endpoint.
265
266   Then, we apply standard techniques for exploratory data analysis, inferential statistics, and machine
267   learning on these tree features. It is important to note that some features are discrete (e.g., number of
268   leaves), others continuous (e.g., average node degree), and that they have different units. Therefore,
269   in our analyses, we standardize the features and use methods capable of handling this diversity in
      data types. In practice, we use Python libraries such as scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011).

      3.2.2    A NALYSE TREE EMBEDDINGS
271
272   Another way to use trees quantitatively is to embed them in a high-dimensional latent space, such
273   that similar trees are close together in that space. Existing approaches of graph embeddings can be
274   applied to trees which are graphs without cycles. While there exists a diversity of graph-embedding
275   methods, our analysis will focus on the graph2vec algorithm (Narayanan et al., 2017). This embed-
276
      ding technique is trained in an unsupervised manner, is task agnostic, and resulting vectors in latent
      space capture structural equivalences between graphs.
277
278   Next we apply standard techniques for exploratory data analysis, inferential statistics, and machine
279   learning on these tree embeddings. It is important to note that the results of these analyses also
280   depend on the quality of the embedding model and the number of dimensions of the latent space.
281   A hyperparameter search is necessary. Additionally, results might be harder to interpret than results
282
      from the approach with the tree features. In practice, we use the Karate-Club Python package
      (Rozemberczki et al., 2020) for the graph embeddings and scikit-learn for the subsequent statistics
283
      and machine learning analyses (Pedregosa et al., 2011).
284
285
      3.2.3    A NALYSE TREE SHAPES
286
287   Tree features only capture a fraction of the information present in the Flow Tree. Embeddings
288   might also collapse information, and additionally depend on the choice of embedding algorithm and
289   dimension of latent space. Consequently, we introduce a third alternative that analyses the shapes of
290   the trees. This approach does not collapse any information about the Flow Tree except the indexing
291   of its nodes, which is information that does not hold scientific meaning. As a drawback, it comes at
292
      an added conceptual and computational complexity compared to the two previous methods.
293   We use the formalism introduced by Calissano et al. (2020) for shapes of graphs, but for the Flow
294   Trees. The shape of the Flow Tree is defined as the adjacency matrix of the Flow Tree after we
295   remove any information related to the indexing of the nodes. Mathematically, the shape [T ] of the
296   Flow Tree T = (VT , ET , r) with adjacency matrix A is defined as:
297                                        [T ] = {P AP T | P ∈ PN },                                      (1)
298
      where N = |VT | the number of nodes in the tree, and PN the set of permutation matrices. In other
299
      words, the shape of the tree T is the set [T ] of adjacency matrices that we obtain by re-indexing
300   the nodes of T . If we have a dataset of Flow Trees with different number of nodes, we pad the
301   smaller adjacency matrices with 0’s. This will allow us to compare matrices of similar sizes for the
302   subsequently statistics and machine learning analyses.
303
      Then, we apply techniques of exploratory data analysis, inferential statistics, and machine learning
304
      on these tree shapes. However, the tree shapes are complicated mathematical objects in that they
305
      are not vectors – in contrast to tree features and tree embeddings. Mathematically, the space of tree
306   shapes is not a vector space, but a so-called metric space, i.e., a set that has a notion of the distance
307   d([T ], [T ′ ]) between every pair of elements [T ], [T ′ ]. This distance is defined as followed:
308
309
                                      d([T ], [T ′ ]) = inf ∥A − P A′ P T ∥2 ,                             (2)
                                                     P ∈PN
310
      for A, A′ the adjacency matrices of T, T ′ respectively, and ∥.∥2 the Frobenius distance between
311   matrices. Intuitively, the distance between two tree shapes is defined as the minimum distance
312   between their adjacency matrices, while going over all possible combinations of node indexing
313   for the second tree. Equipped with a notion of distance, we apply statistics and machine learning
314   approaches to the space of tree shapes Calissano et al. (2020). In practice, we use the Python package
315   geomstats (Miolane et al., 2020) to compute the tree shapes and their distances. Then, we employ
316   machine learning and statistical analyses methods that solely rely on the notion of distances between
317   data points.

               358       4.3    M ACHINE L EARNING ON F LOW T REES
               359
               360       We show that Flow Trees are a powerful representation of navigation data for machine learning tasks.
               361
                         To demonstrate this, we consider the regression task of using Flow Trees to predict path difficulty
                         in three ways: using tree features, embeddings, and shapes. We compare them to two baselines:
               362
                         a set of semantically significant static features (Euclidean distance of the path, the distance along
               363
                         the minimal path, the average number of nodes, distance, and turns traversed by participants) and
               364       a heat map (counting how many times each node has been traversed by people in the group). We
               365       use linear regression when inputs belong to a vector space. Because shape is defined only using a
               366       distance metric, we use k-nearest-neighbors regression to compare across all representations. Using
               367       Linear Regression and KNN Regression (see Figure 4) we find that Flow Tree features are the best
               368       predictor of path difficulty.
               369
                         One difficulty for static metrics and heat maps is directionality. For instance, many more people
               370       successfully navigate from N to Y than they do from Y to N. A difference here will not be captured
               371       in path length, or number of nodes traversed. Instead, the difference lies in how those nodes are
               372       dynamically traversed. This may contribute to Flow Trees’ superior performance.

                        471           4.5   I NFERENTIAL S TATISTICS ON F LOW T REES
                        472
                        473           We define hypothesis tests to determine whether two groups differ in their Flow Trees. Then we
                        474           apply this to answer the following question: do Flow Trees computed from the trajectories of suc-
                        475
                                      cessful navigators differ from those of less successful ones? We chose this group split on purpose
                                      as a proof-of-concept in order to validate this method. The answer should be yes, because groups
                        476
                                      of more successful navigators should make more uniform and correct decisions, leading to simpler
                        477
                                      Flow Trees.
                        478
                        479
                        480
                                      Hypothesis-test definition Flow Trees are represented either as features, embeddings or shapes.
                                      The first two are vectors of scalars, but shapes do not have a quantitative value to represent them
                        481
                                      directly. They do, however, have well-defined distances between them. Therefore, we consider Flow
                        482
                                      Trees as elements of a metric space, where the distance metric is the shape distance (for tree shapes)
                        483           and the Euclidean distance (for tree features and tree embeddings).
                        484
                        485           Suppose that  the observed Flow Trees consist of independent samples T1X , . . . , Tm         X
                                                                                                                                        and
                                       T1Y , . . . , TnY divided into two groups, for example m Flow Trees formed from successful nav-

      igators and n from unsuccessful ones. Assume the number of individuals in each group are the same
487   to not inherently bias the complexity of the tree (larger groups of trajectories give the opportunity
488   for more split decisions). The objective is to test whether the Flow Trees are drawn from the same
489   distribution. The null hypothesis H0 states that the samples are generated from the same probability
490   distribution.

493   To perform the test, we define the test statistic D as the distance between the means of the groups.
494   On the observed trees, the test statistic for shapes takes the value Dobs given by:
495

497   where d is the distance metric of the metric space and µ̂ , µ̂ are the Fréchet means of group X
498   and Y respectively. In a metric space, the Fréchet mean is defined as the point that minimizes the
499
      sum of the squared distances to the data points (Fréchet, 1948). Thus, D only relies on the notion
      of distance and naturally applies to our general framework of metric spaces that encompasses the
500
      representation of Flow Trees as tree features, embeddings or shapes.
501
502   We can then apply a permutation test to the data (defined more formally in Appendix B. We reject
503   the null hypothesis H0 at significance level α if p < α. In that case, we conclude that the two
504   samples of Flow Trees are generated from statistically different distributions. Otherwise, we fail
505
      to reject the null hypothesis. This would mean that there is no significant difference between the
      means, and the groups’ Flow Trees are the same.

      Hypothesis-test application We first split the participants into two groups, based on their average
508
      accuracy during test trials. They we compute two Flow Trees for each start-end combination, yield-

510   these Flow Trees. The null hypothesis in this case is that all the Flow Trees are the same. We run a
511   permutation test looking separately at Flow Tree features (0.448, p = 0.002), embeddings (-4.875,
512   p = 0.002) and shapes (8.08, p < 0.001). We reject the null hypothesis in all cases, indicating as

      5   C ONCLUSION
516
517
      Flow Trees dynamically predict individual success; their features, embeddings and shapes predict of
518   path difficulty; and they can be used to test hypotheses about groups of people or trajectories.
519
520   Hypothesis testing is particularly interesting, because it allows one to ask questions about group
521
      navigational dynamics. Males are more successful at navigating than females (Munion et al., 2019).
      Are the strategies of males different from those of females? What about divisions according to
522
      video-game experience, age, or handedness? By controlling for overall accuracy, Flow Trees can
523
      help to uncover differences in the dynamics of group navigation, beyond just success rates.
524
525   Flow Trees can also represent an individual’s exploration strategy by collecting a set of trajectories
526   by chunking the decisions they make during a prolonged exploration phase. This lets one test hy-
527
      potheses like: exploration strategy changes after one has seen all of the objects, or the strategies of
      successful navigators differ substantially from those of less successful ones.
528
529   Broadly applicable across spatial navigation datasets–whether in discrete mazes or continuous
530   spaces–Flow Trees can also be applied to abstract graph spaces, like the internet or Wikipedia. Their
531   branching structure captures the complexity of paths, highlighting efficiency, node difficulty, and the
532   shared or divergent experiences of users. The Flow Tree’s geometry provides valuable insight into
533
      the structure of any navigable environment.
