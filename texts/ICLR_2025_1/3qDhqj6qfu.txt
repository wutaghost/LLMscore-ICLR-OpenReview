011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Tabular data is the most common type of data in real-life scenarios. In this
014            study, we propose the TabKANet model for tabular data modeling, which tar-
015
               gets the bottlenecks in learning from numerical content. We constructed a
               Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN) based Numerical Embedding Module and
016
               unified numerical and categorical features encoding within a Transformer archi-
017
               tecture. TabKANet has demonstrated stable and significantly superior perfor-
018            mance compared to Neural Networks (NNs) across multiple public datasets in
019            binary classification, multi-class classification, and regression tasks. Its perfor-
020            mance is comparable to or surpasses that of Gradient Boosted Decision Tree mod-
021            els (GBDTs). Our code is publicly available on GitHub: https://github.com/AI-
022            thpremed/TabKANet.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   The tabular dataset is data organized into rows and columns, consisting of typically continuous, cat-
028
      egorical, or ordered different features. It is the most commonly used and oldest data management
      model in building real-world businesses. At the same time, as the foundational data storage struc-
029
      ture of relational databases, tabular data has widespread applications in almost any field, including
030
      medicineI (Johnson et al., 2016; Ulmer et al., 2020), finance (Clements et al., 2020; Arun et al.,
031   2016), e-commerce (McMahan et al., 2013; Richardson et al., 2007), and more (Chandola et al.,
032   2009; Buczak & Guven, 2015).
033
034
      Despite recent advancements in neural network (NN) architectures for tabular data modeling, many
      still believe that the state-of-the-art techniques for performing tasks in tabular data, such as classi-
035
      fication or regression, are based on tree ensemble methods, like Gradient Boosted Decision Trees
036
      (GBDT) (Hollmann et al., 2022; Chen & Guestrin, 2016; Prokhorenkova et al., 2018). This perspec-
037   tive is rooted in the observation that tree-based ensemble models often exhibit competitive predictive
038   accuracy and much faster training speeds. This stands in stark contrast to widely researched fields
039   of AI such as computer vision and natural language processing, where NN have significantly out-
040   performed competing machine learning methods (Vaswani, 2017; Koroteev, 2021; OpenAI, 2023).
041
      Although there is an ongoing debate about whether Neural Networks outperform GBDTs on tabular
042
      data (McElfresh et al., 2024). We must recognize that there are the distinct advantages of NNs in
043   tabular data modeling:
044
045          • Potential for modeling large-scale complex tabular structures: Deep learning models may
046            demonstrate superior performance.
047          • Better scalability at both the input and output ends: This provides a foundation for multi-
048            modal applications.
049          • Self-supervised learning and pre-training schemes for tabular data offer greater potential
050            for NNs in tabular modeling.
051
052   Therefore, modeling tabular data with neural networks is worth studying. There are attempts to
053   utilize advanced neural networks like Transformer in tabular data modeling. More specifically, they
      have used Transformers to encode categorical items in tabular data (Huang et al., 2020), which we


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      believe is insufficient for they did not serve continuous numerical items in the same manner. In
055   real-world datasets, numerical items are crucially important. If only the categorical parts are en-
056   coded using Transformers and continuous numerical items are simply fused through concatenation,
057   it will lead to the unequal weighting of column items in model perception. We need a sensible
058   tool to achieve vector mapping of continuous numerical items at a comparable scale while ensuring
059   sufficient sensitivity to numerical distinctions.
060
      Recently, the introduction of Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN) (Liu et al., 2024b) has garnered
061   significant attention from the AI communities, quickly piquing the interest of researchers and prac-
062   titioners. A vital feature of the KAN is its ability to approximate functions of arbitrary complexity
063   by selecting appropriate activation functions and parameters. This feature offers neural networks
064   more flexible performance compared to Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) (Hornik et al., 1989). KAN
065   shows a natural affinity of continuous numerical values, making it possible to become a significant
066   component in processing the numerical features in tabular data.
067   In this study, we proposed the TabKANet, which conducted a targeted design for extracting numer-
068   ical information from table data based on KAN, unifying category features and numerical features
069   under the Transformers architecture. This is a new architecture designed for modeling tabular data,
070   providing robust performance and a clear business structure framework.
071
      We tested the performance of TabKANet, MLP (Hornik et al., 1989), KAN (Liu et al., 2024b),
072
      TabTransformer (Huang et al., 2020), TabNet (Arik & Pfister, 2021), XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin,
073   2016), and CatBoost (Prokhorenkova et al., 2018), across 12 widely used tabular tasks. TabKANet
074   demonstrated consistent performance improvements across all tasks compared to other NN mod-
075   els, especially when the performance disparity between NNs and GBDTs is more pronounced. Our
076   method achieved identical performance to the GBDT schemes in almost all datasets and even sur-
077   passed in multiple tasks. This indicates that our innovation in the KAN-based Numerical Embedding
078   Module greatly exceeds earlier NN models and fully captures the potential value of numerical fea-
079   tures in tabular data. It is worth noting that our work also emphasizes the combination of batch
080   normalization with KAN has superior performance advantages in mapping multiple continuous nu-
081
      merical information.

      2.1   TABULAR DATA M ODELLING WITH N EURAL N ETWORK
087
      Tabular data is the primary format of data in real-world machine learning applications. Until
088
      recently, these applications were mainly addressed using classical decision tree models, such as
089
      GBDT. The XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) and CatBoost (Prokhorenkova et al., 2018) are per-
090   formance leaders in tabular data modeling for years.
091
092   However, with the development of deep neural networks, the performance gap between NNs and
093
      traditional GBDT models in table data tasks has begun to narrow. Recent advancements, exem-
      plified by TabTransformer, have integrated transformers into tabular modeling approaches (Huang
094
      et al., 2020). TabNet employs a sequential attention mechanism to identify semantically significant
095
      feature subsets for processing at each decision point, drawing insights from decision tree method-
096   ologies (Arik & Pfister, 2021). TabPFN introduces a transformer architecture for in-context learning
097   to approximate Bayesian inference through pre-training, facilitating swift resolutions for small tab-
098   ular classification tasks without necessitating hyperparameter adjustments (Hollmann et al., 2022).
099   FT-Transformer improved the ability of tabular data modeling by numerical embedding through lin-
100   ear transformation (Gorishniy et al., 2021). Recent research suggests that GBDTs perform better in
101   handling datasets with skewed or heavy-tailed feature distributions than NNs, which may be due to
102   their ability to better adapt to the irregularity of the data (McElfresh et al., 2024).
103   In addition, some studies explore the application of NNs in other areas of tabular data modeling,
104   including few-shot learning, pretraining or imporve the performance by ensemble large language
105   models (Hegselmann et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2020; Harari & Katz, 2022; Bertsimas
106   et al., 2022; Nam et al., 2023; Ucar et al., 2021; Somepalli et al., 2021; Rubachev et al., 2022). There
107   are also research in applying generative models for improving tabular task performance (Li et al.,
      2024b; Liu et al., 2024a; Kotelnikov et al., 2023).

      2.2   KOLMOGOROV-A RNOLD N ETWORK
109
110   MLPs have been the fundamental component of neural networks (Hornik et al., 1989). They feature
111   a fully connected architecture that can approximate complex functions and possess expressive solid
112   power, making them widely popular in various applications. However, despite their popularity, the
113   MLP architecture also has some drawbacks. For instance, the activation functions are fixed. This
114   rigidity in the network may limit the model’s flexibility in capturing complex relationships within
115   the data, as it relies on predefined nonlinear functions.
116   Liu et al. introduced the KAN as a alternative to MLPs to address the limitations (Liu et al., 2024b).
117   Their research differs from previous studies because it recognizes the similarity between MLPs and
118   networks that employ the Kolmogorov-Arnold theorem. A notable feature of KANs is the absence
119   of traditional neural network weights. In KANs, each ”weight” is represented as a small function.
120   Unlike traditional NNs, where nodes apply fixed nonlinear activation functions, each edge in a KAN
121   is characterized by a learnable activation function. This architectural paradigm allows KANs to be
122
      more flexible and adaptive than traditional methods, potentially enabling them to model complex
      relationships within the data.
123
124   With the introduction of KAN, researchers have been exploring their application to address scientific
125   problems better, including time series forecasting, image segmentation, and hyperspectral image
126   classification (Li et al., 2024a; Genet & Inzirillo, 2024; Lobanov et al., 2024). Previous work has
127   also proposed to use the KAN network for tabular data modeling (Poeta et al., 2024). However, their
128
      method involves using the entire KAN model for table data classification and does not establish an
      excellent numerical feature embedding module, nor does it include Transformer architecture. This
129
      deficiency resulted in their work not fully demonstrating the potential of the KAN model in tabular
130
      data modeling.

      For the vast majority of tables, the table data inevitably contains both continuous numerical and
      categorical items. In a table with m categorical items and n numerical items, we will handle these
136
      two types of data separately. Let (x, y) denote a feature-target pair, where x ≡ {Xcat , Xnum }.
137
      The Xcat ∈ Rm denotes all the categorical features and Xnum ∈ Rn denotes all of the numerical
138   features. Let Xcat ≡ {x1 , x2 , . . . , xm } with each xi being a categorical feature, for i ∈ {1, . . . , m}.
139   Let Xnum ≡ {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn } with each xi being a numerical feature, for i ∈ {1, . . . , n}. To
140   achieve structural consistency of table data in neural networks, we need to first unify the dimensional
141   expressions of numerical and categorical columns.
142
      For category items, we encode all categories for each column, using methods such as One Hot
143
      Encoding or Label Encoding. For Xcat , the common practice is to map each categorical item to a
144   specific dimensional space through an embedding layer. This results in a matrix that represents the
145   categorical features:

      As mentioned in Sec.2.1, GBDTs outperform NNs in table modeling tasks because of the skewed or
      heavy-tailed features in table information. Current scientific research has not yet proposed a simple,
153
      stable, and universal numerical embedding module, which is an important bottleneck for NNs in
154
      table tasks (McElfresh et al., 2024). Therefore, improving the ability of NN in table modeling
155   requires designing better solutions to achieve numerical embedding.
156
157   In a Kolmogorov-Arnold Network, the concepts of inner functions and outer functions are at the
158
      core of the network architecture:
159   Inner functions ϕq,p are univariate functions that operate on input variables xp . They correspond to
160   the first layer of the network, responsible for transforming each input variable into an intermediate
161   representation. These functions deal with individual input features and output one or more values
      that will subsequently be used by the outer functions.

      Outer functions Φq are functions that operate on the outputs of the inner functions. They correspond
163   to the second layer of the network, responsible for summarizing and combining all intermediate
164   values generated by the inner functions to produce the final output. These functions perform a
165   weighted sum of the outputs of the inner functions to generate the final prediction results.
166
      Mathematically, a multivariate continuous function f can be represented as:

      In KAN, Basis splines (B-splines) are used to construct these inner and outer functions. B-splines
      are smooth functions defined piece-wise and are well-suited for approximating complex function
175
      shapes. By learning the control points of B-splines, KAN can adjust the shape of these functions to
176
      approximate the target function.

212   We have designed a KAN-based Nurmercal Embedding Module to better achieve feature extraction
213   of numerical information. Specifically, we made the following three core improvements:
214
215              1. Replacing Layer Normalization (LN) in tabular data modeling for numerical items with
                    Batch Normalization (BN).


                                                                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
            2. Employing the KAN network for numerical items feature integration and extraction.
217
218         3. Mapping both categorical and numerical items into matrices of the unified dimension to
219
               feed into the Transformer, thereby leveraging the self-attention mechanism for modeling
               all data equally.
220
221
      These improvements are based on the following considerations. Firstly, normalization for numerical
222   items is crucial, which is essential to avoid gradient explosion, especially with real-world data.
223   Previous work used LN to normalize numerical features (Huang et al., 2020). This is a subconscious
224   best solution, which is to distribute numerical information as much as possible based on its global
225   distribution. However, this may not be true. KAN also suffers from overfitting, and we need to
226   ensure sufficient training during the learning process while ensuring authenticity to alleviate the
227   skewed features. We have demonstrated the robust adaptability of BN through experiments in the 4.7
228   section, especially when combined with KAN, which can collect internal information on numerical
229   values to a greater extent. As mentioned earlier, KAN, as a theoretically more powerful model than
230
      MLP, mainly when used on the input side of data, can extract information and stabilize changes
      better. In this process, BN and KAN work together, where BN is primarily used to alleviate data
231
      skew and amplify numerical differences, while KAN is responsible for integrating information,
232
      stabilizing output, and extracting internal value.
233
234   Additionally, since we cannot pre-determine the contribution weights of each column in tabular data,
235   a pragmatic solution is to construct uniform representations for each column’s features. This allows
236
      for the development of a unified downstream model for effective learning. Employing a consistent
      Transformer architecture for learning after encoding the column features also offers a highly scalable
237
      framework for tabular data modeling. Another important task is that when using a KAN-based Nu-
238
      merical Embedding Module, multiple training rounds can result in varying numerical normalization
239   results due to the influence of BN. Repeatedly pairing numerical normalization results and category
240   features will bring additional training data. We integrate these data features in Transformer, which
241   is also important in significantly improving model performance.

463   Normalization for the extraction of numerical entries in tabular tasks (Huang et al., 2020). The
464   rationale behind this approach is that BN can better capture the intrinsic feature differences within
465   each numerical feature earlier in the learning process, thereby enhancing the model’s ability to learn
466
      the skewed or heavy-tailed features in the numerical features.
467   Building on the same TabKANet model, Table 8 illustrates the performance differences between
468   using LN and BN in binary classification tasks, with all models trained using a batch size of 128.
469   TabKANet with LN shows improvement over TabTransformer, especially in tasks heavily reliant on
470   numerical information such as BA and ON. However, when we employ BN for numerical normal-
471   ization, the performance gain becomes even more pronounced.
472   For tasks where the improvement in numerical feature extraction does not yield significant benefits,
473   such as the BL dataset, switching to BN for normalization does not diminish performance. This
474   suggests that our approach is not only effective but also robust across various types of tabular data.

      4.8   L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORKS
487
488   TabKANet has some potential limitations:
489
490         1. Since TabKANet’s inclusion of the KAN network, Transformer, and MLP, despite achiev-
491            ing good results, it would perform even better if more detailed structural adjustments could
492            be made to specific datasets or tasks.
493         2. Compared to traditional GBDT methods or GBDT-inspired methods (such as TabNet), ta-
494            ble modeling developed based on NN, demand a significant increase in training time and
495            hardware resources.
496         3. If some table tasks do not include numerical items or the contribution of numerical items
497            is small, our solution may not be effective.
498
499   For future work, we believe it can focus on the following aspects:
500
501         1. This study is entirely based on supervised learning, which proves the performance of our
502            proposed method in basic experiments. Semi-supervised and unsupervised pre-training
503            methods have the potential to further improve its performance.
504         2. For real world tasks, especially artificial intelligence modeling of industrial processes in-
505            volving large amounts of tabular data, it is necessary to provide guidance on model hy-
506            perparameters, structural scale size, and their relationship with tabular data through more
507            extensive experiments.
508         3. TabKANet has shown performance in our experiments which is close to or even exceeds
509            the GBDT methods, indicating the potential for building larger-scale table models for mul-
510            timodal information.
511
512   5     C ONCLUSIONS
513
514   In our study, we introduced TabKANet, a novel approach to table modeling that leverages a KAN-
515   based numerical embedding module. The impressive performance of our model has been validated
516   across a series of public tabular datasets, showcasing its advantages in terms of stability and ease
517   of implementation. TabKANet’s capability to effectively integrate information opens new proba-
518   bility for constructing intricate multimodal systems, potentially incorporating visual or language
519   models. We are optimistic that TabKANet will serve as a solid foundation for future developments
520   in table modeling, providing a versatile framework that can be expanded to address the challenges
521   of tomorrow’s data-driven landscape. Furthermore, the KAN-based numerical embedding module
522
      can be regarded as a flexible tool for enhancing the representation of numerical features in various
      applications.
