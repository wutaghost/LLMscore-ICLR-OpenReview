011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) and direct preference opti-
014            mization (DPO) are emerging and important techniques to align large language mod-
015            els (LLM) with human preference. However, the quality of RLHF and DPO training
016            is seriously compromised by Corrupted preference, reward Overoptimization, and
017            bias towards Verbosity. To our knowledge, most existing works tackle only one
018            of these important issues, and the few other works require much computation to
019
               estimate multiple reward models and lack theoretical guarantee of generalization
               ability. In this work, we propose RLHF-COV and DPO-COV algorithms that can
020
               simultaneously mitigate these three issues, in both offline and online settings. This
021
               ability is theoretically demonstrated by obtaining length-regularized generalization
022            error rates for our DPO-COV algorithms trained on corrupted data, which match
023            the best-known rates for simpler cases with clean data and without length regular-
024            ization. Moreover, our DPO-COV algorithm is simple to implement without reward
025            estimation, and is proved to be equivalent to our RLHF-COV algorithm, which
026            directly implies the equivalence between the vanilla RLHF and DPO algorithms.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has been widely used in robotics (Christiano
033
      et al., 2017; Bukharin et al., 2024), autonomous driving (Wang et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2024), large
      language models (LLM) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b; Rafailov et al., 2023), image and
034
      video generation (Wallace et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), etc. This work will focus
035
      on the application of RLHF to LLM alignment which makes LLM more helpful, honest, and harmless
036   (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b). LLM alignment has two critical steps. The first step is reward
037   modeling, which estimates the reward model that measures the quality of LLM responses, based on
038   human preference data. The second step is reinforcement learning (RL), which fine-tunes the LLM
039   policy to generate responses with an improved expected value of the learned reward (Ouyang et al.,
040   2022). Direct preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) further simplifies the standard
041   RLHF process by directly fine-tuning the optimal policy without reward estimation.
042   However, the LLM aligned by RLHF and DPO sometimes generates undesirable responses, due to
043   the corruption, overoptimization, and verbosity issues, as introduced below.
044
045
      Corruption. The quality of preference data is essential in RLHF and DPO. However, preference
      labels given by human may be corrupted due to inexperience, inattention, personal bias, unclear
046
      context, and even malicious falsification (Bukharin et al., 2024). For instance, when fine-tuning
047
      LLM for automated content moderation on social media, malicious annotators may mislabel harmful
048   contents like misinformation and hate speech as preferable, which misleads the LLM to generate
049   such harmful contents. Therefore, robustness of RLHF and DPO to such corruption is critical, but is
050   tackled by only a few recent works to our knowledge. For example, Cheng et al. (2024); Mandal et al.
051   (2024); Gao et al. (2024) use confidence-based data filtering. Ethayarajh et al. (2024) maximizes
052   the utility function defined based on the prospect theory of human decision making (Tversky and
053   Kahneman, 1992) to filter out noisy data. Coste et al. (2024); Rame et al. (2024) estimate an ensemble
      of rewards. The recently proposed robust RLHF and robust DPO approaches in (Bukharin et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2024) use noise modeling to automatically select the outliers and the estimated reward provably
055   converges to the true reward.
056
057   Overoptimization. RLHF and DPO may overoptimize the reward model, yielding LLM responses
058
      of high estimated reward but low actual quality (Gao et al., 2023; Casper et al., 2023). Various
      methods have been proposed to tackle such overoptimization issue (a.k.a. reward hacking). For
059
      example, Gao et al. (2023) uses larger reward model which significantly increases the computational
060
      cost of pretraining. Moskovitz et al. (2024) applies constraints to RLHF. The ΦPo method (Azar
061   et al., 2024) optimizes a general preference function. Eisenstein et al. (2024); Coste et al. (2024);
062   Rame et al. (2024); Fisch et al. (2024); Zhai et al. (2023) use an ensemble of estimated rewards.
063
064
      An emerging and popular strategy with provable generalization ability to solve overoptimization is
      to adopt a pessimistic (resp. an optimistic) approach for RLHF and DPO with offline (resp. online)
065
      data. Specifically, in the offline setting where only precollected offline preference data is available for
066
      training, there are many out-of-distribution samples about which we cannot obtain any information.
067   Therefore, Zhu et al. (2023; 2024); Liu et al. (2024c); Cen et al. (2024); Ji et al. (2024); Yang et al.
068   (2024); Huang et al. (2024); Xiong et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2024); Fisch et al. (2024) apply pessimistic
069   principle to RLHF or DPO which penalizes LLM from generating such unknown out-of-distribution
070   responses and thus to mitigate overoptimization. Such pessimism principle has also been used in
071   conventional offline RL (Xie et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2021; Rashidinejad et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022a;
072   Cheng et al., 2022). In contrast, in the online setting where online data can be collected from the
073   up-to-date policy during the training process, optimistic approaches have been used to encourage the
074   collection of unexplored samples to enrich data diversity in RLHF and DPO (Cen et al., 2024; Xie
075
      et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2024) as well as conventional RL (Wei
      et al., 2017; Zhong and Zhang, 2023; Liu et al., 2023a;b).
076
077   Verbosity. LLM aligned by vanilla RLHF and DPO is likely to prefer verbose but possibly low-quality
078   responses (Singhal et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Dong et al., 2024; Fisch et al.,
079   2024). Multiple methods have been used to tackle verbosity. For example, Shen et al. (2023); Chen
080   et al. (2024) disentangle length-related reward component. Guo et al. (2024) instructs the LLM
081
      to prefer concise response. Eisenstein et al. (2024); Fisch et al. (2024); Chakraborty et al. (2024)
      estimate an ensemble of reward models. Singhal et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024a); Dong et al. (2024);
082
      Park et al. (2024) use length penalty and similarly Meng et al. (2024) uses length normalization.
083
084   Our Motivation. However, to our knowledge, most existing works primarily tackle only one of
085   these three issues (corruption, overoptimization and verbosity). The only method to our knowledge
086   that has been used to tackle all these issues is to estimate an ensemble of reward models (Coste et al.,
087
      2024; Fisch et al., 2024; Eisenstein et al., 2024; Rame et al., 2024), which, however, requires much
      computation and lacks theoretical guarantee of generalization ability. Therefore, we are motivated to
088
      ask the following research question.
089
090
091         Q: Can we design RLHF and DPO algorithms that can solve corruption, overoptimiza-
092         tion and verbosity simultaneously with simple implementation and theoretical guarantee of
093
            generalization ability?
094
095
096   1.1     O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
097
098   We answer the above question affirmatively, by proposing RLHF-COV and DPO-COV algorithms
099
      that simultaneously mitigate Corruption, Overoptimization and Verbosity issues, in both offline
      and online settings. Specifically, we tackle Corruption by noise modeling, tackle Overoptimization
100
      by pessimistic and optimistic regularizers in the offline and online settings respectively, and tackle
101
      Verbosity by length regularizer. Our DPO-COV algorithms are almost as simple to implement as
102   the vanilla DPO algorithm without reward model estimation. We prove that our RLHF-COV and
103   DPO-COV are equivalent in the reward-induced policy space in both the offline and online settings.
104   Since our RLHF-COV and DPO-COV algorithms generalize the vanilla RLHF and DPO algorithms
105   respectively, our equivalence result implies that the vanilla RLHF and DPO algorithms are also
106   equivalent. Moreover, we obtain the length-regularized generalization error rates of our DPO-COV
107   algorithms on both offline and online datasets obtained from corrupted preference, and the rates match
      the existing results in the simple special case with clean dataset and without verbosity regularization.

      This theoretically demonstrates that our algorithms can simultaneously mitigate the Corruption,

111   In particular, the effect of noise modeling on the generalization error of learned policy for corrupted
112
      data has not been studied to our knowledge, which requires novel proof techniques. The true and
      estimated noise terms have very different effects on the generalization error, and thus have to be
113
      analyzed at different stages. To elaborate, the estimated noise has to be bounded before applying
114
      concentration inequality, such that this unbounded estimated noise term can be canceled out by the
115   noise regularizer. In contrast, the true noise has to be bounded after applying the concentration
116   inequality, since the concentration inequality bounds the distance between the true data distribution
117   (with the true noise term) and the estimated data distribution.

533   6     C ONCLUSION
534
535   We proposed RLHF-COV and DPO-COV algorithms that can simultaneously mitigate the Corruption,
536   Overoptimization and Verbosity issues, in both offline and online settings. This ability is theoretically
537   proved by length-regularized generalization analysis with corrupted data. In addition, we also proved
538   the equivalence of our proposed RLHF-COV and DPO-COV algorithms. In the future, this work
539   could be extended to account for various preferences among diverse human groups (Ramesh et al.,
      2024; Chakraborty et al., 2024).
