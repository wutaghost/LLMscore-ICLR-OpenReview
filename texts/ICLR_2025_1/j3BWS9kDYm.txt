                                                                 A BSTRACT
023
024
                    As wearable devices become more prevalent, understanding the user’s motion is
025
                    crucial for improving contextual AI systems. We introduce EgoLM, a versatile
026
                    framework designed for egocentric motion understanding using multi-modal data.
027                 EgoLM integrates the rich contextual information from egocentric videos and mo-
028                 tion sensors afforded by wearable devices. It also combines dense supervision
029                 signals from motion and language, leveraging the vast knowledge encoded in pre-
030                 trained large language models (LLMs). EgoLM models the joint distribution of
031                 egocentric motions and natural language using LLMs, conditioned on observa-
032                 tions from egocentric videos and motion sensors. It unifies a range of motion
033                 understanding tasks, including motion narration from video or motion data, as
034                 well as motion generation from text or sparse sensor data. Unique to wearable
035
                    devices, it also enables a novel task to generate text descriptions from sparse sen-
                    sors. Through extensive experiments, we validate the effectiveness of EgoLM in
036
                    addressing the challenges of under-constrained egocentric motion learning, and
037
                    demonstrate its capability as a generalist model through a variety of applications.

040   1       I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Smart wearable devices, such as Ray-Ban Meta (Meta, 2024) and Spectacles (Snap, 2024), offer
043   new opportunities for developing personal AI assistants by capturing the world from the user’s per-
044   spective. They provide real-time egocentric observations about the user’s environment, interactions,
045   and actions. On the other hand, large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
046   2023) encode such context through text in their latent space, which can be leveraged for common-
047   sense reasoning and human understanding. The fusion of egocentric perception and common-sense
048   reasoning presents a unique and exciting opportunity for advancing contextual AI research, among
049   which, egocentric motion understanding is an essential task (Plizzari et al., 2023).
050   However, a key challenge in utilizing egocentric perception is the lack of direct observations of the
051   wearer. Two types of observations are available from wearable devices, i.e., 1) egocentric videos
052   and 2) sparse motion sensors. Egocentric videos, captured by cameras mounted on smart glasses,
053   provide rich contextual information of the wearer’s environment and interactions. But the wearer’s
      body is rarely visible in the video, due to constrained camera mounting position and angle. Sparse

054
      motion sensors provide low-level kinematic motion of a few important body parts, i.e., head motions
055   from glasses and wrists movements from smart watches. However, they are insufficient to inform
056   the full body pose, especially lacking information of the lower body.
057
058
      Our insight is that these two types of indirect observations are complementary to each other.
      Egocentric videos can provide strong clues of the environment, and help disambiguate the lower
059
      body motion. For example, a laptop placed on an office table is a strong indication that the wearer
060
      is sitting rather than squatting. Sparse motion sensors, on the other hand, offer precise tracking of
061   important body parts, such as hand movements, which can help in scenarios where no body part is
062   visible in the video. For example, sparse motion sensors can differentiate between jumping jacks
063   and simple jumps, where egocentric video may appear identical.
064
      Another key challenge in egocentric human understanding is aligning motion and language rep-
065
      resentations, so that we can leverage the vast contextual knowledge embedded in LLMs to describe
066
      motion. While motion signals are continuous, low-level kinematic representations, natural language
067   consists of unstructured and discrete tokens. To bridge this gap, we treat motion as a form of lan-
068   guage. By tokenizing motion and repurposing a pre-trained LLM to model the joint distribution of
069   motion and language, we facilitate an effective alignment between these two distinct representations.
070
      With the above insights, we introduce EgoLM, a versatile framework for egocentric motion under-
071
      standing that leverages rich sensor observations and strong contextual understanding from LLMs.
072
      As shown in Fig. 1, EgoLM takes sparse motion sensor data and egocentric videos as inputs, and is
073   capable of generating motion and natural language as output. The framework unifies a range of mo-
074   tion understanding tasks, at both the kinematic and semantic levels. At the kinematic level, EgoLM
075   can perform motion tracking from three-point (Jiang et al., 2022) or one-point (Li et al., 2023) sensor
076   data, incorporating egocentric videos for disambiguation. At the semantic level, EgoLM can gener-
077   ate motion narration from various combinations of input modalities. More importantly, we highlight
078   a novel task of motion narration from three-points and egocentric videos, unique to AR use cases.
079   Compared with recent VLMs (Liu et al., 2023b;a), our approach tackles a more complex and chal-
080   lenging problem involving more modalities and tasks with greater disparities. In particular, both
081   our input modalities and output tasks encode information at varying levels of granularity. To tackle
082   it, we employ multi-modal multi-task joint training through instruction tuning. Multiple input
083   modalities are aligned to LLM latent space with rich contextual information, and interleaved be-
084   tween text instructions. Multi-task training exploits connections between tasks and benefits each
085   other. For instance, three-points motion tracking bridges the gap between sparse motion sensors and
086   natural languages, improving the performance of motion narration from three-points and videos.
087   To validate the proposed framework, we perform extensive experiments on a large-scale motion
088   dataset, Nymeria (Ma et al., 2024). Compared with previous dedicated motion tracking and under-
089   standing models, we show better performance in both tasks, under different combinations of input
090   modalities, proving EgoLM as a generalist model. Our contributions are summarized below.
091   1) We introduce a egocentric motion generalist model EgoLM, which integrates a variety of
092   motion understanding tasks at both kinematic and semantic levels. By leveraging large language
093   models (LLMs), we aim to enhance egocentric perception, thereby contributing to the advancement
094   of contextual AI research. 2) We address the challenge of under-constrained egocentric motion
095   learning by combining two complementary modalities, i.e., sparse motion sensors and egocentric
096   videos. This new paradigm enables two unique applications for AR use cases: motion tracking
097   and narration from sparse motion sensors and egocentric videos. 3) We employ multi-modal
098   multi-task joint training to bridge substantial gaps between modalities and tasks. Extensive
099
      experiments validate the effectiveness of this training strategy.

      The overview of EgoLM is demonstrated in Fig. 2. There are three key steps in EgoLM training.
145   In the first step, we train a motion VQ-VAE as the motion tokenizer (Sec. 3.2). The second step is
146   motion pre-training for motion distribution learning (Sec. 3.3). The last step is multi-modal multi-
147   task joint training to guide the model to perform various egocentric motion tasks (Sec. 3.4).

149   3.1     P RELIMINARIES
150
151   Language Model. Language models (LMs) model the distribution of natural languages. Recent
152   breakthroughs in LMs suggest the effectiveness of the transformer-based architecture (Vaswani et al.,
153   2017). A normal LM consists of three parts. The first is a codebook that stores the embeddings for
154   each text token. The second part is the transformer backbone that takes text embeddings as inputs.
155   Output features are mapped to probabilities of the next tokens by the third part, LM head.
156   Motion Representation. Human motions are represented as sequences of poses, global translations
157   and rotations defined on the root joint. Each frame of pose is represented by joint angles, defined
158   on a kinematic tree. For better learning of motion dynamics, we also include joint angle velocity in
159   the representation. To avoid the normalization of global translation, we use the translation velocity
160   Vtr ∈ R3 for each frame, which can be integrated back to global translations. To ease the regression
161   difficulty of rotation angles, we use 6D rotation representations (Hempel et al., 2022) for the root
      rotation Rtr ∈ R6 , root rotation velocity Rtrv ∈ R6 , joint angles Rtj ∈ R22×6 , and joint angle

184   processing motions with arbitrary lengths. The encoder embeds raw motion representation to latent
185   features f m = E(M ), where f m ∈ RT /r×c , M ∈ RT ×279 . r is the down-sample rate.

      As previously discussed, EgoLM addresses a more complex and challenging problem, involving
      multiple modalities and tasks with significant disparities. On the modality side, in addition to motion
246
      and natural languages, we need to integrate data from sparse motion sensors and egocentric videos,
247
      which capture information at varying levels of granularity. Furthermore, EgoLM approaches ego-
248   centric motion understanding tasks from both kinematic and semantic perspectives. To tackle the
249   challenge, we propose to employ multi-modal multi-task joint training to bridge the gaps between
250   modalities and uncover the inherent connections between tasks.
251
      Recent research on multi-modal LLMs has demonstrated that instruction tuning (Liu et al., 2023b;
252
      Achiam et al., 2023; cha, 2022; Zheng et al., 2023) effectively aligns different modalities and in-
253
      tegrates multiple tasks. In our approach, various modalities are encoded differently. For motions
254   and natural languages, both serve as inputs and outputs; thus, they are tokenized for auto-regressive
255   modeling. Sparse motion sensors and egocentric videos are used exclusively as inputs. It is more
256   efficient to encode these into continuous features that align with the LM latent space. Different
257   tasks are differentiated by text instructions. Specifically, the instruction template typically includes:
258   1) text instructions specifying the tasks to perform; 2) inputs relevant to the task; and 3) expected
259   outputs. Below, we provide two instruction examples for motion tracking and narration.

      Dataset. We use the Nymeria dataset (Ma et al., 2024) to train and validate our method. The dataset
      includes: a) full-body motions captured by the Xsens Mocap system (Roetenberg et al., 2009), b)
302
      egocentric videos recorded with Aria glasses (Somasundaram et al., 2023), and c) motion narrations
303
      by human annotators. Three-point 6-DoF poses are derived from ground truth joints for comparison
304   with prior work. The motion tracking training set comprises 147.89 hours of data, with a test set of
305   41.93 hours. For motion understanding, the training set includes 16, 673 segments (totaling 15.77
306   hours), while the test set contains 7, 468 segments (totaling 6.76 hours).
307
      Training Details. Motion VQ-VAE utilizes two codebooks, each containing 8, 192 entries with a
308
      code dimension of 64. The down-sample rate is set to r = 4. For motion tracking, all experiments
309
      use a window size of 60 frames (equivalent to 1 second), with random rotation augmentation applied
310   to the motions. We employ GPT-2 Medium (Radford et al., 2019) as the language backbone.
311
312   Evaluation Protocols. For motion tracking, we calculate joint position errors (for full, upper and
313
      lower body), joint angle errors (for full body and root joint). For motion narration, the outputs
      are natural languages. Therefore, we adopt NLP metrics, including BERT (Zhang et al., 2019),
314
      BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), and ROUGE (Lin, 2004) scores. For more details about the evaluation
315
      protocols, please kindly refer to the appendix.

399   4.3         M OTION NARRATION
400
401   Quantitative Results. We report the quantitative results of motion narration in Tab. 3. This task
402   involves three input modalities, i.e., three-points (“3pts”), motions, and egocentric videos (“Vid”).
403   with various combinations evaluated. We first compare EgoLM with two existing motion narration
404   methods that utilize motion as their sole input, i.e., TM2T (Guo et al., 2022b) and MotionGPT (Jiang
405
      et al., 2024). TM2T trains language generation from scratch and consequently exhibits poor perfor-
      mance. MotionGPT leverages a pre-trained T5 model (Raffel et al., 2020). EgoLM(M2T&T2M)
406
      outperforms these methods, benefiting from the scalability of its decoder-only architecture. When
407
      we combine egocentric videos with motion inputs (MV2T&T2M), we achieve the best overall per-
408   formance, as this combination offers comprehensive information for motion narration.
409
410   Using motion as input requires precise motion tracking, which is not always feasible, prompting us
411
      to explore sensor inputs instead. We tested two variants: three-points-only (TP2T) and egocentric
      videos only (V2T). The TP2T variant demonstrated a noticeable drop in performance compared to
412
      the motion-only version, as three-points provide limited information about body motion. Conversely,
413
      the V2T variant outperformed the motion-only version because egocentric videos capture relevant
414   environmental context for our motion narrations. This underscores the significance of egocentric
415   videos in understanding motion.
416
417
      We then evaluate our highlighted setup of combining three-points and egocentric videos for motion
      narration. There are three approaches to achieve this. The first involves integrating two existing
418
      setups: 1) three-points motion tracking and 2) motion-to-text generation (TPV2M + MV2T). This
419
      variant shows a slight performance drop compared to MV2T due to error accumulation and requires
420   a time-consuming two-pass inference. The second approach directly trains a three-points plus ego-
421   centric videos to text generation model (TPV2T) using our proposed multi-modal instruction tuning.
422   While this outperforms using only egocentric videos or motions, it still lags behind the MV2T vari-
423   ant due to missing lower body information. To address this, we propose joint training of four tasks
424   to establish connections between three-point poses and motion narrations, achieving optimal perfor-
425   mance in a single forward pass for this new task.

522   5     D ISCUSSION
523
524   We propose EgoLM, an egocentric motion generalist model, that empowers egocentric motion un-
525   derstanding using LLMs. To address the challenge of limited wearer observation in egocentric per-
526   ception, EgoLM integrates two complementary modalities to disambiguate the under-constrained
527   scenarios. We also introduce multi-modal multi-task joint training to bridge gaps between differ-
528   ent modalities and tasks, thereby implicitly connecting them and improving individual task perfor-
529   mance. We hope our exploration of the fusion between egocentric perception and LLMs will inspire
530
      future research in contextual AI.
531   Limitations. Firstly, our motion tokenizer uses VQ-VAE, which introduces reconstruction errors
532   and sets an upper bound for motion tracking performance. Additionally, during motion tracking
533   training, the loss is calculated on discrete motion tokens rather than raw representations, which
534   may further impact performance. Secondly, for motion narration, each egocentric video frame is
535   compressed by the CLIP encoder into a one-dimensional vector, making it difficult for the model to
536
      accurately identify the objects the person is interacting with. Furthermore, as commonly observed
      in language models (Ji et al., 2023), EgoLM also experiences the hallucination problem.
537
538   Potential Societal Impact. While contextual AI presents opportunities for efficiency and societal
539   advancement, the collection and analysis of human data may raise privacy concerns for users and
      those around them.

      A       I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
773
774
      A.1     AUTO - REGRESSIVE I NFERENCE FOR M OTION T RACKING
775
776   At inference time, motion understanding is the same as the language model inference. For motion
777   tracking, it usually requires online inference over a long period. With a language model, which is an
778   auto-regressive model, it is straight-forward to perform online motion tracking. As shown in Fig. 9,
779   firstly, an initialization over the first t frames of data is required. When the new data frame t + 1
780   comes in, the input conditions are updated accordingly. Then, it is not necessary to predict all the
781
      motion tokens from frame 2 to frame t + 1. We take the previously generated motion tokens from
      frame 2 to frame t as inputs and prompt the network to generate one more token for frame t + 1.
782
783
784
      A.2     E VALUATION M ETRICS
785
      For motion tracking, we use joint position errors and joint angle errors to evaluate the performance.
786   Specifically, for the joint position errors, we first align ground truth skeletons and generated skele-
787   tons by the head positions only by translation. Then full body, upper body and lower body joint po-
788   sition errors are calculated separately. Joint angle errors are calculated on full body and root joints.
789   For the evaluation of motion VQ-VAE in main paper Tab. 4, we apply widely adopted metrics for
790   motion regression, i.e., Mean Per-Joint Position Error (MPJPE) (Ionescu et al., 2013), Procrustes-
791   Aligned (PA-)MPJPE (Kanazawa et al., 2018), and joint position acceleration (ACCL) error. For the
792   motion understanding, we use standard NLP metrics, please kindly refer to corresponding papers
793   for more details.

797   B.1     T HREE -P OINTS M OTION T RACKING
798
799   We show four more visual examples of three-points motion tracking in Fig. 10, Fig. 11 and Fig. 12.
800   AvatarPoser (Jiang et al., 2022) and BoDiffusion (Castillo et al., 2023) are solid baselines that per-
801
      form well on easy walking cases, e.g., upper example in Fig. 11. For the workout sequence, i.e.,
      lower example in Fig. 12, even only given three points of upper body, the distribution of lower body
802
      motion can be collapsed and generate reasonable motions that matches the ground truth. In Fig. 12,
803
      we demonstrate the effectiveness of including egocentric videos as inputs. Without any environment
804   context, AvatarPoser and BoDiffusion often fail to distinguish standing and sitting down. We do not
805   assume the knowledge of the head height over the floor, meaning that the three-points positions are
806   normalized to the local coordinates of the first frame. Therefore, it is hard for baseline methods to
807   disambiguate certain scenarios. We propose to introduce contexts using egocentric videos, which
808   contains rich information about the environment and how the person is interacting with it. There-
809   fore, our model can generate the most accurate motions by utilizing these information. For more
      visualization of three-points motion tracking, please kindly refer to our supplementary videos.
