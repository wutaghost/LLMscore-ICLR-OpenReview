011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            With the explosive influence caused by the success of large language models
014            (LLM), there has been an extensive amount of recent work showing that foun-
015
               dation models can be used to solve a large variety of tasks. However, there is very
               limited work that shares insights on multi-agent planning. Multi-agent planning
016
               is different from other domains by combining the difficulty of multi-agent coor-
017
               dination and planning, and making it hard to leverage external tools to facilitate
018            the reasoning needed. In this paper, we focus on the problem of multi-agent path
019            finding (MAPF), which is also known as multi-robot route planning, and study the
020            performance of solving MAPF with LLMs. We first show the motivating success
021            of single-agent planning and multi-agent pathfinding in an empty room map with-
022            out obstacles, then the failure to plan on the harder room map and maze map of
023            the standard MAPF benchmark. We present our position on why directly solving
024            MAPF with LLMs has not been successful yet, and we use various experiments to
025            support our hypothesis. Based on our results, we discussed how researchers with
026
               different backgrounds could help with this problem from different perspectives.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      In the past year since ChatGPT came out, large language models (LLMs) have been shown to go
031   beyond strictly language-related tasks like translation, and to be a powerful tool in all kinds of do-
032   mains. Training with very rich and diverse datasets, LLMs incorporate a large variety of knowledge
033   and do not require fine-tuning before generating good solutions in many real-world applications.
034   Over time, recent studies have shown that combining LLMs with different ways of prompting can
035   help solve problems that have some aspects of reasoning, including examples from logical problems
036   Yao et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2023) to controlling a robot dog without finetuning Wang et al. (2023a).
037   Motivated by the success of LLMs, people are studying how well pre-trained models are in all
038   different kinds of domains. Some recent works studied the performance of LLMs on multi-agent
039   problems and showed LLM can also help multi-agent coordination Chen et al. (2023b); Agashe
040   et al. (2023). However, they barely cover multi-agent route planning and did not look specifically
041   into the difficulties in the domain. In this paper, we consider the problem of multi-agent path finding
042   (MAPF), also known as multi-agent route planning. MAPF is the problem of moving a group
043   of agents from their respective start locations to their goal locations without collisions. MAPF
044   can be used directly to formulate real-world applications like warehouse management Sharon et al.
045
      (2015); Han & Yu (2020), swarm control Li et al. (2020), among others. In a typical warehouse
      scenario, around a thousand warehouse robots (agents) could be running simultaneously in one
046
      warehouse room (scenario), and each agent needs to plan its path, which could be as long as a
047
      hundred timesteps. Previous methods for MAPF can be mainly classified into 1) classic methods like
048   heuristic search and SAT Sharon et al. (2015); Han & Yu (2020), and 2) learning-based approaches
049   that mostly use reinforcement learning Sartoretti et al. (2019). MAPF is a unique problem in multi-
050   agent coordination in that coordination is required within the planning, so it is highly challenging
051   to facilitate the reasoning of coordination by using LLMs to generate useful high-level guidance
052   combined with a low-level path planner to ensure the solution is valid, an approach that will be
053   parallel to this taken in the setting of overcooked Agashe et al. (2023). On the other hand, path
      planning is one of the easiest parts of planning, and constraints only concern obstacles and moving

068   In this paper, we propose to use an LLM directly to solve MAPF without any additional heuristic

075

077   are evolving extremely fast, and current failures may not apply to the next generation of LLMs,
078   we elaborate on the current failing scenarios and give our position on three aspects as a breakdown
079   of what kind of capability is currently missing in the LLM workflow, namely 1) the capability to
080   understand the scenario, 2) the context length limit, and 3) the reasoning capability. We incorporate
081   a list of experiments featuring various prominent prompt design alternatives, such as image-based
082   and text-only inputs, and with or without single-step observation information, among others. Finally,

      work can serve as a building block for future research in foundation models for MAPF.

087   2     LLM S FOR M ULTI - AGENT PATH F INDING
088
089   2.1   P RELIMINARY
090
091
      The multi-agent path finding (MAPF) problem is the problem of finding a set of collision-free paths
      for a set of agents in a known environment while minimizing their travel times. Specifically, in this
092
      paper, we consider the problem in Stern et al. (2019); Li et al. (2022), which is a four-connected grid
093
      map, where each agent is given a start cell and a goal cell. A scenario is defined as the combination
094   of the description of the map, indicating which cells have obstacles, and the start cells and goal cells
095   of each agent. At each timestep, an agent can move to an adjacent cell or stay in its current cell. A
096   collision happens if two agents end up in the same cell at the same timestep. Each agent remains
097   at its goal cell after it arrives until all agents arrive at their goals. The objective is to minimize the
098   makespan of the solution, i.e., minimize the time when the last agent arrives at its goal location.
099
      With the number of parameters of LLMs being exponentially larger than the degree of freedom in
100   small reasoning problems, LLMs have the potential to solve some easy problems in reasoning with
101   certain prompts Wei et al. (2022), and break down harder problems into smaller ones to get subop-
102   timal solutions Yang et al. (2023). When applying to MAPF, we hope LLMs can be an alternative
103   model to the current MAPF reinforcement learning-based models without any additional training.
104
105   2.2   M ETHODS
106
107   In this paper, we focus on using LLMs to solve the MAPF problem directly. However, it is obvious
      that current ML models cannot be perfect solvers in their first trial, and we introduce a high-level


                                                          2

108
      collision checker to ensure the plan generated by the LLM is valid. We inform the LLM about the
109   mistake in the current solution, if any. Unlike some previous works Yao et al. (2022); Yang et al.
110   (2023), our checker is not another LLM both because it is extremely easy and efficient to detect
111   collisions by a rule-based detector in linear time, and also because the LLM fails to always correctly
112   identify the collisions. A detailed comparison is provided in the appendix. In this checker, we not
113   only check for agent-to-agent collisions but also check for any collision with the fixed obstacles.
114   We do not provide any additional guidance on how the LLM should resolve this. By default, all
115   the information, like coordinates, is provided in text, and we will later discuss the performance
116   difference caused by different input formats.
117   Following the common practice of LLMs Kambhampati et al.; Chen et al. (2023b), we build our
118   workflow shown in Fig. 1. As existing learning-based approaches Damani et al. (2021), we give
119   LLMs stepwise local information and let the LLM choose the actions of agents step-by-step. This
120   step-by-step (SBS) generation is different from the popular chain-of-thoughts idea Wei et al. (2022)
121   used in LLMs by not introducing more intermediate reasoning processes in the generation. Instead,
122   it breaks down the whole planning task into smaller single-step tasks, so the LLM does not need
123
      to be fully correct before we can use some results from it. We start by giving the LLM the system
      prompt to become a solver for the MAPF problem. This part is fixed within each map, and different
124
      in the map description part in different maps. Then, we start the user prompt by providing the sce-
125
      nario information, which includes where the obstacles are, as well as the start location and the goal
126   locations of each agent. We found that providing LLMs with specific local information about viable
127   actions for the immediate next step significantly helps them avoid collisions with static obstacles,
128   so we provide a single-step observation (SSO) in the prompt by default. Then we start to read the
129   output of the LLM, use the checker to determine whether the output of the LLM is valid, and either
130   output the errors to let the LLM correct on the current step, or tell the LLM to move on to the next
131   step with the information that is specifically for the next step. To address the total token limit, we
132   leverage the fact that MAPF is a Markov decision process where each state is independent of its
133   previous states, and we restart the prompt from scratch, i.e., treat the current agent locations as their
134
      starting point, whenever we got a rate limit error. We provide all our prompts in the appendix.

149   standard MAPF benchmark Stern et al. (2019), each different in terms of the start and goal location

246   3       C AUSE OF FAILURES
247
248
      Because LLMs are evolving rapidly, it is unclear how long LLMs will still fail. Although our meth-
249   ods do not combine any external tools like low-level planning Agashe et al. (2023), it is important
250   for us to break down the failures and identify the cause of the failures. We observe that LLMs fail
251   only once with 16 agents in the empty map because of consecutive failures in a single step, and all
252   other times, they fail because they use many more steps than they should. More specifically, 77% of
253   the failures occurred because the LLM agents began to oscillate in a specific area of the map, while
254   the remaining failures were due to excessively long detours.
255   Based on our observations, we break down the reasons into three deeper parts from the perspective of
256   natural language models: limitation from the model, understanding, and reasoning. For simplicity,
257   we first discuss the lack of reasoning capability, which is common in many tasks.
258
259
260

262

263

264   standard normalization practice in MAPF as this provides a valid lower bound on the optimal MAPF
265   solution), and in scenarios that are successfully solved, this average is 1.5. This means that LLMs

268   they need path finding in complex environments. A simple example of the failure of path finding is

322   rejected. This is a long enough length for many tasks and even able to read a textbook, and many
323   users are satisfied with the length. Recent studies have demonstrated that the performance of large
      models, such as GPT-4 Turbo with 128K context length, is not consistent when processing inputs of

357   3.3      U NDERSTANDING O BSTACLE L OCATIONS
358
359   MAPF scenarios can be broken down into a pair of start location and goal locations, together with
360
      the map information. While understanding the coordinate version of start goal location pairs is
      relatively easy, understanding the map information is hard. Trained with publicly available text,
361
      LLM learns what a specific map means by finding similar contexts online, which mostly comes
362
      from other related fields in planning, like solving a maze. However, one problem is that people
363   barely provide any such information online since people have the common knowledge of what to do
364   with a map with code and preprocess the map information in the code rather than explicitly provide
365   the set of where the obstacles are as the original input. Therefore, this leads to a lack of training
366   data with related context about the information in the pictures or in a symbol-based input. As we
367   mentioned earlier, 77% of the overall failure is caused by agents detouring back and forth in a certain
368   area blocked by obstacles. These failures directly indicate that the LLM fails to understand the map
369   information provided.
370   Because of this, our main prompt included guidance on what action could be taken in the current
371   step to increase the success rate a little bit. In Table. 3, we find that while global observation can
372   achieve a high win rate with the support of our high-level checkers, it also increases the average
373   number of iterations per step. Furthermore, it fails in scenarios where SSO would be effective, as it
374   exceeds the step limits defined in Sec. 2.3.1. In general, LLMs fail in medium complexity maps like
375   ROOM , and completely fail in much harder maps like MAZE , even with the help of SSO.

      In all the experiments above, we use pure-text-based input because of the popularity and simplicity
379   of pure-text input. However, this was not the case before: In earlier work of reinforcement learning
380   for MAPF, people transformed the input into image-based input within the field of view and used
381   neural networks like convolutional neural network (CNN) to read the information. Therefore, as
382   shown in Fig. 5, we propose three different methods, which all share the same prompt of describing
383   the problem definition and use different versions of the prompt describing the scenario:
384
385         1. A multimodal-based method MM where we use an image to give the picture of the map,
386            and text to describe the problem and the scenario.
387         2. A text-only method TOO that describes the map by listing the coordinates of the obstacles.
388         3. A text-only method that describes the map by feeding the text version of the map with ’@’
389            denoting the obstacles and ’.’ denoting an empty space, named TOM.
390
391   We test the performance of these three variants, and show the results in Table. 5. We found that with
392   an image-based input, the results are even worse. For example, large models can only have a high-
393   level idea that there is a wall in the middle area, rather than knowing there is a wall exactly from
394   (3, 10) to (20, 10). The failure of TOO further indicates that LLMs do not know how to combine
395
      high-level intuitions of where obstacles are with concrete reasoning during planning. Overall, in
      complex environments such as mazes and real-world city maps, LLMs cannot offer any form of
396
      guidance, not necessarily the specific action recommendations tested in this paper, until they can
397
      accurately comprehend the relationship between obstacles and the paths of agents.
398
399
400   4   D ISCUSSION
401
402   We would like to note that the methods in this paper are not designed to either outperform the state-
403   of-the-art classic solvers or surpass the performance of learning-based solvers. Instead, the goal is
404   to demonstrate that LLMs can solve small problems simply through prompting and discussing what
405   is stopping them from solving larger scenarios. Specifically, current heuristc-based algorithms like
406
      conflict based search Sharon et al. (2015) can solve all the scenarios tested in the paper in less than
      0.1 second. Moving on, we believe the three reasons for failures can each independently relate to
407
      different research directions. Improving capability in a long context is already a popular direction in
408
      NLP, and many papers are also looking into improving the general reasoning capability. We believe
409   that much ongoing research has the opportunity to improve the performance of LLMs on MAPF,
410   and we are pleased to have more researchers test this unique problem. Also, the recent development
411   of building foundation models that combine language and image generation could potentially help
412   with the reasoning in MAPF. Image generation models like stable diffusion Rombach et al. (2022)
413   have shown to be a strong tool in single agent path finding Janner et al. (2022). While these diffusion
414   models are not designed for multi-agent planning and are even hard to apply to MAPF, strategically
415   using them could partially help the lack of reasoning capability. Regarding potential finetuning, we
416   believe more annotated data are needed to help foundation models connect the abstract description to
417
      concrete coordinate-level knowledge of where obstacles are, and how to coordinate between agents.
      Future research could finetune using their data, or at least open their data to the general public, and
418
      let the next version of released models from industry companies include such data by automatically
419
      getting data online. Hopefully, such data could help foundation models know that position encodings
420   are more important in planning problems. Besides, our results on current rule-based checker v.s.
421   human checker also indicate that future research could work on building a good checker, i.e., a
422   reward model in large language models, to improve the performance of LLMs.
423
      Besides, although the success rate is one of the most important factors in measuring the performance
424
      of a solver, there are also other obstacles that need to be solved before using LLMs in real-world
425
      scenarios. First of all, success does not indicate anything about the quality of the solution. In
426   our experiment, we found that the success scenarios usually come from easier scenarios that agents
427   mostly only need to follow their direct shortest path. When planning gets harder, the success rate gets
428   much worse. To improve the reasoning capability, future researchers and users could choose to make
429   a dataset of scenarios and their good solutions publicly available on the internet, so when training
430   with newer data, this specific problem could be directly included in the training set. Furthermore,
431   the current workflow does not include any heuristics or tool use during the generation but only as a
      solution checker. While we are unclear on how such things should be included, this will be a very


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      challenging but meaningful direction. For potential researchers going in this direction, we want to
433   remind them to keep looking at the consistency of the deconflict of agents, which could be seen as
434   moving obstacles, and the deconflict of the fixed obstacles on the map. A successful method should
435   consider both difficulties in a similar manner.
436
      On the other hand, latency is always a problem for LLMs when used in real-world production. If one
437
      wants to use their own models, which is currently 500 tokens per second after using state-of-the-
438
      art system-wise optimization like vLLM Kwon et al. (2023) for LLAMA-2 Touvron et al. (2023).
439   However, this speed is not enough as a typical round of output will have 1500 tokens, and it may
440   take a few rounds before the LLM generates a valid solution. If one wants to use models provided
441   through API like GPT-4 from OpenAI, the latency of the models will depend on a list of factors like
442   network connection and server availability. In our paper, we typically need to wait around 15-30
443   seconds for one step to get completed, where less than 0.1 seconds are used for local processing.

      5   R ELATED W ORKS
446
447   LLM for Reasoning Since the release of GPT-3, researchers have studied the effect of how they
448   are performing on diverse sets of problems. Ideas like chain of thought Wei et al. (2022), in context
449   learning Ye et al. (2023) have been shown to significantly improve the performance of LLMs in
450   different tasks related to reasoningFu et al. (2022); Shum et al. (2023). A recent line of work has
451   been introduced to use natural language as feedback in the process to introduce iterations and give
452   LLMs more than one chance to generate correct solutions, which has been shown to significantly
453   help in code generation and reasoning Chen et al. (2023a); Yang et al. (2023); Shinn et al. (2023).
454   Specifically in LLMs for planning, there is a line of work that shows how bad LLMs are in general
455
      planning domains Valmeekam et al. (2023; 2024a), and in this paper, we are specifically looking
      into a specific problem in the planning domain.
456
457
      LLM for Multi-agent Systems While there is extensive research on using LLMs for many differ-
458
      ent problems, there is little work that addresses the problem of LLM for multi-agent systems in the
459   beginning. As time proceeds, there has been a list of works that promote research in social behav-
460   ior by creating multi-agent environment powered by a lot of LLM agents Tan et al. (2023), create
461   dialogue-based games with LLM Schlangen (2023). Recent work has shown that dialogue between
462   multiple agents can help remove factual errors Du et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023b). When it comes
463   to solving problems related to multi-agent system, there are some works that are related to robotics
464   Zhang et al. (2023); Mandi et al. (2023) while limited to the scale of two to three agents. Chen et al.
465   (2023b) studied whether using a more decentralized controller can help to solve the context length
466   limit problem when the number of agents is higher, and concluded that in a centralized controller
467
      environment, creating separate LLMs for different agents does not help improve the success rate.
      While they succeed in their domains, in this paper, we specifically look at the problem of MAPF,
468
      and we show the cause of failure in the MAPF problem because of its unique challenges.
469
470
      Multi-agent Path Finding Multi-agent path finding (MAPF) is a problem that has won much at-
471
      tention in recent years because of its close relationship to real-world applications. It has previously
472   been solved with more classic methods like heuristic search algorithms Sharon et al. (2015); Li
473   et al. (2021); Okumura (2023), rule-based algorithms Han & Yu (2020), and reduction-based algo-
474   rithms Surynek et al. (2016). While learning-based approaches have not yet outperformed classic
475   approaches, they have also begun to win a lot more focus for their fast inference time and generaliz-
476   ability. PRIMAL Sartoretti et al. (2019) proposed to learn a policy for MAPF using a combination
477   of reinforcement learning and imitation learning. Following that, a group of works proposed a di-
478   verse set of methods from building curriculum Damani et al. (2021) to follow guidance from classic
479   methods in each step Skrynnik et al. (2023). In this paper, we focus on using LLM, which is a
480
      learning-based approach but is not specifically fine-tuned for MAPF problems.
481

483
484   In this paper, we investigate using LLMs to solve the MAPF problem. We first show that LLMs
485   could solve easy scenarios, while being unable to generate valid solutions when the problem gets
      harder stably. To make our work applicable to the future, we elaborate on the failures and show

      that they can be broken down into three different aspects. We used extensive experiments to sup-
487   port our breakdown and discussed how the difficulties could be addressed by people from different
