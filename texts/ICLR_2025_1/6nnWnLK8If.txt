009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In the context of the mineral fertilizer industry, a crucial sector for global food
012            production, which faces challenges in production efficiency and fast quality con-
013            trol, this work introduces the Mineral Fertilizer Dataset (MFD), a novel annotated
014            segmentation dataset comprising 1,608 images and 125,648 instances of various
015            fertilizer granules with different colors. Addressing the lack of datasets in this
016            field, the MFD supports both semantic and instance segmentation tasks, with seg-
017            mentation masks that facilitate the computation of the equivalent area diameter of
018            granules. Periodic checks of the area equivalent diameter based on customer spec-
019
               ifications are essential to prevent potential defects, such as caking and dustiness, in
               the produced fertilizer granules. Baseline models based on Feature Pyramid Net-
020
               work (FPN), UNet, and MANet were trained for semantic segmentation, while
021
               baseline models based on Mask R-CNN, YOLOv8, YOLOv9, and Mask2Former
022            were trained for instance segmentation. Our experiments demonstrate the efficacy
023            of these models, as well as the robustness of the trained models in identifying fer-
024            tilizer granules of different colors not included in our dataset, fertilizer granules
025            under 365 nm ultraviolet light, as well as other granular objects such as Polyethy-
026            lene Terephthalate (PET) pellets, corn, beans, and even pharmaceutical tablets.
027            This dataset, along with its benchmark results on existing semantic and instance
028            segmentation algorithms, aims to facilitate further advancements in computer vi-
029            sion applications for quality control in the fertilizer industry and related sectors.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In the current era of big data and advanced data analysis, many industrial production lines, includ-
035   ing those in the mineral fertilizer production, have yet to fully leverage the potential of machine
036   learning methods due to a lack of specialized datasets and hard to get data from those type of
037   production (Yunovidov et al., 2020). Despite being a crucial and rapidly growing sector, mineral
038
      fertilizer production faces significant challenges in meeting the rising global demand driven by pop-
      ulation growth. As production and consumption levels increase, so do the quality requirements for
039
      these products (Ulrich, 2019). Consequently, large-scale facilities are under pressure to enhance
040
      production efficiency and control mechanisms to optimize resource utilization and meet consumer
041   expectations.
042
043   Various methods are employed to control particle size in the mineral fertilizer industry, including
044
      sieve analysis (Besler, 2008; Kimura et al., 2013), laser scattering (Low-Angle Laser Light Scatter-
      ing (LALLS)) (ISO, 2009; Lilkov et al., 1999), and opto-electronic control methods (Standardiza-
045
      tion, 2006; Bjørk et al., 2009; Chávez et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2022). However, each method has
046
      its own limitations that restrict its optimal application.
047
048   Sieve analysis, while providing high accuracy, does not support continuous monitoring of particle
049   size distribution and is significantly influenced by particle shape Besler (2008). LALLS, despite
050
      being fundamentally accurate, is limited by the maximum analyzable particle size (up to 3 mm) and
      cannot assess shape and color parameters Lilkov et al. (1999); ISO (2009).
051
052   Opto-electronic control methods are notable for their versatility, utilizing image analysis to estimate
053   a broad spectrum of parameters, including size, shape, and color (Standardization, 2006; Chávez
      et al., 2015). Furthermore, this method is currently extensively used in the manufacture of mineral

054
      fertilizers. However, the widespread adoption of these methods is hindered by the lack of readily
055   available datasets and the need for specialized equipment.
056
057   In response to the rising trend of optical quality control in manufacturing processes, we constructed
058
      and evaluated a dataset comprised of images of mineral fertilizer granules. In this work, we pro-
      vided an overview of existing semantic and instance segmentation techniques, reviewed existing
059
      literature on image-based analysis of fertilizer granules, proposed a novel annotated dataset of min-
060
      eral fertilizer granules designed for segmentation tasks called the Mineral Fertilizer Dataset (MFD),
061   and trained semantic segmentation models (FPN (Lin et al., 2017b), UNet (Ronneberger et al.,
062   2015), MANet (He et al., 2022)) and instance segmentation models (Mask R-CNN (He et al., 2017),
063   Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022), YOLOv8 (Jocher et al., 2023), YOLOv9 (Wang et al., 2024)) on
064   the proposed dataset to serve as baseline benchmarks.

      2   R ELATED WORKS
068
069   Pixel classification and image segmentation form the foundation of machine vision. Over the years,
070
      image segmentation algorithms have evolved from traditional methods such as thresholding (Otsu,
      1979), conditional random fields and global classification (Plath et al., 2009), and k-means clustering
071
      (Dhanachandra et al., 2015), to more recent deep learning-based methods, which have proven to be
072
      significantly more effective for semantic and instance segmentation. Predicted segmentation masks
073   enable the computation of the area equivalent diameter of granules, following the principles of par-
074   ticle size analysis outlined in ISO 13322-1 (International Organization for Standardization, 2014).
075   Periodic checks of the area equivalent diameter based on customer specifications are crucial for pre-
076   venting potential defects, such as caking and dustiness, in the produced fertilizer granules. Below,
077   we provide an overview of some existing deep learning-based methods for performing semantic and
078   instance segmentation.
079
080   Semantic segmentation Semantic segmentation may be described as a process of classifying pix-
081   els with semantic labels. A typical advantage semantic segmentation has over instance segmentation
082   is that, it is less computationally expensive, and can be more readily applied in industrial settings
083   especially when the computers are only equipped with a central processing unit (CPU). Semantic
084
      segmentation finds application in various sectors including in to inspect belt conveyor idlers (Siami
      et al., 2024), concrete surface engineering (Hao & Qi, 2022), pedestrian segmentation (Ullah et al.,
085
      2018), recognition of navigable areas (Kim et al., 2023), analyzing medical images (Hatamizadeh
086
      et al., 2021; Dhamija et al., 2023), and document scanning and optical character recognition (OCR)
087   (Patil et al., 2022) to mention but a few. Deep learning-based semantic segmentation methods maybe
088   grouped into convolutional neural network (CNN) based methods (U-net (Ronneberger et al., 2015),
089   Unet++ (Zhou et al., 2018), FPN (Lin et al., 2017b)), vision transformer based methods (SegFormer
090   (Xie et al., 2021), Swin-Unet (Cao et al., 2021), SegViT (Zhang et al., 2022)), and methods that
091   utilize both transformers and CNNs (Transunet (Chen et al., 2021), MedT (Valanarasu et al., 2021),
092   Transfuse (Zhang et al., 2021b)). In this work, we have performed experiments using three CNN
093   based models: FPN (Lin et al., 2017b), UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015), and MANet (He et al.,
094   2022). However, to make this method more applicable to the mineral fertilizer industry and bulk
095
      material analysis, we isolated individual granules from the overall mask. To achieve this, the con-
      tours of the granules in the predicted binary masks were estimated using topological analysis (Suzuki
096
      & be, 1985), allowing the instances of each granule to be obtained from the trained semantic seg-
097
      mentation models.
098
099
      Instance segmentation Instance segmentation involves detecting and drawing masks on each in-
100
      stance of an object of interest in an image. Instance segmentation methods can be classified into
101
      three main categories namely: single-stage, dual-stage, and multi-stage. A single-stage instance
102   segmentation method predicts both object masks and class labels without a separate region proposal
103   neural network. Examples of single-stage instance segmentation methods include: Fully convolu-
104   tional instance segmentation (FCIS) (Li et al., 2017), Instance-sensitive fully convolutional network
105   (InstanceFCN) (Dai et al., 2016), PolarMask (Xie et al., 2020) and You only look at Coefficients
106   (YOLACT) (Bolya et al., 2019; 2022). The dual-stage instance segmentation method involves first
107   proposing regions of interest, followed by predicting the object masks and class labels using differ-
      ent neural networks. A typical example of the dual-stage method is Mask-RCNN (He et al., 2017).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      As the name implies, the multi-stage instance segmentation method involves multiple sequential
109   stages of processing, where each stage refines the instance segmentation results iteratively. Typ-
110   ical examples of multi-stage instance segmentation models include: Cascade Mask R-CNN (Cai
111   & Vasconcelos, 2019), and Recurrent neural networks for semantic instance segmentation (RSIS)
112   (Salvador et al., 2017). Besides the aforementioned models, transformer-based models have been
113   utilized for instance segmentation tasks as well such as: SOLQ (Dong et al., 2021), K-Net (Zhang
114   et al., 2021a), Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022), OneFormer (Jain et al., 2023), and Mask DINO
115   (Li et al., 2023). Recently, there has been an increase in zero-shot object detection and instance seg-
116   mentation methods. Some notable models that have been developed include: the segment anything
117
      model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023), and fast segment anything model (FastSAM) (Zhao et al., 2023)
      which is reported as being fifty times faster than the SAM model. However, these zero-shot models
118
      are not yet suitable for deployment in industrial tasks because, they are too slow and hard to main-
119
      tain. In this work, we performed experiments using Mask R-CNN (He et al., 2017), Mask2Former
120   (Cheng et al., 2022), YOLOv8 (Jocher et al., 2023), and YOLOv9 (Wang et al., 2024) for instance
121   segmentation.
122
123   Image-based analysis of fertilizer granules To increase production efficiency, the fertilizer in-
124   dustry is leaning more towards using optical control to assess the quality of produced goods (Wang
125   et al., 2022). Quality control in mineral fertilizer production relies on assessing individual granule
126   characteristics like size, area, and color (UNIDO and International Fertilizer Development Center,
127   1998). This ensures adherence to customer specifications and identifies anomalies in the production
128   process. By inspecting the quality of produced fertilizer granules, possible environmental pollution
129   is also tackled. Yunovidov et al. (2020) explored a robotic system utilizing classical computer vi-
130   sion for online monitoring of granule size. This system captured images using a high-speed camera
131
      and employed image processing techniques to estimate size via ellipses. While color analysis was
      not implemented, its potential was acknowledged. The system’s performance was comparable to
132
      the industry-standard Camsizer P4 machine. Building upon their previous work (Yunovidov et al.,
133
      2020), Yunovidov et al. (2021) expanded the system’s capabilities to encompass granule area, color,
134   and sphericity estimation. Software improvements like adaptive equalization and distance separa-
135   tion enhanced image processing. Additionally, a data recording system documented quality analysis
136   results, enabling both process control and data collection. The upgraded system again demonstrated
137   comparable performance to the Camsizer P4 machine.
138
      Based on our research, there are no similar publicly available datasets. Hence, MFD is the first
139
      such dataset to be made publicly available to the research community. The closest datasets we
140   found, which are commonly used in related fields, include the Rice Image Dataset (Koklu et al.,
141   2021), the Corn Grain Dataset (Ribeiro, 2015), and a dataset consisting of 409 images of well-
142   sorted and poorly sorted sediment, terrigenous, carbonate, and volcaniclastic sands and gravels, and
143   their mixtures, used to develop the SediNet model (Buscombe, 2020). All three datasets are suitable
144   for image classification tasks but are not designed for semantic or instance segmentation, which are
145   critical for our intended application. These segmentation tasks enable the computation of the area
146   equivalent diameter of produced fertilizer granules, in accordance with the ISO 13322-1 standard
147   (International Organization for Standardization, 2014).

149   3   M INERAL F ERTILIZER DATASET
150
151   The MFD dataset comprises 1,608 annotated real images of various types of mineral fertilizer gran-
152   ules captured in different fertilizer production plants, encompassing 125,648 instances of fertilizer
153   granules. Figure 1 displays sample images of these mineral fertilizer granules, including Potas-
154   sium ore (KCl), Ammonium Nitrate (N H4 N O3 ), and mineral fertilizers containing phosphorus
155   (Diammonium Phosphate (DAPh) and NPK). Static images of sampled 100g of DAPh, and NPK
156   granules were captured using a camera with a rolling shutter at a resolution of 1920 x 1080 pix-
157   els. On the other hand, images of the other fertilizer granule types were captured using a camera
158
      with a global shutter at a resolution of 1280 x 1024 pixels. Images of N H4 N O3 were captured
      dynamically on the conveyor belt.

288   4.1   S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION U SING FPN, UN ET AND MAN ET
289
      We explored the performance of three semantic segmentation models (FPN (Lin et al., 2017a), UNet
290
      (Ronneberger et al., 2015), and MANet (He et al., 2022)) on the mineral fertilizer dataset. For
291
      these three models, mobilenet v3 large (Howard et al., 2019) was used as the backbone for feature
292   extraction.
293
294   The semantic segmentation experiments were conducted in three stages. First, the binary masks of
295
      the fertilizer granules were preprocessed using three iterations of erosion with a 3×3 elliptical kernel
      to separate granules in the masks that appeared to be joined. Second, the segmentation models were
296
      trained using a combination of binary cross-entropy (BCE) (Yi-de et al., 2004), dice (Sudre et al.,
297
      2017), and boundary difference over union (Sun et al., 2023) loss functions as shown in Equation 2.
298   Third, based on the predicted binary masks from the trained models, the contours of each granule
299   instance were estimated using topological analysis (Suzuki & be, 1985).
300
301
                            L = 0.2 · BCE + 0.4 · Dice Loss + 0.4 · Boundary DoU                          (2)

303
      Using the predicted binary masks from the trained semantic segmentation models, the contours
304   of the granules were estimated through topological analysis (Suzuki & be, 1985), implemented in
305   OpenCV (Bradski, 2000), allowing the instances of each granule to be obtained. The trained models
306   can operate on a CPU and are suitable for fast assessments. The results obtained using these three
307   semantic segmentation models are summarized in Table 2. Among them, MANet outperformed FPN
308   and UNet.

      Limitations We considered only the primary fertilizers produced in large-scale continuous pro-
478
      cesses, which are subsequently used as bases for more complex fertilizers. Additionally, there are
479
      many specialized fertilizer blends used in various geographic regions, which we had not test yet.
480   The fertilizer types we have described represent only a small portion of the existing brands and
481   types of such products. We will include more annotated data of DAPh, KCl, N H4 N O3 , NPK, and
482   other fertilizer granule types in the MFD dataset to increase its size and variety, which will also
483   enhance the capability of models trained on it. Furthermore, the YOLOv8l-seg, YOLOv9c-seg,
484   and YOLOv9e-seg models were trained on 320 x 320 pixel images due to our currently available
485   computing resources. However, with 640 x 640 pixel images—the default size used to train these
      models—better performance metrics can be achieved.
