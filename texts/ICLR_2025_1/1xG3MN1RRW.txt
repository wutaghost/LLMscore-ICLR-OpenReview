009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In vision-language models (VLMs), visual tokens usually consume a significant
012            amount of computational overhead, despite their sparser information density com-
013            pared to text tokens. To address this, most existing methods learn a network to
014            prune redundant visual tokens and require additional training data. Differently,
015            we propose an efficient training-free token optimization mechanism dubbed Spar-
016            seVLM without extra parameters or fine-tuning costs. Concretely, given that vi-
017            sual tokens complement text tokens in VLMs for linguistic reasoning, we select
018
               visual-relevant text tokens to rate the significance of vision tokens within the self-
               attention matrix extracted from the VLMs. Then we progressively prune irrelevant
019
               tokens. To maximize sparsity while retaining essential information, we introduce a
020
               rank-based strategy to adaptively determine the sparsification ratio for each layer,
021            alongside a token recycling method that compresses pruned tokens into more com-
022            pact representations. Experimental results show that our SparseVLM improves
023            the efficiency of various VLMs across a range of image and video understand-
024            ing tasks. In particular, LLaVA equipped with SparseVLM reduces 61% ⇠ 67%
025            FLOPs with a compression ratio of 78% while maintaining 93% of the accuracy.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Benefiting from tremendous advancements in large language models (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2019;
031   Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Bi et al., 2024),
032   the realm of vision-language models (VLMs) has undergone a revolutionary progress. To combine
033
      visual signals with textual semantics, the mainstream practice in VLMs (Team et al., 2023; Bai
      et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b; 2023a) employs sequential visual representation,
034
      where images are extracted into vision tokens and sent into an LLM decoder. With modal alignment
035
      and instruction fine-tuning (Du et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2023b), recent VLMs
036   successfully adapt LLMs to the vision domain and inherit their perception and reasoning abilities.
037
038   Despite the promising performance, further incorporation of visual tokens inevitably introduces a
039
      huge memory and computation overhead when compared to LLMs, particularly for high-resolution
      images (Li et al., 2024b) and multi-frame videos (Lin et al., 2023). For instance, a 672 ⇥ 672 image
040
      in LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024) yields 2304 vision tokens that span over half of the context length.
041
      However, the information in images is typically more sparse than in natural languages (Marr, 2010),
042   resulting in inefficiency in directly processing both modalities. To address this, existing methods
043   extract more compact image representations by modifying the image encoder or projector (Alayrac
044   et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2023; Cha et al., 2024). While some recent works further
045   sparsify vision tokens during the decoding (Ye et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Cai et al., 2024), they
046   still ignore the guidance from the language tokens, which contradicts the multimodality paradigm.
047   We argue that visual tokens should be sparsified adaptively based on the question prompt, as the
048   model might focus on different parts (e.g., foreground or background) when dealing with various
049   questions, as shown in Figure 1. Furthermore, current approaches generally train a network to prune
050
      redundant visual tokens and require additional training data (Li et al., 2024a; Ye et al., 2024).
051   In this paper, we introduce a text-guided training-free framework dubbed SparseVLM for efficient
052   vision language model inference. We reuse the self-attention matrix of visual-text tokens directly
053   from the decoder layers without extra training parameters for sparsification. We ascertain that not
      all prompt tokens should be considered as some could be less relevant, which leads to inaccurate

132   Visual Compression for VLMs. Compression of vision tokens is necessary because, on the one
133   hand, their quantity is usually tens to hundreds of times that of language tokens. On the other hand,
134   visual signals are inherently redundant compared to dense human-designed texts (Marr, 2010). Past
135   efforts to address the above problem can be categorized into two directions. The first one centers
136
      on the vision tower or projection of visual modality and cuts vision tokens with external modules.
      For instance, LLaMA-VID (Li et al., 2024a) exploits the use of Q-Former with context token while
137
      DeCo (Yao et al., 2024) employs an adaptive pooling to downsample the visual tokens at the patch
138
      level. The other type methods (Ye et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Cai et al., 2024) go deeper into the
139   text modality and sparsify vision tokens during LLM decoding, but they still neglect the guidance
140   from the text tokens. In our paper, SparseVLM takes note of this and improves performance upon it.
141
142
      Token Merging for VLMs. Token merging has recently received much attention in the field of
      VLMs where its algorithms mainly fall into two directions. One focuses on the matching algorithm
143
      using the Bipartite Soft Matching (BSM) (Bolya et al., 2022). For example, ToMe (Bolya et al.,
144
      2022) merges similar visual patches in Transformer blocks and speeds up the match process through
145   the Bipartite Soft Matching (BSM) algorithm. Other methods rely on clustering methods for token
146   merging. For instance, LLaVolta (Chen et al., 2024a) proposes the visual context compressor (an
147   average pooling) to merge the output tokens from the vision tower and progressively enhance the
148   VLMs’ efficiency by training. Inspired by TCFormer (Zeng et al., 2024), we apply k-nearest neigh-
149   bor density peak aggregation algorithm (Rodriguez, 2014) to the field of vision-language models.
150   Unlike TCFormer, our method focuses on merging the dropped tokens and makes full use of their
151   detailed information.

457   To validate the effectiveness of our token recycling strategy, we perform ablation experiments on
458   the LLaVA model (Liu et al., 2023a). The results are presented in Table 3. Across multiple sparsity

460

      Notably, as the number of pruned vision tokens increases, the benefit brought by our recycling
461
      method increases. For instance, when pruning from 192 to 64 tokens, the pruned token recycling
462
      significantly boosts the accuracy from 1.5% to 17.7% on POPE. We argue that when the size of the

464   lost information and compresses it into few slots using the proposed reconstruction mechanism.

527   6   C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper introduced an efficient text-aware training-free token optimization mechanism called
530   SparseVLM which significantly enhanced the efficiency of various VLMs in image and video un-
531   derstanding tasks. Unlike existing methods, SparseVLM was able to optimize VLMs without intro-
532   ducing extra parameters and fine-tuning costs. We achieved a more compact visual representation
533   by progressively pruning the less relevant vision tokens. In addition, we employed the matrix rank
534   to adaptively determine sparsification ratios and recycled the pruned tokens via reconstruction to
535
      reduce the information loss. Experiments demonstrated that SparseVLM increased the efficiency
      of various VLMs. Particularly, LLaVA equipped with SparseVLM achieved a reduction of 53.9%
536
      latency with a compression ratio of 88.9% while maintaining 87% accuracy. Moreover, our method
537
      exceeded FastV accuracy by 34.4% in video understanding tasks. Our SparseVLM can provide
538   practical benefits for deploying off-the-shelf VLMs on edge devices and in the cloud.
