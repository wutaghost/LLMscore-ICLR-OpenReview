                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Over-parameterized deep learning models, characterized by their large number of
010            parameters, have demonstrated remarkable performance in various tasks. Despite
011            the potential risk of overfitting, these models often generalize well to unseen data
012            due to effective regularization techniques, with data augmentation being one of the
013            most prominent methods. This strategy has proven effective in classification tasks,
014            where label-preserving transformations are applicable. However, the application
015            of data augmentation in regression problems remains underexplored. Recently, a
016            new manifold learning approach for sampling synthetic data has been introduced,
017            and it can be viewed as utilizing a first-order approximation of the data manifold.
018
               In this work, we propose to extend this direction by providing the fundamental
               theory and practical tools for approximating and sampling general data manifolds.
019
               Further, we introduce the curvature enhanced manifold sampling (CEMS) data
020
               augmentation method for regression. CEMS is based on a second-order encoding
021            of the manifold, facilitating sampling and reconstruction of new points. Through
022            extensive evaluations on multiple datasets and in comparison to several state-of-
023            the-art approaches, we demonstrate that CEMS is superior in in-distribution and
024            out-of-distribution tasks, while incurring only a mild computational overhead.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Deep neural networks have demonstrated remarkable performance across a wide range of applica-
029
      tions in various fields (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Long et al., 2015; Mnih et al., 2015; Noh et al., 2015;
030
      Vinyals et al., 2015; He et al., 2016; Nam & Han, 2016; Wu et al., 2016). Despite their success, these
031   models are often significantly over-parameterized, meaning they possess more parameters than the
032   number of training examples. As a result, deep neural networks are prone to overfitting, whereby
033   they “memorize” the training set rather than learning generalizable patterns, thus compromising
034   their performance on unseen data. Regularization techniques are crucial to address this issue, as they
035   modify the learning process to prevent overfitting by reducing the variance and increasing the bias of
036   the underlying model (Goodfellow, 2016). Classical regularization methods, such as weight decay,
037   dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014), and normalization techniques like Batch Normalization (Ioffe &
038   Szegedy, 2015) and Layer Normalization (Ba et al., 2016), have been effective in many scenarios.
039
      In addition to these methods, recent research has explored the potential of data augmentation (DA)
      as a form of regularization. In this paper, we focus on the problem of regularizing regression models
040
      via data augmentation. That is, we explore how to artificially expand the train set (DA) for models
041
      that predict continuous values (regressors) to improve generalization and robustness.
042
043   Early work in modern computer vision revealed the effectiveness of basic image transformations
044   such as translation and rotation (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), promoting data augmentation to become
045
      one of the key components in designing generalizable learning models (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar,
      2019). In particular, classification tasks, whose goal is to predict a discrete label, benefited notably
046
      from the rapid development of DA (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015; DeVries, 2017; Zhang et al.,
047
      2018; Zhong et al., 2020). The discrete and categorical nature of classification labels makes it eas-
048   ier to define label-preserving transformations and apply interpolations without compromising data
049   integrity. In contrast, regression tasks, where the outputs are continuous, face unique challenges in
050   ensuring that transformations produce valid input-output pairs and that interpolations maintain the
051   underlying functional relationships. While certain regression challenges have adopted standard data
052   augmentation approaches successfully (Redmon et al., 2016), existing DA methods are generally
053   less effective for regression problems (Yao et al., 2022). For this reason, developing data augmen-
      tation tools for general regression problems is an emerging field of interest with a relatively small

054
      number of available effective techniques. One of the recent state-of-the-art (SOTA) works intro-
055   duced FOMA, a data-driven and domain-independent approach based on the theory and practice of
056   manifold learning (Kaufman & Azencot, 2024). Our work is also inspired by manifold learning,
057   where we consider DA as a manifold approximation and sampling challenge.
058
      Manifold learning is fundamental to modern machine learning primarily through the manifold hy-
059
      pothesis (Belkin & Niyogi, 2003; Goodfellow, 2016), where complex and high-dimensional data
060
      is assumed to lie close or on an associated low-dimensional manifold. Multiple works leveraged
061   the relation between data and manifolds (Zhu et al., 2018; Ansuini et al., 2019), and particularly,
062   FOMA (Kaufman & Azencot, 2024) can be viewed as a method for generating new examples by
063   sampling from the tangent space of the data manifold, approximated using the training distribution.
064   The tangent space at a point is a linear approximation of the manifold at that point (Lee, 2012),
065   and thus, FOMA is a first-order approach. However, while first-order approximations work well for
066   relatively simple or well-behaved data, they often fall short when dealing with complex, curved real-
067   world data. We demonstrate this effect in Fig. 1B-C, where first-order approximations of points with
068   high curvature fail to capture the structure of the manifold. While it is natural to consider higher-
069
      order approximations for improving FOMA, their computational burden may be too limiting. In this
      work, we advocate that second-order manifold representations offer a compelling trade-off between
070
      effectiveness and compute requirements for data augmentation for regression problems.
071
072   We propose the curvature enhanced manifold sampling (CEMS) approach, which generates new
073   examples by drawing from a second-order representation of the data manifold. Particularly, CEMS
074   randomly generates points in the tangent space of the manifold, whereas FOMA is different as
075
      it scales down the orthogonal complement of the tangent space which captures how the manifold
      deviates from the linear approximation. FOMA and CEMS are data-driven and domain-independent,
076
      i.e., their samples are based on the underlying data distribution, whose domain can be arbitrary, e.g.,
077
      time series, tabular, images, and other data forms. The inclusion of curvature information allows
078   CEMS to better capture the intrinsic geometry of the data manifold, as it accounts for the non-
079   linearities and complex structures that first-order methods might miss. In general, second-order
080   methods are infeasible in modern machine learning due to computational costs incurred by high-
081   dimensional vectors. Nevertheless, our analysis shows that CEMS is governed by the intrinsic
082   dimension d of the manifold, and its value is much smaller than the data dimension D, i.e., d ≪ D.
083   We extensively evaluate CEMS and show it is competitive in comparison to SOTA data augmentation
084   approaches on in-distribution and out-of-distribution tasks. The contributions of our work can be
085   summarized as follows:
086
087         1. We consider data augmentation for regression as a manifold learning problem, extending
088
               and generalizing prior approaches through providing the foundational theory and practice.
089         2. We introduce CEMS, a novel fully-differentiable, data-driven and domain-independent data
090            augmentation technique that is based on a second-order approximation of the data manifold.
091
            3. Across nine datasets, featuring numerous large-scale, real-world in-distribution and out-of-
092
               distribution tasks, we demonstrate that CEMS performs competitively or even surpasses
093            other augmentation strategies.

      DA for Classification. Zhang et al. (2018) introduced mixup, a well-known domain-independent
128   DA method that convexly combines pairs of input samples and their one-hot label representations
129   during training. Following their work, a plethora of mixup-based techniques have been suggested
130   such as manifold mixup (Verma et al., 2019) which extends the idea of mixing examples to the latent
131   space. CutMix (Yun et al., 2019) implants a random rectangular region of the input into another and
132   many others (Guo et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2020; Berthelot et al., 2019; Greenewald et al.,
133   2023; Lim et al., 2022). Recently, Erichson et al. (2024) extended the work (Lim et al., 2022) via
134   stable training and further noise injections. While the family of mixup techniques have been shown

138   DA for Regression. Unfortunately, there has been considerably less focus on developing data
139
      augmentation methods for regression tasks in comparison to the classification setting. Due to the
      simplicity and effectiveness of mixup-based tools in classification, a growing body of literature is
140
      drawn to adapting and extending the mixing process for regression. For instance, RegMix (Hwang
141
      & Whang, 2021) learns the optimal number of nearest neighbors to mix per sample. C-mixup (Yao
142   et al., 2022) employs a Gaussian kernel to create a sampling probability distribution for each sample,
143   taking label distances into account, and selecting samples for mixing according to this distribution.
144   Anchor Data Augmentation (Schneider et al., 2023) clusters data points and adjusts the original
145   points either towards or away from the cluster centroids. R-Mixup (Kan et al., 2023) focuses on
146   enhancing model performance specifically for biological networks, whereas RC-Mixup (Hwang
147   et al., 2024) extends C-mixup to be more robust against noise. Perhaps closest to our work is the
148   recent FOMA method (Kaufman & Azencot, 2024) that does not rely on mixing samples, but rather,
149   it samples from a first-order approximation of the data manifold. Still, to the best of our knowledge,
150
      our work is first in suggesting fundamental manifold learning theory and tools for DA, accompanied
      by an effective second-order augmentation technique.

154
      work focused on dimensionality reduction of points and preserving their geometric features (Tenen-

      baum et al., 2000; Roweis & Saul, 2000; Belkin & Niyogi, 2003; Weinberger & Saul, 2004; Zhang

158   & Azencot, 2023), and autoencoding (Chen et al., 2020a), among other applications. Recent ad-
159   vancements in manifold learning have enhanced anomaly detection and out-of-distribution (OOD)
160   recognition. (Li et al., 2024) leveraged submanifold geometry, estimating tangent spaces and curva-
161   tures to define in-distribution regions for OOD detection. Gao et al. (2022) proposed a hyperbolic
      feature augmentation method, using the Poincaré ball model for distribution estimation and infinite

173   3.1   M ANIFOLD LEARNING
174
175   A manifold M ⊂ Rd is a mathematical structure that locally resembles an Euclidean space near each
176   of its points (Lee, 2012). A ubiquitous assumption in machine learning states that high-dimensional
177   point clouds Z ⊂ RD satisfy the manifold hypothesis. Namely, the data Z lie on a manifold M
178   whose intrinsic dimension d is significantly lower than the extrinsic dimension of the ambient space
179   D, i.e., d ≪ D (Goodfellow, 2016). Manifold learning is a field in machine learning that develops
180
      theory and tools for analyzing and processing high-dimensional data under the lens of geometric
      manifolds.
181

      3.2   C URVATURE - AWARE MANIFOLD LEARNING
184
      Our curvature enhanced manifold sampling (CEMS) data augmentation method is based on a
185   second-order approximation of the data manifold. There are several existing practical approaches
186   for parameterizing a manifold given a collection of data points Z = {z 1 , z 2 , · · · , z N } ⊂ RD . Here,
187   we focus on the curvature-aware manifold learning (CAML) algorithm (Li, 2018), since it scales to
188   high-dimensional problems, it is numerically stable, and it is easy to code. Below, we include the
189   necessary details for describing our approach, and we refer the reader to (Lee, 2012; 2018; Li, 2018)
190   for additional details on Riemannian geometry and its realization in machine learning.
191   Following our discussion above, we assume Z satisfies the manifold hypothesis. Formally, it means
192   that there exists an embedding map f : M → RD such that
193
194                                        z i = f (ui ),   i = 1, . . . , N ,                               (1)
195   where U = {u1 , u2 , · · · , uN } ⊂ Rd are low-dimensional representations of Z. In practice, CAML
196   parameterizes f by projecting z ∈ Z to its tangent and normal spaces at u ∈ M, where the tangent
197   space is obtained by a linear transformation and the normal space is provided via a second-order
198   local approximation. Specifically, given a point z ∈ Z, we find close points Nz = {zj }kj=1 , forming
199   the neighborhood of z. Next, we construct an orthonormal basis Bu := [BTu , BNu ] for the tangent
200   space Tu M and the normal space Nu M at u. We then project Nz and z onto BTu and BNu ,
201   yielding Uz = {uj }kj=1 , u and Gz = {gj }kj=1 , g respectively. To allow arbitrary sampling from M,
202   we assume that g is a map from the tangent space to the normal space, i.e., g : Tu M → Nu M. The
203   second-order Taylor expansion of g around a point u ∈ M is given by

      Neighborhood extraction. The approach we consider in this work is local in the sense that we rep-
241
      resent the manifold structure in the vicinity of a specific point z ∈ D or within its neighborhood Nz .
242
      High-quality approximations of local properties of the manifold ∇g(u) and H(u) depend directly on
243   the proximity of the elements in Nz to z. A straightforward approach to extracting Nz is to compute
244   the k-nearest neighbors (kNN) (Cover & Hart, 1967) of z. Specifically, we construct neighborhoods
245   in the joint input-output space X × Y to align with the manifold hypothesis, preserving the local
246   continuity of the data and avoiding the artificial separations introduced by clustering-based methods.
247   For each point zi ∈ Z, Nzi is defined as the k closest points in feature space, which ensures the local
248   geometry is captured reliably while minimizing diversity within the neighborhoods. Furthermore,
249   our reliance on the local-Euclidean prior assumes that the manifold is sufficiently smooth at small
250   scales, justifying the validity of linear approximations such as those employed by our method. This
251
      assumption underpins the extraction of neighborhood sets and their utility in manifold analysis, as
      it guarantees that the neighborhoods respect the underlying manifold structure.
252
253   Our analysis below and in Sec. B shows that the computational complexity of CEMS is governed
254   by singular value decomposition (SVD) calculations, required for basis construction. To reduce
255   runtime, D can be pre-processed, storing ∇g(u) and H(u) for every z := f (u) on the disk, as
256   these properties remain unchanged during training. While this pre-computation reduces runtime
257
      significantly, it incurs high memory complexity, O(2d(D − d)), and the choice of neighbors is fixed
      during training. To address these limitations, we use the same neighborhood for all points in Nz ,
258
      re-using neighborhoods and basis computations for every zj ∈ Nz . This improves efficiency, though
259
      at the cost of accuracy, since every zj ∈ Nz is assumed to share the same neighborhood. Finally,
260   the batch size determines the number of neighbors for each point, providing a balance between
261   computational efficiency and accuracy.

      Memory analysis. The memory requirements of CEMS are primarily dictated by the compu-
325   tation of the SVD. Notably, the SVD is computed independently for each batch rather than for
326   the entire dataset. In our PyTorch implementation, we leverage the economy/reduced SVD vari-
327   ant, which significantly reduces memory usage compared to the full SVD. For a batch matrix of
328   size b × D (where b is the batch size and D is the ambient dimension), the space complexity is
329   O(bD + min(b, D)(b + D)). This is substantially more efficient than the full SVD, which requires
330   O(bD + b2 + D2 ) memory. In practice, CEMS is particularly effective in scenarios where the batch
331   size b is much smaller than the ambient dimension D (common in deep learning), resulting in a
332   memory complexity that is approximately proportional to D.

334   Comparison with FOMA. FOMA (Kaufman & Azencot, 2024) can be interpreted as a special
335
      case of CEMS. Specifically, we can describe FOMA using our notations as follows: given a sample
      z and its neighborhood Nz , FOMA constructs a basis Bu := [BTu , BNu ] for the tangent space
336
      Tu M and the normal space Nu M and projects the neighborhood onto it, yielding Uz = {uj }kj=1
337
      and Gz = {gj }kj=1 , respectively. Rather than estimating the gradient ∇g(uj ) and Hessian H(uj ) at
338
      each point uj ∈ Uz and then sampling using the Taylor expansion as performed in CEMS, FOMA
339
      generates new samples by scaling down Gz . That is, for each zj ∈ Nz , the corresponding g˜j = λgj
340   is scaled where λ ∈ (0, 1). To complete the sampling process, every uj is un-projected back to the
341   original coordinates by computing z̃ := f (uj ) = Bu · [uj , λg(uj )]. Therefore, FOMA does not

343   this sampling technique may yield new points that are not on the data manifold, especially on highly
344   curved locations, as is also illustrated in Fig. 1.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
511
512
      This work introduces CEMS, a novel data augmentation method tailored specifically for regression
513
      problems, framed within the context of manifold learning. By leveraging second-order manifold
514   approximations, CEMS captures the underlying curvature and structure of the data more accurately
515   than previous first-order methods. Our extensive evaluation across nine diverse benchmark datasets,
516   spanning both in-distribution and out-of-distribution tasks, shows that CEMS achieves competitive
517   performance compared to SOTA techniques, often surpassing them in challenging settings. The
518   main contributions of this work are threefold: (1) extend the view of DA for regression as a man-
519   ifold learning problem, thereby providing a principled foundation and practical tools; (2) propos-
520   ing CEMS, a fully differentiable, data-driven, and domain-independent second-order augmentation
521   method; and (3) empirically validating CEMS across a variety of regression scenarios, showing its
522
      potential to serve as a robust and effective regularization technique for models predicting continu-
      ous values. Our results suggest that higher-order manifold sampling approaches hold promise for
523
      improving the generalization of regression models, especially in scenarios with limited data.
524
525   One limitation of CEMS is that the linear system in Eq. 9 is might be underdetermined for data
526   sets with a large intrinsic dimension d. In practice, the number of neighbors has to be O(d2 ),
527   for an overdetermined system. Our implementation sets the number of neighbors to be a constant
528
      size and thus it is independent of d. While this requirement is reasonable for low d values, it can
      become expensive for large d. This can be resolved by regularizing the linear system via, e.g., ridge
529
      regression. Another limitation is related to the SVD computation, where CEMS needs at least d
530
      columns. This may require a full SVD calculation, demanding O(bD2 ) memory, where b is the batch
531   size and D is the extrinsic dimension, which may be impractical for datasets with many features.
532                                                                          ˜ such that d˜ < d. In future
      A potential solution is to consider a different intrinsic dimension d,
533   work, we plan to investigate these ideas, and, in addition, we plan to explore extensions of CEMS to
534   include adaptive strategies for dynamically selecting the appropriate order of approximation based
535   on local data properties. By pushing the boundaries of data augmentation for regression, we hope to
536   pave the way for more robust and versatile learning systems capable of tackling complex, real-world
537   prediction tasks.

788   General analysis of n-th order embedding maps: To estimate the n-th order Taylor approxima-
789   tion of the embedding g, we need to find all the partial derivatives up to and including order n. The
790   gradient ∇ vector is composed of d first-order partial derivatives, the Hessian matrix is composed of
791   d2 second-order partial derivatives. Third-order and higher partial derivatives are represented using
792   mathematical objects called tensors. In general, the number of partial derivatives of a multi-input
793   function grows exponentially with the order.
794   The order n of the partial derivatives determines the number of unknowns X and the size of the
795   matrix Ψ which used to solve Eq. 9 via least squares. The number of columns in the matrix Ψ is
796
      P n    i        n                                                     n
        i=1 d = O(d ), and thus, the dimensions of the matrix Ψ are k × d . It is preferred to solve an
797   over-determined set of equations such that the number of neighbors k > dn , which is memory and
798   computationally expensive. For small values of d and n it is feasible to solve Eq. 9 in a run time
799   complexity of O(kd2n ). For larger values of n, it becomes extremely computationally and memory
800   expensive to achieve k ≥ dn , therefore, we can assume that k < dn .
801   There are two computationally expensive operations used to estimate the n-th order approximation
802   of the manifold, SVD and least squares. The matrix on which we perform SVD is of shape Nz ∈
803   Rk×D where k represents the number of neighbors and D is the extrinsic dimension. Thus, the run
804   time complexity of the SVD operation per batch is O(min(k 2 D, kD2 )). The complexity of least

886   C.1   N EIGHBORHOOD C ONSTRUCTION
887
888   The validity of CEMS relies on three key theoretical foundations. First, our neighborhood construc-
889   tion approach balances computational efficiency with geometric fidelity by sharing neighborhoods
890   across points in close proximity. While this might appear to reduce sampling diversity, it actually
891   preserves manifold structure because points that are close in the normalized input-output space typ-
892   ically share similar geometric properties. The shared neighborhood assumption is particularly valid
893   because we normalize both input X and output Y features to the same range, ensuring that proximity
894   in the combined space meaningfully reflects similarity in both domains.
895   The reliability of our construction is maintained even in regions of high output diversity through
896   our careful treatment of the input-output space. For a point zi = [xi , yi ], its neighborhood Nz is
897   constructed considering distances in both input and output spaces simultaneously, naturally limiting
898   the diversity of outputs within each neighborhood. This approach ensures that points sharing a
899   neighborhood basis have similar geometric properties, maintaining the validity of our second-order
900   approximation.
901   The stochastic nature of mini-batch training provides an additional beneficial property for CEMS.
902   As different batches are sampled each epoch, the method naturally explores varying neighborhoods
903   and their associated tangent spaces. This dynamic sampling process enables CEMS to build a com-
904   prehensive representation of the manifold’s local geometry, adapting to variations in data density
905   across different regions. The continuous exploration of diverse local structures throughout training
906   enhances the method’s ability to capture the full geometric complexity of the underlying manifold.
907   The local-Euclidean structure of CEMS is supported by two complementary mechanisms. First, the
908   second-order approximation naturally captures local curvature through the Hessian term, enabling
909   accurate representation of nonlinear geometries. Second, our stochastic batch sampling strategy
910   ensures exposure to different neighborhoods and tangent spaces throughout training. The projec-
911   tion of points onto the tangent space at µ (the neighborhood mean) maintains validity through the
912   second-order terms in our Taylor expansion, which account for the primary nonlinearities within
913   each neighborhood. This approach is particularly robust because the neighborhood size adapts with
914
      the batch size, preserving accurate local approximations even in regions of high curvature.
915   The empirical success of this construction is demonstrated in our ablation studies (Table 3), where
916   we compare point-wise basis computation (CEMSp ) with our more efficient shared neighborhood
917   approach (CEMS). The comparable performance across multiple datasets validates our theoretical
      assumptions about the effectiveness of shared geometric information within local neighborhoods.

      C.2                     L OCAL VS . G LOBAL S AMPLING
919
920   It is important to note that CEMS focuses on local sampling within neighborhoods where the second-
921   order approximation is valid. While alternative approaches based on geodesics can enable global
922   sampling along the manifold, they typically incur significantly higher computational costs. Our
923   local approach strikes a balance between sampling accuracy and computational efficiency, making
924   it particularly well-suited for data augmentation during training.
925   The theoretical guarantees provided by Theorem 4.1 hold within the neighborhood where our Taylor
926   approximations are valid. This aligns with the manifold hypothesis, which posits that real data
927   typically lies on or near a lower-dimensional manifold with locally Euclidean structure (Goodfellow,
928   2016; Belkin & Niyogi, 2003). By focusing on accurate local sampling, CEMS can effectively
929   augment the training data while maintaining the essential geometric structure of the underlying
930   manifold.
