011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved robust
014            performance across diverse tasks, but fine-tuning these models for specific do-
015
               mains remains resource-intensive. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) meth-
               ods like Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) address this challenge by fine-tuning a
016
               small subset of parameters. However, existing methods for fusing multiple LoRAs
017
               lack dynamic fusion based on contextual inputs and often increase inference time
018            due to token-level operations. We propose DLP-LoRA, a Dynamic Lightweight
019            Plugin that employs a mini-MLP module with only 5M parameters to dynami-
020            cally fuse multiple LoRAs at the sentence level using top-p sampling strategies.
021            This approach reduces inference time to less than twice that of single LoRA infer-
022            ence by leveraging parallel computation. Evaluations across 26 tasks—including
023            multiple-choice questions and question answering—demonstrate that DLP-LoRA
024            achieves an average accuracy of 92.34% on multiple-choice datasets and signifi-
025            cant improvements in BLEU and ROUGE scores on QA datasets, outperforming
026
               different LLMs backbones under composite task settings. DLP-LoRA effectively
               balances performance and efficiency, making it a practical solution for dynamic
027
               multi-task adaptation in LLMs.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) such as LLaMA 3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024),
033   Qwen 2.5 (Team, 2024), and Gemma 2 (Team et al., 2024) have led to robust and superior perfor-
034   mance across multiple benchmarks (Muennighoff et al., 2022; Ilyas Moutawwakil, 2023; Fourrier
035   et al., 2024). These models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in diverse areas, including
036   code generation (Bai et al., 2023), mathematical reasoning (Ahn et al., 2024), and question answer-
037   ing (Achiam et al., 2023). Despite these achievements, fine-tuning all parameters of such large
038
      models for specific domains remains resource-intensive and time-consuming.
039   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods (Houlsby et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2023) address
040   this challenge by enabling the fine-tuning of a small subset of parameters, thereby improving per-
041   formance in various applications like multi-task learning (Xu et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2024), mul-
042   tilingual summarisation, and transfer learning (Whitehouse et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024). One
043   prominent PEFT approach is Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), which fine-tunes
044
      low-rank matrices to capture domain-specific knowledge and merges them with pre-trained LLMs.
045   To enhance the multi-task learning capabilities of LLMs, several methods have been proposed to
046   fuse task-specific LoRAs, including MoLE (Wu et al., 2024b), S-LoRA (Sheng et al., 2023), and
047   LoRAHub (Huang et al., 2023). These approaches primarily use learnable gating networks or au-
048   tomatic loading mechanisms to combine multiple LoRAs. For instance, MeteoRA (Xu et al., 2024)
049   introduces a token-level gating network to all attention and MLP layers for dynamic LoRA fusion.
050   However, most of these methods lack the ability to dynamically fuse LoRAs based on contextual
051   prompt inputs during inference. They either require manual selection before combining LoRAs or
052   necessitate additional fine-tuning when tasks change. Moreover, existing LoRA mixture strategies
053   like MeteoRA focus on token-level Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) gating across all attention heads and
      MLP layers, which significantly increases inference time for next-token generation. Observations

      from prior studies (Xu et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024b; Muqeeth et al., 2024) indicate that within the
055   same sentence of a task, the same LoRA is consistently assigned to each token. This suggests that
056   token-level LoRA MoE might be unnecessary and computationally inefficient.
057
058
      In this paper, we propose a Dynamic Lightweight Plugin for LoRA fusion (DLP-LoRA), which
      employs a lightweight MLP module to dynamically fuse multiple LoRAs based on top-p sampling
059
      strategies. This mini-MLP plugin, containing only 5M parameters, is fast to train for multi-task
060
      classification and easily adaptable to new domains. By leveraging sentence-level LoRA selection
061   and fusion guided by the mini-MLP plugin, DLP-LoRA requires less than twice the inference time
062   compared to manually selecting and loading a single LoRA, making it comparable in efficiency.
063
064
      We evaluate DLP-LoRA across 26 tasks, including 17 multiple-choice question (MCQ) datasets
      spanning mathematical QA, logical reasoning, language identification, and reading comprehension,
065
      as well as 9 question-answering (QA) datasets focused on summarisation, machine translation, and
066
      open-domain QA. Under comparable inference times to single LoRA setups, DLP-LoRA achieves
067   an average accuracy of 92.34% across the 17 MCQ datasets and average BLEU, ROUGE-1, and
068   ROUGE-L scores of 57.62, 56.03, and 53.96, respectively, across the 9 QA datasets. These evalua-
069   tions are conducted using Qwen-2 1.5B, Qwen-2 7B, LLaMA-2 7B, and LLaMA-3 8B backbones.
070   Additionally, our model demonstrates relative improvements of 92.95% and 13.2% for the MCQ
071   and QA tasks, respectively, compared to different LLM backbones under composite task settings.
072   With DLP-LoRA, the inference speed and performance of the Qwen-2 1.5B backbone are improved
073   by over 90.90% and 82.55% under composite-26 task setting, respectively, when compared to the
074   baseline LLaMA-2 13B. Our case studies further illustrate that sentence-level DLP-LoRA effec-
075
      tively balances the trade-off between multi-LoRA inference and fusion.
076   In summary, our contributions are threefold:
077
078          • We introduce DLP-LoRA, a dynamic and lightweight plugin for multi-LoRA selection and
079            fusion that is fast to train and easily adaptable to new domains.
080          • By employing sentence-level multi-LoRA selection and fusion, DLP-LoRA leverages par-
081            allel CUDA acceleration, achieving less than twice the inference time compared to single
082            LoRA inference and outperforming token-level MoE gating routers in efficiency.
083          • Through extensive evaluations on 26 tasks including MCQ and QA, DLP-LoRA achieves
084            performance comparable to single-task LoRA models and significantly improves accuracy
085            and ROUGE metrics under composite task settings.

      2   BACKGROUND
089
090
      Low-Rank Adaption. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) is a method developed to
      fine-tune large language models (LLMs) for specific downstream tasks with enhanced efficiency
091
      by minimising the number of trainable parameters. Instead of updating all the model’s parameters
092
      during training, LoRA introduces supplementary low-rank matrices. In Transformer-based autore-
093   gressive LLMs, this technique involves freezing the pre-trained weights and integrating trainable
094   low-rank matrices into designated layers, thereby substantially reducing computational overhead.
095   The primary motivation for LoRA stems from the recognition that many parameter updates dur-
096   ing fine-tuning occur within a low-dimensional subspace, indicating that full-rank weight updates
097   are often unnecessary. By employing low-rank approximations, LoRA significantly decreases the
098   number of parameters required for training—sometimes by factors as large as 10,000—while still
099   maintaining competitive performance levels.
100   Formally, consider a weight matrix W ∈ Rh×d within the original LLMs. LoRA introduces two
101   low-rank matrices, A ∈ Rh×r and B ∈ Rr×d , where r ≪ min(h, d). Instead of directly updating
102   the weight matrix, LoRA modifies the model’s forward pass according to the following equation:
103
104
                                          W ′ = o + ∆o = W + AB                                          (1)
105   Here, W ′ represents the adjusted weight matrix, while A and B are the trainable matrices incorpo-
106   rated by LoRA. Consequently, the forward computation for an input x ∈ R1×d is expressed as:
107
                          h = xW ′ = x(o + ∆o) = x(W + AB) = xW + xAB                                    (2)

      This approach guarantees that during the inference phase, after the training process is finalised, the
      low-rank matrices A and B can be integrated with the original weights W , thereby removing any

132
133   LoRA is predominantly applied to the attention projection matrices within the self-attention mech-
134   anisms of Transformer architectures, specifically targeting the query, key, and value projections, as
135   well as the output projection. Recently, MLP layers can also be applied by LoRA (Dou et al., 2024;
136
      Li et al., 2024). This targeted application enhances the method’s overall efficiency.
137   The minimalistic design of LoRA renders it especially beneficial for environments with limited com-
138   putational resources or for applications necessitating the swift adaptation of extensive models. By
139   keeping the majority of the model’s parameters fixed and concentrating solely on learning the low-
140   rank modifications, LoRA substantially decreases both memory usage and computational demands
141   during the fine-tuning process.
142

      Multi-task LoRA Mixture A LoRA adapter is fine-tuned for a specific downstream task, limiting
144   its utility to that particular application. To enhance the ability of LLMs to handle multiple tasks, two
145   primary approaches are commonly employed. The first approach involves combining datasets from
146   multiple tasks and fine-tuning a single LoRA module on this aggregated dataset. However, Lin et al.
147   (2024b) have identified significant challenges in encapsulating the specialised knowledge required
148   for diverse domains within a single LLM, often leading to suboptimal performance.
149
      The second approach leverages existing LoRA adapters as interchangeable modules that can be
150   directly integrated into a base LLM. Within this strategy, two distinct directions have emerged.
151   The first direction focuses on architectural designs that combine multiple LoRAs using a learnable
152   weighted sum (Huang et al., 2023) or by implementing unified memory pool designs in CUDA
153   kernels (Sheng et al., 2023). However, these frameworks often require continuous few-shot learning
154   or in-context learning for each individual downstream task and necessitate manual assignment of
155   active LoRAs. This manual intervention poses a significant drawback, as it lacks the capability for
156   autonomous selection and dynamic switching of LoRAs during the inference phase.
157   The second direction involves developing frameworks that enable dynamic fusion of LoRAs. For
158   instance, Xu et al. (2024) introduced MeteoRA, a token-level Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) style multi-
159   task LoRA framework. MeteoRA incorporates a trainable gating mechanism across all attention and
160   MLP layers to automatically select and fuse different LoRAs based on input tokens. While MeteoRA
161   successfully facilitates dynamic management of multiple tasks, the inclusion of a trainable gating
      module at every attention and MLP layer with token-level routing significantly increases inference

      time compared to single LoRA inference. This performance drawback remains substantial even with
163   the development of GPU kernel acceleration methods.

166   3     M ETHODOLOGY
167
168   Our proposed DLP-LoRA framework comprises three key components: a lightweight mini-MLP
169
      plugin CMLP , a base LLM backbone M, and a set of N fine-tuned LoRA modules L{1...N } cor-
      responding to different tasks D{1...N } , as illustrated in Figure 1. Initially, we train the mini-MLP
170
      classifier CMLP on these tasks to achieve high task classification accuracy (we evaluate 26 tasks in
171
      this work; see Appendix C for details). Once trained, the LLM backbone M utilises the mini-MLP
172   plugin to dynamically fuse the appropriate fine-tuned LoRAs L{1...N } at the sentence level, enabling
173   efficient multi-task learning.
174
175   3.1   L IGHTWEIGHT M ULTI - TASK C LASSIFICATION P LUGIN
176
177   Previous methods that perform token-level task classification and routing within the LLM back-
178   bone—by injecting a trainable gating network at each attention and MLP layer—are computation-
179   ally intensive and inefficient during inference (Xu et al., 2024). Observing that most tokens within a
180   sentence typically pertain to the same task, we propose a more efficient sentence-level task detection
181   approach. Specifically, we introduce an off-the-shelf 4-layer mini-MLP plugin CMLP that requires
182
      training only once on the sentence level for the selected tasks.
183   Given N distinct tasks D{1...N } and a collection of M sentences S{1...M } ∈ Dn , our lightweight
184   4-layer CMLP encodes each input sentence Sm using a specific tokenizer (we utilise the ALBERT
185   tokenizer (Lan, 2019) in this work) and classifies Sm to the correct task Dn :
186
                                  Yn = CMLP (Sm ),     where Yn ∈ D{1...N } .                            (3)
187
188
      3.2   DYNAMIC L O RA F USION
189
190   Once the CMLP classifier is well-trained on the tasks D{1...N } , it serves as a plugin to the LLM
191   backbone M for dynamically fusing multiple LoRAs L{1...N } at the sentence level. For the current
192   input sentence Sm ∈ Dn , we consider the first token w1 and the previous contextual history H{1...k} .
193   We employ a top-p sampling scheme via CMLP to dynamically select the possible LoRAs to fuse,
194   using probability p as the threshold:
195
                           Ip = {Y{1...R} | w1 ∈ Sm , H{1...k} },    where Yr ≥ p.                       (4)
196
197
      Using the set Ip for the current sentence Sm , we fuse the selected LoRAs based on normalised
198   weights obtained via a softmax function:
199
200                                 Wm = Softmax(Ip ) = {w1 , . . . , wR }.                              (5)
201
      Importantly, the CMLP classifier is only activated when the first token w1 of the current sentence Sm
202   is generated, leveraging the contextual information H{1...k} . This approach significantly accelerates
203   the inference time of M compared to token-level gating network classification (Xu et al., 2024), as
204   it avoids the overhead of per-token classification.
205
206   3.3   PARALLEL M ULTI -L O RA ACCELERATION
207
208   Beyond the efficiency gained from sentence-level LoRA sampling and fusion—which avoids the
209   inefficiency of repetitive per-token LoRA classification—a significant advantage of our approach is
210   the ability to fully exploit parallel multi-LoRA acceleration.
211   Given N fine-tuned LoRAs, we construct two tensors A ∈ RN ×h×r and B ∈ RN ×r×d , which are
212   allocated contiguously in High Bandwidth Memory (HBM). In contrast to token-level LoRA classi-
213   fication and forward computation—where each token in the batch operates independently, limiting
214   the effectiveness of General Matrix Multiplication (GEMM) optimisations in frameworks like Py-
215   Torch—our sentence-level LoRA classification removes the independence constraints among tokens
      within a sentence. By iterating over all N LoRAs using a hash table stored in HBM, we retrieve the

423   Limitations of Top-k Selection Most existing Multi-LoRA or LoRA-MoE methods employ a top-
424   k router to manually determine the fixed number of LoRAs to use for multi-task learning (Li et al.,
425   2024). This manual selection can restrict the model’s ability to dynamically select and fuse multiple
426   LoRAs based on the task requirements. In our approach, we utilise top-p selection, which leverages
427   the probabilities assigned by the mini-MLP plugin to each LoRA, using a threshold p. This allows
428   DLP-LoRA to adaptively decide both the number and combination of LoRAs to fuse for different
429   tasks, enhancing flexibility and performance.

      6   R ELATED W ORK
496
497
      In the area of multi-task learning with LoRA, two primary research directions have emerged be-
498   yond the straightforward approach of fine-tuning a single LoRA on a combined dataset of multiple
499   tasks (Lin et al., 2024b). The first direction focuses on developing libraries or frameworks to reuse
500   and integrate existing LoRAs, while the second aims to design router networks based on MoEs to
501   dynamically fuse multiple LoRAs.
502
503   Multiple LoRA Architectures Several works have proposed frameworks for combining and man-
504   aging multiple LoRAs. Huang et al. (2023) introduced LoRAHub, a framework that combines
505   existing fine-tuned LoRAs using a learnable weighted sum, allowing for more flexible adaptation
506   across tasks. S-LoRA (Sheng et al., 2023) emphasises unified memory pool design to manage dy-
507   namic LoRA weights with varying ranks and key-value cache tensors for CUDA kernels, enhancing
508   computational efficiency. Additionally, Model-Based Clustering (MBC) (Ostapenko et al., 2024)
509   employs clustering techniques to group tasks based on the similarity of their LoRA parameters,
510
      facilitating better parameter sharing and task generalization.
511
512   Mixture-of-Experts with Multiple LoRAs Another line of research integrates Mixture-of-
513
      Experts mechanisms to control and fuse multiple LoRAs dynamically. In these approaches, multiple
      LoRAs are fine-tuned and injected into the model’s MLP layers, with a router network determin-
514
      ing which LoRA to activate for a given task. Examples include LoRAMoE (Dou et al., 2024),
515
      PHATGOOSE (Muqeeth et al., 2024), MoLE (Wu et al., 2024b), and LoRA-Switch (Kong et al.,
516   2024). Some methods extend this fusion to both MLP and attention layers, such as MixLoRA (Li
517   et al., 2024) and Mixture of Adaptations (MoA) (Feng et al., 2024), enabling more comprehensive
518   adaptation across model components.
519
      Furthermore, token-level routing strategies have been proposed to enhance the granularity of LoRA
520
      selection. MeteoRA (Xu et al., 2024) introduces a token-level MoE-style multi-task LoRA frame-
521
      work with trainable gating mechanisms across all attention and MLP layers, allowing for dynamic
522   selection and fusion of different LoRAs based on input tokens. Similarly, AdaMoE (Zeng et al.,
523   2024) presents an adaptive MoE approach that leverages token-level routing within transformer
524   models to improve performance across diverse tasks. Apart from the token-level gating mechanism
525   for multiple LoRAs, some existing works also proposed sentence-level routing, for instance Polytro-
526   pon (Ponti et al., 2023) and FLix (Lin et al., 2024a). However, Flix mainly focuses on multilingual
527   task settings and Polytropon mainly explores the encoder-decoder architecture. It is unclear whether
528   those works can maintain superior inference efficiency when loading high volumes of LoRAs across
529   different tasks.
530
531   7   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We introduced DLP-LoRA, a dynamic and lightweight plugin that employs a mini-MLP module
534   with only 5 million parameters to dynamically fuse multiple LoRAs at the sentence level using
535   top-p sampling strategies. Our comprehensive evaluation across 17 MCQ tasks and 9 QA tasks
536   demonstrates that DLP-LoRA not only closely matches the performance of individually fine-tuned
537   single LoRAs but also surpasses them on certain tasks, all while incurring less than twice the in-
538   ference time. Through detailed discussions and ablation studies, we have shown that DLP-LoRA
539   effectively balances performance and efficiency in multi-task learning, making it a practical solution
      for dynamic multi-task adaptation in LLMs.

714   A    L IMITATIONS
715
716   Our evaluation of DLP-LoRA was primarily conducted on LLM backbones ranging from 1.5 bil-
717   lion to 8 billion parameters, constrained by the computational limitations of our GPU resources.
718   Consequently, we were unable to assess the performance of DLP-LoRA on larger models such as
719
      Qwen-2.5 32B (Hui et al., 2024) and LLaMA-3.1 70B (Dubey et al., 2024), which may exhibit
      different behaviors and performance characteristics. Additionally, when composite tasks include a
720
      higher proportion of MCQ datasets, DLP-LoRA tends to assign higher probabilities to the specific
721
      MCQ LoRA, potentially limiting its ability to effectively fuse and utilize QA LoRAs. This tendency
722   might restrict the diversity of generated outputs and the fusion capabilities of DLP-LoRA across a
723   broader range of tasks.
724
725
726
      B    B ROADER I MPACTS
727
728
      The lightweight design of DLP-LoRA, featuring a mini-MLP with only 5 million parameters, of-
      fers significant flexibility and efficiency, making it suitable for deployment on smaller devices with
729
      limited computational resources. Moreover, DLP-LoRA facilitates easy integration of new LoRAs
730
      corresponding to additional tasks without necessitating further fine-tuning of the entire model. This
731   capability enhances the accessibility and adaptability of LLMs in various applications, promoting
732   broader utilisation in resource-constrained environments.
