009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Probabilistic circuits (PCs) enable us to learn joint distributions over a set of random
012            variables and to perform various probabilistic queries in a tractable fashion. Though
013            the tractability property allows PCs to scale beyond non-tractable models such
014            as Bayesian Networks, scaling training and inference of PCs to larger, real-world
015            datasets remains challenging. To remedy the situation, we show how PCs can be
016            learned across multiple machines by recursively partitioning a distributed dataset,
017            thereby unveiling a deep connection between PCs and federated learning (FL).
018            This leads to federated circuits (FCs)—a novel and flexible federated learning (FL)
019
               framework that (1) allows one to scale PCs on distributed learning environments
               (2) train PCs faster and (3) unifies for the first time horizontal, vertical, and hybrid
020
               FL in one framework by re-framing FL as a density estimation problem over
021
               distributed datasets. We demonstrate FC’s capability to scale PCs on various large-
022            scale datasets. Also, we show FC’s versatility in handling horizontal, vertical, and
023            hybrid FL within a unified framework on multiple classification tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Probabilistic Circuits (PCs) are a family of models that provide tractable inference for various
029
      probabilistic queries (Poon & Domingos, 2011; Choi et al., 2020). This is achieved by representing a
030
      joint distribution by a computation graph on which certain structural properties are imposed. While
031   PCs offer significant computational advantages over traditional probabilistic models such as Bayesian
032   networks (Pearl, 1985), further performance gains can be realized by optimizing the compactness of
033   PC representations and tailoring them to specific hardware architectures (Peharz et al., 2020a; Liu
034   et al., 2024). However, another natural way to scale up PCs by distributing the model over multiple
035   machines is so far underexplored. While models like neural networks can be partitioned over multiple
036   machines with relatively low efforts, partitioning PCs is more challenging as they come with certain
037   structural constraints to ensure the validity of the represented joint distribution. Interestingly, we find
038   an inherent connection between the structure of PCs and the paradigm of federated learning (FL). In
039
      PCs, sum nodes combine probability distributions over the same set of variables via a mixture. This
      resembles the horizontal FL (Konečnỳ et al., 2016; Li et al., 2020) setting, where all clients hold the
040
      same features but different samples. In contrast, the case of vertical FL (Yang et al., 2019; Wu et al.,
041
      2020) in which the same samples are shared, but features are split across clients, can be linked to the
042   product nodes used in PCs, which combine distributions of a disjoint set of variables. Consequently,
043   the hybrid FL (Zhang et al., 2020) setting, where both samples and features are separated across
044   clients, can be represented by a combination of sum and product nodes. Thus, PCs are well positioned
045   to connect all three FL settings in a unified way – an endeavor considered hard to achieve in the FL
046   community (Li et al., 2023a; Wen et al., 2023).
047
      As a result of this connection, we introduce federated circuits (FCs), a novel FL framework that
048   re-frames FL as a density estimation problem over a set of datasets distributed over multiple machines
049   (subsequently called clients). FCs naturally handle all three FL settings and, therefore, provide a
050   flexible way of scaling up PCs by learning a joint distribution over a dataset arbitrarily partitioned
051   across a set of clients (see Fig. 1 for an illustration). Imposing the same structural properties
052   as for PCs, FCs achieve tractable computation of probabilistic queries like marginalization and
053   conditioning across multiple machines. To this end, we propose a highly communication-efficient
      learning algorithm that leverages the semi-ring structure within the design of FCs. Our experimental

093
094   2.1      P ROBABILISTIC C IRCUITS
095
      PCs encode a probability distribution as a computation graph that allows for tractable inference of a
096
      wide range of queries such as conditional (partial evidence) and marginalization. Peharz et al. (2015b)
097
      define a PC over random variables X as a tuple (G, ϕ) where G = (V, E) is a rooted, Directed
098   Acyclic Graph (DAG) and ϕ : V → 2X is the scope function assigning a subset of random variables
099   to each node in G. For each internal node N of G the scope is defined as the union of scopes of its
100   children ch(N). Each leaf node L computes a distribution/density over its scope. All internal nodes of
101   G are either a sum node P S or a product node P where each sum node computes a convex combination
102   of its children, i.e. S = N∈ch(S) wS,N N, and each product node computes a product of its children,
                 Q
103   i.e. P = N∈ch(P) N. To ensure tractability of probabilistic queries such as marginalization, a PC
104   must be decomposable. Decomposability requires that for all P ∈ V it holds that ϕ(N) ∩ ϕ(N′ ) = ∅
105   where N, N′ ∈ ch(P). To further ensure that a PC represents a valid distribution, smoothness must
106   hold, i.e., for each sum S ∈ V it holds that ϕ(N) = ϕ(N′ ) where N, N′ ∈ ch(S) (Peharz et al., 2015b).

      Decomposable and smooth PCs are often referred to as Sum-Product Networks (SPNs) (Poon &
109   Domingos, 2011; Peharz et al., 2015a; Sánchez-Cauce et al., 2021).
110
111   Several works have tackled the goal of scaling PCs. On the architecture side, it was shown that large,
112
      random structures can be used to scale to larger problems more easily (Peharz et al., 2020b). Changes
      in the model layout, such as parallelizable layers and the einsum-operation (Peharz et al., 2020a) and
113
      a reduction in IO operations (Liu et al., 2024), were also shown to drastically reduce the speed of
114
      computation. Liu et al. (2022) improved the performance of PCs by latent variable distillation, where
115   deep generative models give additional supervision during the learning process.
116
117
      2.2   F EDERATED L EARNING
118
119   In federated learning (FL), a set of data owners (or clients) aim to collaboratively learn an ML model
120   without sharing their data. One distinguishes between horizontal, vertical, and hybrid FL depending
121   on how data is partitioned. In horizontal FL, a dataset D ∈ Rn×d is partitioned s.t. each client holds
122   the same d features but different, non-overlapping sets of samples. In vertical FL, D is partitioned s.t.
123   each client holds the same n samples but different, non-overlapping subsets of the d features. Hybrid
124
      FL describes a combination of horizontal and vertical FL where clients can hold both different (but
      possibly overlapping) sets of samples and features (Wen et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a).
125
126   For all three FL settings, specifically tailored methods have been proposed to enable collaborative
127   learning of models. The most common scheme in horizontal FL is to average the models of all clients
128   regularly during training (McMahan et al., 2016; Karimireddy et al., 2020a;b; Sahu et al., 2018).
129   However, model averaging requires each client to share the same model structure. In vertical FL,
130
      clients hold different feature sets; thus, there is no guarantee that the model structure can be shared
      among clients. In these cases, tree-based and neural models are the predominant choice and are
131
      typically learned by sharing data statistics or feature representations among clients (Kourtellis et al.,
132
      2016; Cheng et al., 2021; Vepakomma et al., 2018; Ceballos et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Liu
133   et al., 2019). Similar to tree-based vertical FL, tree-based hybrid FL approaches share data statistics
134   (such as histograms) or model properties (such as split rules) among clients (Li et al., 2023b; 2024).
135   However, tree-based approaches often require complex training procedures.

      3     F EDERATED C IRCUITS
139
      This work aims to scale up PCs by splitting data and the model across multiple machines, thus
140
      harnessing the availability of compute clusters to train PCs in a federated fashion. In the following,
141   we present an elegant and effective way to achieve that using our novel federated learning framework
142   called federated circuits (FCs). FCs unify horizontal, vertical, and hybrid FL by hierarchically
143   learning mixtures (horizontal part) and fusing marginals (vertical part).
144

178   3.2   B RIDGING P ROBABILISTIC C IRCUITS AND F EDERATED L EARNING
179
      We now illustrate an inherent connection between PC semantics and FL. This will allow us to train
180
      PCs on data partitioned over a set of clients and thus greatly increase the scaling potential of PCs.
181
182   Sum Nodes and Horizontal FL. In horizontal FL, each client is assumed to hold the same set
183   of features, i.e., Xc = Xc′ for all c, c′ ∈ C. However, each client holds a different subset of the
184
      data. Prominent horizontal FL methods solve this task by aggregating the model parameters of
      locally learned models regularly. However, the horizontal FL setting also precisely corresponds to
185
      the interpretation of sum nodes in PCs: A sum node splits a dataset into multiple disjoint clusters.
186
      The distribution over the entire data is then represented as a mixture of the distributions learned from
187   the disjoint clusters. Thus, instead of aggregating model parameters, we aggregate the distributions
188   learned by each client on its data partition.
189
      Definition 1 (Horizontal FL). Assume a set of samples Dc ∼ pc on each client c ∈ C, a joint
190
      distribution p adhering to Assumption 1 and thatP    Xc = Xc′ for all c, c′ ∈ C s.t. c ̸= c′ . We define
191   horizontal FL as fitting a mixture distribution p̂ = c∈C q(c) · p̂c such that d(p̂, p) and d(pc , p̂c ) are
192   minimal for all c ∈ C where d is a distance metric and p̂c local distribution estimates.
193
194   This view on horizontal FL has an appealing positive side effect: Aggregating model parameters can
195   lead to divergence during training if the client’s data distributions significantly differ. We circumvent
196   the burden of aggregating model parameters by forming a mixture of local models that can be learned
197
      independently. Thus, we do not require further assumptions on the client’s distributions. Also, since
      clients can train models independently, the communication cost of the training is minimized.
198
199   Product Nodes & Vertical FL. In vertical FL, each client is assumed to hold a disjoint set of
200   features, i.e., Xc ∩ Xc′ = ∅ for all c, c′ ∈ C. In contrast to horizontal FL, all clients hold different
201   features belonging to the same sample instances. As in horizontal FL, there is a semantic connection
202   between vertical FL and PCs. Product nodes in PCs compute a product distribution defined on a
203
      disjoint set of random variables. Thus, a product node separates the data along the feature dimension,
      corresponding to the vertical FL setting. However, a product node assumes the random variables of
204
      the child distributions to be independent of each other. Obviously, this is an unrealistic assumption
205
      for vertical FL, where features held by different clients might be statistically dependent. To capture
206   such dependencies, Assumption 2 can be exploited, and a mixture over multiple product distributions
207   can be formed. We will discuss this in detail in Sec. 3.3.
208
      Definition 2 (Vertical FL). Assume a set of samples Dc ∼ pc on each data owner c ∈ C, the
209   existence of a joint distribution p adhering to Assumptions 1 and 2 and that Xc ∩ Xc′ = ∅ holds
210   for all c, c′ ∈RC s.t. c ̸= c′ . We define vertical FL as estimating a joint distribution p̂ s.t. d(p, p̂) is
211   minimal and X\Xc p̂(x) = p̂c (x) for all x ∈ X where d is a distance metric and p̂c are estimates of
212   client distributions.
213
214   PCs & Hybrid FL. Given Defs. 1 and 2, hybrid FL is a combination of both. In terms of PC
215   semantics, this amounts to building a hierarchy of fusing marginals and learning mixtures. Provided
      with these probabilistic semantics, we can now formally bridge PCs and FL. In the following, we

      distinguish between clients C and servers S and define the set of machines participating in training as
217   N = C ∪ S. Bringing everything together and abstracting from the probabilistic interpretation, we
218   define federated circuits (FCs) as follows.
219
      Definition 3 (Federated Circuits). A federated circuit (FC) is a tuple (G, ψG , ω) where G = (V, E)
220
      is a rooted, Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG), ψG : V → N assigns each N ∈ V to a compute node
221
      n ∈ N based on the structure of G and ω : V → O assigns an operation o ∈ O to each node N ∈ V
222   where o : dom(ch(N)) → dom(N) computes the value of N given the values of the children of N.
223
224   FCs extend the definition of PCs in the sense that FCs represent a computational graph G = (V, E)
225   distributed over multiple machines where arbitrary operations can be performed in each node N ∈ V .
226   Depending on the parameterization of leaves and nodes N, FCs are not restricted to the probabilistic
227   interpretation presented above. For example, parameterizing leaves by decision trees and introducing
228   a node N that performs averaging yields a bagging model.

230   3.3   F EDERATED P ROBABILISTIC C IRCUITS
231
232
      Let us now dive deeper into the probabilistic interpretation of FCs. To that end, we present a concrete
      instantiation of FCs leveraging Probabilistic Circuits (PCs) as leaf models, resulting in federated PCs
233
      (FedPCs). Following the probabilistic interpretation from Sec. 3.2, we align the PC structure with the
234
      communication network structure to form a federated PC.
235
236
      Definition 4 (Federated PC). A Federated PC (FedPC) is a FC where each leaf node C is a density
      estimator and each node N s.t. ch(N) ̸= ∅ is either a sum node (S) or a product node (P).
237
238
      Note that only the client nodes C hold a dataset and we only demand the clients to be parameterized
239   by a density estimator. In order for FedPCs to be computationally efficient, these density estimators
240   should be tractable. In the following, we parameterize the leaf nodes C as PCs.
241
242
      The operation assignment ω is omitted in FedPCs as the operations performed by each node are
      implicitly defined (sum or product). The assignment function ϕ transforms the PC’s computation
243
      graph into a distributed computation graph aligned to the communication network. This establishes a
244
      direct correspondence between PC semantics (computation graph) and the communication network
245   structure in FedPCs. Inference is performed as usual in PCs by propagating likelihood values from
246   the leaf nodes to the root node. The only difference is that the result of a node N has to be sent to its
247   parent(s) pa(N) over the communication network if ψ(N) ̸= ψ(N′ ) holds for N′ ∈ pa(N).
248
      Training FedPCs requires adapting the regular training procedure for PCs. This is mainly because
249
      not all clients can access the same samples if data is partitioned horizontally or hybrid. Since a
250
      forward pass through a PC requires the same sample to be available on each leaf, prominent learning
251   algorithms such as Expectation Maximization (EM) are not directly applicable in horizontal and
252   hybrid FL settings. In the following, we propose a one-pass training procedure of FedPCs that does
253   not require a full forward or backward pass over the model.
254

255

261

262

263

340   3.4     A NALYSIS OF C OMMUNICATION E FFICIENCY
341
      Communication efficiency is a key requirement for efficient training when learning models on scale
342
      on partitioned data, such as in FL. We now analyze the communication efficiency of FedPCs.
343
344   Horizontal FL. Assume a client set C where each client holds a model with M parameters. Further,
345   assume models are aggregated K times during training (K communication rounds). Then, model
346   aggregation-based algorithms like FedAvg commonly used in horizontal FL send O(M · |C| · K)
347
      messages over the network as each client sends M model parameters to a server in each communi-
      cation round. Training FedPCs with one-pass training, in contrast, only requires O(|C| · (M + 1))
348
      messages over the network as models are learned locally and independently of each other, followed
349
      by setting the parameters (O(|C|) messages) of the sum nodes and aggregating the model on the
350   server (O(M |C|) messages).
351
352   Vertical FL. In vertical settings, SplitNN-like architectures are commonly used. Assume training a
353
      SplitNN architecture for E epochs that output a feature vector of size F for each sample of a dataset
      with S samples, vertically distributed over clients C. The training requires sending O(E · |C| · F · S)
354
      messages over the network. In contrast, with one-pass training of FedPCs, each client learns a
355
      dedicated PC with M parameters for each of the K clusters that are learned. The last layer of the
356   FedPC is a mixture of P products of clusters. The mixture parameters are set after training each
357   client’s model. Aggregating the learned models and setting the network-sided mixture parameters
                                                                           P
358   requires O(K · M · |C| + P ) messages to be sent. If (K · M + |C|      ) < (E · F · S) holds, training
359   FedPCs is more communication efficient than training SplitNN-like architectures. In practice, this
360   is likely to hold: The number of clusters is usually smaller than 100 while feature vectors can have
361   hundreds of dimensions (i.e., F > 100). Further, models should have fewer parameters than samples
362   in the dataset to ensure generalization (i.e., M < S). P can be set to an arbitrary value, depending on
363   |C| and the data. App. E provides more details and an intuition on communication costs.
364   Hybrid FL. In hybrid FL, FedPCs are trained on several subspaces: There are subspaces present on
365   all or a subset of clients (denoted as Rs ) and there are subspaces only available on one client (denoted
366   as Rd ). Further denote communication costs of FedPCs in horizontal FL and vertical FL as Ch and
367   Cv , respectively. Since the training procedure in hybrid cases essentially performs horizontal FL on
368   shared feature spaces and vertical FL on disjoint feature spaces, O(|Rs | · Ch + |Rv | · Cv ) messages
369   are sent over the network during training.
370   Remark 1. When scaling PCs using FedPCs, we do not aggregate the models after training. This
371   distributes computation load across multiple machines also during inference and further decreases
372   communication costs during training.

506   5   C ONCLUSION
507
508   In this work, we introduced federated circuits that hinge on an inherent connection between PCs
509   and FL. We demonstrated that both the training speed and expressivity of PCs can be increased
510   by learning PCs on scale across partitioned data. Since our framework allows for the integration
511
      of various types of density estimators, other models and advances of PCs and other fields can be
      integrated seamlessly, maintaining the relevance of the federated approach for scaling.
512
513   Limitations and Future Work. While our experiments showed that scaling PCs can considerably
514   improve training speed and performance, scaling to such large-scale models requires sufficient
515   computational resources. For future work, investigating other parametrizations for FCs beyond PCs
516   is promising. Additionally, it is interesting how the probabilistic framework for hybrid FL could also
517
      benefit more traditional FL applications, apart from scaling PCs.

898   F.1   DATASETS
899
900   The following describes the datasets used in our experiments. If not stated differently, the datasets
901   were distributed across clients as follows:
902
      In horizontal cases, we either split samples randomly across clients (done for all binary classification
903
      tasks) or we distribute a subset of the dataset corresponding to a certain label (e.g. the 0 in MNIST)
904
      to one client.
905
906   In vertical cases, we split tabular datasets randomly along the feature-dimension, i.e. each client
907   gets all samples but a random subset of features assigned. For image data, we split the images into
908
      non-overlapping patches which were then distributed to the clients.
909   In hybrid cases, we split tabular datasets along both, the feature and the sample-dimension. We do
910   this s.t. at least two clients have at least one randomly chosen feature in commeon (but hold different
911   samples thereof). For image data, we split images into overlapping patches, sample a subset of the
912   dataset and assign the resulting subsets to clients.
913   Income Dataset. We used the Income dataset from https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/
914   wenruliu/adult-income-dataset. This dataset represents a binary classification problem
915   with 14 features and approximate 450K samples in the train and 900 samples in the test set. We
916   encoded discrete variables to numerical values using TargetEncoder from sklearn. Additionally,
917   missing values were imputed using the median of the corresponding feature. Further we standardized
      all features.


                                                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Breast Cancer Dataset. We used the Breast Cancer dataset from https://www.kaggle.com/
919   datasets/uciml/breast-cancer-wisconsin-data. It represents a binary classifica-
920   tion problem with 31 features and 570 samples. We split the dataset into 450 training samples and
921   120 test samples. We standardized all features for training.
922
      Credit Dataset. We used the Give Me Some Credit dataset from https://www.kaggle.com/
923
      c/GiveMeSomeCredit. The dataset represents a binary classification task with 10 features, 1.5M
924
      training samples and 100K test samples. We encoded discrete variables to numerical values using
925   TargetEncoder from sklearn. Additionally, missing values were imputed using the median of the
926   corresponding feature. Further we standardized all features.
927
928
      MNIST. We used the MNIST dataset provided by pytorch. It contains 70K hand-written digits
      between 0 and 9 as 28x28 images (60K train, 10K test). We standardized all features as preprocessing.
929
930   Imagenet/Imagenet32. We used the Imagenet dataset provided by pytorch. It consists of about
931   1.2M images showing objects of 1000 classes. The images come in different resolutions; we
932   resized each image to 112x112 (Imagenet) and 32x32 (Imagenet32) pixels, applied center cropping,
933   and standardized all features as preprocessing. In our experiments, we used a pre-trained Vision
934
      Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) to obtain encodings of each image. Then, we applied
      KMeans to cluster the dataset into n clusters (depending on the number of clients participating).
935
      Images of each cluster were then distributed to the clients, defining the client’s datasets.
