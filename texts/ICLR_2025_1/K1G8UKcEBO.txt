009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We consider a class of structured, nonconvex, nonsmooth optimization problems
012            under orthogonality constraints, where the objectives combine a smooth function,
013            a nonsmooth concave function, and a nonsmooth weakly convex function. This
014            class of problems finds diverse applications in statistical learning and data science.
015            Existing methods for addressing these problems often fail to exploit the specific
016            structure of orthogonality constraints, struggle with nonsmooth functions, or re-
017            sult in suboptimal oracle complexity. We propose OADMM, an Alternating Di-
018
               rection Method of Multipliers (ADMM) designed to solve this class of problems
               using efficient proximal linearized strategies. Two specific variants of OADMM
019
               are explored: one based on Euclidean Projection (OADMM-EP) and the other on
020
               Riemannian Retraction (OADMM-RR). Under mild assumptions, we prove that
021            OADMM converges to a critical point of the problem with an ergodic convergence
022            rate of O(1/Пө3 ). Additionally, we establish a super-exponential convergence rate
023            or polynomial convergence rate for OADMM, depending on the specific setting,
024            under the Kurdyka-Lojasiewicz (KL) inequality. To the best of our knowledge,
025            this is the first non-ergodic convergence result for this class of nonconvex nons-
026            mooth optimization problems. Numerical experiments demonstrate that the pro-
027            posed algorithm achieves state-of-the-art performance.
028            Keywords: Orthogonality Constraints; Nonconvex Optimization; Nonsmooth
029            Composite Optimization; ADMM; Convergence Analysis

058   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
059
060   в–¶ Optimization under Orthogonality Constraints. Solving Problem (1) is challenging due to
061   the computationally expensive and non-convex orthogonality constraints. Existing methods can be
062   divided into three classes. (i) Geodesic-like methods (Edelman et al., 1998; Abrudan et al., 2008;
063   Absil et al., 2008b; Jiang & Dai, 2015). These methods involve calculating geodesics by solv-
064
      ing ordinary differential equations, which can introduce significant computational complexity. To
      mitigate this, geodesic-like methods iteratively compute the geodesic logarithm using simple lin-
065
      ear algebra calculations. Efficient constraint-preserving update schemes have been integrated with
066
      the Barzilai-Borwein (BB) stepsize strategy (Wen & Yin, 2013; Jiang & Dai, 2015) for minimizing
067   smooth functions under orthogonality constraints. (ii) Projection and retractions methods (Absil
068   et al., 2008b; Golub & Van Loan, 2013). These methods maintain orthogonality constraints through
069   projection or retraction. They reduce the objective value by using its current Euclidean gradient
070   direction or Riemannian tangent direction, followed by an orthogonal projection operation. This
071   projection can be computed using polar decomposition or singular value decomposition, or approx-
072   imated with QR factorization. (iii) Multiplier correction methods (Gao et al., 2018; 2019; Xiao
073   et al., 2022). Leveraging the insight that the Lagrangian multiplier associated with the orthogonality
074   constraint is symmetric and has an explicit closed-form expression at the first-order optimality con-
075
      dition, these methods tackle an alternative unconstrained nonlinear objective minimization problem,
      rather than the original smooth function under orthogonality constraints.
076
077   в–¶ Optimization with Nonsmooth Objectives. Another challenge in addressing Problem (1) stems
078   from the nonsmooth nature of the objective function. Existing methods for tackling this challenge
079   fall into three main categories. (i) Subgradient methods (Ferreira & Oliveira, 1998; Hwang et al.,
080   2015; Li et al., 2021). Subgradient methods, analogous to gradient descent methods, can incorpo-
081
      rate various geodesic-like and projection-like techniques. However, they often exhibit slower con-
      vergence rates compared to other approaches. (ii) Proximal gradient methods (Chen et al., 2020).
082
      These methods use a semi-smooth Newton approach to solve a strongly convex minimization prob-
083
      lem over the tangent space, finding a descent direction while preserving the orthogonality constraint
084   through a retraction operation. (iii) Operator splitting methods (Lai & Osher, 2014; Chen et al.,
085   2016; Zhang et al., 2020b). These methods introduce linear constraints to break down the original
086   problem into simpler subproblems that can be solved separately and exactly. Among these, ADMM
087   is a promising solution for Problem (1) due to its capability to handle nonsmooth objectives and
088   nonconvex constraints separately and alternately. Several ADMM-like algorithms have been pro-
089   posed for solving nonconvex problems (BotМ§ & Nguyen, 2020; BotМ§ et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019; Li
090   & Pong, 2015; He & Yuan, 2012; Yuan, 2024; Zhang et al., 2020b), but these methods fail to exploit
091   the specific structure of orthogonality constraints or cannot be adapted to solve Problem (1). (iv)
092
      Other methods. OBCD (Yuan, 2023) has been proposed to solve a specific class of our problems,
      while the exact augmented Lagrangian method ManIAL was introduced in (Deng et al., 2024).
