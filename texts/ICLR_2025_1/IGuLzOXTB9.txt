009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Many existing evaluation benchmarks for Large Language Models (LLMs)
012            quickly become outdated due to the emergence of new models and training data.
013            These benchmarks also fall short in assessing how LLM performance changes
014            over time, as they consist of static questions without a temporal dimension. To
015            address these limitations, we propose using future event prediction as a contin-
016
               uous evaluation method to assess LLMs’ temporal generalization and forecast-
               ing abilities. Our benchmark, Daily Oracle, automatically generates question-
017
               answer (QA) pairs from daily news, challenging LLMs to predict “future” event
018
               outcomes. Our findings reveal that as pre-training data becomes outdated, LLM
019            performance degrades over time. While Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)
020            has the potential to enhance prediction accuracy, the performance degradation pat-
021            tern persists, highlighting the need for continuous model updates.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Traditional Large Language Model (LLM) benchmarks are often static, and do not reflect real-world
027
      information that evolves over time. This presents two significant challenges. First, as LLMs are
      updated, there is a risk that the static benchmarks become outdated and more vulnerable to data
028
      leakage, where their content might end up in the training data of newer models. This undermines
029
      the reliability of performance assessments on these benchmarks (Sainz et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024;
030   McIntosh et al., 2024; Li & Flanigan, 2024). Second, the static benchmarks often lack the temporal
031   information to track the model’s performance variations over time (McIntosh et al., 2024). This cre-
032   ates a need for evaluation methods that stay relevant over time and incorporate temporal dynamics.
033
      Daily news provides a natural setting for continuous evaluation of LLMs. Since the world is con-
034
      stantly changing, a benchmark designed around forecasting the next day’s news will never be out of
035
      date by construction. In addition to enabling continuous evaluation, forecasting is itself a longstand-
036   ing challenge with significant implications across various domains, including healthcare, finance,
037   and policymaking (Tetlock & Gardner, 2016; Dempsey et al., 2017; Gillingham et al., 2018; Lopez-
038   Lira & Tang, 2023). While human experts have traditionally made such forecasts, machine learning
039   models, particularly LLMs, have emerged as promising alternatives due to their capability to learn
040   from vast and diverse corpora (Halawi et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2023). Several recent
041   forecasting question-answer (QA) datasets have been developed (Jin et al., 2021; Zou et al., 2022;
042   Zhang et al., 2024), however, they are limited in either size, scope, or do not continuously keep pace
043   with the rapidly changing world. More critically, the extent to which LLMs’ predictive abilities
044
      change over time remains underexplored.
045   In this work, we propose Daily Oracle—a continuous evaluation benchmark that uses automatically
046   generated QA pairs from daily news to assess how the future prediction capabilities of LLMs evolve
047   over time. The QA pairs are generated on a daily basis, consisting of True/False (TF) and Multiple
048   Choice (MC) questions across various categories such as business, politics, and arts. Unlike tradi-
049   tional reading comprehension tasks, these QA pairs are designed to challenge LLMs to predict future
050
      events based on their own existing knowledge, effectively evaluating their temporal generalization
      and forecasting abilities.
051
052   We continuously evaluate various LLMs, both with and without access to a limited archive of news
053   articles. Our experiments reveal that LLMs experience an average performance decline of 20.14%
      on True/False (TF) questions and 23.26% on Multiple Choice (MC) questions between January 2020

      and September 2024, with degradation becoming more pronounced before and after the models’
055   knowledge cutoff dates. Although models utilizing Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis

060
      To summarize, our key contributions are two-fold:
061
062   • Continuous Forecasting Evaluation Benchmark: We present Daily Oracle, the largest and most
063     up-to-date forecasting dataset, composed of automatically generated QA pairs. This benchmark
064     continuously evaluates LLMs’ temporal generalization and future prediction abilities using daily
065     news, ensuring relevance over time and offering a challenging evaluation framework.
066
      • Empirical Findings on Performance Degradation: Our experiments demonstrate a consistent
067
        decline in LLM prediction accuracy over time, even when supplemented with recent “open-book”
068
        information. The persistent degradation highlights the challenges posed by outdated pretraining
069     data in LLMs and underscores the necessity for continuous model updating.

072   2   R ELATED W ORK
073
074   Temporal Generalization of LLMs. Lazaridou et al. (2021) define temporal generalization as the
075
      ability of Language Models to generalize well to future data from beyond their training period. They
      demonstrate that Transformer-XL’s performance deteriorates over time, evidenced by increasing
076
      perplexity when evaluated on post-training data. However, perplexity-based metrics have two main
077
      limitations: they cannot be applied to closed-source models lacking accessible logits, and increased
078   perplexity does not necessarily indicate degraded performance on downstream tasks (Röttger &
079   Pierrehumbert, 2021; Agarwal & Nenkova, 2022). Zhu et al. (2024) investigate temporal generation
080   using the Bits Per Character (BPC) metric. Similar to perplexity, BPC fails to capture higher-level
081   performance on downstream tasks. In contrast, our work focuses on evaluating how well models
082   acquire and utilize real-world event knowledge in downstream forecasting tasks, providing a more
083   nuanced assessment of temporal generalization.
084
085   Dynamic QA Datasets. While static QA datasets evaluate models on fixed knowledge snapshots,
086   dynamic QA datasets incorporate a temporal dimension, allowing assessment of how models adapt
087
      to evolving information. Several dynamic QA datasets are proposed. Chen et al. (2021) construct
      TimeQA by using time-sensitive facts in WikiData with aligned Wikipedia passages to synthesize
088
      QA pairs. Zhang & Choi (2021) introduce SituatedQA by manually annotating temporally and ge-
089
      ographically dependent questions. StreamingQA (Liska et al., 2022) and RealtimeQA (Kasai et al.,
090   2024) are both dynamic benchmarks with QA pairs answerable from news articles. StreamingQA,
091   however, does not provide continuous evaluation with always-relevant data. RealTimeQA does not
092   address forecasting and is more like a plugin for a search engine, in the sense that it tests whether
093   a model has updated its knowledge as facts change, rather than testing whether it can predict what
094   will change given its knowledge of the past. FreshQA (Vu et al., 2024) contains a fixed set of
095   human-written open-ended questions whose answers by nature can change based on new develop-
096   ments in the world, but is smaller and does not address forecasting. It is also updated weekly rather
097   than daily. While all these datasets have some form of time-sensitivity like the Daily Oracle, they
098
      either do not provide continuous evaluation or do not evaluate forecasting capabilities, or neither.
099
      Forecasting Datasets. Forecasting questions aim to assess a model’s ability to predict the out-
100
      comes of future events based on its existing knowledge. Several datasets in the event forecasting
101
      field have been introduced. ForecastQA (Jin et al., 2021) used crowdworkers to collect 10,392 QA
102   pairs from news articles. Zou et al. (2022) argue that the QA pairs from ForecastQA are often non-
103   sensical or ambiguous since they are written by humans without forecasting expertise. They further
104   introduce AutoCast, a forecasting dataset from popular human forecasting tournaments containing
105   6,707 QA pairs. In contrast, our Daily Oracle dataset is generated automatically from daily news
106   articles, which means that it is never out of date, can easily grow its size automatically without addi-
107   tional inputs from human forecasters, and provides more comprehensive event coverage than human
      forecasting tournaments.

126   Similar to our generation method, TCELongBench (Zhang et al., 2024) has an automatic forecasting
127   QA generation framework using news articles. However, their TLB-forecast dataset is constrained
128
      both temporally and topically, and only contains cooperation and conflict events in Middle-Eastern
      countries from 2015 to 2022. This restricts the dataset from evaluating more general event-prediction
129
      abilities. Furthermore, considering most of the powerful LLMs have been developed after 2020,
130
      the portions of the dataset covering earlier years may contain answers already seen during training.
131   This prior exposure compromises the dataset’s effectiveness as a forecasting benchmark. In contrast,
132   our dataset spans a broader timeframe and covers more topics, offering a more comprehensive and
133   reliable forecasting benchmark.
134
      Note that none of the aforementioned datasets provide insights into how prediction ability changes
135
      over time. Zhu et al. (2024) introduce FreshBench, a forecasting dataset scraped from the Good-
136
      JudgmentOpen platform, and also study temporal generalization. However, they report accuracy
137   in a relatively short time window (from January 2023 to April 2024) with only 2,532 questions.
138   While we observe a gradual performance decline in our dataset, they report significant fluctuations
139   in model accuracy shortly after release. We argue that our dataset presents a more challenging task,
140   as evidenced by the continuous degradation in model performance over time, making it a robust
141   benchmark for assessing LLMs’ temporal generalization.
142
      In order to clearly showcase the differences between our dataset and prior work, we highlight a few
143   key features in Table 1. The Daily Oracle is the only benchmark which is continuously updated on
144   a daily basis and evaluates forecasting ability. Additionally, at the fixed size we use for analysis we
145   provide significantly more evaluation examples than the other automatically updated benchmarks.

      3     T HE DAILY O RACLE DATASET
148
149   In this section, we present Daily Oracle, a continuously updated QA benchmark of forecasting
150   questions that are automatically generated from daily news. For our current analysis of LLM perfor-
151   mance, we utilize a subset of the data consisting of 16,082 TF questions and 13,906 MC questions,
152   covering a diverse range of forecasting topics, which are generated using daily news articles from
153   January 2020 up until September 2024. However, our QA generation framework is continuous and
154
      updates daily. In section 3.1, we describe our LLM-based dataset construction pipeline, detailing
      the data sources and the four-step construction process. Section 3.2 provides an analysis and general
155
      overview of the dataset.

188   LLM-based Construction Process. QA pairs are generated from articles published between Jan-
189   uary 2020 and September 2024.1 Due to budget constraints, for each day, we select six articles for
190   QA generation: three are chosen randomly, and three are selected from hot topics. Details for how
191   hot topics are chosen can be found in Appendix A.1. For each selected article, we then use LLM to
192   generate two TF QA pairs and two MC QA pairs with the few-shot prompting technique.2
193
      Inspired by Zhang et al. (2024), our QA construction follows four steps, as illustrated in Figure 1(a):
194
195   (1) Article Summary. We generate a summary for each article, focusing on new events from the
196       publishing date, instead of opinion articles discussing events from the past. This approach
197       allows us to use the publication date as the resolution date of the generated question. Questions
198       can then be regarded as valid forecasting questions since they are prior to the resolution date.
199   (2) QA Generation. After filtering out the articles that do not introduce new events, two TF ques-
200       tions and two MC questions are generated together with the answers per article. To ensure
201       balance in the TF questions, we instruct the LLM to generate the first question with a “Yes”
202       answer and the second with a “No.”
203
      (3) Misleading Choices Generation. For MC, we provide the article, its publishing date, and the
204       QA pair to the LLM, which then generates three misleading choices.
205
206   (4) QA Filtering. In the final step, we prompt the LLM to check seven principles: correctness of
207
          answers, non-answerability before the publication date, absence of information leakage, objec-
          tivity, inclusion of a clear temporal element, public interest, and non-obviousness of the answer.
208
          Each principle is scored with 0, 1, or 2 points, and we selected the questions that received at
209
          least 13 points in total.
210
211   These principles are detailed in Appendix A.2. We use GPT-3.5 (OpenAI, 2024) for steps (1) and
212   (4), while GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) is utilized for steps (2) and (3) to ensure high data quality, as
213   GPT-4 is currently the best-performing LLM available.

253   Past and Future Information Usage. Each question in Daily Oracle implicitly requires the model
254   to retrieve relevant knowledge. How do these requirements change day by day over the course of
255   our benchmark? Anderson & Schooler (1991) study a similar relationship in human information
256   environments, specifically in New York Times headlines, children’s verbal interactions, and emails.
257   In Figure 2, we take inspiration from their work and analyze whether a word’s frequency of occur-
258   rence in the past 100 days predicts its occurrence on the next day. In other words, if over the past
259   100 days we have frequently required the model to retrieve specific knowledge, e.g. if there are
260
      many questions about the unemployment rate, is it likely it will have to retrieve this knowledge in
      the future?
261
262   We analyze this relationship for words in the titles of the articles we use to generate questions as
263   well as in the text of the TF and MC questions themselves. Past frequency is computed by checking,
264   for each day in the 100 day window, if a word has occurred in any article title (so, the maximum
265   frequency is 100). We find that there is a linear relationship between the frequency of usage in the
266
      past 100 days and the probability of occurrence on the 101st day in all cases, replicating Anderson
      & Schooler’s findings for New York Times headlines. Interestingly, there is a drop in probability for
267
      both TF and MC questions for words with a frequency of 40, though it is unclear why. There is also
268
      some clustering at lower frequencies, particularly in the TF and MC question plots. Many words
269   appear less than 20 times during the 100 day window. The temporal structure exhibited in the daily
      news stream may be of a future point of interest from a modeling perspective.

288   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
289
      Closed-Book Setting. We evaluate various LLMs on Daily Oracle to assess their understanding
290
      of real-world events and temporal generalization abilities, i.e., how accurately LLMs can answer
291
      forecasting questions based on the knowledge they learned from their training data. Our evaluation
292   differentiates between two scenarios based on the question’s resolution date and model’s knowledge
293   cutoff date: (1) Pre-Knowledge Cutoff Questions: These questions have resolution dates before the
294   model’s knowledge cutoff, testing the model’s understanding of past events. (2) Post-Knowledge
295   Cutoff Questions: These have resolution dates after the knowledge cutoff, requiring models to pre-
296   dict future events and test their forecasting and temporal generalization abilities.
297
      Constrained Open-Book Setting. In addition to a closed-book evaluation, we explore the con-
298
      strained open-book setting: how access to news articles up to different time cutoffs influences LLM
299   performance using RAG (Lewis et al., 2020). We introduce the concept of the RAG cutoff (R-
300   Cutoff ), which limits the latest accessible date for retrieving articles. To prevent the models from
301   leveraging information beyond the resolution date, for any question with a resolution date (dres ),
302   the accessible articles span from January 1st, 2019 (the start of our news corpus) up to whichever
303   comes first between the day before the resolution date and the RAG cutoff date (dR-Cutoff ). Formally,
304   the accessible date range is [01/01/2019, min(dres − 1, dR-Cutoff )). Following prior work (Jin et al.,
305   2021; Zou et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024), we employ BM25 (Robertson et al., 1995) as the re-
306   triever and select the top 5 articles relevant to each question. We truncate each retrieved article to a
307
      maximum length of 512 words. These articles are then incorporated into the input prompts to serve
      as additional information.
308
309   Gold Article Setting. We further include a setting where models are provided direct access to
310   the gold article, from which the question is generated. This transforms the forecasting questions
311   into reading comprehension ones, which can also access LLMs’ general question-answering capa-
312   bilities. Achieving high accuracy here ensures that the questions from our Daily Oracle dataset are
313
      answerable.
314   Metrics. Accuracy score is used as the evaluation metric. Though LLMs are tested daily, to show
315   clearer trends, we plot the monthly performance in Figure 3, and apply a 5-month moving average to
316   smooth the curve. We also report yearly averages and average year-over-year (YoY) accuracy change
317   before and after models’ knowledge cutoff dates in Table 3. Additionally, despite prompting the
318   models to avoid responses like “I cannot predict the future” and instead provide definitive answers,
319
      there are cases where such refusals still occur. The rejection rates are provided in the Appendix B.2,
      and these cases are counted as incorrect to ensure comparability across model results.

      Need for Continuous Pretraining. The overall decline trend may come from two sources, the
      missing knowledge of future and a lack of up-to-date language representation. While the lack of
467
      knowledge can be partially recovered with information retrieval as shown in the constrained open-
468

469   provided the gold articles, the remaining decline in the model’s performance suggests continuous
470   pre-training of LLMs (Jang et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022; Ke et al., 2022a;b; Yıldız et al., 2024) is
471   still needed in the context of news event forecasting to address outdated representations.
472
473   TF & MC Comparison. All models except for Claude-3.5-Sonnet struggle with TF questions,
474   where the degradation trends towards the random baseline accuracy of 50%, indicating that predict-
475   ing if a future event will happen or not can be sometimes challenging for LLMs. In contrast, on MC
476   questions, models tend to perform much better than the random baseline at 25%. There are two po-
477
      tential reasons that can explain the disparity. First, TF questions can be considered more open-ended
      than MC because the “No” answer contains other possible open-ended outcomes. Second, since the
478
      distractor choices are created by an LLM, they may not be as likely to happen as the true answer.

515     Limitations. On the data generation side, the generated questions as well as the distractor answers
516     could contain biases from an outdated LLM, making the benchmark less reliable in the long run
517     unless we upgrade the models. Additionally, generating questions from news articles can introduce
518     bias by focusing only on events that have definitively occurred or not occurred. This approach
519     overlooks speculative events, which may be of interest in forecasting questions but may not be
520
        covered in news articles, limiting the scope of the dataset. On the evaluation side, our paper proposes
        the continuous evaluation benchmark but at the time of the writing there isn’t a long enough time
521
        horizon on each model, especially after the cutoff dates, for a thorough analysis. Ideally, we would
522
        like to analyze the relation between the effect of knowledge and RAG cutoff dates but the trend
523     seems to be weak within the time horizon available.

         5                             C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
526
527     We introduce Daily Oracle, a continuously updated QA benchmark leveraging daily news to evalu-
528     ate the temporal generalization and future prediction capabilities of LLMs. Our experiments reveal
529     that while LLMs maintain a degree of predictive power over future events, their prediction accu-
530     racy exhibits a gradual decline over time across various models. Notably, while the stronger model
531     Claude-3.5-Sonnet outperforms others significantly, it still exhibits around 12% performance drop
532
        in its post-knowledge cutoff period. Although RAG mitigates the effect of outdated knowledge,
        a noticeable decline in performance remains. Our findings underscore the necessity for ongoing
533
        model updates with more current information and emphasize the importance of disentangling miss-
534
        ing knowledge from the lack of up-to-date representations.
535
536     We hope this work will draw attention to the need for more practical applications of the continuous
537     training of LLMs, driving advancements in adapting models to real-time data changes. In the future,
538
        alongside maintaining Daily Oracle, we plan to incorporate a broader range of models and explore
        how continuous pre-training and efficient adaptation can address the performance degradation
539
        challenges presented in our work.

760
761   A.1   D ETAILS FOR A RTICLE S ELECTION
762
763   We select daily articles that generate the QA pairs in two ways: (1) Random Selection: We
764   randomly sample three articles each day. (2) Hot Topic Selection: To better capture daily events and
765   reduce noise, we select three articles from the top three hot topics of the day. We identify these hot
766
      topics by applying the density-based clustering algorithm DBSCAN (Ester et al., 1996) to the new
      articles based on TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) representations, forming
767
      clusters of news articles for each day. We filter out chaotic clusters by removing those with low
768
      average in-cluster cosine similarity scores, which typically correspond to clusters containing a large
769   number of diverse articles. The top three clusters, determined by size, are assumed to represent the
770   most discussed events, i.e. hot topics, since larger clusters indicate more articles covering the same
771   event. One article is picked randomly from each of the top three clusters.
772
773
      A.2   QA F ILTERING P RINCIPLES
774
775   Seven principles of QA Filtering step in the data construction process: (1) Correctness of Answers:
776   The answer must be factually accurate and fully aligned with the information in the given article. (2)
777   Non-answerability Before the Publication Date: Since we treat the article’s publication date as the
778   question’s resolution date, the question should not be definitively answerable based on information
779   available before the article’s publication. (3) Absence of Information Leakage: Questions must avoid
780   revealing information that became known only after the article’s publication, maintaining fairness for
781   pre-publication evaluation. (4) Objectivity: Both questions and answers must rely on objective facts,
782   avoiding subjective ideas from the authors. (5) Inclusion of a Clear Temporal Element: Questions
783
      must contain a specific and clear reference to time, avoiding vague phrases like “in the future” or
      “soon.” (6) Public Interest: The questions should address topics of broad public concern. (7) Non-
784
      obviousness of the Answer: The answer should not be immediately predictable from the question
785
      and must provide new or non-trivial insights.
