009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning large models presents technical challenges such as catastrophic for-
012            getting and parameter inefficiency. Low-rank Adaptation (LoRA) and Propmt
013            Learning can help address some of these challenges by providing more compact
014            and flexible representations. However, Low-rank approximation is susceptible
015            to outliers and relies on the assumption of a global low-rank structure, which
016
               can be suboptimal. Additionally, Prompt learning can overfit to specific down-
               stream tasks, reducing its effectiveness when adapting to new tasks. In this paper,
017
               we introduce Dynamic Prompt-Driven Low-Rank Adaptation (DPD-LoRA), a
018
               novel framework that seamlessly integrates task-specific guidance using hierarchi-
019            cal prompt tokens and parameter-efficient adaptation. Unlike traditional methods,
020            task-aware prompts in the DPD-LoRA dynamically influences low-rank updates in
021            the model’s parameters, thus enabling robust adaptation and generalization across
022            diverse tasks and mitigating the forgetting issues. We further improve the learning
023            capabilities of the model by breaking down the standard LoRA into multiple low-
024            rank sub-matrices, without adding additional parameters. Further, we use an adap-
025            tive loss function to guarantee alignment with the distribution of the pre-trained
026            model. Specifically, we introduce a self-regulated mechanism to improve stability,
027
               and a soft-gated selection mechanism to decide when to activate adaptation mod-
               ules to improve performance on unseen categories. Extensive experiments on 11
028
               benchmark datasets demonstrate that DPD-LoRA significantly outperforms state-
029
               of-the-art methods in both accuracy and generalization, offering a comprehensive
030            solution to the challenges of fine-tuning large-scale models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large Models (LMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various domains, often
035
      achieving state-of-the-art performance. Their robust zero-shot and few-shot learning abilities have
036   positioned them as foundational models in both Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Computer
037   Vision (CV). However, their enormous parameter counts pose computational challenges for train-
038   ing and fine-tuning. For instance, BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) contains approximately 300 million
039   parameters, GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) has 175 billion parameters, and the visual model SAM
040   (Kirillov et al., 2023) possesses 632 million parameters. This trend extends to Vision-Language
041   Models (VLMs) like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024a), which range from 7 billion to 70 billion parameters,
042   following the scaling laws in machine learning.
043   To address this issue, research topics differ in two mainstreams. The parameter-efficient fine-tuning
044   (PEFT) was earlier discovered to fine-tune pre-trained models with only a few parameters. Among
045   PEFT methods, LoRA(Hu et al., 2021) is an undoubtedly notable method that decomposes pre-
046   trained weights into two low-rank matrices and trains only these low-rank matrices, without chang-
047   ing the structure of the original model. Consequently, LoRA can outperform full fine-tuning in some
048   cases. However, LoRA faces limitations, including the risk of catastrophic forgetting of pre-trained
049   knowledge and overfitting to specific downstream tasks (Kalajdzievski, 2024). To address these
050
      shortcomings, we explore the integration of task-specific guidance into the adaptation process. One
      promising direction is the use of prompts, which have been successful in guiding large models to
051
      perform new tasks without modifying their parameters significantly.
052
053   Building on this, prompt learning, initially developed in the NLP community, uses strategically de-
      signed prompts to steer models towards desired behaviors. This approach has been extended to CV

084   Recognizing the limitations of existing approaches, we propose a new paradigm for adapting large

086   introduces a dynamic adaptation process where task-aware signals influence the low-rank updates of

      the model’s parameters. This not only enhances the model’s ability to generalize across diverse tasks

088

      VLMs:Recent developments in foundational vision-language models (VLMs) suggest that multi-
      modal learning has significantly advanced through the integration of paired images and text. Utiliz-
112
      ing massive image-text pair datasets from the web, CLIP(Radford et al., 2021) employs contrastive
113
      learning to enable the model to understand similarities between the visual and textual branches.
114   Similar methodologies are adopted in ALIGN(Jia et al., 2021), LiT(Zhai et al., 2022), FLIP(Li
115   et al., 2023), and Florence(Yuan et al., 2021). These notable approaches involve scaling up the
116   models by using large amounts of data, increasing batch sizes, and enhancing model dimensions.
117   Consequently, these models provide robust few-shot or zero-shot capabilities due to their enormous
118   parameter sizes. However, a large number of parameters also introduces a side-effect; it becomes
119   impractical to adapt these models to challenging downstream tasks. Our study is motivated to inte-
120   grate Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods and Prompt Learning to minimize the need
121   for parameter tuning in pretrained models while maintaining their generalization capabilities.
122   PEFT: PEFT reduces the cost of fine-tuning large models by updating a small subset of parameters.
123   Beyond prompt learning, PEFT methods mainly involve two approaches: adapter-based learning
124   and low-rank adaptations. Adapter-based learning inserts modules into a frozen backbone (Houlsby
125   et al., 2019; Mahabadi et al., 2021; He et al., 2021) but can increase inference latency. Low-rank
126   adaptations like LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and its variants (Liu et al., 2024b) introduce low-rank
127   matrices to approximate weight updates, integrating them with frozen weights during inference.
128
      Zhang et al. (2023) enhances this by using SVD decomposition and pruning insignificant singular
      values. Subsequent variants like QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024) combine quantization with low-
129
      rank adapters, and VeRA (Kopiczko et al., 2023) uses vector-based random matrices for adaptation.
130
      However, dependency on model dimensions limits scalability. To address this, LoRA-XS (Bałazy
131   et al., 2024) employs SVD of pre-trained weights to initialize and freeze projection matrices, greatly
132   reducing trainable parameters independently of model size. Similarly, SVFT (Lingam et al., 2024)
133   leverages outer products of singular vectors, training only coefficients to achieve higher accuracy
134   and fine-grained control. Both methods emphasize the role of singular vectors in weight updates,
135   suggesting potential for further SVD-based PEFT advancements.
136
      Prompt learning: Prompts initially started with discrete, human-crafted templates in the NLP com-
137   munity, such as “a photo of a <CLASS>” for CLIP-like models(Cherti et al., 2023). However,
138   designing an appropriate prompt can be abstract, as it is challenging to determine which types of
139   prompts best fit specific tasks. Recent methods propose treating prompts as learnable vectors in an
140   end-to-end manner while keeping the model’s parameters frozen. Specifically, CoOP(Zhou et al.,
141   2022b) demonstrates that randomly initializing a set of vectors as prompts in the text encoder of
142   the CLIP model achieves results comparable to those of human-designed prompts. Furthermore,
143   CocoOp(Zhou et al., 2022a) and MaPLE(Khattak et al., 2023a) further utilize prompts on the image
144   encoder. The former employs a lightweight network on the image encoder to generate an instance-
145
      wise constraint for textual-branch prompts, while the latter adopts similar ideas from VPT(Jia et al.,
      2022) to innovatively add prompts on both visual and linguistic branches with a coupled relationship.
146
      Although all these methods demonstrate the effective transfer of prior knowledge from CLIP, they
147
      may also negatively affect generalization capabilities. Thus, PromptSRC(Khattak et al., 2023b) pro-
148   poses a self-regulated strategy to minimize the divergence between the zero-shot CLIP and Prompted
149   CLIP models. And many other approaches(Li et al., 2024) utilize more powerful models to provide
150   prior to increase model’s capability, or like(Yao et al., 2023; Roy & Etemad, 2023) introduce Large
151   language models to implement augmentations in textual branch to help generalization.

      3.4   P ROMPT-C ONDITIONED G ATING M ECHANISM (PCGM)
292
293   Effective adaptation in large models requires a cohesive integration of task-specific information into
294   the model’s internal parameters. A straightforward approach might involve independently applying
295   prompts to guide LoRA layers without coordination. However, such methods lack synergy between
296   the input prompts and the adaptation layers, potentially leading to suboptimal performance, as the
297   ablation study shows in Tab. 5.
298   To address this, we propose a soft-gated mechanism, Prompt-Conditioned Gating (PCGM), that
299   dynamically modulates LoRA layers based on task-specific prompts. Specifically, DPD-LoRA in-
300   troduces a gating function G(·), which takes prompt tokens P as input and outputs gating values
301   for each LoRA layer, controlling their contributions during inference. Acting as a bridge between
302   prompt embeddings and LoRA adaptations, the gating function ensures task-relevant information
303   directly influences internal computations. Implemented as a learnable mapping, this mechanism
304   promotes interaction between prompts and parameter updates through a shared gate, fostering co-
305
      herent and synergistic adaptation. Using a single gating function across prompts ensures consistent

      D.5    I NTERPRETATION
865
866   The introduction of the prompt P into the LoRA mechanism does not alter the fundamental role or
867   operation of the low-rank matrices A and B. Instead, it extends their application scope by adding
868   new inputs for the model to handle. Specifically, A and B continue to adjust the weights WQ ,
869   WK , and WV through low-rank updates. However, with P ’s incorporation, these matrices now
870   accommodate both the original input X and the additional information provided by P .
871   Our experimental results (see Tab. 5) indicate that prompts contribute significantly to the LoRA
872   layer. The bolded rows show that prompts provide the low-rank matrices A and B with additional
873   information to guide the weight updates, enhancing the model’s performance.
