011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we propose a novel framework, the Sampling-guided Heterogeneous
014            Graph Neural Network (SHT-GNN), to effectively tackle the challenge of miss-
015            ing data imputation in longitudinal studies. Unlike traditional methods, which
016
               often require extensive preprocessing to handle irregular or inconsistent missing
               data, our approach accommodates arbitrary missing data patterns while maintain-
017
               ing computational efficiency. SHT-GNN models both observations and covariates
018
               as distinct node types, connecting observation nodes at successive time points
019            through subject-specific longitudinal subnetworks, while covariate-observation
020            interactions are represented by attributed edges within bipartite graphs. By lever-
021            aging subject-wise mini-batch sampling and a multi-layer temporal smoothing
022            mechanism, SHT-GNN efficiently scales to large datasets, while effectively learning
023            node representations and imputing missing data. Extensive experiments on both
024            synthetic and real-world datasets, including the Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging
025            Initiative (ADNI) dataset, demonstrate that SHT-GNN significantly outperforms
026            existing imputation methods, even with high missing data rates (e.g., 80%). The
027
               empirical results highlight SHT-GNN’s robust imputation capabilities and superior
               performance, particularly in the context of complex, large-scale longitudinal data.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      With the advancement of data collection and analysis techniques, longitudinal data has gained
      increasing importance across various scientific fields, such as biomedical science, economics, and
034
      e-commerce (Pekarčı́k et al., 2022; Sadowski et al., 2021; Mundo et al., 2021). Observation schedules
035
      in longitudinal studies vary widely as some follow regular intervals for each subject while others
036   follow irregular patterns. For example, clinical visits scheduled every two months represent a regular
037   observation schedule, whereas follow-ups at 6 months, 1 year, and 2 years post-treatment is an
038   irregular schedule. Additionally, observation schedules may be either consistent or inconsistent across
039   different subjects—some studies may impose uniform intervals for all participants, while others allow
040   for variability due to individual health conditions or other circumstances.
041
      Imputation of missing values poses a major challenge in longitudinal data analysis, both from
042   theoretical and practical standpoints (Daniels & Hogan, 2008; Little & Rubin, 2019). In fact, in
043   complex longitudinal data analysis, such as those in neuroimaging or electronic health record studies,
044   patient follow-up data is collected over time, often resulting in missing values across variables
045   (cross-sectional missingness) and across time (longitudinal missingness). Ideally, as illustrated in
046   Figure 1, each subject would have observations taken at consistent intervals, with an equal number of
047   observations per subject, and each observation would contain fully observed covariates and response
048   variable. However, when missing data arises at specific time points, this misalignment of observations
049   across time can occur. Furthermore, the absence of entire observations at certain time steps can
050
      transform regular observation schedules into irregular ones and consistent schedules into inconsistent
      ones, significantly complicating longitudinal data analysis.
051
052   Given the frequent occurrence of missing measurements in studies with extensive data collection,
053   addressing the missingness of both covariates and response variables is crucial for accurate predictions
      of the target response variable. For instance, in Alzheimer’s disease research, the prediction of key

066   advancements, managing missing data in longitudinal studies presents several ongoing challenges.
067   Firstly, how can we effectively model longitudinal data that is irregularly and inconsistently observed?
068   Secondly, how can we design a cohesive forward pass process for data imputation that accommodates

072
073   In this paper, we address the key challenges by introducing the Sampling-guided Heterogeneous
074   Graph Neural Network (SHT-GNN). Unlike traditional imputation methods, our GNN leverages
075
      scalable modeling of complex data structures to effectively learn from irregular and inconsistent
      longitudinal observations. Our SHT-GNN incorporates three key innovations:
076
077         1. We handle longitudinal data by sharing trainable parameters across sampled graphs con-
078            structed from subject-wise mini-batches. This sampling-guided process ensures scalability
079            for massive longitudinal datasets.
080
            2. Subject-wise longitudinal subnetworks are constructed by connecting adjacent observations
081            with directed edges, while covariate values are transformed into attributed edges, linking
082            observation nodes with covariate nodes. This structure allows SHT-GNN to flexibly model
083            longitudinal data under arbitrary missing data conditions.
084
            3. We introduce a temporal smoothing mechanism across observations for each subject using a
085            multi-layer longitudinal subnetwork. The novel MADGap statistic controls the smoothness
086            within the subnetwork, balancing temporal smoothing with the specificity of observation
087            node representations.
088
089   We conducted extensive experiments on both real data and synthetic data. The results demonstrate
090   that SHT-GNN consistently achieves state-of-the-art performance across different temporal charac-
091
      teristics and performs exceptionally well even under high missing rates for both covariates and
      response variable. Furthermore, we applied the proposed SHT-GNN model to the Alzheimer’s Disease
092
      Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) dataset to predict the critical biomarker Aβ42/40. SHT-GNN’s
093
      strong performance in predicting Aβ42/40 is confirmed through validation with ground truth data
094   and in downstream analyses. Additionally, we conduct extensive ablation studies, demonstrating
095   the longitudinal network’s ability to perform temporal smoothing through multi-layer longitudinal
096   subnetworks and the critical role of MADGap in guaranteeing variance between observations.

      2.1   S TATISTICAL IMPUTATION METHODS
101
102   Statistical methods for imputing missing values in longitudinal data are often derived from multiple
103   imputation techniques originally developed for cross-sectional data. For example, the 3D-MICE
104   method extends the MICE (Multivariate Imputation by Chained Equations) framework to account
105   for both cross-sectional and longitudinal dependencies in data (Luo et al., 2018). Some methods
106   like trajectory mean and last observation carried forward (LOCF) leverage the time-series nature
107   for missing data imputation (Lane, 2008). However, these basic models often fail to capture the
      complex, nonlinear spatio-temporal dependencies inherent in longitudinal data and lack the scalability


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to manage both continuous and discrete covariates simultaneously. The multi-directional multivariate
109   time series (MD-MTS) method seeks to address these limitations by integrating temporal and cross-
110   sectional covariates into a unified imputation framework through extensive feature engineering (Xu
111   et al., 2020). A notable limitation of MD-MTS is its reliance on manually crafted features can be
112   labor-intensive and prone to errors. To overcome such challenges, the time-aware dual-cross-visit (TA-
113   DualCV) method leverages both longitudinal dependencies and covariate correlations using Gibbs
114   sampling for imputation (Gao et al., 2022). Nonetheless, when applied to large-scale longitudinal
115   datasets with numerous repeated observations, those methods involving chained equations and Gibbs
116   sampling can become computationally expensive.
117
118   2.2   M ACHINE LEARNING IMPUTATION METHODS
119
      Machine learning methods for imputing missing values in longitudinal data have primarily utilized
120
      recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and their advanced variants, such as long short-term memory
121
      (LSTM) networks and gated recurrent units (GRUs). BRITS, for example, leverages bidirectional
122   LSTMs to capture both longitudinal and cross-sectional dependencies by utilizing past and future
123   trends (Cao et al., 2018). Similarly, NAOMI employs a recursive divide-and-conquer approach with
124   bidirectional RNNs to handle missing data imputation (Woillez et al., 2019). CATSI enhances this
125   by combining bidirectional LSTMs with a context vector to account for patient-specific temporal
126   dynamics (Kazijevs & Samad, 2023). A significant limitation of all RNN-based methods, however,
127   is their reliance on consistent time step lengths across different subjects, often requiring padding
128   or truncation to achieve this uniformity. When applied to large datasets, where the number of
129   observations can vary greatly between subjects, padding and truncation may lead to excessive and
130
      inefficient computations due to the presence of numerous invalid time steps. Beyond RNN-based
      approaches, the GP-VAE model integrates deep variational autoencoders with Gaussian processes to
131
      address missing data in time series. However, the computational complexity of Gaussian processes,
132
      which scales at O(N 3 ), introduces significant computational bottlenecks when processing large-scale
133   longitudinal data (Fortuin et al., 2020).
134
135
      2.3   G RAPH - BASED IMPUTATION METHODS
136
137   Numerous advanced graph-based methods have been developed for data imputation tasks (Zhang
138   & Chen, 2019; Li et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). However, these methods face limitations when
139   handling mixed discrete and continuous features and often fail to capture the temporal dependencies
140   inherent in longitudinal data. For example, RAINDROP employs a graph-guided network to handle
141   irregularly observed time series with varying intervals, effectively addressing missing data imputation
142
      and outperforming other methods in downstream prediction tasks (Zhang et al., 2021). Similarly,
      TGNN4I, which integrates GNNs with gated recurrent units, has shown strong performance in
143
      imputing missing data for longitudinal graph data (Oskarsson et al., 2023). Despite their strengths,
144
      both RAINDROP and TGNN4I rely on predefined node connections within graph datasets, limiting
145   their applicability to longitudinal tabular data where such connections between subjects do not
146   inherently exist. Additionally, GRAPE demonstrated success using a bipartite graph for feature
147   imputation, but its edge size increases linearly with the number of observations, posing scalability
148   challenges (You et al., 2020). IGRM (Zhong et al., 2023), an extension of GRAPE, constructs a
149   friend network among sample nodes to capture similarities and improve imputation. However, IGRM
150   requires continuous updating of a fully connected graph among all samples, making it computationally
151   expensive and lacking scalability for large-scale longitudinal data.

      4     M ETHOD
186
      We propose SHT-GNN to address the challenges of missing data in irregular or inconsistent longi-
187
      tudinal data mentioned in Section 1. SHT-GNN models the observations and covariates as nodes,
188
      connecting them with attributed edges that represent the observed covariate values. Additionally,
189   it employs specially designed longitudinal subnetworks to perform temporal smoothing among
190   observations within the same subject. Initially, observations in longitudinal data can be represented
191   as a graph GL (VO , EOO ) with subject-wise longitudinal subnetworks, where the observation node
192   set VO = {u1 , u2 , . . . , uN } represents all observations. The observation nodes that belong to the
193   same subject form their own independent longitudinal subnetwork. In each longitudinal subnetwork,
194   observation nodes at adjacent time points are connected by directed edges. The directed edge set
195   in GL is denoted as EOO = {(ui , ui′ , ei→i′ )|ui , ui′ ∈ VO , S(ui ) = S(ui′ ), ui ≺ ui′ }, where S(u)
196   denotes the subject that observation u belong to, and ui ≺ ui′ represents ui is the direct predecessor
197
      of ui′ in terms of time.

199   measured different covariates. SHT-GNN establishes a covariate node set VC = {v1 , v2 , . . . , vp },

353   5.1     BASELINES
354
355   We consider eight baseline methods as follows. 1. Mean (Huque et al., 2018): imputes missing values
356
      using the covariate-wise mean. 2. Copy-mean Last Observation Carried Forward (LOCF) (Jahangiri
      et al., 2023): first imputes using the LOCF, then refines the results based on the population’s mean
357
      trajectory. 3. Multivariate Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE)(Van Buuren & Groothuis-
358
      Oudshoorn, 2011): employs multiple regressions to model each missing value conditioned on other
359   observed covariate values. 4. 3D-MICE (Kazijevs & Samad, 2023): combines MICE and Gaussian
360   processes for longitudinal data imputation. 5. GRAPE: handles feature imputation through graph
361   representation learning. (You et al., 2020) 6. CASTI (Yin et al., 2020): handles missing data in
362   longitudinal data by employing bidirectional LSTM and MLPs. 7. IGRM (Zhong et al., 2023):
363   enhances feature imputation by leveraging the similarity between observations. 8. Transformer (Zeng
364   et al., 2023): handles missing data imputation in time series with self-attention mechanism. 9. GP-
365   VAE (Fortuin et al., 2020): enhances data imputation with Gaussian Process Variational Autoencoder.
366   10. CTA (Wi et al., 2024): enhances feature imputation via continuous-time Autoencoders.

      5.6   S CALABILITY OF S HT-GNN
521
522   In longitudinal data, a large number of repeated observations often lead to massive data size, making
523   scalability a critical concern for missing data imputation methods. Specifically, we compare the
524   scalability of SHT-GNN with two cutting-edge GNN-based imputation methods: GRAPE and IGRM.
525   For experimental simplicity, we conduct imputation on datasets with varying observation sizes of
526   N ×10 (where N = 50, 500, 1000, and 5000). In SHT-GNN, we fix the subsample size at 500 subjects,
527   resulting in a total of 5000 observations. For all three methods, we report the memory consumption
528   and forward computation time per epoch during training. The results show that both GRAPE and
529   IGRM exhibit significant increases in memory usage and computation time as observation size grows.
530
      However, by fixing the sampled subject batch size, SHT-GNN achieves consistent memory usage and
      computation time per epoch, regardless of the observation size.

534
535   In this paper, we present SHT-GNN, a scalable and accurate framework for longitudinal data impu-
536   tation. Our method combines a sampling-guided training policy with inductive learning, temporal
537   smoothing, and a custom-designed loss function to address the challenges posed by irregular and
538   inconsistent longitudinal missing data. Compared to state-of-the-art imputation techniques, SHT-GNN
539   consistently improves response prediction across both synthetic and real-world datasets. Extensive
      experiments confirm the efficacy of our multi-layer longitudinal subnetwork for temporal smoothing.

      A.2    D ETAILS FOR BASELINES
688
689   Mean: For each covariate with missing, we fill in the missing values using the mean of the observed
690   values for that covariate across all observations.
691
692
      Copy-mean LOCF: Following the process described in (Jahangiri et al., 2023), missing values are
      initially imputed using the LOCF (Last Observation Carried Forward) method within each subject
693
      to provide an approximation. Next, the population’s mean trajectory is used to further refine these
694
      imputed values.
695
696   MICE: As outlined in (Van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011), MICE (Multiple Imputation
697   by Chained Equations) performs multiple imputations by modeling each missing value conditioned
698
      on the non-missing values in the data. A maximum of 20 iterations is used during the imputation
      process.
699
700   3D-MICE: Following the procedure described in (Luo et al., 2018), MICE is configured to perform
701   cross-sectional imputation with a maximum of 20 iterations. And Gaussian Process Regression is
      applied longitudinally to the time-indexed data for each feature. The Gaussian Process uses an RBF


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      kernel combined with a constant kernel (Kernel = C(1.0) × RBF(1.0)), and the predictions from
703   the Gaussian Process are averaged with the MICE-imputed values to capture both temporal and
704   cross-sectional patterns.
705
706
      GRAPE: Following the setup in (You et al., 2020), GRAPE is trained for 20,000 epochs using the
      Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001. We employ two GNN layers with 16 hidden units and
707
      ReLU activation. The AGGl function is implemented as a mean pooling function MEAN(·). Both the
708
      edge imputation and response prediction neural networks are implemented as linear layers.
709
710   CATSI: Following the setup in (Yin et al., 2020), CATSI is trained for 3,000 epochs using the Adam
711   optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001. We employ the default settings for MLPs and LSTM models
712
      in CASTI to impute covariate values.
713   IGRM: Following the default settings described in (Zhong et al., 2023), IGRM employs three
714   GraphSAGE layers with 64 hidden units for bipartite Graph Representation Learning (GRL), and one
715   GraphSAGE layer for friend network GRL. The Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001 and
716   ReLU activation function is used. For initializing the friend network, observation nodes are randomly
717   connected with |U | edges to form the initial network structure, which is updated every 100 epochs
718
      during bipartite graph training.

768   A.5   A DNI DATASET INTRODUCTION
769
770
      We apply SHT-GNN to the real data from Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI)
      study. ADNI is a multi-centre longitudinal neuroimaging study with the aim of developing effective
771
      treatments that can slow or halt the progression of Alzheimer’s Disease (AD). The ADNI participants
772
      were followed prospectively, with follow-up time points at 3 months, 6 months, then every 6
773   months until up to 156 months. The ADNI study includes a wide range of clinical data such as
774   cognitive assessments, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) biomarkers.
775   Numerous studies show that CSF biomarkers are strong indicators of AD progression, but collecting
776   CSF requires invasive procedures like lumbar puncture, leading to high missing data rates. We
777   propose the SHT-GNN model to predict the CSF biomarker Amyloid beta 42/40 (Aβ42/40), which
778   has been a key biomaker. ADNI dataset containing 1,153 subjects and 10,033 observations. The
779   covariate matrix has 83 dimensions, with a missing ratios of 0.32 for covariate matrix and a missing
780   ratio of 0.83 for Aβ42/40.
