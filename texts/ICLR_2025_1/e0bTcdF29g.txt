011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Training LLMs presents significant memory challenges due to growing size of
014            data, weights, and optimizer states. Techniques such as data and model paral-
015            lelism, gradient checkpointing, and offloading strategies address this issue but are
016
               often infeasible due to hardware constraints. To mitigate memory usage, alterna-
               tive methods like Parameter-Efficient-Fine-Tuning (PEFT) and GaLore approxi-
017
               mate weights or optimizer states. PEFT methods, such as LoRA, have gained pop-
018
               ularity for fine-tuning LLMs, though they require a full-rank warm start. In con-
019            trast, GaLore allows full-parameter learning while being more memory-efficient.
020            This work introduces Natural GaLore, a simple drop in replacement for AdamW,
021            which efficiently applies the inverse Empirical Fisher Information Matrix to low-
022            rank gradients using Woodbury’s Identity. We demonstrate that incorporating
023            second-order information speeds up optimization significantly, especially when
024            the iteration budget is limited. Empirical pretraining on 60M, 130M, 350M, and
025            1.1B parameter Llama models on C4 data demonstrate significantly lower perplex-
026            ity over GaLore without additional memory overhead. By fine-tuning RoBERTa
027
               on the GLUE benchmark using Natural GaLore, we demonstrate significant re-
               duction in gap 86.05% vs 86.28% for full-finetuning. Furthermore, fine-tuning
028
               the TinyLlama 1.1B model for function calling using the TinyAgent framework
029
               shows that Natural GaLore achieving 83.09% accuracy on the TinyAgent dataset,
030            significantly outperforms 16-bit LoRA at 80.06% and even surpasses GPT4-Turbo
031            by 4%, all while using 30% less memory.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable performance across various disciplines,
035
      including conversational AI and language translation. However, training and fine-tuning these mod-
036   els demand enormous computational resources and are highly memory-intensive. This substantial
037   memory requirement arises from storing billions of trainable parameters along with associated gra-
038   dients and optimizer states.
039
      To quantify this, consider a model with Ψ parameters which is being trained using the Adam opti-
040
      mizer. In this case, storing parameters and their gradients in 16-bit precision formats like FP16 or
041
      BF16 requires 2Ψ bytes each. The associated optimizer states are typically stored in 32-bit precision
042   (FP32) for numerical stability, necessitating an additional 4Ψ bytes for each parameter, gradient mo-
043   mentum, and variance, amounting to 12Ψ bytes. Therefore, the total memory requirement sums up
044   to 16Ψ bytes. When accounting for model-dependent memory, such as activations during forward
045   and backward passes, and residual memory, like temporary buffers and memory fragmentation, the
046   overall memory footprint can easily exceed 18Ψ bytes (Raffel et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023;
047   Chowdhery et al., 2022).
048   This enormous memory demand poses significant challenges, especially when training LLMs on
049   hardware with limited memory capacity. As models continue to scale, efficient memory utilization
050   becomes critical for making training feasible and accessible. In this work, we develop an efficient
051   adaptation to the GaLore algorithm (Zhao et al., 2024a), which significantly reduces the memory
052   footprint during training and fine-tuning of LLMs by approximating the optimizer state. Our ap-
053   proach, Natural GaLore, leverages the low-rank structure of gradients and incorporates second-order
      information to achieve faster convergence and higher performance without additional memory over-

      Parallel and Distributed Training Techniques Researchers have developed various distributed
058   computing techniques that leverage system-level optimizations and hardware resources to mitigate
059   the substantial memory requirements in training LLMs.
060
061
      One prominent framework is Distributed Data-Parallel (DDP) that combines data parallelism where
      the training dataset is partitioned across multiple devices or nodes, with efficient gradient synchro-
062
      nization mechanisms, minimizing communication overhead. While data parallelism efficiently uti-
063
      lizes multiple GPUs, it can still face memory bottlenecks when model sizes exceed the memory
064   capacity of a single device.
065
066   Model parallelism addresses this limitation by partitioning the model across multiple devices, allow-
067
      ing for the training of models that are too large to fit into the memory of a single GPU. Techniques
      like pipeline parallelism (Huang et al., 2019) and tensor parallelism (Shoeybi et al., 2019) enables
068
      the distribution of different layers or partitions of layers across devices. However, model parallelism
069
      introduces communication overhead and can be complex to implement effectively.
070
071   Another effective technique is gradient checkpointing (Chen et al., 2016), which reduces memory
072   usage by selectively storing only a subset of activations during the forward pass and recomputing
073
      them during the backward pass as needed. This approach trades increased computational overhead
      for reduced memory consumption, enabling the training of deeper models without exceeding mem-
074
      ory constraints.
075
076   Memory offloading strategies, such as those implemented in ZeRO-Offload (Rajbhandari et al.,
077   2020), move optimizer states and gradients to CPU memory when not actively in use, freeing up
078   GPU memory for other operations. ZERO can also partition optimizer states and gradients across
079
      DDP processes, eliminating redundancy and significantly reducing memory footprint. Fully Sharded
      Data Parallel (Zhao et al., 2020) extends this concept by sharding model parameters in addition to
080
      optimizer states and gradients.
081
082   These system-level optimizations have been instrumental in training state-of-the-art LLMs such as
083   LLaMA3 (Touvron et al., 2023), GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), and Gopher
084   (Rae et al., 2021) on multi-node, multi-GPU clusters.
085   While these distributed computing solutions enable the training of large models by leveraging exten-
086   sive hardware resources, they come with increased system complexity and operational costs. There-
087   fore, there is a pressing need for alternative approaches that reduce memory consumption without
088   relying solely on distributed computing resources. Optimization techniques that approximate pa-
089   rameters or optimizer states offer a promising direction for making LLM training more accessible
090   and efficient.
091
092   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning PEFT techniques efficiently adapt pre-trained language mod-
093
      els to various downstream applications without fine-tuning all the model’s parameters (Ding et al.,
      2022), significantly reducing the computational and memory overhead.
094
095   Among these techniques, the popular LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) parametrizes a weight matrix W ∈
096   Rn×m as:
097
                                                     W = W0 + BA,                                        (1)
098
099   where W0 is a frozen full-rank pre-trained weight matrix, and B ∈ Rn×r and A ∈ Rr×m are
100   trainable low-rank adapters to be learned during fine-tuning. Since the rank r ≪ min(m, n), the
101   adapters B and A contain significantly fewer trainable parameters, reducing memory requirements
102   for both parameter and optimizer states.
103   LoRA has been extensively used to reduce memory usage during fine-tuning, effectively enabling
104   large models to be adapted to new tasks with minimal additional memory overhead. There are a few
105   variants of LoRA proposed to enhance its performance (Renduchintala et al., 2023; Sheng et al.,
106   2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024), supporting multi-task learning (Wang et al., 2023), and
107

      further reducing the memory footprint (Dettmers et al., 2023). Its variant, ReLoRA (Lialin & Schatz,
109   2023), extends LoRA’s approach to pre-training by periodically updating the frozen weight matrix
110   W0 using the previously learned low-rank adapters. This incremental updating allows for continual
111   learning without storing entire optimizer states for all parameters, leading to faster training times

113   multiple downstream tasks without storing separate copies of the entire model for each task.

      Despite their benefits, recent works have highlighted several limitations of low-rank reparameteri-
115   zation approaches. LoRA does not consistently achieve performance comparable to full-rank fine-
116   tuning, particularly in complex tasks (Xia et al., 2024). In pre-training from scratch, methods like
117   ReLoRA require an initial phase of full-rank model training as a warmup before optimizing in the
118   low-rank subspace (Lialin & Schatz, 2023). The shortcomings of low-rank parameter reparameter-
119   ization suggest that alternative strategies are needed to achieve both memory efficiency and high
120   performance.
121
122   Gradient Low-Rank Projection (GaLore) An alternative to parameter approximation is the ap-
123   proximation of the optimizer states. By reducing the memory footprint associated with optimizer
124
      states, it is possible to maintain full-parameter learning—thus preserving model capacity and per-
      formance—while achieving significant memory savings.
125
126   The core idea behind GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024a) is to exploit the slowly changing low-rank structure
127   of the gradient matrix g ∈ Rn×m , rather than approximating the weights. During neural network
128   training, gradients naturally exhibit low-rank properties, a phenomenon studied extensively in both
129   theoretical and practical settings (Zhao et al., 2022; Cosson et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). This
130
      intrinsic low-rank structure of gradients has been applied to reduce communication costs (Wang
      et al., 2018; Vogels et al., 2020) and to decrease memory footprints during training (Gooneratne
131
      et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2024b).
132
133   Specifically, consider the compact SVD decomposition of the gradient matrix g = PΣQT , where
134   P ∈ Rn×r and Q ∈ Rm×r are the associated semi-orthognal matrices. Then, GaLore projects the
135   gradient matrix g into a low-rank form:
136                                               glow-rank = PT g.                                   (2)
137   Here, r ≪ min(n, m) is the target rank, n is the parameter count, m is the batch size and glow-rank
138   serves as an efficient approximation of the original gradient. The projection matrix P is updated
139   periodically (e.g., every 200 iterations), which incurs minimal amortized computational cost.
140
      By operating on low-rank approximations of the gradients, GaLore significantly reduces the memory
141
      footprint, leading to up to 30% memory reduction compared LoRA (Zhao et al., 2024a). Moreover,
142   GaLore maintains full-parameter learning, allowing updates to all model parameters, leading to bet-
143   ter generalization and performance than low-rank adaptation methods. Further, GaLore is agnostic
144   to the choice of optimizer and can be easily integrated into existing optimization algorithms with
145   minimal code modifications.
146
      While GaLore offers significant memory savings and enables full-parameter learning, its perfor-
147
      mance has yet to match that of optimizers in full optimizer state space. Reliance on low-rank gradi-
148   ent approximations may not fully capture the rich optimization dynamics. These limitations suggest
149   that while GaLore is a valuable step toward memory-efficient training, further enhancements are
150   necessary to bridge the performance gap with standard optimizers.
151
152   Our Approach In this work, we propose to bridge the gap by incorporating a second-order regu-
153   larizer into the low-rank gradient estimate, which adjusts parameter updates more effectively, lead-
154   ing to faster convergence. We show that applying the inverse of the empirical Fisher Information
155   Matrix (FIM) to the low-rank gradients leads to variance reduction of the gradient estimate, incorpo-
156   rates information about the curvature of the loss landscape, and reduces dependence on the starting
157   point. All of these lead to significantly faster convergence, especially in a limited iteration regime.
158   We introduce the Natural GaLore algorithm, a matrix-free algorithm for efficiently applying the
159   inverse FIM to the low-rank gradients, using Woodbury Identity, Cholesky Decomposition, and
160   Matrix-Vector Products, all of which can be efficiently implemented on the GPU. Further, our ap-
161   proach does not require any explicit layer-wise information or significant computational overhead,
      as is seen in existing approaches like K-Fac (Martens & Grosse, 2015).

      We validate the effectiveness of Natural GaLore through extensive empirical evaluations. Pre-
163   training experiments on LLaMA models with 60M, 300M, and 1.1B parameters using the C4 dataset
164   demonstrate that Natural GaLore achieves significantly lower perplexity than GaLore without addi-
165   tional memory overhead, indicating faster convergence within the same computational budget.
166
      Furthermore, we showcase the practical benefits of Natural GaLore in fine-tuning tasks. We fine-
167
      tune the TinyLlama 1.1B model for function calling using the TinyAgent framework. Our results
168
      show that Natural GaLore significantly outperforms LoRA in this setting, achieving an accuracy
169   of 83.09% on the TinyAgent dataset. This performance significantly surpasses 16-bit LoRA and
170   exceeds that of GPT-4-turbo by 4%, all while using 30% less memory.

258   2.3   NATURAL G A L ORE AND F ISHER E FFICIENCY
259
260
      Despite clear advantages, the performance of GaLore is not on par with AdamW (Loshchilov & Hut-
      ter, 2017) optimization on the original space. To bridge this gap, we propose Natural GaLore, which
261
      uses the full empirical FIM, thereby incorporating the missing second-order interaction information
262
      in the optimization process.
263
264   As we now argue, this leads to a much more favorable dependence on the starting point, which
265   means that the optimizer can make much more progress given a limited iteration budget. Further,
266
      when using a decaying learning rate schedule like with AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017), the
      asymptotic convergence rate can be faster (Martens, 2020) by a significantly large constant factor.
267
268   Natural gradient descent is known (Martens, 2020) to be Fisher efficient, precisely for our loss
269   function [4]. Fisher efficiency means that the natural gradient estimator asymptotically achieves the
      lowest possible variance among all unbiased gradient estimators.

271   variance satisfies (Amari, 1998):

277   bound, that any unbiased estimator computed from mk training samples can have, with m being
278   the batch size.
279
      Here, θk∗ is the local optimum in the neighborhood defined by the Taylor series expansion [5] around
280
      the update direction. This is an important caveat, as the guarantee is only for local convergence in a
281
      convex neighborhood. The loss function is non-convex, so the property can not be stated to hold for
282   the global optimum.
283
284   The result also relies on the computation of the exact FIM Fk (θk ) using the entire data distribution,
285
      which is not practical. The Fisher efficiency guarantee is, however, only approximately satisfied
286   when using the empirical FIM F̂k instead. Nevertheless, we still get a variance reduction in the
287
      gradient estimates, leading to faster convergence and better optimization performance in the early
      stages of training large-scale models, making it especially valuable for training with a limited itera-
288
      tion budget.
289
290   Further, incorporating second-order information through the empirical FIM allows the optimizer
291   to account for the curvature of the loss landscape, enabling natural gradient descent to take more
292   informed steps than standard gradient descent, potentially escaping flat regions or navigating steep
293
      ravines more effectively.
294   In (Martens, 2020), it was shown that the expected update direction can be expressed as a sum of
295   two terms, one that scales as O(1/k), which is independent of the starting point and another that
296   scales as O(1/k 2 ), which is dependent on the starting point. If momentum is applied to the gradient
297   estimator, the first term becomes independent of the choice of FIM estimator, thereby not leading
298   to any asymptotic improvements. However, regularizing with the empirical FIM estimate can sig-
299
      nificantly reduce the constant factor associated with the starting-point-dependent second term. This
      leads to practical performance gains in finite iteration regimes (although negligible for large k).
300
301   Finally, the Fisher efficiency result also assumes that the model can perfectly capture the data distri-
302   bution, a condition known as realizability. However, with the growing size of LLMs, this assumption
303   is likely to hold, thereby satisfying the conditions for the guarantee. Therefore, especially in low-
304   resource settings, Natural GaLore can be a promising approach for training LLMs under memory
305
      constraints.

      3.3   F INE -T UNING T INY L LAMA 1.1B FOR F UNCTION C ALLING IN A DVANCED AGENTIC
426
            S YSTEMS
427
428   Advanced Agentic Systems (AAS) require language models that can understand and generate code
429   snippets to integrate various tools and APIs, fulfilling user queries through function-calling. We
430   utilize the TinyAgent framework, which provides an end-to-end pipeline for training and deploying
431   task-specific LLM agents capable of efficient and accurate function-calling (Erdogan et al., 2024) to
      drive agentic systems at the edge.


                                                            8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Given a natural language query, the LLM agent must generate a sequence of pre-defined function-
433   calls that accomplish the desired tasks. The challenge lies in determining the appropriate arguments,
434   to call the correct functions, in the right order while respecting interdependencies among the func-
435   tions.
436
      LLMCompiler Kim et al. (2023), is a framework that enables language models to perform function-
437
      calling by first generating a function-calling plan, which includes the required functions and argu-
438
      ments. The LLMCompiler then compiles this plan into an executable sequence of function-calls.
439   The critical aspect is training the model to produce a function-calling plan with the correct syntax
440   and dependencies.
441
442
      The off-the-shelf pre-trained TinyLlama 1.1B (instruct-32k) model performs poorly on this task.
      The model generates incorrect sets of functions, hallucinated function names, fails to respect depen-
443
      dencies, and passes arguments incorrectly. This underperformance is expected, as the model was ini-
444
      tially trained on datasets like SlimPajama and StarCoder, which are not specific to function-calling
445   tasks. To address this, we follow the TinyAgent framework (Erdogan et al., 2024) and fine-tune the
446   TinyLlama 1.1B model on a high-quality, curated dataset designed for function-calling.
447
448
      TinyAgent Dataset The TinyAgent dataset (Erdogan et al., 2024) is a meticulously curated col-
449
      lection aimed at building a local agentic system for function-calling on Apple MacBooks for day-to-
450
      day tasks. It contains 40K examples of natural language queries and corresponding function-calling
451   plans. The dataset is divided into 38K training examples, 1K validation examples, and 1K test ex-
452   amples. It encompasses 16 tasks, including Email, Contacts, SMS, Calendar, Notes, Reminders,
453   File Management and Zoom Meetings. Each task has predefined scripts that the model needs to
454   generate. The dataset is intentionally challenging, requiring the model to understand dependencies
455   between function-calls and the arguments to be passed.
456
457   Fine-Tuning Procedure We fine-tune the TinyLlama 1.1B model on the TinyAgent dataset for
458   three epochs using a batch size of 32. The learning rate is set to 7 × 10−5 . After each epoch,
459   the model is evaluated on the validation set, and the best-performing model is selected based on
460   validation performance to be evaluated on the test set.
461
      During fine-tuning, the prompt includes descriptions of the ground truth functions and irrelevant
462
      functions serving as negative samples. This strategy encourages the model to learn to select the
463   correct functions rather than merely memorizing the ground truth. Additionally, several in-context
464   examples demonstrate how queries are translated into function-calling plans. These examples are
465   selected using a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) process based on the user’s query from the
466   training data and a DeBERTa-v3-small model (He et al., 2021) fine-tuned for multi-label classifica-
467   tion for retrieval among the 16 tools.
468
      The training objective is then to maximize the accuracy of the generated function-calling plans.
469   Success is defined by the model generating the correct plan with the proper set of function-calls,
470   correct arguments, and the appropriate order of function-calls. Verifying the selection of the correct
471   set of functions involves straightforward set comparison. However, ensuring the correctness of
472   arguments and the order of function-calls is more complex and requires constructing the associated
473   Directed Acyclic Graph to check for equality.

      Results and Discussion After fine-tuning, the TinyLlama 1.1B model’s success rate on the test set
487   improved significantly. Table 3 presents the latency, model size, and success rate of various models
488   on the TinyAgent dataset. As shown, Natural GaLore improves the success rate of the 1.1B model
489   from 80.06% (16-bit LoRA) to 83.09%, also surpassing GPT-4-Turbo by 4% and approaching the
490   performance of the larger TinyAgent-7B model, which achieves 84.95%.
491
      These results demonstrate that Natural GaLore not only enhances the performance of smaller mod-
492
      els like the 1.1B parameter TinyLlama but also makes them competitive with significantly larger
493   models. By efficiently incorporating second-order information through low-rank natural gradient
494   updates, Natural GaLore enables smaller models to achieve higher accuracy without additional
495   memory overhead.

      4   C ONCLUSION
499
500
      We have introduced Natural GaLore, a memory-efficient pre-training and fine-tuning strategy for
      large language models. Natural GaLore significantly reduces memory usage—by up to 65.5% in op-
501
      timizer states—while maintaining or even improving performance in large-scale LLM pre-training
502
      and fine-tuning tasks. By incorporating second-order information through an efficient approxima-
503   tion of the inverse Empirical Fisher Information Matrix, Natural GaLore enhances convergence
504   rates, especially in regimes with a limited iteration budget.
505
506
      Importantly, Natural GaLore can serve as a drop-in replacement for standard optimizers like
      AdamW and integrates seamlessly into existing training pipelines. Our experimental results high-
507
      light the reproducibility and effectiveness of Natural GaLore across various tasks, including pre-
508
      training LLaMA models and fine-tuning on the GLUE benchmark, as well as the TinyAgent func-
509   tion calling tasks. This makes it a compelling choice for large-scale pre-training scenarios where
510   both memory efficiency and model performance are critical.
511
512
      In the future we want to explore (1) further enhancing memory efficiency by employing low-memory
      and structured projection matrices, and (2) more extensive empirical evaluation on fine-tuning AAS
513
      on a wide variety of tasks. We also hope that our work will inspire future research on memory-
514
      efficient training methods from the perspective of optimizer state approximation. We believe that
515   Natural GaLore will be a valuable tool for the community, enabling the training of large-scale mod-
516   els on consumer-grade hardware with limited resources.
