011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Mechanism design is a crucial topic at the intersection of computer science and
014            economics. This paper addresses the automated mechanism design problem by
015            leveraging machine learning and neural networks. The objective is to design a
016            truthful, expressive and efficient mechanism that maximizes the platform’s ex-
017            pected utility, given that the players’ types are drawn from a pre-specified distri-
018            bution. We present a general mechanism design model that captures two critical
019
               features: hidden information and strategic behavior. Subsequently, we propose
               the PFM-Net framework, which parameterizes the menu mechanism class by
020
               function approximation and identifies an optimal mechanism through ingenious
021
               optimization techniques. We also provide both theoretical and empirical justifi-
022            cations for the advantages of our approach. Experimental results demonstrate the
023            effectiveness of PFM-Net over traditional and learning-based baselines, enabling
024            the PFM-Net framework to serve as a new paradigm for automated mechanism
025            design.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Designing a truthful mechanism that maximizes the platform’s expected utility is a fundamental
032   problem in computational economics, with important application in market design and resource
033
      allocation (Börgers et al., 2015; Golowich et al., 2018). In a typical mechanism design problem,
      the market consists of two kinds of players: platforms (sellers) and customers (buyers), both with
034
      given utility functions. The utility functions are determined by the item price, item allocation and
035
      the player’s own value of items. Typically, the mechanism is required to possess truthfulness (or
036   equivalently, “strategy-proof” & “DSIC and IR”) (Likhodedov & Sandholm, 2005) such that the
037   customers have the incentive to report their types honestly and are always willing to participate.
038
039
      The seminal work of (Myerson, 1981) solved the optimal strategy of selling one item with indepen-
      dent bidder valuations, yet analytical results have been limited to specific simple settings thereafter
040
      (Manelli & Vincent, 2006; Giannakopoulos & Koutsoupias, 2014). The machine learning approach
041
      to this problem has become the mainstream method, which can be classified into three categories.
042   VCG-based approaches (Sandholm & Likhodedov, 2015) define a parameterized class of truthful
043   mechanisms and then optimize within this class. Regret-based approaches (Dütting et al., 2019;
044   Ivanov et al., 2022) capture a broad class of mechanisms by incorporating untruthfulness (i.e., re-
045   gret) as a penalty in the loss function to optimize the mechanism. Discretization-based approaches
046   (Duan et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024b), including menu-based and mixed integer programming-
047   based methods, discretize the allocation or type space to approximate the optimal mechanism while
048   preserving truthfulness.
049   Each of these existing approaches has notable drawbacks. VCG-based methods are inherently lim-
050   ited in expressive power, making them insufficient to find the optimal mechanism. Regret-based
051   methods suffer from untruthfulness, which makes outcomes unpredictable and the mechanism po-
052   tentially unstable. Discretization-based approaches often needs an exponential number of param-
053   eters to capture the full type space or allocation space, which becomes prohibitively expensive even
      for problems with moderate size.

      Our Contributions In this paper, we close the joint gaps of truthfulness, full expressive power
055   and efficiency in general multi-player mechanism design. We propose a machine learning-based
056   framework called PFM-Net (Parameterized Full-Menu Network) to derive the optimal mechanism.
057
058
      To achieve this, we first construct a general mechanism design setting in a quasi-linear context,
      which generalizes auction settings and other scenarios, such as welfare-maximizing platforms. We
059
      then characterize truthful mechanisms within this setting, demonstrating that the class of truthful
060
      mechanisms is equivalent to the class of menu mechanisms with convex pricing functions, substan-
061   tially generalizing the results of Rochet (1987) and Hammond (1979).
062
063   Building on this characterization, we utilize representations of convex functions, such as PICNN
064
      (Amos et al., 2017) and GroupMax (Warin, 2023), to construct the pricing network. We also derive
      a training procedure to train the optimal parameterized function, with the objective function formu-
065
      lated as a penalized utility function of the platform. Experimental results validate the effectiveness
066
      of PFM-Net framework, by demonstrating that such framework obtain the ability to capture the
067   non-trivial component even in the moderate-sized problems while other methods fail, highlighting
068   its superior performance in moderate-sized problems and its empirical success in avoiding the curse
069   of dimensionality and enabling the PFM-Net framework to serve as a new paradigm for automated
070   mechanism design1 .

178   As is inspired by Wang et al. (2024b) and Dütting et al. (2024), we focus on the menu mecha-
179   nism class in this paper. We will show that a specific class of menu mechanism characterizes the
180   class of truthful mechanisms in this section. Due to space limits, the formal definition of the menu
181   mechanism is leaved to Appendix B.

      For completeness, we briefly introduce menu mechanisms in few words. Consider a mecha-
183   nism design problem with one player, with type space T and feasible allocation set X . A menu
184   Mm = (X m , pm ) specifies a subset of feasible allocation, ∅ ̸= X m ⊆ X , and a pricing rule,
185   pm : X m → R. The mechanism discloses the menu to the player at first, then the player buy the
186   utility-maximizing allocation x ∈ X m and pay pm (x) money, which depends on her private type
187   t ∈ T . The case of multi-players is similar. In that case, the platform plays the mechanism with
188   each player independently, with the only difference that the mechanism to each player i can depend
189   on the types of all other players t−i .
190   With a little abuse of notations, we also call Mm as a menu mechanism with menu Mm . If X m =
191   X always hold, such menu mechanism is called full-menu mechanism. Since the properties of
192   truthfulness and full expressiveness are originally defined for direct mechanisms, it naturally leads to
193   a question that, can we extend such properties to menu mechanisms? Though not intuitive, we shall
194   emphasize that a menu mechanism Mm can be easily transformed into a direct mechanism Md .
195   The insight is following: as long as the platform knows the exact types of players, the platform can
196   simulate the player’s behaviors as if players’ are rationally playing the game. The formal definition
197
      is also leaved to Appendix B.

199   “equivalent” mechanism, then consider the properties of menu mechanisms as the properties of

380   discretization-based approaches.9 Without the flexibility of network architectures, discretization-
381   based approaches are particularly susceptible to the curse of dimensionality (Bellman, 1966).

383   C OMPARISON WITH AMA- BASED METHODS An AMA mechanism is determined by positive
384   weights w = (w1 , ..., wn ) ∈ Rn+ of players as well as a shift function λ(x) on allocations. The
385   formal definition of how AMA mechanism would work in our model is leaved to Appendix B.3. We
386   have following comparison:
387   Proposition 5.5. Consider an AMA mechanism M AM A with positive weights w1 , . . . , wn and a
388   shift function λ(x). Assume more that an oracle OAM A of AMA mechanism exists that can run the
389   mechanism M AM A under input t. Formally, OAM A receives M AM A (or equivalently, w and λ)
390   and t as inputs, and output the resulting allocation x and payment p.
391   Given any AMA mechanism M AM A , we can explicitly construct a full-menu mechanism M F with
392   pricing functions {pfi (xi ; t−i )}i∈[n] , that receives type profile t, outputs the full menu pi : Xi →
393
      R, i ∈ [n], and is equivalent to M AM A .
394
395   Additionally, querying {pi (xi )}i∈[n] at some point x ∈ X needs polynomial-time computation and
396   O(n) black-box queries of the oracle OAM A .
397
398
      Proposition 5.5 states that, our framework can efficiently simulate AMA mechanisms.
399   In the reverse direction, it is well-known that the AMA mechanism class lacks full expressive power
400   (Carbajal et al., 2013). Given that PFM-Net exhibits full expressive power, there must exist an
401   instance of PFM-Net that cannot be expressed by an AMA mechanism.

410
411   We present the manually defined baselines and learning-based baselines we compared in this part.
412   The manually defined baselines include,
413       1. VCG (Vickrey, 1961): The most classical mechanism with strong versatility.
414       2. Item-wise Myerson: Item-wise Myerson is a auction baseline used in Dütting et al. (2019),
415          that sells the m items independently and optimally to the players.
416       3. Bundle-OPT: this mechanism bundles all items together at a specific price when selling items
417          to buyers. The price is parameterized, and the optimal price is selected for each setting by
418          one-dimensional grid search. This mechanism is particularly effective when there is only one
419          player in the game. This baseline is also used in Curry et al. (2023).
420
      The learning-based baselines include:
421
422       1. Lottery-AMA (Curry et al., 2023): Lottery-AMA is an AMA-based approach that sets bidder
423          weights, along with the discretization of the allocation menu and shift values, as learnable
424          parameters. We also made appropriate extensions to fit it into our actual experimental setting.
425       2. UM-GemNet: An extension of GemNet (Wang et al., 2024b) that can fit our generalized mech-
426          anism design setting. GemNet is a menu-based approach that discretes the menu for each bid-
427          der, which is computed by a fully-connected neural network taking others’ types as input. 10

517   6.3      E XPERIMENTAL A NALYSIS
518
519   Selling to single buyer We find that the performance of all methods exceeds the strong baseline of
520   Bundle-OPT when m ≤ 5, except for lottery-AMA. This is not surprising, as Bundle-OPT involves
521   only a single parameter, making it an easy baseline to learn. In the case of m = 2, these methods
522   also nearly approach the optimal mechanism. However, when m ≥ 5, we observe that UM-GemNet
523   performs very similarly to Bundle-OPT. In comparison, the 3-layer GroupMax significantly out-
524
      performs both UM-GemNet and Bundle-OPT, suggesting that the GroupMax network learns some
      nontrivial components beyond the simple mechanism of selling the full bundle at a fixed price,
525
      whereas UM-GemNet does not. These findings support our conjecture that UM-GemNet, as well
526
      as other discretization-based methods, are vulnerable to problems of moderate size. More in-depth
527   analysis of the “non-trivial components” in the learned pricing rule is provided in Appendix G.3.
528
529   Social planner of a market The performance of GroupMax exceeds that of GemNet and VCG
530   across all settings. We derive the optimal solution (OPT) in cases where the analytical optimal
531   solution exists. Additionally, we find that the value with n ≥ 2 players is greater than n times the
532   value with a single player, except in the case of VCG. This observation is due to the insight that if
533   one player wants to buy an item (i.e., t > 0), and another player is willing to sell it (i.e., t < 0),
534   they can reach an agreement that enhances social welfare. Specifically, in all scenarios, the value
535   obtained by PFM-Net with n players exceeds n times the optimal value achieved with a single player.
536
      In a demonstration of the pricing rule of GroupMax in Appendix G.3, we randomly selected three
      type profiles and examined the learned pricing rule for player 1. We observed that the pricing rule
537
      significantly changes with the types of other players, indicating that PFM-Net successfully learns a
538
      conditional pricing rule based on the other players’ types.11

      Automated Mechanism Design Automated mechanism design was first proposed by Sandholm
      (2003), with various applications including ad auctions (Benisch et al., 2009), combinatorial auc-
760
      tions (Sandholm & Likhodedov, 2015), and mechanism design without money (Narasimhan et al.,
761
      2016). Balcan et al. (2016) explored the sample complexity of automated mechanism design, while
762   Albert et al. (2017) investigated the robust automated mechanism design problem. Additionally,
763   Zhang & Conitzer (2021) studied dynamic automated mechanism design.
764
765
      Differential Economics for Mechanism Design Differential economics aims at parameterizing
766
      differential functions to represent the optimal economic solutions, and find the optimal solution from
767
      gradient, which can be seen as a sub-field of automated mechanism design (Wang et al., 2024a).
768   Shen et al. (2018) uses the neural network to assist the mechanism design problem. Dütting et al.
769   (2019) begins with optimal auction design by auction. Ivanov et al. (2022) incorporates transformer
770   architecture into auction design. Curry et al. (2023) proposes lottery-AMA, which discrete the
771   platform’s allocation space and optimize an AMA mechanism. Duan et al. (2024b) later studies the
772   optimal combinatorial auction design within VVCG mechanism class. The most related works with
773   us should be Wang et al. (2024b), which studies the menu mechanism, with the menu depends on
774   other players types. The menu discretes each player’s allocation space.
775
776   Characterization of Truthful Mechanisms Hammond (1979) showed that menu-based mech-
777   anism is a sufficient condition for IC, and IC mechanism is in some sense a menu mechanism.
778   Rochet (1987) showed that an truthful mechanism will induce the utility of player convex on her
779   type. Though similar to our results, we argue that our characterization in Section 3 is different
780   from theirs and more general. Compared with Hammond (1979), they mainly focus on the discrete
781   menu mechanism, and their results has no convexity characterization. Our characterization show the
782
      nature of full-menu and convexity, making the truthful mechanisms and convex full-menu mecha-
      nisms “equivalent class” rather than sufficient and necessary condition, making the characterizations
783
      of Hammond (1979) more concise. Compared with Rochet (1987), they studies the convex utility
784
      of truthful mechanism, having no connection on menu mechanism. Our results state that, if we
785   transform a truthful mechanism into menu mechanism, the pricing rule of the menu is also convex.
786   These two results have different perspectives, which are complementary to each other.
787
788
      There are also a plenty of works characterized the relation between truthful mechanisms and VCG-
      based mechanisms. Roberts (1979) shows that if the valuation spaces are full domain and player
789
      number is no less than 3, then any implementable mechanism must be an AMA. A simplified proof
790
      is later provided by Lavi et al. (2009). In a general setting of combinatorial auctions, Lavi et al.
791   (2003) proves that any implementable mechanism should be “almost” AMA. All of their works
792   studies deterministic mechanism. To the best of our knowledge, there are no full characterization
793   about the general class of randomized mechanism.
794
795
      Characterization of Optimal Auctions Since the seminal work of Myerson (1981) for optimally
796
      selling one item to independent buyers, there are only special cases that optimal solution has been
797   found over the past 40 years (Manelli & Vincent, 2006; Pavlov, 2011; Giannakopoulos & Kout-
798   soupias, 2014; Daskalakis et al., 2015; Yao, 2017) Among these, Manelli & Vincent (2006) studies
799   bundling mechanism, showing the condition such that bundling mechanism is optimal among all
800   truthful mechanisms and deriving the optimal mechanism in selling 2 or 3 uniform items. Gi-
801   annakopoulos & Koutsoupias (2014) generalizes the results to up to 6 uniform items, though the
802   optimal mechanism is not analytically given. Yao (2017) studies the optimal mechanism when there
803   are two items with discrete distributions.
804
805   Representing Convex Functions Max-of-Affine (MoA) functions and Log-sum-exp (LSE) func-
806   tions are well-known functions that are convex by design. Calafiore et al. (2019) demonstrates that
807   both the maximum-of-affine and log-sum-exp functions are universal approximators for the class
808   of convex functions under the L∞ norm. Later, Kim & Kim (2022) further shows that conditioned
809   maximum-of-affine and conditioned log-sum-exp are also universal approximators for the class of
      continuous functions that exhibit convexity over partial inputs. Additionally, Warin (2023) proves

       F.2   D ISCUSSIONS ON M ECHANISM P ROPERTIES
1485
1486   We discuss the three properties we emphasized in Section 2.
1487
1488
              • Potentially exact truthfulness. The approach should return a mechanism that meets po-
1489            tentially exact truthfulness. Since it’s hard to verify whether a mechanism is truthful, we
1490            often require that any mechanism that can be represented within the parameterized mech-
1491            anism class should be potentially exact truthful. The potentially exact truthfulness means
1492            that there is no endogenous factor that makes the mechanism untruthful, e.g., the forced and
1493            unreasonable allocation and payment rule. Exogenous factors are acceptable. For example,
1494            exogenous factors consist of: floating-point error in computation; irrational behaviors of
1495            players when maximizing their utilities; the non-existence of optimal choice when utilities
1496            without an upper bound (it can potentially appear in a poor-initialized mechanism). In a
1497
                nutshell, potentially exact truthfulness requires that players have no reason to complaint
                about the untruthfulness of the mechanism structure. The regret-minimization-based mod-
1498
                els (e.g., RegretNet) do not satisfy this property, as the regret can not minimized to be zero.
1499
                (Dütting et al., 2019; Duan et al., 2022)
1500
1501          • Full expressive power. The optimal mechanism can be expressed or approached with
1502            arbitrarily small error within the parameterized mechanism class. Since the agnosticism of
1503            the optimal mechanism, we often require that any truthful mechanism can be approached
1504            with arbitrarily small error within the parameterized mechanism class. The AMA-based
1505
                model does not satisfy this property, for bounded representative power of AMA model.
                (Curry et al., 2023; Duan et al., 2024a;b)
1506
1507          • Efficiency in moderate-size problem. As the problem size (the number of players and
1508            items) increases, there is only polynomial time scale-up for achieving a good-enough mech-
1509            anism in practice. The menu-based approaches (Wang et al., 2024b; Curry et al., 2023;

1601   Complexity Analysis of these networks In this section, we analyze the computational complexity
1602   of our approach. Let n and m represent the number of players and items, respectively. The number
1603
       of training epochs (300 + 1000 = 1300), iterations per epoch (approximated as 4 + 8 + 32 = 44),

1604

1605

1606   for all n players. Consequently, the total computational cost within our framework scales as nm2 .

1609   coefficient in O(nm2 ) approximates as 65536 × 1300 × 44 × 256 ≈ 1 × 1012 .

       PRICING RULE FOR SELLING GOODS TO ONE BUYER Figure 2 represent the pricing rule learned
1675   by PFM-Net with 1-layer GroupMax and 3-layer GroupMax architecture, in the setting of selling
1676   m = 3 items to one buyer. The x-axis represents the allocation on the first item, i.e., x1 = x, while
1677   the y-axis represents the allocation on the second and third item, i.e., x2 = x3 = y. The pricing rule
1678   is almost piece-wise linear, thus we can approximately take the pricing rule as a bundle mechanism
1679   that sells all items at a price ≈ 1.2, sells one items at a price ≈ 0.8, and sells two items at the
1680   price ≈ 1.4. Notice that if a player want to buy two items then she must want to buy all items more.
1681   Therefore, the mechanism actually do not sell two items, only bundle all items together or sell single
1682   item independently. Buyer will get a cheaper average price if she choose to buy the full bundle. This
1683
       result coincides with the existing finding that optimal mechanism may sometimes bundle all items
       with a lower price sometimes. The pricing rule of 3-layer GroupMax has a similar regularity. The
1684
       only difference is that, the price of selling two items is very high in 3-layer GroupMax.
1685
1686   We need to point out that in the characterization of optimal mechanism in selling 3 items (Gian-
1687   nakopoulos & Koutsoupias, 2014), there has small probability that the platform sells exactly 2 items
1688   to the player. Our experiments show that if we give up selling exactly 2 items, we will not lose too
1689
       much.

1691   and 3-layer GroupMax architecture, in the setting of selling m = 20 items to one buyer. The x-axis
1692   represents the allocation on the first 10 items, i.e., xi = x for 1 ≤ i ≤ 10, while the y-axis represents
1693   the allocation on the last 10 items, i.e., xi = y for 11 ≤ i ≤ 20. The pricing rule is almost piece-
1694   wise linear again. The mode in 1-layer PICNN and 1-layer GroupMax is similar: selling both full
