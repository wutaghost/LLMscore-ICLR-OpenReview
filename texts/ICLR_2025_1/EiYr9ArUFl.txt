011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            When training large models, such as neural networks, the full derivatives of order 2
014            and beyond are usually inaccessible, due to their computational cost. This is why,
015            among the second-order optimization methods, it is very common to bypass the
016            computation of the Hessian by using first-order information, such as the gradient
017            of the parameters (e.g., quasi-Newton methods) or the activations (e.g., K-FAC).
018            In this paper, we focus on the exact and explicit computation of projections of
019            the Hessian and higher-order derivatives on well-chosen subspaces, which are
020            relevant for optimization. Namely, for a given partition of the set of parameters,
021
               it is possible to compute tensors which can be seen as “higher-order derivatives
               according to the partition”, at a reasonable cost as long as the number of subsets of
022
               the partition remains small.
023
024
               Then, we propose an optimization method exploiting these tensors at order 2 and 3
               with several interesting properties, including: it outputs a learning rate per subset
025
               of parameters, which can be used for hyperparameter tuning; it takes into account
026
               long-range interactions between the layers of the trained neural network, which
027            is usually not the case in similar methods (e.g., K-FAC); the trajectory of the
028            optimization is invariant under affine layer-wise reparameterization.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
034
      The appealing theoretical properties of Newton’s method have led to numerous attempts to adapt
      it to neural network optimization. Therefore, the study of the Hessian of a loss according to many
035
      parameters has become a research area in itself, leading to a large number of methods to approximate
036
      it accurately with low computational cost.
037
038
039
      Newton’s method applied to neural network optimization. When it comes to neural networks,
      Newton’s method suffers from several problems. Some are technical, such as building an accurate
040
      and computationally efficient method for estimating the Hessian. But some of them are essential, in
041
      the sense that they cannot be solved by a perfect knowledge of the full Hessian alone. For instance,
042   several works (Sagun et al., 2018) have shown that many eigenvalues of the Hessian are close to zero
043   when training neural networks, making it impossible to use Newton’s method in practice, even if the
044   Hessian is perfectly known.
045
      Therefore, in this work, we do not aim to build a technique to estimate the Hessian accurately and
046
      efficiently. Instead, we propose to take a step aside and focus on two related goals. First, we aim
047
      to efficiently access higher-order information in order to use it for optimization. To do this, we
048   leverage the available computational tools, that is, automatic differentiation and partition of the set
049   of parameters into tensors stored on a GPU. Second, we aim to build a second-order optimization
050   method with properties similar to those of Newton’s method (but inevitably weaker). In particular,
051   we want a method that is invariant by layer-wise affine reparameterizations of the model, and that
052   provides a Hessian-inspired matrix showing the interactions between each pair of tensors, along with
053   layer-wise step sizes. Naturally, the computational cost should remain reasonable, and the problem of
      close-to-zero eigenvalues of the Hessian has to solved.

      First contribution: extracting higher-order information. Formally, we study a loss L to minimize
055   according to a vector of parameters θ ∈ RP , which can be represented as a tuple of tensors
056   (T1 , · · · , TS ). In a multilayer perceptron with L layers, the S = 2L tensors (Ts )1≤s≤2L are the
057   tensors of weights and the vectors of biases of each layer. In that case, S ≪ P . Within this framework,
058   we propose a technique summarizing the order-d derivative of the loss, which is a tensor belonging to
         d                                   d
059   RP , into a tensor belonging to RS , which is significantly smaller and easier to compute.
060
061   Second contribution: second-order optimization method. Then, we make use of the preceding
062   technique at orders 2 and 3 to build a second-order optimization method. Formally, the method
063   presented here and Newton’s method look alike: in both cases, a linear system H0 x = g0 has
064   to be solved (according to x), where g0 and H0 contain respectively first-order and second-order
065   information about L. Despite this formal resemblance, the difference is enormous: with Newton’s
066   method, H0 is equal to the Hessian H of L of size P × P , while with ours, H0 is equal to a matrix
067   H̄ of size S × S. Thus, H̄ is undoubtedly smaller and easier to compute than H when S ≪ P .
068   Nevertheless, since H̄ is a dense matrix, it still contains information about the interactions between
069
      the tensors Ts when they are used in L. This point is crucial because most second-order optimization
      methods applied to neural networks use a simplified version of the Hessian (or its inverse), usually a
070
      diagonal or block-diagonal approximation, which ignores the interactions between layers. Finally, we
071
      propose an anisotropic version of Nesterov’s cubic regularization (Nesterov & Polyak, 2006), which
072   uses order-3 information to regularize H̄ and avoid instabilities when computing H̄−1 ḡ.
073
074   A proof-of-concept of this method is given in Section 5. Apart from that, this method provides a way
075
      to evaluate the interactions between layers from a training perspective, it outputs by-layer learning
      rates (which makes it applicable in hyperparameter tuning), and its training trajectory is invariant
076
      by layer-wise affine reparameterizations (so it preserves some interesting theoretical properties of
077
      Newton’s method).

      Structure of the paper. First, we show the context and motivation of our work in Section 2.

081   higher-order information extraction technique and the second-order optimization method. In Section

088   2.1   H IGHER - ORDER INFORMATION
089
      It is not a novel idea to extract higher-order information about a loss at a minimal computational
090
      cost to improve optimization. This is typically what is done by Dangel (2023), although it does
091
      not go beyond the second-order derivative. In this line of research, the Hessian-vector product
092   (Pearlmutter, 1994) is a decisive tool, that allows to compute the projection of higher-order derivatives
093   in given directions at low cost (see App. A). For derivatives of order 3 and beyond, Nesterov’s cubic
094   regularization of Newton’s method (Nesterov & Polyak, 2006) uses information of order 3 to avoid
095   too large training steps. Incidentally, we develop an anisotropic variant of this in Section 4.2.
096
097   2.2   S ECOND - ORDER METHODS
098
099   The Hessian H of the loss L according to the vector of parameters θ is known to contain useful
100   information about L. Above all, the Hessian is used to develop second-order optimization algorithms.
101   Let us denote by θt the value of θ at time step t, gt ∈ RP the gradient of L at step t and Ht its
102   Hessian at step t. One of the most widely known second-order optimization method is Newton’s
103   method, whose step is (Nocedal & Wright, 1999, Chap. 3.3):

123
124   Quasi-Newton methods. When the parameter space is high-dimensional, computation of the
125   Hessian Ht and inversion of the linear system gt = Ht x are computationally intensive. Quasi-
126
      Newton methods are designed to avoid any direct computation of the Hessian, and make extensive
      use of gradients and finite difference methods to approximate the direction of H−1t gt . For a list of
127
      quasi-Newton methods, see (Nocedal & Wright, 1999, Chap. 8). However, Nocedal & Wright (1999)
128
      argue that, since it is easy to compute the Hessian by using Automatic Differentiation (AutoDiff),
129   quasi-Newton methods tend to lose their interest. Nevertheless, they should remain useful when such
130   computation is too difficult.
131
132   Applications to deep learning. Many methods overcome the curse of the number of parameters by
133   exploiting the structure of the neural networks. It is then common to neglect interactions between
134   layers, leading to a (block)-diagonal approximation of the Hessian. A first attempt has been made by
135   Wang & Lin (1998): they divide the Hessian into blocks, following the division of the network into
136   layers, and its off-diagonal blocks are removed. From another perspective, Ollivier (2015) keeps this
137   block-diagonal structure, but performs an additional approximation on the remaining blocks.
138   More recently, K-BFGS has been proposed (Goldfarb et al., 2020), which is a variation of the
139   quasi-Newton method BFGS with block-diagonal approximation and an approximate representation
140   of these blocks. In a similar spirit, the Natural Gradient method TNT (Ren & Goldfarb, 2021) also
141   exploits the structure of neural networks by performing a block-diagonal approximation. Finally,
142   AdaHessian (Yao et al., 2021) efficiently implements a second-order method by approximating the
143   Hessian by its diagonal.
144
145   Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature (K-FAC). This approximation of the Hessian was
146   proposed in (Martens & Grosse, 2015) in the context of neural network training. K-FAC exploits the
147   specific architecture of neural networks to output a cheap approximation of the true Hessian. Despite
148
      its scalability, K-FAC suffers from several problems. First, the main approximation is quite rough,
      since “[it assumes] statistical independence between products [...] of unit activities and products [...]
149
      of unit input derivatives” (Martens & Grosse, 2015, Sec. 3.1). Second, even with an approximation
150
      of the Hessian, one has to invert it, which is computationally intensive even for small networks. To
151   overcome this difficulty, a block-(tri)diagonal approximation of the inverse of the Hessian is made,
152   which eliminates many of the interactions between the layers.
153
154   Summarizing the Hessian. Also, (Lu et al., 2018) proposes to approximate the Hessian with
155   a matrix composed of blocks in which all coefficients are identical. Thus, the Hessian can be
156   compressed into a smaller matrix that looks like the summary of Hessian matrix H̄ used in Section 4.
157   In a completely different setup, Yuan et al. (2022) proposes a “Sketched Newton-Raphson”, which is
158   driven by the same spirit as the method presented in Section 4: instead of dealing with a complicated
159   large matrix, one should “project” it onto spaces of lower dimension.
160
161   Invariance by affine reparameterization. Several optimization methods, such as Newton’s, have
      an optimization step invariant by affine reparameterization of θ (Amari, 1998) (Nesterov, 2003, Chap.

      4.1.2). Specifically, when using Newton’s method, it is equivalent to optimize L according to θ and
163
      according to θ̃ = Aθ + B (A ∈ RP ×P invertible, B ∈ RP ). This affine-invariance property holds
164
      even if the function L to minimize is a negative log-likelihood, and one chooses to minimize θ by the
165   natural gradient method (Amari, 1998). This method also requires computing the Hessian of L at
166   some point.
167
168   Methods based on the moments of the gradients. Finally, many methods acquire information
169   about the curvature of the loss surface by using only the gradients. For instance, Shampoo (Gupta
170   et al., 2018) uses second-moment information of the accumulated gradients.

172   2.3       M OTIVATION
173
174   What are we really looking for? The methods that aim to estimate the Hessian matrix H or its
175
      inverse H−1 in order to imitate Newton’s method implicitly assume that Newton’s method is adapted
      to the current problem. This assumption is certainly correct when the loss to optimize is strongly
176
      convex. But, when the loss is not convex and very complicated, e.g. when training a neural network,
177
      this assumption is not justified. Worse, it has been shown empirically that, at the end of the training
178   of a neural network, the eigenvalues of the Hessian are concentrated around zero (Sagun et al., 2018),
179   with only a few large positive eigenvalues. Therefore, Newton’s method itself does not seem to be
180   recommended for neural network training, so we may not need to compute the full Hessian at all,
181   which would relieve us of a tedious, if not impossible, task.
182
      To avoid such problems, it is very common to regularize the Hessian by adding a small, constant term
183
      λI to it (Nocedal & Wright, 1999, Chap. 6.3). Also, trust-region Newton methods are designed to
184   handle non-positive-definite Hessian matrices (Nocedal & Wright, 1999, Chap. 6.4) (Nash, 1984).
185
186   Importance of the interactions between layers. Also, some empirical works have shown that the
187   role and the behavior of each layer must be considered along its interactions with the other layers,
188   which emphasize the importance of off-diagonal blocks in the Hessian or its inverse. We give two
189   examples. First, Zhang et al. (2022) has shown that, at the end of their training, many networks
190   exhibit a strange feature: some (but not all) layers can be reinitialized to their initial value with little
191   loss of the performance. Second, Kornblith et al. (2019) has compared the similarity between the
192   representations of the data after each layer: changing the number of layers can qualitatively change
193   the similarity matrix of the layers (Kornblith et al., 2019, Fig. 3). Among all, these results motivate
194
      our search for mathematical objects that show how layers interact.
195
      Per-layer scaling of the learning rates. A whole line of research is concerned with building a
196
      well-founded method for finding a good scaling for the initialization distribution of the parameters,
197
      and for the learning rates, which can be chosen layer-wise. For instance, a layer-wise scaling for
198   the weights was proposed and theoretically justified in the paper introducing the Neural Tangent
199   Kernels (Jacot et al., 2018). Also, in the “feature learning” line of work, (Yang & Hu, 2021) proposes
200   a relationship between different scalings related to weight initialization and training. Therefore, there
201   is an interest in finding a scalable and theoretically grounded method to build per-layer learning rates.
202
203   Unleashing the power of AutoDiff. Nowadays, several libraries provide easy-to-use automatic
204   differentiation packages that allow the user to compute numerically the gradient of a function, and
205   even higher-order derivatives.1 Ignoring the computational cost, the full Hessian could theoretically
206   be computed numerically without any approximation. To make this computation feasible, one should
207
      aim for an simpler goal: instead of computing the Hessian, one can consider a smaller matrix,
      consisting of projections of the Hessian.
208
209   Moreover, one might hope that such projections would “squeeze” the close-to-zero eigenvalues of the
210   Hessian, so that the eigenvalues of the projected matrix would not be too close to zero.

      4.2      R EGULARIZING H̄ BY USING ORDER -3 INFORMATION
305
306   The method proposed in Section 4.1 requires to compute η ∗ = H̄−1 ḡ. Usually, inverting such a
307   linear system at every step is considered as hazardous and unstable. Therefore, when using Newton’s
308   method, instead of computing descent direction u := H−1 g, it is very common to add a regularization
309                           −1
      term: uλ := (H + λI) g (Nocedal & Wright, 1999, Chap. 6.3).
310
      However, the theoretical ground of such a regularization technique is not fully satisfactory. Basically,
311
      the main problem is not having a matrix H̄ with close-to-zero eigenvalues: after all, if the loss
312
      landscape is very flat in a specific direction, it is better to make a large training step. The problem
313   lies in the order-2 approximation of the loss made in the training step 8, as well as in Newton’s
314   method: instead of optimizing the true decrease of the loss, we optimize the decrease of its order-2
315   approximation. Thus, the practical question is: does this approximation faithfully model the loss at
316   the current point θt , in a region that also includes the next point θt+1 ?
317
      To answer this question, one has to take into account order-3 information, and regularize H̄ in such
318
      a way that the resulting update remains in a region around θt where the cubic term of the Taylor
319   approximation is negligible. In practice, we propose an anisotropic version of Nesterov’s cubic
320   regularization (Nesterov & Polyak, 2006).

347   Is = {s} for all s, and Cauchy’s steepest descent is recovered when using the trivial partition, that
348   is, S = 1 with I1 = {1, · · · , P }. See Appendix C for more details.
349
350   No need to compute or approximate the full Hessian. The full computation of the Hessian Ht ∈
351   RP ×P is not required. Instead, one only needs to compute the S×S matrix H̄t := IS:P Ut Ht Ut IP :S ,
352   which can be done efficiently by computing uT Ht v for a number S × S of pairs of well-chosen
353   directions (u, v) ∈ RP × RP . This property is especially useful when S ≪ P . When optimizing a
354   neural network with L = 10 layers and P = 106 parameters, one can naturally partition the set of
355   parameters into S = 2L subsets, each one containing either all the weights or all the biases of each
356   of the L layers. In this situation, one has to solve a linear system of size 2L = 20 at each step, which
357   is much more reasonable than solving a linear system of P = 106 equations. We call this natural
358
      partition of the parameters of a neural network the canonical partition.
359
360
      No need to solve a large linear system. Using Equations 8 or 10 requires solving only a linear
      system of S equations, instead of P in Newton’s method. With the cubic regularization, only a
361
      constant term is added to the complexity, since it is a matter of scalar root finding.
362
363
      The interactions between different tensors are not neglected. The matrix H̄t , which simulates
364
      the Hessian Ht , is basically dense: it does not exhibit a (block-)diagonal structure. So, the interactions
365   between subsets of parameters are taken into account when performing optimization steps. In the
366   context of neural networks with the canonical partition, this means that interactions between layers
367   are taken into account during optimization, even if the layers are far from each other. This is a major
368   advantage over many existing approximations of the Hessian or its inverse, which are diagonal or
369   block-diagonal.
370
371   Invariance by subset-wise affine reparameterization. As showed in Appendix E, under a condi-
372   tion on the directions ut ,4 the trajectory of optimization of a model trained by Method 1 is invariant
373   by affine reparameterization of the sub-vectors of parameters θIs := vec({θp : p ∈ Is }). Let
374   (αs )1≤s≤S and (βs )1≤s≤S be a sequence of nonzero scalings and a sequence of offsets, and θ̃ such
375   that, for all 1 ≤ s ≤ S, θ̃Is = αs θIs + βs . Then, the training trajectory of the model is the same
376   with both parameterizations θ and θ̃. This property is desirable in the case of neural networks, where

393   Hessian and its inverse H̄−1 are available and all their off-diagonal coefficients have been computed

395
396   Setup. We have trained LeNet-5 and VGG-11’5 on CIFAR-10 using SGD with momentum. Before
397   each epoch, we compute the full-batch gradient, denoted by u, which we use as a direction to compute
398   H̄, again in full-batch. We report submatrices of H̄ and H̄−1 at initialization and at the epoch where
399   the validation loss is the best in Figure 1a (LeNet) and Figure 1b (VGG-11’).
400
      For the sake of readability, H̄ has been divided into blocks: a weight-weight block H̄WW , a bias-bias
401
      block H̄BB , and a weight-bias block H̄WB . They represent the interactions between the layers: for
402   instance, (H̄WB )l1 l2 represents the interaction between the tensor of weights of layer l1 and the vector
403   of biases of layer l2 .

524   6          D ISCUSSION
525
526   Convergence rate. Method 1 does not come with a precise convergence rate. The rate proposed in
527   Appendix F (Theorem 1) gives only a heuristic. Given the convergence rates of Newton’s method and
528   Cauchy’s steepest descent, we can expect to find some in-between convergence rates. Since Cauchy’s
529   steepest method is vulnerable to a highly anisotropic Hessian, it would be valuable to know how
530   much this weakness is overcome by our method.
531
532   Practicality. Despite the interesting properties of Method 1 (scalability, invariance by reparameteri-
533
      zation, evaluation of long-range interactions between layers), we have proposed nothing more than a
      proof-of-concept. This method remains subject to instabilities during training, which is expected for
534
      a second-order method, but not acceptable for the end user. Therefore, some additional tricks should
535
      be added to improve the stability of the training, which is a common practice, but usually comes with
536   additional hyperparameters to tune. Also, improvements are needed to reduce the duration of each
537   epoch, which is longer than with K-FAC.

                     combinations_with_replacement(range(S), d-1)]

       Diagonal approximation of Method 1. Throughout this paper, we have emphasized the importance
       of considering the interactions between layers when training a neural network. In fact, Method 1
1378
       allows the user to keep track of them at a reasonable computational cost. But is it useful to take these
1379
       interactions into account?
1380
1381   If the computational cost is really an issue, one can compute only the diagonal coefficients of H̄ and
1382   set the off-diagonal coefficients to zero. Let H̄0 be this diagonal approximation of H̄:
1383                                          H̄0 := Diag((h̄ii )1≤i≤S ),
1384
1385   where (h̄ii )1≤i≤S are the diagonal coefficients of H̄.
1386
       Then, we call the diagonal approximation of Method 1, Method 1 where H̄ has been replaced by H̄0 .
1387
1388
       Experiments. We have tested Method 1 with the hyperparameters we have used in Section 5.2
1389   and its diagonal approximation with a grid of hyperparameters λ1 and λint . The results are shown in
1390   Figure 6. Note that the configuration λ1 = 1 was tested with VGG11’, but resulted in instantaneous
1391   divergence, so we have not plotted the corresponding training curves.
1392
       According to Figure 6, the diagonal approximation of Method 1 performs worse or is more unstable
1393
       than 1. Therefore, when training LeNet or VGG11’ with CIFAR10, it is better to keep the off-diagonal
1394
       coefficients of H̄.
1395
1396   In short, one should worry about the interactions between layers.
