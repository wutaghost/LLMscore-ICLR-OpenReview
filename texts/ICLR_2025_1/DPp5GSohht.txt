009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite being pretrained on large-scale data, multimodal models such as CLIP
012            can still learn spurious correlations. However, CLIP does not seem to learn the
013            same spurious correlations as standard vision models, performing worse on some
014            benchmark datasets (Waterbirds) yet better on others (CelebA). We investigate this
015            discrepancy and ﬁnd that CLIP’s robustness on these datasets is highly sensitive
016            to the choice of class prompts. Worst-group accuracy can be arbitrarily improved
017            or worsened by making minute, single-word changes to prompts. We further pro-
018
               vide evidence that the root cause of this phenomenon is coverage — using class
               prompts that are out-of-distribution with respect to pretraining can worsen spuri-
019
               ous correlations. Motivated by these ﬁndings, we propose using class prompts that
020
               are generated from a public image-to-text model, such as BLIP. We show that per-
021            forming k-nearest neighbors on these prompt embeddings improve downstream
022            robustness without needing to ﬁne-tune CLIP.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Icarus, who perished by ﬂying too close to the sun, made the fatal mistake of ignoring distribution
028   shift — namely, that proximity to the sun would increase ambient temperature, melting the wax that
029   held his wings together.1 Much like Icarus’ wings, we too desire our machine learning models today
030   to be robust — an umbrella term that describes the model’s ability to maintain good performance in
031
      the face of distribution shifts at test time.
032   Of the many ﬂavors of distribution shifts that have been studied, one such pernicious phenomenon
033   is the presence of spurious correlations or shortcuts. These are features that are highly correlated
034   to the label under the training distribution, however, this relationship breaks down on unseen test
035   distributions. One canonical example is the image background, with distribution shift occuring
036   when objects are photographed at a different place or time of day (Beery et al., 2018; Zech et al.,
037
      2018). Models trained under empirical risk minimization (ERM) have been observed to rely on a
      combination of shortcut and salient features and therefore fail to generalize at test time (Hovy &
038
      Søgaard, 2015; Hashimoto et al., 2018; Puli et al., 2023).
039
040   In recent years, large pretrained models have enjoyed notable success on a wide range of tasks.
041   These refer to models with billions of parameters that are typically trained in a self-supervised
042   manner on broad Internet-scale data (Bommasani et al., 2021). The resulting model can then be
043
      adapted to downstream applications by ﬁne-tuning the model’s parameters on the smaller dataset of
      interest. For image classiﬁcation tasks, the most widely-used pretrained model is CLIP (Radford
044
      et al., 2021), whereby an image and text encoder are jointly trained using the contrastive InfoNCE
045
      objective (Oord et al., 2018) on a huge corpus of image-caption pairs. Downstream classiﬁcation is
046   performed in a zero-shot manner by codifying class labels as text prompts, then predicting the class
047   whose label embedding has the highest inner product with the image embedding.
048
049
      It was hoped that CLIP and other large pretrained models would be more robust to spurious corre-
      lations than smaller bespoke vision models, having been exposed to data orders of magnitude larger
050
      than the downstream dataset containing the spurious correlation. Results, however, paint a more
051

      complicated picture. Zhang & Ré (2022) show that the gap between average accuracy and worst-
055   group accuracy ranged from 55.6% in Waterbirds (Wah et al., 2011; Sagawa et al., 2019) to 7.9% in
056   CelebA (Liu et al., 2018), two widely-used benchmark datasets. Clearly, large-scale pretraining is
057   not the one-stop solution for mitigating spurious correlations.
058
      Existing literature has simply sought to remedy this gap by ﬁne-tuning CLIP on the biased training
059
      dataset (Zhang & Ré, 2022; Yang et al., 2023), either through explicitly using additional labels of
060
      the spurious feature or by making certain assumptions of model behavior. These methods can indeed
061   be viewed as contrastive analogues of methods originally proposed in the targeted setting (Sagawa
062   et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021).
063
064
      Motivated instead by the unexplained difference in worst-group accuracy between CelebA and Wa-
      terbirds, we set out to more closely probe zero-shot behavior in these datasets. We expand our
065
      experiments to include OpenCLIP (Ilharco et al., 2021) in addition to the original CLIP models.
066
      We observe that CLIP’s zero-shot prediction of background in Waterbirds is just as poor as fore-
067   ground. Further probing of image embedding space shows poor separability by both foreground and
068   background features. These observations cannot be fully explained by spurious correlations.
069
070
      Our ﬁrst contribution is to show that the choice of class prompt greatly affects zero-shot accuracy
      in both CelebA and Waterbirds. Arbitrary changes to prompt templates can worsen or improve
071
      worst-group performance. Delving deeper, we show that the root cause of this discrepancy is due to
072
      the class prompts being out-of-distribution (OOD) during CLIP’s pretraining. We verify this directly
073   on OpenCLIP and MetaCLIP (Xu et al., 2023) by counting token frequencies.
074
075   From these experiments, we conclude that choosing in-distribution class prompts that CLIP has
076
      seen during pretraining is critical to zero-shot success, particularly in datasets containing spurious
      correlations where OOD prompts can reinforce such biases. To this end, our second contribution
077
      is leveraging the use of large, public image-to-text models to automatically generate proxy class
078
      prompts for downstream classiﬁcation.
079
080   We show that such a model — we use BLIP (Li et al., 2022a) in our experiments — can be used
081   to generate captions on the downstream dataset, which are then used to classify test samples via
082
      k-nearest neighbors. Our approach achieves comparable results on spurious correlation datasets
      without needing to ﬁne-tune CLIP’s embeddings on the downstream dataset and without requiring
083
      any spurious labels. We verify our method on ImageNet-1K (Deng et al., 2009) in addition to
084
      Waterbirds and CelebA. Beyond robustness, our work is also a step towards automating downstream
085   classiﬁcation without requiring human input to generate class prompts.

      For a downstream image classiﬁcation task, we let x ∈ X denote covariates, y ∈ Y the class label
      and s ∈ S the spurious label. We consider a family of data-generating distributions pe (x, y, s)
091
      indexed by the environment e, of which the training (e = tr) and test (e = te) distributions are two
092
      such environments. Spurious correlations happen when pe (y, s) changes across environments.
093
094   Most spurious correlation datasets contain salient features h := h(x) that can predict y perfectly.
095   That is, there exist some deterministic function f1 such that f1 (h) = y for all e. Furthermore, there
096
      is no deterministic function f2 such that f2 (h) = s for all e. The existence of such h implies that
      ptr (y|h) = pte (y|h). As such, the optimal predictor that minimizes training loss will also minimize
097
      test loss and the Bayes optimal predictor ptr (y|x) should be robust to test-time distribution shift.
098
      Unfortunately, empirical risk minimization (ERM) generally fails to learn h, instead learning a
099   representation of s that breaks at test time (Sagawa et al., 2019; Geirhos et al., 2020).

      CLIP and its variants Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021)
152
      pioneered the use of contrastively matching (Oord et al., 2018) image-caption pairs as an effective, at-
153
      scale pretraining task to learn useful image representations for downstream tasks. Later works have
154   extensively studied CLIP’s effectiveness and proposed various improvements. Some have tangential
155   relevance to robustness, for example, a ﬁne-grained variant that matches regions of the image to
156   speciﬁc textual concepts (Zhong et al., 2022), exploiting Hopﬁeld networks to encourage the encoder
157   to extract richer features from the image (Fürst et al., 2022), and performing max-pooling in CLIP’s
158   vision encoder to reduce background bias (Li et al., 2022b). Petryk et al. (2022) uses CLIP to
159   improve the robustness of a vision model by guiding it to use speciﬁc parts of the image.

187   Spurious Correlations in CLIP Zhang et al. (2022) ﬁrst proposed a contrastive approach for mit-
188   igating spurious correlations in (unimodal) vision models, whereby spurious attributes are inferred
189   through Liu et al. (2021) and then used to learn similar representations between majority and minor-
190   ity samples from the same class. Zhang & Ré (2022) follow up by identifying spurious correlations
191   as a problem in CLIP speciﬁcally, and propose ﬁne-tuning CLIP embeddings in such a contrastive
192   manner. Yang et al. (2023) also propose ﬁne-tuning CLIP embeddings contrastively but they make
193
      explicit use of group labels. Unlike these earlier work, our paper is the ﬁrst to (i) highlight that

194
      ing zero-shot classiﬁcation without ﬁne-tuning embeddings on downstream datasets.
195
196   Finally, recent work by Adila et al. (2023) also aim to improve CLIP’s robustness without ﬁne-
197   tuning. They query a large language model (LLM) for additional knowledge of the salient feature

199
      with a publicly-available large pretrained model — namely, the image-to-text model BLIP (Li et al.,

        4.1    S PURIOUS ATTRIBUTE P REDICTION
248
249     It is not possible to directly compare the two pretraining datasets without access to CLIP’s pretrain-
250     ing dataset. Instead, our ﬁrst proxy is to establish how strongly each model has learnt the spurious
251     concept. We perform zero-shot classiﬁcation using the spurious attribute as label, i.e. predicting
252     background on Waterbirds and gender on CelebA. Table 1 (last three rows) shows these results.
253     Our ﬁndings are counterintuitive. On the two CLIP models, the Waterbirds WGA is ∼50% — no
254     better than random and only slightly higher than true label prediction. In other words, CLIP’s zero-
255     shot performance on background prediction is almost as poor as foreground (label) prediction.
256     If CLIP’s pretraining distribution was skewed towards majority groups and had allowed the model
257     to learn background as a spurious correlation, we would accordingly expect the encoder to learn
258     a strong representation of background features. However, our results show that CLIP is unable to
259     (correctly and non-spuriously) associate background features with their label.
260     Furthermore, we observe that the average accuracy has also decreased by 5% to 10% compared
261     to true label prediction. This indicates that background prediction is relatively poorer for majority
262     groups than minority groups — further contradicting the naive explanation that pretraining coverage
263     of Waterbirds is biased towards majority groups.

      Spurious correlations alone fail to explain our ﬁndings above. How, then, can we resolve the dis-
352
      crepancy between the expected and observed WGAs in these benchmark datasets? Our hypothesis
353
      is that choosing class prompts that are OOD with respect to CLIP’s pretraining distribution
354   signiﬁcantly impairs zero-shot accuracy. In datasets where spurious correlations occur, this effect
355   can arbitrarily reinforce or mitigate the worst-group gap.

402    5     AUTOMATING C LASS P ROMPTS USING I MAGE - TO -T EXT G ENERATION
403
404   Our ﬁndings in Section 4 highlight the necessity of using class prompts that have pretraining sup-
405   port. This begets the key question: how do we ensure that the prompts we use for downstream
406   classiﬁcation are in-distribution? Our proposal is simple. Much like we used Llama-2 as a proxy
407
      to to approximate the marginal distribution of speciﬁc class prompts, we can similarly leverage a
      large pretrained model to generate class prompts, under the same assumption that pretraining on
408
      large-scale data would ensure similar support over joint image-text space.
409
410   Instead of manually generating K prompts (one for each class), we propose using a separate image-
411   to-text model to generate Ntr captions, one for each sample of the downstream training set. These
412   captions are passed through CLIP’s text encoder to be converted into text embeddings, resulting in a
                                                                  ∗
413   set of Ntr embeddings: {txi }Ni=1 . For a given test image x , prediction is carried out by performing
                                      tr


414   k-nearest neighbors (k-NN) algorithm on tx∗ and the support set {txi }N       tr
                                                                                  i=1 . We experiment with
415   two variants of this approach: (1) performing k-NN on the image embedding of x∗ , i.e. by passing
416
      the test image into CLIP’s image encoder, and (2) performing k-NN on the text embedding of x∗ , i.e.
      by passing test image into the image-to-text model, and then converting the resulting caption into a
417
      text embedding via CLIP’s text encoder. The full algorithm (both variants) is shown in Algorithm 1.

499     6    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
500
501     Our work is the ﬁrst to investigate the unexplained differences in spurious correlation behavior be-
502     tween CLIP and unimodal vision models. In doing so, we uncover the key ﬁnding that the choice
503     of text prompts matters greatly for zero-shot robustness, with CLIP’s performance suffering when
504     OOD class prompts are used. This is especially harmful in spurious correlation datasets, where the
505     OOD prompts can reinforce spuriousness. We note that our results and our proposed approach are
506
        not restricted to CLIP or even CLIP-like models: they can be extended to multimodal generative
        models more generally, where text is one of the modes of information. As the ImageNet-1K exper-
507
        iment shows, our work also extends to broader distribution shift tasks beyond spurious correlations,
508
        so long as the distribution shift arises from OOD text prompts.
509
510     Future Work (1) Testing BLIP-CLIP on non-spurious correlation datasets, to understand if BLIP-
511     generated captions are universally useful in improving accuracy even when spurious correlations do
512     not exist. (2) BLIP-CLIP is not fully automated as there is still some manual input in the form of
513     choosing a reasonable preamble prompt to query BLIP for completion. An automated system for
514     choosing the preamble prompt will be useful.
