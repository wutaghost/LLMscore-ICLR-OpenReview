009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Capturing the continuous underlying dynamics of irregular time series is essential
012            for accurately reflecting the ongoing evolution and intricate correlations within
013            the data. The discrete nature of current models, including RNN-based models
014            and transformer variants, poses challenges when it comes to generalizing to the
015            continuous-time data paradigms, which is necessary for capturing ongoing dy-
016            namics of irregular time series. Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (NODEs)
017            assume a continuous latent dynamic and provide an elegant framework for irregular
018
               time series analysis. However, integrating new information while maintaining the
               continuity of latent dynamics remains challenging. To tackle this problem, we intro-
019
               duce Differentiable Hidden State (DHS) enhanced neural ODE, a data-dependent
020
               framework that is capable of effectively capturing temporal dependencies and
021            ensuring the continuity of the hidden process. We leverage the theory of general-
022            ized inverses to innovatively compute attention mechanism in reverse and obtain a
023            continuous representation. To capture more accurate temporal relationships, we in-
024            troduce Hoyer metric and maximize the sparsity of it. Experimental results on both
025            synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of our model. The
026            code is provided on anonymous link https://anonymous.4open.science/status/DHS-
027            5F24.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Irregular time series data are ubiquitous across a variety of real-world applications, including disease
033
      prevention, financial decision-making, and earthquake prediction Bauer et al. (2016); Jia & Benson
      (2019); Zuo et al. (2020). Irregular time series data are characterized by non-uniform sampling,
034
      with observations occurring at variable time intervals Chen et al. (2024); Che et al. (2018). This
035
      irregularity, coupled with frequent missing data due to technical issues or data quality concerns, poses
036   challenges for existing time series analysis methods, including RNN-based models Rangapuram et al.
037   (2018); Salinas et al. (2020); Chung et al. (2014) and Transformer variants Zhou et al. (2021); Child
038   et al. (2019); Li et al. (2019); Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2022).
039
      Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (NODEs) have become a favored and promising approach
040
      for irregular time series modeling, due to their sequential processing capabilities and ability to
041
      manage irregularly sampled data Chen et al. (2018). By employing appropriate Ordinary Differential
042   Equations (ODEs) to model the dynamics of irregular time series, it becomes feasible to reconstruct a
043   continuous and complete time series from the irregularly sampled data through the application of
044   integration techniques to the ODEs.
045
      NODE-based methods Rubanova et al. (2019); Lechner & Hasani (2020); Chien & Chen (2022);
046
      De Brouwer et al. (2019); Herrera et al. (2020); Poli et al. (2019); Oskarsson et al. (2023); De Brouwer
047
      & Krishnan (2023) face a fundamental challenge on irregular time series modeling. They integrate
048   from an initial value to derive all subsequent values, without considering observed data points
049   later than the initial point. They integrate latent state at each time points with observations, i.e.,
050   having different initial value at different time intervals. Though such mechanism can achieve a
051   certain accuracy, it considers only one observation at each time point, and the correlations among
052   observations are ignored. Meanwhile, such mechanism results in a fragmented latent process that
053   may not accurately reflect the true dynamics, as shown in Fig. 1 (a). Tackling the issue of fragmented
      latent state of NODEs, NCDE approaches Kidger et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024)

075   In response to the limitations inherent in existing solutions, this paper presents the Differentiable
076   Hidden State (DHS) enhanced neural ODE framework, a data-driven approach designed to adeptly
077
      capture temporal dynamics while ensuring the seamless continuity of the latent process. An attention-
      based differential hidden state is introduced, which considers irregular sampled observations as
078
      a projection matrix mapping time series into hidden state space. Since the projection is linear,
079
      the hidden states preserve the continuity of original time series. Our methodology harnesses the
080   power of generalized inverses to innovatively reverse-engineer the attention mechanism, yielding
081   ODEs describing the dynamics of hidden states. To enhance the precision of temporal relationships,
082   we integrate the Hoyer metric Hurley & Rickard (2009), an advanced tool of sparsity metric. By
083   strategically maximizing Hoyer metric, our framework refines the model’s ability to discern subtle
084   yet significant temporal shifts, thereby improving the accuracy and reliability of predictions.
085
      Our contribution is summarized as follows,
086
087              • We propose an attention-based differential hidden state to maintain the continuity of time
088                series and applies generalized inverses to innovatively derive ODEs describing the dynamics
089                of the hidden states of irregular time series.
090              • A deep model is constructed based on the derived ODEs, and Hoyer metric is integrated to
091                enhance the precision of temporal correlation modeling.
092
                 • Extensive experiments are conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets, and the
093                result demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model.

096   2       R ELATED W ORK
097
098   Deep models for time series modeling have been extensively studied. Time series modeling, with its
099
      significant practical applications, has been a focal point of research for many years Box & Jenkins
      (1968). The recent surge in deep learning has led to the introduction of numerous deep learning-based
100
      models Salinas et al. (2020); Qin et al. (2017); Sen et al. (2019); Tuncel & Baydogan (2018); Kalpakis
101
      et al. (2001), many of which employ recurrent neural networks (RNNs) Bai et al. (2020) and temporal
102   convolutional networks (CNNs) Yu et al. (2018) for series modeling. However, RNNs are known
103   for their limited parallelization capabilities, and both RNN- and CNN-based models struggle to
104   effectively capture long-term temporal dependencies. The advent of the self-attention mechanism in
105   fields like natural language processing Vaswani et al. (2017) and computer vision Rao et al. (2021)
106   has inspired substantial efforts to adapt and apply transformers to time series forecasting Zeng et al.
107   (2022); Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2022; 2021). Nevertheless, all the above methods are designed
      for regular time series data and fail to be extended to the problem of irregular time series analysis.

108
      Owing to their exceptional ability to capture temporal dynamics, Neural Ordinary Differential
109   Equations (NODEs) have recently gained popularity in the analysis of irregular time series. Existing
110   works of NODE family can be divided into two kinds. In the first kind of methods, NCDE Kidger et al.
111   (2020) is a pioneering work that introduces controlled differential equations into NODE, obtaining
112   a rough estimate of the latent process using simple numerical differentiation. Subsequent work
113   builds on this foundation, with NRDE Morrill et al. (2021) further utilizing rough path theory to
114   model long-term sequence dependencies. ContiFormer Chen et al. (2024) constructs a continuous
115   extension of the Transformer, where the query is obtained by interpolation. Neural Lad Li et al.
116   (2024) models periodicity, trend information, local information, and multidimensional structural
117
      information, with the modeling of local information based on the differential of the interpolated
      sequence. However, such kind of methods generate the whole complete time series with only a
118
      given initial value, and fail to fully leverage the contextual information of the data. In the second
119
      kind of methods, ODE-RNN Rubanova et al. (2019) and ODE-LSTM Lechner & Hasani (2020)
120   both use gating mechanisms in the update step to encode new information. CADN Chien & Chen
121   (2022) is based on the ODE-RNN framework and incorporates an attention mechanism to strengthen
122   modeling. GRU-ODE-Bayes De Brouwer et al. (2019) and Neural Jump ODE Jia & Benson (2019)
123   use Bayesian estimation methods in the update step. GNODE Poli et al. (2019) models potential state
124   changes using the extension of ODE on graphs, GDE, in the integral step, while TGNN4I Oskarsson
125   et al. (2023) assumes that ODE changes are linear; both use a GNN+GRU approach for updates.
126   ANDE De Brouwer & Krishnan (2023) introduces the HIPPO matrix in the integral step, working in
127   conjunction with NODE to strengthen the modeling of historical information, and updates directly by
128
      assignment in the update step. Nevertheless, the process of these methods results in fragmented latent
      representations, which can not accurately represent the true dynamics of continuous time series.

537
538   This paper tackles a significant challenge faced by current neural ODE methods: their inability to
539   seamlessly integrate contextual information while preserving the continuity of the latent dynamics of
      irregular time series. To overcome this, we introduce an attention-based differential hidden state space,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      leveraging irregularly sampled observations as Key and Value matrices to enrich the model’s context
541   awareness. Building upon this novel hidden state space, we employ the theory of generalized inverses
542   to formulate an ODE that encapsulates the dynamics of the hidden states over time. To enhance
543   the precision of temporal relationships, we incorporate the Hoyer metric, aiming to maximize the
544   sparsity of attention scores during the generation of hidden states. Our approach has been rigorously
545   compared with existing state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets, with
546   experimental results consistently showcasing the superior effectiveness of our model in irregular time
547   series analysis.

706   solution and we say the system is compatible. If rank(A|b) ̸= rank(A), then the system Ax = b
707   has no solution and the system is incompatible.

880   C      I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
881
882   There are three small neural networks in DHS, namely the input map from observations, the output
883   map and the one in the derivative of DHS. We use a one-layer GRU to map the observations into
884   latent states. An MLP with one hidden layer is used for model the dynamics of DHS. For output, we
885   use an MLP with 1 hidden layer. For all datasets, the hidden size of MLPs is set to 32. Integration
886   method is implicit adams, an adaptive method with tiny numerical error. We use early stopping when
887   the validation loss has not increase in 20 epochs. Learning rate is set to 0.001 and weight decay is set
888   to 0.001.
889   For classification tasks, batch size is set to 128 and the dimension of DHS and information state rt is
890   set to 16. The integration step of ODE solution is set to 0.05. When we train the model, we have 250
891   max epochs. For interpolation and extrapolation tasks, batch size is set to 32. The dimension of DHS
892   and information state rt is set to 32. The integration step of ODE solution is set to 5. Max epochs is
893   set to 100.
