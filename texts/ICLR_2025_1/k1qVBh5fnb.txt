009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent progress in robotic imitation learning has been enabled by policy archi-
012            tectures that scale to complex visuomotor tasks, multimodal distributions, and
013            large datasets. However, these methods rely on supervised learning of actions
014            from expert demonstrations, which can be challenging to scale. We propose
015            Latent Diffusion Planning, which forecasts future states as well as actions via dif-
016            fusion. This objective can scalably leverage heterogeneous data sources and pro-
017            vides a denser supervision signal for learning. To plan over images, we learn a
018
               compact latent space through a variational autoencoder. We then train a planner to
               forecast future latent states, and an inverse dynamics model to extract actions from
019
               the plans. As planning is separated from action prediction, LDP can leverage sub-
020
               optimal or action-free data to improve performance in low demonstration regimes.
021            On simulated visual robotic manipulation tasks, LDP outperforms state-of-the-art
022            imitation learning approaches as they cannot leverage such additional data.1

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Combining large-scale expert datasets and powerful imitation learning policies has been a promising
028   direction for robot learning. Recent methods using transformer backbones or diffusion heads (Octo
029   Model Team et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Chi et al., 2023) have capitalized
030   on new robotics datasets pooled together from many institutions (Khazatsky et al., 2024; Open X-
031   Embodiment Collaboration et al., 2023), showing potential for learning generalizable robot policies.
032   However, this recipe is fundamentally limited by data, as robotics demonstration data is limited and
033   expensive to collect. While it is often easier to collect in-domain data that is suboptimal or action-
034   free, these methods are not designed to use such data, as they rely on directly modeling optimal
035
      actions.
036   Prior work in reinforcement learning has explored using heterogeneous data sources. Approaches
037   that can be scaled to the imitation learning setting include conditioning the policy on optimality
038   (Chen et al., 2021), and relabeling action-free trajectories using an inverse model (Baker et al.,
039   2022). However, many of these approaches have not been shown to be competitive with state-of-
040   the-art robotic imitation learning (Mirchandani et al., 2024). Recent work in robotics has leveraged
041
      heterogeneous data for pretraining via representation learning (Radosavovic et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
      2023b; Cui et al., 2024). However, only using the data for representation learning is limited, as
042
      it does not necessarily improve the planning capabilities of the method. In this work, we investi-
043
      gate how a simple planning-based method can leverage heterogeneous data in a principled way be
044   decoupling forecasting future states from extracting actions.
045
046   We propose Latent Diffusion Planning (LDP), which learns a planner that can be trained on data
047
      does not require actions; and an inverse dynamics model that can be trained on data that may be sub-
      optimal. While prior planning-based works (Du et al., 2023a; Black et al., 2023) improve high-level
048
      decision making by producing subgoals, we focus on forecasting a dense trajectory of latent states
049
      as an alternative method for imitation learning. As diffusion objectives proved to be effective for
050   imitation learning (Chi et al., 2023), we use diffusion for both forecasting state and actions, which
051   enables competitive performance. LDP plans across latent image embeddings, scaling up gracefully

099

      Imitation Learning in Robotics. One popular approach to learning robot control policies is imita-
101
      tion learning, where policies are learned from expert-collected demonstration datasets. This is most
102   commonly done via behavior cloning, which reduces policy learning to a supervised learning objec-
103   tive of mapping states to actions. Recently, Diffusion Policy (Chi et al., 2023) and Action Chunking
104   with Transformers (Zhao et al., 2023) have shown successful results in complex manipulation tasks
105   using action chunking and more expressive architectures. Similarly, Behavior Transformer (Shafi-
106   ullah et al., 2022) and VQ-BeT (Lee et al., 2024) have focused on improving the ability of policies
107   to capture multimodal behaviors. In this work, we focus on forecasting a sequence of future states
      instead of actions, and use diffusion to capture multimodal trajectories.

      Learning from Unlabelled Suboptimal and Action-Free Data. Learning from suboptimal data
109   has long been a goal of many robot learning methods, including reinforcement learning. A typical
110   approach is offline reinforcement learning, which considers solving a Markov decision process from
111   an offline dataset of states, actions, and reward (Levine et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2020; Kostrikov
112   et al., 2021; Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2022). Particularly relevant are the approaches
113   that use supervised learning conditioned on rewards (Schmidhuber, 2019; Kumar et al., 2019a; Chen
114   et al., 2021). In this work, we want to leverage suboptimal, reward-free data, such as play data or
115   failed trajectories. In addition, we would like to avoid the additional complexity of annotating the
116   data with rewards or training a value function which the offline RL methods rely on.
117   Autonomous imitation learning methods seek to self-bootstrap from a pretrained imitative policy.
118   Typically, these methods assume learning from online, autonomous rollouts and reward labels from
119   trained classifiers or vision-language models (Konstantinos Bousmalis* & Heess, 2023; Zhou et al.,
120   2024b; Mirchandani et al., 2024). Unlike these works, we assume access to a static, offline dataset,
121   and we do not label the dataset with pseudo-rewards.
122   Several works have also addressed learning from action-free data, such as using inverse models
123   (Torabi et al., 2018; Baker et al., 2022), latent action models (Edwards et al., 2019; Schmeckpeper
124   et al., 2020; Bruce et al., 2024), or representation learning (Radosavovic et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
125   2023b; Cui et al., 2024). In this work we focus on a simple recipe for robotic imitation learning that
126   is naturally able to leverage action free data through state forecasting.
127   Diffusion and Image Prediction in Robot Learning. Diffusion models, due to their expressivity
128   and training and sampling stability, have been applied to robot learning tasks. Diffusion has been
129   used in offline reinforcement learning (Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023) and imitation learning (Chi
130   et al., 2023). Diffuser (Janner et al., 2022) learns a denoising diffusion model on trajectories, includ-
131   ing both states and actions, in a model-based reinforcement learning setting. Decision Diffuser (Ajay
132   et al., 2023) extends Diffuser by showing compositionality over skills, rewards, and constraints, and
133   instead diffuses over states and uses an inverse dynamics model to extract actions from the plan.
134   Due to the complexity of modeling image trajectories, Diffuser and Decision Diffuser restrict their
135
      applications to low-dimensional states.
136   To scale up to diffusing over higher-dimensional plans, UniPi (Du et al., 2023a; Ko et al., 2023)
137   adapts video models for planning. Unlike works that rely on foundation models and video models
138   for planning (Du et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024a), our method avoids computa-
139   tional and modeling complexities of generative video modeling by planning over latent embeddings
140   instead.
141   Previous works have used world models to plan over images in a compact latent space (Hansen et al.,
142   2024; Hafner et al., 2019; 2020). In contrast with these works, we focus on single task imitation
143   instead of reinforcement learning.
144
      Many prior works argued that state forecasting objectives are uniquely suitable for robotics to im-
145   prove planning quality with trajectory optimization or reinforcement learning Finn & Levine (2017);
146   Yang et al. (2023), by using the model directly to plan future states Du et al. (2023b;a), as well as
147   representation learning (Wu et al., 2023a; Radosavovic et al., 2023). We follow this line of work
148   by proposing a planning-based method competitive to state-of-the-art robotic imitation learning that
149   can leverage heterogeneous data sources.

154   Diffusion Models Diffusion models are likelihood-based generative models that learn an iterative
155   denoising process from a Gaussian prior to a data distribution (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho
156   et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020). Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho et al.,
157   2020) optimizes a variational lower bound on data likelihood, derived in a similar way to variational
158   autoencoders (Kingma & Welling, 2014; Rezende et al., 2014). DDPMs are trained to reverse a
159   single noising step, formally:

      and Decision Diffuser can be conditioned on reward, skills, and constraints.

200   Recent generative models have used Latent Diffusion Models, which trains a diffusion model in
201   a learned, compressed latent space (Rombach et al., 2022; Peebles & Xie, 2023; Blattmann et al.,
202   2023) to improve computational and memory efficiency. The latent space is typically learned via an
203   autoencoder, with encoder E and decoder D trained to reconstruct x ≈ x̂ = D(E(x)). Instead of
204
      diffusing over x, the diffusion model is trained on diffusing over z = E(x).
205   Imitation Learning In the imitation learning framework, we assume access to a dataset of expert
206   demonstrations, D ≜ {(s0 , x0 , a0 ), .., (sT , xT , aT )}, generated by πE , an expert policy. si , xi , ai
207   correspond to the state, image, and action at timestep i respectively. The imitation learning ob-
208   jective is to extract a policy π̂(a|s, x) that most closely imitates πE . In robotics, this is typically
209   approached through behavior cloning, which learns the mapping between states and actions directly
210   via supervised learning. We consider single-task imitation, where the dataset corresponds to a single
211   task.
212   Datasets of expert demonstrations often do not provide sufficient state distribution coverage to ef-
213   fectively solve a given task with imitation learning. However, there often exists additional data in
214   the form of action-free or suboptimal data, which may consist of failed policy rollouts, play data,
215   or miscellaneous environment interactions. Unfortunately, behavior cloning assumes access to data
      annotated with optimal actions, so such additional data cannot be easily incorporated into training.

253   4.1   L EARNING THE L ATENT S PACE
254
255   We circumvent planning over high-dimensional image observations by planning over a learned latent
256   space. Similar to prior work in planning with world models (Watter et al., 2015; Ha & Schmidhuber,
257   2018; Hafner et al., 2020), we learn this latent space using an image reconstruction objective. Our
258
      planner thus becomes similar to video models that forecast image frames in a learned latent space
      (Yan et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023).
259
260   In practical scenarios, we may have a limited demonstration dataset, but much larger and diverse
261   suboptimal or action-free datasets. In this phase of learning, we can make use of the visual informa-
262   tion in such datasets for training a more robust latent encoder.
263   In this work, we train a variational autoencoder (Kingma & Welling, 2014; Rezende et al., 2014) to
264   obtain a latent encoder E and decoder D. Specifically, we optimize the β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017)
265   objective:
266                    LVAE (θ, ϕ; x, z, β) = Eqϕ (z | x) [log pθ (x | z)] − βDKL (qϕ (z | x)||p(z))  (3)
267
268   where x is our original image, z is our learned latent representation of the image, θ are the parameters
269   for our decoder, ϕ are the parameters for our encoder, and β is the weight for the KL regularization
      term.

      4.2   P LANNER AND I NVERSE DYNAMICS M ODEL
271
272   Our policy consists of two separate modules: (1) a planner over latent embeddings, and (2) an inverse
273   dynamics model. The planner and IDM both optimize the DDPM objective.
274
      The planner is conditioned on the current latent embedding, which consists of the concatenated
275
      latent image embedding and robot proprioception, and diffuses over a horizon of future embeddings.
276   We use Diffusion Policy’s Conditional U-Net architecture. Concretely, we optimize the following
277   objective:
278                        Lplanner (ψ, z) = Et,ϵ [||ϵψ (ẑk+1 , ..., ẑk+H ; zk , t) − ϵ||2 ]       (4)
279
280   where zk is the latent embedding at timestep k of the trajectory; ẑk+1 , ..., ẑk+H is the noised latent
281   embedding sequence, with corresponding noise ϵ; H is the maximum horizon of the forecasted latent
282
      plan; t is the diffusion noise timestep; and ψ are the parameters of the planner diffusion model.
283   Our inverse dynamics model is trained to reconstruct the action between a pair of states, conditioned
284   on their associated latent embeddings.
285
                                  LIDM (ξ, z) = Et,ϵ [||ϵξ (âk ; zk , zk+1 , t) − ϵ||2 ]                  (5)
286
287
      where zk is the latent embedding at timestep k of the trajectory; âk is the noised action, with corre-
288
      sponding noise ϵ; t is the diffusion noise timestep; and ξ are the parameters of the inverse dynamics
289   diffusion model.

291   During inference, the planner forecasts a future horizon of states. Like Diffusion Policy, we employ
292

454   which can more faithfully extract actions from the diffused plan.

459   data may lead to further improvements.

      6   D ISCUSSION
464
465
466
      We presented Latent Diffusion Planning, a simple planning-based method for imitation learning. We
467
      show that our design using powerful diffusion models for latent state forecasting enables competitive
468   performance with state-of-the-art imitation learning. We further show this latent state forecasting
469   objective enables us to easily leverage heterogeneous data sources. In low-demonstration data imi-
470   tation learning regime, LDP outperforms prior imitation learning work that does not leverage such
471   additional data as effectively.
472
      Limitations. One limitation of the current approach is that the latent space for planning is sim-
473
      ply learned with a variational autoencoder and might not learn the most useful features for control.
474   Future work will explore different representation learning objectives. Furher, our method requires
475   diffusing over states, which incurs additional computational overhead as compared to diffusing ac-
476   tions. However, we expect continued improvements in hardware and inference speed will mitigate
477   this drawback. Finally, we did not explore applying recent improvements in diffusion models (Pee-
478   bles & Xie, 2023; Lipman et al., 2022), which will be important to scale to real-world applications.
479
      Future work. We have validated in simulation the hypothesis that latent state forecasting can lever-
480   age heterogeneous data sources. Future work will evaluate whether this can be used to improve
481   practical real-world applications. One direction is to use a diverse dataset of human collected data,
482   such as with handheld data collection tools (Young et al., 2021). Another approach would be to use
483   autonomously collected robotic data (Konstantinos Bousmalis* & Heess, 2023). As these alternative
484   data sources are easier to collect than demonstrations, they represent a different scaling paradigm
485   that can outperform pure behavior cloning approaches. By presenting a method that can leverage
      such data, we believe this work makes a step toward more performant and general robot policies.

      A.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
759
760   Diffusion Policy We use a Jax reimplementation of the convolutional Diffusion Policy, which we
761   verify can reproduce reported Robomimic benchmark results. For improved performance, we pro-
762   cess the 512-dimensional ResNet feature with an MLP [512, 256, 32] with ReLU activations and a
763   final tanh activation.
764   UniPi We use the open-source implementation of UniPi (Ko et al., 2023). For UniPi-OL and UniPi-
765   CL, we predict 7 future frames. During training time, for UniPi-OL, the 7 future frames are evenly
766   sampled from a training demonstration. For UniPi-CL, the 7 future frames are the next consecutive
767   frames.
768
      The goal-conditioned behavior cloning agent is implemented as a goal-conditioned Diffusion Pol-
769   icy (Chi et al., 2023) agent, and it is trained on chunks of 16. The inverse dynamics model is based
770   off of Hansen-Estruch et al. (2023), and shares the same architecture as the IDM used in LDP.
771
772
      We train the video prediction models for 100k gradient steps with batch size 16.
773   LDP The LDP VAE is adapted from Diffusion Transformer (Peebles & Xie, 2023). The plan-
774   ner is based directly off of the convolutional U-Net from Diffusion Policy (Chi et al., 2023), with
775   modifications to plan across latent embeddings instead of action chunks. The IDM is based off of
776   Hansen-Estruch et al. (2023).

839   In our experiments, we report results on 2 seeds, across the best performing checkpoint from last
840
      5 saved checkpoints. PushT results are based on environment reward, and Robomimic results are
      reported as success rates. For UniPi, we train two goal-conditioned or inverse dynamics models and
841
      report success from the 200k checkpoint.
842
843   For UniPi-OL evaluations, we predetermine the number of steps for the GCBC to reach each image
844   subgoal based on the demonstration lengths. For PushT, evaluation episode lengths are 200 steps;
845   Lift is 60 steps; Can is 140 steps; and Square is 160 steps. This maximum horizon is also enforced
846
      for UniPi-CL evaluations, for consistency.
