009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Maximum entropy algorithms have demonstrated significant progress in Rein-
012            forcement Learning (RL), which offers additional guidance in the form of en-
013            tropy, which is particularly beneficial in tasks with sparse rewards. Nevertheless,
014            current approaches grounded in policy entropy encourage the agent to explore di-
015            verse actions, yet they do not directly help the agent explore diverse states. In
016
               this study, we theoretically reveal the challenge of optimizing the next-state en-
               tropy of the agent. To address this limitation, we introduce Maximum Next-State
017
               Entropy (MNSE), a novel method that maximizes next-state entropy through an
018
               action mapping layer following the inner policy. We provide a theoretical analy-
019            sis demonstrating that MNSE can maximize next-state entropy by optimizing the
020            action entropy of the inner policy. We conduct extensive experiments on vari-
021            ous continuous control tasks and show that MNSE can significantly improve the
022            exploration capability of RL algorithms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
      Maximum entropy RL algorithms have demonstrated remarkable performance across various do-
027
      mains, including games (Gao et al., 2018), robotic control (Haarnoja et al., 2018a;b), and au-
028
      tonomous navigation (Sun et al., 2022). The maximum entropy framework enhances policy explo-
029   ration and robustness by optimizing both reward and policy entropy simultaneously (Ziebart et al.,
030   2008; 2010). Recent advancements adapt the temperature dynamically (Hu et al., 2021)to improve
031   the trade-off between reward and entropy. Meanwhile, Deep Soft Policy Gradient (DSPG) Shi et al.
032   (2019) integrates soft policy gradients with the soft Bellman equation to address stability issues in
033   off-policy learning.
034
      However, maximizing policy entropy may not directly help the agent explore diverse states due to
035   redundancy in the action space. For example, in real-world tasks, actuators often exhibit saturation
036   and dead zone issues due to design redundancies or equipment aging (Galuppini et al., 2018; Bai,
037   2002). This leads to a redundant action space, where multiple actions can result in the same state.
038     If we cannot deal with this issue, the nonlinear actuators can reduce control accuracy, thereby
039   significant degrading the control performance.
040
      To help the agent directly explore diverse states, current researchers propose novelty-based explo-
041   ration methods, such as random network distillation (Burda et al., 2018) and pseudo-count tech-
042   niques (Lobel et al., 2023; Machado et al., 2020). These methods aim to drive agents toward dis-
043   covering more diverse states by directly evaluating the novelty of a state and incorporating it as
044   an exploration bonus into the extrinsic reward. Current novelty-based and pseudo-count exploration
045   methods have achieved considerable success across various domains. However, it is unclear whether
046   state diversity can provably benefit maximum entropy RL and how to bridge the gap under theoreti-
047   cal guarantees, which naturally leads to the following question:
048
              How can we maximize the next-state entropy of the RL agent in a principled way?
049
050   To answer this question, we start with the analysis of next-state entropy. Intuitively, if the policy
051   is non-redundant, we can optimize the next-state entropy by optimizing policy entropy. However,
052   the condition of non-redundant policy is hard to meet in the stochastic transition case. To solve
053   this issue, we first introduce the inner policy and reversible action mapping layer. Then, we rigor-
      ously derive the gap between the next-state entropy and the policy entropy of the inner policy. We


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      demonstrate that by optimizing both the action mapping layer and inverse dynamics model, we can
055   maximize the next-state entropy by optimizing the inner policy entropy. In practice, our method
056   also achieves superior performance. We conduct extensive experiments on various environments,
057   including MuJoCo (Todorov et al., 2012) and Meta-World (Yu et al., 2020). Results show that our
058   algorithm performs better than baselines.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
061
062
      Maximum Entropy Reinforcement Learning has been widely adopted for improving policy ex-
063
      ploration and robustness in RL. Early work introduced Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) (Haarnoja et al.,
064   2018c;d), an off-policy actor-critic algorithm that formalizes MaxEnt RL by balancing the goals of
065   maximizing expected return and policy entropy. Deep Soft Policy Gradient (DSPG) by (Shi et al.,
066   2019) integrates soft policy gradients with the soft Bellman equation to address stability issues in
067   off-policy learning. Count-Based Soft Q-Learning (CBSQL) by (Hu et al., 2021) adapts the tem-
068   perature dynamically to improve the trade-off between reward and entropy. Additionally, Han &
069   Sung (2021) propose a max-min entropy framework to improve exploration in model-free learning
070   by promoting low-entropy state visitation.
071   Action Representation. Extensive efforts have been made to effectively represent actions within
072   large action spaces. Zahavy et al. (2018b) propose a method that directly identifies redundant or irrel-
073   evant actions using external elimination signals provided by the environment, removing them from
074   the sampling process in text-based games. Tennenholtz & Mannor (2019) adopt a negative sampling
075   procedure, leveraging expert demonstrations to better understand the action space. However, valu-
076   able prior information is often scarce and expensive, limiting the scalability of these approaches.
077   Chandak et al. (2019) demonstrates how to learn and utilize action representations without rely-
078
      ing on prior knowledge by embedding them within the policy structure to train agents effectively.
      Similarly, Metz et al. (2017) introduces a novel approach to discretize high-dimensional continuous
079
      action spaces by sequentially combining one-dimensional discrete actions.
080
081   Exploration is a cornerstone of reinforcement learning, with various strategies enhancing agents’
082   ability to learn from complex environments. For example, the Go-Explore strategy (Ecoffet et al.,
083   2019) advocates for a phased approach to overcoming challenging exploration dilemmas. Count-
084
      based methods (Bellemare et al., 2016) capitalize on environmental novelty by employing pseudo-
      counts. Disagreement-based exploration (Pathak et al., 2019) harnesses the variance in model pre-
085
      dictions to propel the agent toward exploration. Curiosity-driven exploration mechanisms, such as
086
      ICM (Pathak et al., 2017), utilize prediction errors to incentivize exploration. RND (Burda et al.,
087   2018) employs a novel neural network to generate intrinsic rewards based on the prediction error of
088   environmental dynamics, driving the agent towards unexplored territories. NGU (Badia et al., 2020)
089   integrates intrinsic motivation with an episodic memory mechanism to encourage the revisitation of
090   novel states, promoting long-term exploration.
091
      State Entropy Maximization aims to learn a reward-free policy in which state visitations are uni-
092
      formly distributed across the state space, thus promoting robust policy initialization and efficient
093   adaptation. Additionally, when task rewards are available, incorporating state entropy as an intrinsic
094   reward has proven to be an effective approach for enhancing exploration. Lee et al. (2019) propose
095   optimizing the state marginal distribution to align with a target distribution, effectively enhancing
096   exploration. Building on this idea, Islam et al. (2019) introduce entropy regularization based on
097   the marginal state distribution, achieving superior state space coverage in complex domains. Fur-
098   ther advancements include the work of Guo et al. (2021), who incorporate geometry-aware Shannon
099   entropy of state visitations in both discrete and continuous domains, framing exploration as a compu-
100   tationally tractable problem. Additionally, Hazan et al. (2019) provide a provably efficient algorithm
101
      for state entropy maximization, leveraging a black-box planning oracle. Expanding on these meth-
      ods, Liu & Abbeel (2021) maximize a particle-based entropy in an abstract representation space,
102
      demonstrating human-level performance in navigating complex environments.

      4     A NALYSIS OF N EXT-S TATE E NTROPY
124
125
      How do we maximize the state entropy of the agent? Current approaches have been to encourage
126
      exploration by adding bonus rewards related to the new state (Burda et al., 2018; Badia et al., 2020;
127   Zhang et al., 2021). Adding an exploration bonus has achieved considerable success, while theoret-
128   ical analysis of the state entropy has not been explored well. To bridge this gap, in this section, we
129   theoretically analyze the optimization objective of the next-state entropy. Firstly, we define the next-
130   state entropy as H(St+1 | St = s, π), which represents the entropy of the next state after executing
131   policy π in state s.
132   Definition 4.1 (Next-State Entropy). We define the next-state entropy under policy π following state
133   s by
134                     H(St+1 | St = s, π) = −Ea∼π(·|s) Es′ ∼P (·|s,a) log [P π (s′ | s)]             (2)
135
136   where P π (s′ | s) = a∈A π(a | s)P (s′ | s, a). The next-state entropy, as defined above, measures
                            R
137   the diversity of the subsequent states under the policy π. In classical entropy-regularized reinforce-
138   ment learning, the policy entropy is often used to encourage diversity in the actions taken. However,
139   these two concepts are not generally equivalent.
140
      In this section, we will illustrate the discrepancy between policy entropy and next-state entropy
141
      during policy updates in an illustrative example (Section 4.1) and then reveal their relationship under
142   both deterministic (Section 4.2) and stochastic transitions (Section 4.3) with theoretical analysis.
143

190   4.2   D ETERMINISTIC CASE
191
192   Section 4.1 demonstrates the superiority of next-state entropy over policy entropy in terms of ex-
193   ploration. To better understand the relationship between next-state entropy and policy entropy, we
194   start with an MDP with deterministic transitions. As discussed by Baram et al. (2021), the following
195
      corollary provides insights into the equivalent relationship between next-state entropy and policy
      entropy under deterministic case:
196
197   Corollary 4.2. Let M be a deterministic MDP with a transition function T : S × A → S. If
198   T (s, a) ̸= T (s, a′ ) , ∀s ∈ S, ∀a, a′ ∈ A, then
199                                H(St+1 | St = s, π) = H(π(· | st )) ,         ∀s ∈ S
200
201   This corollary indicates that, in the deterministic case, if there are no redundant actions in the action
202   space A, next-state entropy and policy entropy are equivalent. Therefore, recent studies (Zahavy
203   et al., 2018a; Tennenholtz & Mannor, 2019; Zhong et al., 2024) have focused on eliminating re-
204   dundant actions in the action space A and significantly enhance the exploration and performance in
205   various domains.
206
207   4.3   S TOCHASTIC C ASE
208
209   Similarly, in the stochastic case, we can define a non-redundant policy and reveal the relationship
210
      between next-state entropy and policy entropy. Firstly, we define the non-redundant policy as fol-
      lows:
211
212   Definition 4.3 (Non-Redundant Policy). Given a stochastic MDP with a transition dynamics P , π
213   is a non-redundant policy if
214           ∀s, s′ ∈ S,      ∀ai , aj ∈ {a ∈ A | π(a | s) > 0},      P (s′ | s, ai ) ∗ P (s′ | s, aj ) = 0.
215

      In the stochastic case, we define π as a non-redundant policy only if there is an absolutely non-
217   intersection between probability distribution P (s′ | s, a) of possible actions sampling from π. In
218   other words, for any reachable next state s′ , only one action can lead to it under a non-redundant
219   policy. Under the above strong assumption on policy π, we can reveal the relationship between
220   next-state entropy and policy entropy in the stochastic case:
221   Theorem 4.4. If π is a non-redundant policy, then

      5     M ETHOD
233
234   Based on the analysis in Section 4, we can directly optimize state entropy by maximizing the entropy
235   of the non-redundant policy. However, constructing a non-redundant policy in the stochastic transi-
236   tion setting is extremely challenging since we must strictly satisfy the Definition 4.3. To solve this
237   issue, we decompose the overall policy into an inner policy πi and a parameterized, reversible ac-
238   tion mapping layerf . Based on this framework, we rigorously derive the gap between the next-state
239   entropy of the overall policy and the policy entropy of the inner policy. Meanwhile, we demonstrate
240   that by optimizing both the action mapping layer and an inverse dynamics model to minimize the
241   gap term, the inner policy’s entropy will be equivalent to the next-state entropy of the overall policy.

      Answer of Question 2: To show that MNSE can maximize next-state entropy, we conduct ex-

      outperforms baseline approaches across various experimental environments. NovelD and MinRed

      employ bonus-based strategies to promote the exploration of diverse states. Specifically, NovelD

515   7   DISCUSSION
516
517   Why Maximize Next-State Entropy in Reinforcement Learning? Entropy regularization is a fun-
518
      damental technique in reinforcement learning. By integrating an entropy maximization term, it
      enhances robustness to model and estimation errors (Ziebart et al., 2010), promotes the acquisi-
519
      tion of diverse behaviors (Haarnoja et al., 2017), facilitates broader exploration (Fox et al., 2015;
520
      Haarnoja et al., 2018c;d) and accelerates the learning process by smoothing the optimization land-
521   scape (Ahmed et al., 2019). However, maximizing policy entropy may not directly promote policy
522   optimization due to redundancy in the action space. In such cases, next-state entropy extends the
523   concept of policy entropy more directly. Specifically, next-state entropy measures the entropy of
524   the next state resulting from the policy, rather than the action itself. This shift allows next-state en-
525   tropy to capture the diversity of effects induced by actions. By bridging the gap between next-state
526   and policy entropy, our method retains the benefits of policy entropy while addressing inefficiencies
527   caused by action redundancy.
528
529   8   CONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we demonstrate a critical problem: the maximum next-state entropy of the RL agent.
532   We first systematically elucidate the distinctions and interrelationships between next-state entropy
533   and policy entropy. Then, to bridge the gap between these two concepts, we integrate inverse dynam-
534   ics with an action mapping layer. We demonstrate that by optimizing both the action mapping layer
535   and inverse dynamics model, we can maximize the next-state entropy by optimizing the inner policy
536   entropy. We conduct extensive experiments and demonstrate that our method outperforms baseline
537   methods across various domains. Future research will focus on extending MNSE to accommodate
538
      more complex action space structures and exploring its potential applications in robotics.
