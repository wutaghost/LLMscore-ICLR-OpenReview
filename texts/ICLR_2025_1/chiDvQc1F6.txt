009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have greatly enhanced
012            their ability to generate natural and contextually relevant text, making AI in-
013            teractions more human-like. However, generating and understanding interactive
014            human-like motion, where two individuals engage in coordinated movements, re-
015            mains a challenge due to the complexity of modeling these coordinated interac-
016            tions. Furthermore, a versatile model is required to handle diverse interactive
017            scenarios, such as chat systems that follow user instructions or adapt to their as-
018
               signed role while adjusting interaction dynamics. To tackle this problem, we in-
               troduce VIM, short for the Versatile Interactive Motion language model, which
019
               integrates both language and motion modalities to effectively understand, gener-
020
               ate, and control interactive motions in multi-turn conversational contexts. To ad-
021            dress the scarcity of multi-turn interactive motion data, we introduce a synthetic
022            dataset called I NTER -MT2 ; where we utilize pre-trained models to create diverse
023            instructional datasets with interactive motion. Our approach first trains a motion
024            tokenizer that encodes interactive motions into residual discrete tokens. In the pre-
025            training stage, the model learns to align motion and text representations with these
026            discrete tokens. During the instruction fine-tuning stage, VIM adapts to multi-turn
027            conversations using I NTER -MT2 . We evaluate the versatility of our method across
028            motion-related tasks—motion-to-text, text-to-motion, reaction generation, motion
029
               editing, and reasoning about motion sequences. The results highlight VIM’s ver-
               satility and effectiveness in handling complex interactive motion synthesis.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Agents that reflect how humans communicate and interact with each other through motion have the
035
      potential to revolutionize our interaction with technology. By capturing the subtleties of human
036   communication, including gestures, expressions, and interactive behaviors, these agents can offer
037   more intuitive and natural interfaces. This holistic understanding enables technology to adjust its
038   responses and behaviors based on the user’s physical motions and situational context, leading to
039   more personalized and engaging interactions. Such capabilities are crucial for enhancing support
040   across various domains, including robotics, virtual humans, entertainment, and more.
041
      Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) (Dubey et al., 2024; Team et al., 2024; Yang
042   et al., 2024) have demonstrated significant potential in generating human-like text and understanding
043   complex linguistic interactions. They have even extended their capability to multi-modal contexts,
044   successfully integrating various input sources such as images, speech, and videos (Ge et al., 2024;
045   Liu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Tang et al., 2024; Shu et al., 2023). Building upon these develop-
046   ments, there is a growing interest in incorporating human (or robot) motion as a new modality (Jiang
047   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a), leading to the emergence of the “motion-language models” (MLM).
048   However, existing approaches (Zhang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024a; 2022; Zhang et al., 2024d; Cai
049   et al., 2024) often focus on single tasks, such as text-to-motion or motion-to-text translation, and
050
      consider only single motions without interactions. This limitation hinders the agents’ ability to
      handle scenarios involving multi-agents, complex interactions, and multi-turn conversations.
051
052   Beyond modeling the motion of a single person, interactive motion, where two individuals partic-
053   ipate in interactions, allows the model to learn about social behavior. Modeling such interactions
      requires versatility to effectively control interactions, allowing users to provide instructions, assign

078   One of the primary challenges in developing these interactive agents is the lack of multi-turn in-
079   teractive motion data. Datasets containing motions of two individuals interacting with each other,
080
      along with multi-turn conversational instructions, are scarce and challenging to collect. This makes

081
082   To address this, we present a new synthesized dataset called I NTER -MT2 , which includes various
083   instructions about the interactive motions, in a multi-turn conversational format. We utilize a large
084   language model to produce diverse instructions with motion captions and a diffusion-based text-to-
085   motion model to generate corresponding motions. We expect that leveraging such foundation models
086
      to construct training data allows the model to generalize more effectively with prior knowledge.
087   Building upon our synthesized dataset, we present VIM, a Versatile Interactive Motion-language
088   model designed for multi-turn conversations involving interactive motions. We pursue the versatility
089   of VIM through a unified architecture that can simultaneously input and output both motion and text
090   modalities. Based on the pre-trained LLMs, our training process can be divided into three stages:
091   (1) training of the interaction motion tokenizer, (2) pre-training for motion and text representation
092
      alignment, and (3) instruction tuning with our synthesized dataset, I NTER -MT2 , to handle more
      complex and multi-turn instructions. This enables VIM to effectively comprehend, generate, and
093

095   clude editing motions and reasoning about motion sequences based on contextual cues, highlighting
096   its versatility and effectiveness in complex motion interaction scenarios.

098
      In summary, the main contributions of this paper are threefold: (1) We propose VIM that can simul-
      taneously process and generate both motion and text modalities, along with a three-stage training
099
      pipeline consisting of motion tokenizer training, pre-training for modality alignment, and instruction
100
      tuning. (2) We present I NTER -MT2 , a multi-turn interactive motion-text dataset, to address the lack
101   of multi-turn interactive motion data. (3) We introduced a new evaluation protocol to evaluate the
102   performance of motion-language models on complex motion interaction scenarios.

132

133

      Human-Human Interactive Motion Modeling Modeling human-human interactions has gar-
136
      nered increasing attention in recent research. Several multi-person interaction datasets (Ng et al.,
137
      2020; Fieraru et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2023) have been developed, and recent efforts like Inter-X (Xu
138   et al., 2024a) and InterHuman (Liang et al., 2024) have collected interactive motions paired with tex-
139   tual descriptions for text-based motion control. In text-to-motion tasks, InterGEN (Xu et al., 2024a)
140   introduces a diffusion-based model with spatial constraint loss. PriorMDM (Shafir et al., 2024)
141   leverages pre-trained motion diffusion models with slim communication blocks. For reaction gener-
142   ation, ReMoS Ghosh et al. (2023) synthesizes reactive motion using spatio-temporal cross-attention,
143   while ReGenNet Xu et al. (2024b) employs a transformer-based model with distance-based inter-
144   action loss to predict human reactions. While existing models have advanced interactive motion
145   modeling, they lack versatility and focus on specific tasks, failing to capture complex multi-turn
146
      dynamics. To address this, we introduce I NTER -MT2 , enabling agents to generate sophisticated
      motions, respond to instructions, adapt roles, and adjust behaviors based on context.

      4.3     T RAINING
262
263   Motion Tokenizer The first stage is to train a motion tokenizer composed of an encoder, decoder,
264   and quantizer. We followed the original objective functions from Lee et al. (2022) to train this
265   model, minimizing the reconstruction loss, the codebook loss to align the encoder’s outputs with the
266   codebook, and the commitment loss to ensure encoder consistency. Once the encoder and decoder
267   are optimized, we maintain this model freezed during the rest of the training stage.
268
269   Pre-training Strategy In the pre-training stage, we train a pre-trained large language model to
      align motion representations with textual representations. We design tasks including motion-to-

273   motion caption. Since both motion tokens and text tokens are discrete, we train our model with the

      4.4   A DVANCED D OWNSTREAM I NTERACTIVE M OTION TASKS
291
292   After training, our model can perform complex reasoning and generate interactive motions within
293   multi-turn conversations. To verify this, we introduce two additional tasks requiring advanced capa-
294   bilities: motion reasoning and editing. Motion reasoning involves predicting past or future events, or
295   reasoning about current motions, based on prior conversational data. This task requires the model to
296   understand the context of the conversation, interpret how the given motion fits within that context,
297   and adjust its reasoning accordingly. In the motion editing task, we focus on altering a person’s
298
      persona or shifting scenarios, such as emotions or relationship dynamics. This adds complexity, as
      changes to one person’s behavior affect the other’s motion. The model must edit the target motion
299
      while maintaining contextual coherence, requiring a deep understanding of social dynamics.

313

314

315   sponses. Content alignment evaluates how accurately the text reflects the given motion data, logical
316   coherence checks the consistency and reasoning accuracy of inferences made about past or future

318   scale. In addition, we utilized linguistic metrics like Rouge-L (Lin (2004)), METEOR (Banerjee &
319   Lavie (2005)), and MAUVE (Pillutla et al. (2021)), to quantitatively assess the relevance, accuracy,
320   and naturalness of the generated responses compared with labeled texts in I NTER -MT2 test dataset.

360   Baselines Since our interactive multi-turn scenarios and tasks are novel, there is no exact compar-
361   ison method. We consider and compare reasonable baselines that handle both motion and texts as
362   input and output. We first employ a two-stage approach using off-the-shelf methods. For the motion
363   reasoning task, we convert all motions into text descriptions using the state-of-the-art motion-to-text
364   method TM2T (Guo et al., 2022). These textual descriptions are then used for text-based reason-
365
      ing with large language models such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) and LLaMA-3.1-8B (Dubey et al.,

518   6     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSIONS
519
520   Conclusion In this paper, we introduced VIM, a versatile motion-language model designed to
521   model, understand, and reason about interactive motions. We outlined its architecture and provided
522   detailed training strategies to create a unified framework integrating large language models with
523   interactive motion modality. To enhance the model’s reasoning capabilities and versatility, we pre-
524   sented a specialized dataset, I NTER -MT2 , which incorporates a variety of reasoning tasks set within
525   multi-turn conversations centered on interactive motions. Our experiments demonstrated VIM’s
526
      ability to effectively follow instructions, edit motions, and reason about interactive motions.
527
528
      Limitations and Impact Statement There are several limitations that warrant attention. First,
      the model’s expressiveness remains limited when handling complex or previously unseen actions,
529
      indicating a need for further diverse motion source data in its ability to generalize across diverse
530
      motion scenarios. Second, the sequence length becomes excessively long as we flatten the resid-
531   ual motion tokens, which can impact efficiency and computational resources. Leveraging addi-
532   tional transformer models to predict the residual token can reduce this work. Lastly, our method
533   faces challenges in personalization and interpretability, as motion is inherently ambiguous and users
534   may interpret the same motion in different ways. Addressing this issue will require incorporating
535   more tailored approaches that adapt to individual user preferences and expectations through further
536   human-in-the-loop feedback and refinement processes. In terms of broader impact, VIM opens up
537   new possibilities for interactive motion modeling and understanding in AI, potentially benefiting
538   fields like robotics, virtual environments, and human-computer interaction. However, careful con-
539
      sideration of ethical concerns, such as misinterpretation of motions or unintended behavioral biases,
      is crucial as the model evolves.
