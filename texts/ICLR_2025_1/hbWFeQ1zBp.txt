009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we propose SWIFT, a lightweight model that is not only powerful,
012            but also efficient in deployment and inference for Long-term Time Series Fore-
013            casting (LTSF). Our model is based on two key points: 1. decomposition of se-
014            quences using wavelet transform. 2. using only one shared single layer for sub-
015            series’ mapping. We conduct comprehensive experiments, and the results show
016            that SWIFT achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on multiple datasets,
017            offering a promising method for edge computing and deployment in this task.
018
               Moreover, it is noteworthy that the number of parameters in SWIFT is only 25%
               of what it would be with a single-layer linear model for time-domain prediction.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) finds broad applications across various domains, includ-
024   ing energy management, financial market analysis, weather prediction, traffic flow monitoring, and
025   healthcare monitoring. Accurate prediction is crucial for them. At the same time, many applica-
026   tions require real-time prediction on edge devices, e.g., in latency-sensitive tasks such as energy
027   scheduling or intelligent transportation systems, where models need to be responsive to real-world
028   demands, and where edge computing and fast inference are critical. Additional challenges are posed
029   under conditions of limited computational resources (Deng et al., 2024).
030   Achieving precise forecasts typically relies on powerful yet complex deep learning models, such as
031   RNNs (Grossberg, 2013), LSTMs (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), TCNs (Hewage et al., 2020;
032   Wu et al., 2022), and Transformers (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022). Thanks
033   to the self-attention mechanism, Transformers can capture long-range dependencies in sequences,
034   which improves prediction accuracy and makes it the most powerful of the existing LTSF forecast-
035   ers. However, these models also encounter several challenges stemming from their computational
036   complexity and the large scale of their model weights, which restrict their practical applicability,
037   particularly in environments with limited computational resources.
038   Since recently a solid paper (Zeng et al., 2023) shows that even a simple one-layer linear model
039   can outperforms Transformer-based models in almost all cases, more and more efficient linear fore-
040   casters are proposed (Das et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). While improving predic-
041   tion accuracy, these linear forecasters are constantly becoming more efficient, with faster inference
042   speed and less deployment costs, pushing the boundary of this field forward. Recently, FITS (Xu
043   et al., 2024) modeling time-series with a complex-valued neural network, surpassed several exist-
044
      ing Transformer models in both inference speed and forecasting performance with 10k parameters,
      establishing itself as a benchmark in the field.
045
046   However, one disadvantage of interpolation models like FITS is that the output will contain the
047   length of the input, which creates parameter redundancy for cases where the input window is long.
048   Besides, most current lightweight models are based on Fast Fourier Transforms (FFT) which can
049   be used to extract periodic features and trends in time-series. But FFT is not suitable for handling
050
      non-smooth data, and the data generated in real-world applications are generally non-stationary.
051   Motivated by the above observations, we present SWIFT, a lightweight model based on first order
052   wavelet transform Gupta et al. (2021) and only one linear layer. For the first time, we deal with the
053   time-series only in the time-frequency domain, replacing the FFT with Discrete Wavelet Transform
      (DWT). SWIFT achieves good performance on both smooth and non-smooth data, and it is approx-


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      imately 100K times lighter than some mainstream models. Even when compared to a lightweight
055   model like FITS, our model still has only 15% of its number of parameters.
056
057   In summary, our contributions can be delineated as follows:
058
             • We propose SWIFT, a powerful lightweight model for time-series forecasting tasks, which
059            is four times smaller than the single-layer linear model for time-domain prediction.
060
             • We present the first method for processing time-series only in the time-frequency domain,
061
               applicable to both smooth and non-smooth series.
062
063
             • We show that wavelet coefficients in different frequency bands can be mapped through the
               same representation space after aggregating features in a convolution layer.
064
065          • We conduct extensive experiments on predicting long multivariate sequences on several
066            real-world benchmarks showing the superiority of our method. In addition, we have done
067
               experiments on anomaly detection tasks, demonstrating the strong generalization of our
               method.

072   2.1   E FFICIENT LINEAR FORECASTERS
073
      Since (Zeng et al., 2023) shows that a simple one-layer linear model can outperforms Transformer
074
      forecasters (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022) in almost all cases, there has been
075
      a rapid emergence of linear forecasters (Oreshkin et al., 2020; Das et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) in
076   LTSF. The impressive performance and efficiency continuously challenge this direction.
077
078   Recently, FITS (Xu et al., 2024) introduced a frequency-domain interpolation strategy that utilizes
079
      low-pass filters and FFT (Brigham & Morrow, 1967) for time series modeling. By maintaining a
      parameter scale at 10k level, FITS surpassed several existing Transformer models in both inference
080
      speed and forecasting performance, establishing itself as a benchmark in the field.
081
082   However, FFT assumes that signals are stationary, limiting its ability to capture the temporal lo-
083   calization of transient or non-stationary signals (Liu et al., 2022). Furthermore, its approach of
084   applying a global frequency domain transformation to the entire signal often results in suboptimal
085
      performance when dealing with strong boundary effects. Meanwhile, the limited representational
      capacity of a single-layer linear model typically necessitates a longer look-back window to prevent
086
      underfitting and distribution shifts. The parameter count of FITS is primarily determined by the
087
      length of the look-back window due to its interpolation-based prediction approach. Consequently,
088   The efficiency of FITS decreases significantly as the lookback window increases.
089
090   Our proposed model, SWIFT, aims to enhance the field of efficient time series forecasting through
091
      the introduction of DWT. This approach not only improves SWIFT’s capacity to handle non-
      stationary signals but also significantly reduces model’s parameter count, thereby enhancing effi-
092
      ciency while preserving predictive performance.
093
094
      2.2   DWT METHOD
095
096   As a powerful method for time-frequency analysis, DWT is widely used in tasks dealing with time
097   series. (Yang et al., 2022) decomposed the time-series using wavelet decomposition and then utilized
098   CNN and LSTM for prediction. FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022) combines Wavelet Transform with
099   frequency enhanced strategy and attention mechanism to capture long range dependencies. Sasal
100   et al. (2022) utilize a maximal overlap discrete wavelet transformation and build a local transformer
101   model for time-series forecasting. Besides, DWT is often used for anomaly detection. For instance,
102   (Bhattacharya et al., 2022) used wavelet transform for signal denoising and damage localization. Re-
103   cently, (Arabi et al., 2024) proposed a method for data augmentation in time-series prediction tasks,
104
      which is used to obtain more diverse sequences by eliminating or swapping wavelet coefficients.
105   However, none of these approaches provided significant insight into the wavelet transform for LTSF.
106   In our work, we explored the wavelet coefficients in depth and found that the high-frequency coeffi-
107   cients and low-frequency coefficients of the historical wavelet can be mapped to the coefficients of
      the future wavelet in the same representation space.

      3.1   LTSF PROBLEM DEFINITION
111
112   In multivariate LTSF, time series data contain multiple variables or channels at each time step. Given
113   historical values X = {x1 , . . . , xLx | xi ∈ Rd } where d represents the number of variables and Lx
114   represents the length of the lookback window, the goal of LTSF is to predict future values Y =
115   {y1 , . . . , yLy | yi ∈ Rd }. The output length Ly is usually much longer than the length of the
116   lookback window Lx , and the feature dimension is not limited to univariate case (d ≥ 1).
117

146
147   We propose the SWIFT which is shown in Figure 1. Firstly, the time-series is decomposed by 1st or-
148   der DWT. Then the high-frequency component and the low-frequency component are concatenated

212   Convolution layer In our experiments, it has been found that there is some commonality in the
213   different band coefficients, with the potential to map through the same representation space. In
214   addition, the timing characteristics of the coefficient vector need to further aggregated. Therefore,
215   we add a 1D convolutional layer with input channel 2 and output channel 2. By presetting the kernel

      In SWIFT, the convolution layer has three main functions: (i) Convolution layer can denoise the
217   signal and enables feature enhancement. (ii) Convolution layer can aggregate local information and
218   capture long and short term dependencies of time-series. (iii) Convolutional layers enable cross-
219   band information fusion, facilitating coefficients from different bands to share a linear layer.

      After the aggregation of information, we are able to get new components:

      Sub-series mapping strategy As mentioned in the previous sections, we use DWT to handle the
225   time series and divide it into two sub-series (YL and YH ) by 1st order decomposition. The obtained
226   components are concatenated as the time-frequency representation of whole sequence Y.

      Single-layer linear models, despite their widespread use, are constrained by inherent limitations
      in their representational capacity. These limitations often manifest as underfitting or overfitting
229
      phenomena, particularly when applied to complex, non-stationary time series data. Such models are
230
      susceptible to being disproportionately influenced by specific patterns within the data, potentially
231   leading to degraded predictive performance.
232
233   To address these challenges and enhance the robustness of single-layer linear models, while simul-
234
      taneously reducing the model’s parameter count and improving inference speed, we propose a novel
      mapping strategy. Our approach employs a shared weight matrix for mapping both low-frequency
235
      and high-frequency components of the input series:
236
237
238                                            Y ′ = YC W + b                                           (7)
239                         ′                                        ′
240   where W ∈ RT /2×T /2 is the weight matrix, and b ∈ RT /2 is the bias vector. The resulting
                       ′
241   Y′ ∈ RN ×2×T /2 , where T ′ is the prediction length. After mapping, we reshape Y′ back into
242   approximation and detail coefficients, and apply the Inverse Discrete Wavelet Transform (IDWT) to
243   obtain the final prediction:
244
                                                ′    ′        ′    ′
245                                            YL = Y:,0,: , YH = Y:,1,:
246                                             ′    ′                    ′                             (8)
                                     Ŷ = IDWT(YL , YH ), Ŷ ∈ RN ×T

      The shared mapping strategy has several advantages. (1) It enhances the robustness of the model by
249   jointly handling the low and high frequency components, reduces the sensitivity to the presence of
250   a single specific pattern in the time series, and mitigates the occurrence of overfitting. The shared
251   weights encourage the model to learn generalized features applicable to both frequency ranges, thus
252   enhancing the robustness of the model. (2) It improves parameter efficiency by using a single weight
253   matrix for both components, which significantly reduces the total number of parameters, improves
254   the computational efficiency of the model and speeds up inference time compared to mapping the
255   low-frequency and high-frequency components separately. Together, these advantages enhance the
256   prediction performance in time series forecasting involving complex, non-stationary data.

      Datasets We use 4 benchmark datasets used by (Xu et al., 2022): SMD (Server Machine Dataset),

      PSM (Polled Server Metrics), MSL (Mars Science Laboratory rover) and SMAP (Soil Moisture
367   Active Passive satellite).
368
369   Baseline We compare SWIFT with models such as TimesNet (Wu et al., 2022), Anomaly Trans-
370   former (Xu et al., 2022), THOC (Shen et al., 2020), Omnianomaly (Su et al., 2019), DGHL (Challu
371   et al., 2022). Following TimesNet, we also compare the anomaly detection performance with other
372   models (Zeng et al., 2023).
373
374   Main results Table 6 demonstrates the performance of SWIFT on various datasets.SWIFT
375   achieves near-perfect F1 scores of 99.92% and 96.487% on the SMD and PSM datasets, respec-
376   tively, and achieves about 2.5% outperformance on PSM compared to FITS, demonstrating its high-
377   precision performance in anomaly detection. By introducing the time-frequency domain, SWIFT
      can easily identify the anomalies that are difficult to recognize in the time domain, including the

378
      anomalies that introduce unexpected time-frequency components. In contrast, models such as
379   TimesNet, Anomaly Transformer and Stationary Transformer do not perform as well as SWIFT
380   on these datasets.
381
382
      However, SWIFT performs relatively low on the SMAP and MSL datasets. Due to the binary event
      data nature of these datasets, time-frequency feature representations may have difficulty capturing
383
      these features effectively. In this case, time-domain modeling is more desirable because the raw
384
      data format is already compact enough. As a result, models designed for anomaly detection, such as
385   THOC and Omni Anomaly, achieve higher F1 scores on these datasets.
386
387   It is worth noting that SWIFT has an extremely low number of parameters ranging from 0.625k-
388
      2.5k for the anomaly detection task. Such a feature allows SWIFT to be deployed on almost any
      edge device. In addition, SWIFT’s inference speed is impressive, reaching sub-millisecond levels,
389
      much faster than the latency associated with larger models or communication overheads. This speed
390
      underscores SWIFT’s suitability as a first-response tool for rapid detection of critical errors.

401   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE WORK
402
403   In this paper, we propose SWIFT for time series analysis, an efficient linear-based model with
404   performance comparable to state-of-the-art models that are typically several orders of magnitude
405
      larger. In future work, we plan to evaluate SWIFT in more real-world scenarios, such as in anomaly
      detection and classification tasks, and to improve its interpretability. In addition, we plan to explore
406
      large neural networks in the time-frequency domain to perform scaling up operations on SWIFT and
407
      improve its prediction performance, such as large transformer models based on DWT.
