011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have sparked interest in their abil-
014            ity to solve Olympiad-level math problems. However, the training and evalua-
015            tion of these models are constrained by the limited size and quality of available
016
               datasets, as creating large-scale data for such advanced problems requires exten-
               sive effort from human experts. In addition, current benchmarks are prone to
017
               contamination, leading to unreliable evaluations. In this paper, we present an
018
               automated pipeline that leverages the rich resources of the Art of Problem Solv-
019            ing (AoPS) forum, which predominantly features Olympiad-level problems and
020            community-driven solutions. Using open-source LLMs, we develop a method
021            to extract question-answer pairs from the forum, resulting in AoPS-Instruct, a
022            dataset of more than 650,000 high-quality QA pairs. Our experiments demon-
023            strate that fine-tuning LLMs on AoPS-Instruct improves their reasoning abili-
024            ties across various benchmarks. Moreover, we build an automatic pipeline that
025            introduces LiveAoPSBench, an evolving evaluation set with timestamps, de-
026            rived from the latest forum data, providing a contamination-resistant benchmark
027
               for assessing LLM performance. Notably, we observe a significant decline in
               LLM performance over time, suggesting their success on older examples may
028
               stem from pre-training exposure rather than true reasoning ability. Our work
029
               presents a scalable approach to creating and maintaining large-scale, high-quality
030            datasets for advanced math reasoning, offering valuable insights into the capa-
031            bilities and limitations of LLMs in this domain. Our benchmark is available at
032            https://livemathbench.github.io/leaderboard.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large language models (LLMs) have shown tremendous success in solving various tasks such as
038   code generation (Li et al., 2022), math reasoning (Shao et al., 2024), and commonsense reason-
039   ing (Zellers et al., 2019; Achiam et al., 2023), suggesting that current models may show signs of
040   artificial general intelligence (AGI) (Bubeck et al., 2023). Math reasoning is perhaps one of the
041   most challenging tasks for the LLMs, since mathematics is inherently structured, requiring not just
042   recall of facts but also rigorous logical inference, abstraction, and understanding of formal sym-
043   bolic systems. As such, there have been grand challenges (Selsam et al., 2019) and million-dollar
044
      prizes AIMO (2023) established for a model capable of solving Olympiad-level math problems.
045   On the training side, despite significant progress in certain areas, such as geometry, particularly
046   with the assistance of symbolic methods (Trinh et al., 2024), the performance of LLMs remains
047   limited on Olympiad-level problems (He et al., 2024). One of the key challenges in advancing
048   competition-level math reasoning, compared to other domains like coding or grade-school math, is
049   the scarcity of large-scale data. Creating valid and challenging math questions, along with providing
050
      correct solutions, is costly. This is especially true for Olympiad-level problems, which can be time-
      consuming even for experts. This highlights the need for scalable and automated methods to collect
051
      high-quality data for Olympiad-level problems to facilitate further advancements in this field.
052
053   On the evaluation side, in contrast to the rapid advancements in LLMs, the evaluation of their
      math reasoning capabilities remains relatively underdeveloped. First, as aforementioned, the cost


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      of creating and annotating advanced math problems is high. Second, popular datasets such as
055   MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) have been saturated by both
056   open-source and closed-source models (Achiam et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024b). Third, bench-
057   marks whose test sets are publicly available online (Hendrycks et al., 2021b; Cobbe et al., 2021; He
058   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b) are prone to potential contamination. Although techniques like
059   n-gram matching and locality-sensitive hashing have been applied as a common practice (Achiam
060   et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a) to reduce contamination, they still suffer low ac-
061   curacy and would not be able to rule out rephrased questions, as shown by Yang et al. (2023). Given
062   these limitations, it is crucial to develop an evolving evaluation benchmark that contains abundant
063
      and up-to-date test samples, and designed with appropriate difficulty to fairly assess a model’s math
      reasoning abilities.
064
065   The Art of Problem Solving1 (AoPS) forum is a rich resource for Olympiad-level math problems,
066   featuring discussions on topics such as algebra, geometry, combinatorics, and number theory from
067   competitions like AMC (AOPS, 2023), AIME (AOPS, 2024), and the International Mathematical
068   Olympiad (IMO). However, the forum’s unstructured nature, including irrelevant comments and
069
      incomplete solutions, poses challenges in extracting high-quality, structured question-answer (QA)
      pairs. Developing an effective automated pipeline to curate these QA pairs is essential to address
070
      the scarcity of large-scale, high-quality data for training and evaluating models in Olympiad-level
071
      math reasoning. In this paper, we utilize the posts from the AoPS forum to create a large-scale
072   training and a contamination-resistant evaluation set. Our pipeline is designed to run automatically,
073   enabling us to build and maintain evolving train/evaluation datasets. This automated approach is
074   crucial, as it allows for continuously updating the datasets, ensuring they are less likely to suffer
075   from contamination, even as existing data potentially becomes compromised over time. In summary,
076   our key contributions are as follows:
077
078             • We build a pipeline to extract questions and solutions from raw AoPS forum data, con-
079
                  structing the AoPS-Instruct, a novel large-scale dataset with 666.1K Olympiad-level math
                  QA pairs.
080
081             • Using the most recent QA pairs, We build an automatic pipeline that introduces LiveAoPS-
082               Bench, a contamination-resistant evaluation set for assessing the math reasoning capabili-
083               ties of LLMs.
084             • Our experiments on LiveAoPSBench show a declining performance trend over time for
085               various LLMs, indicating potential data contamination, and stressing the need for up-to-
086               date evaluation data.
087             • Fine-tuning various LLMs on AoPS-Instruct lead to improved performance on standard
088               benchmarks such as OlympiadBench, Omni-Math, and our LiveAoPSBench dataset, veri-
089               fying the effectiveness of our dataset in enhancing math reasoning capabilities of LLMs.

139   Training Datasets for Math. Training datasets for mathematical reasoning can be categorized into
140   two types: pretraining and supervised fine-tuning (SFT) datasets. First, pretraining datasets consist
141   of large-scale math data, e.g., billions of tokens used during the pretraining phase of LLMs. No-
142   table examples include Open-Web-Math (Paster et al., 2024) and Minerva (Lewkowycz et al., 2022),
143   which contain 38.5B and 14.7B tokens of math data, respectively. Second, SFT datasets focus on
144   high-quality question-answer pairs. Examples include Open-Math-Instruct (Toshniwal et al., 2024),
145   Orca-math (Mitra et al., 2024), and the training sets of widely used benchmarks such as GSM8K
146   (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b). However, these datasets are generally
147
      limited to grade-school level mathematics and do not target more advanced topics like Olympiad-
      level math. One of the most closely related datasets to ours is Numina (Li et al., 2024), which com-
148
      bines popular SFT datasets like Orca-math, MATH, and GSM8K, along with approximately 190K
149
      new Olympiad-level QA pairs. Concurrently, Yue et al. (2024) introduced a large-scale instruc-
150   tion fine-tuning dataset for math and science, which has also shown improvements in mathematical
151   reasoning. Table 1 presents a detailed comparison of our dataset with these related datasets.

      Contamination-Resistant Evaluation. Benchmarks that are publicly accessible are prone to be
      contaminated due to the potential inadvertent data overlap during training. The typical decontam-
154
      ination method involves using exact substring (e.g., n-gram) matching to detect overlaps with the
155
      target evaluation sets (Zhuo et al., 2024). However, this approach fails to catch rephrased examples
156   and can not eliminate all overlaps with the test set.(Yang et al., 2023). While alternative LLM-based
157   methods for decontamination have been proposed, they often lack guarantees and may result in high
158   false-positive rates (Yang et al., 2023). A reliable way to mitigate contamination is to select data that
159   appeared after LLMs were trained, known as the knowledge cut-off. In the code generation domain,
160   LiveCodeBench (Jain et al., 2024) addresses this issue by categorizing data based on timestamps,
161   setting a cutoff date, and designating data beyond this point as unseen. We adopt a similar strategy
      in the math domain, partitioning the dataset by timestamps and enabling users to select data based

197   3.1    M ATH I NSTRUCTION F INE - TUNING DATASET: AO PS-I NSTRUCT
198
199   We now describe the five steps of our automated pipeline for constructing the instruction fine-tuning
200
      dataset AoPS-Instruct.
201   Step 0: Raw Forum Discussion Collection. We begin by collecting raw discussions from the forum
202   website, where each discussion is called a “topic”. In these topics, the author presents math problems
203   (e.g., competition-level problems) or general questions, such as seeking advice or resources. Our raw
204   dataset consists of 1, 076, 712 topics. Topics posted up until December 2023 are used as the training
205   set, while those posted between January and June 2024 are reserved as the evaluation dataset.
206   Step 1: Math Question Detection. We then filter out irrelevant topics, specifically those not con-
207   taining a mathematical question. To achieve this, we use Llama-3.1 8B (Dubey et al., 2024) to
208   decide the relevance of each topic. The first post of each topic determines whether the topic is a
209   mathematical question or not, so we manually design a few-shot prompt, provide the first post of the
210   topic to the model, and prompt the model to output if the topic is a math question or not. This step
211   reduces the dataset to 478, 337 topics with math questions after pruning 598, 375 irrelevant ones.
212   Step 2: Question-Answer Extraction. After filtering, we extract the math question from the first
213   post of each topic and identify potential solutions provided in subsequent posts. Since this task
214   requires understanding the entire conversation and determining which responses contain valid so-
215   lutions, we employ the 70B variant of Llama 3.1 for this step, enabling the detection of both the
      question and all relevant answers from the discussion.

247   Step 3: Solution rewriting. Math solutions generated by users on the AOPS forum are often con-
248   cise, omitting details assumed to√be common knowledge among the target audience. For instance, a
249
      user might write (x + yz)/2 ≥ xyz without explicitly mentioning the application of the AM-GM

250
      these succinct solutions often struggle to maintain their chain-of-thought reasoning capabilities.

252   Our experiments show that fine-tuning a model on these concise solutions significantly degrades

255

256

261
      approach used in DeepSeekMath (Shao et al., 2024), we employ a 10-gram exact match decontami-

305   Filtering. The correctness of the solution is typically verified by comparing the final answer to the

316

      ing step twice for each question. Consequently, for each question Q, we obtain a triplet:
318   (Aqwen , Allama , Aoriginal ). If a boxed answer is detected in Aoriginal , it is automatically accepted as
319   a candidate answer for the question. Following this, we performed a cross-check between Aqwen and
320   Allama , removing all cases with inconsistent answers. This was done through string matching for text

415   4.4              A BLATION S TUDIES
416
417   Evaluation Quality Assessment. We assess the quality of our evaluation set in two ways: by mea-
418   suring its correlation with a well-established dataset and through manual evaluation over a subset
419   of the data. First, He et al. (2024) compiled an Olympiad-level math evaluation set using manual
420   assessment, which we leverage in our context to verify the quality of our method through the corre-
421   lation between accuracies. Figure 5a, demonstrates that the evaluation on LiveAoPSBench is highly
422   correlated with carefully established benchmarks such as OlympiadBench. This demonstrates that
423
      our automatically generated benchmark aligns closely with the quality of those created through ex-
      tensive human effort. Next, we subsample 10% of our evaluation set and ask human annotators to
424
      verify the correctness of the final parsed answers by referring to the original post. Annotators are
425
      given three options: yes, no, and no-answer. “Yes” and “no” indicate whether the answer is deemed
426   correct, while “no-answer” is selected when a concrete answer is not appropriate (e.g., abstract con-
427   cept questions answered with concrete examples). As a result, we found that 88% of the annotations
428   were marked as correct, while 8% were incorrect and 4% fell under the no-answer category. To
429   understand the gap from perfect accuracy here, we further measure the correlation between groups
430   of human annotators by computing the percentage of choices that were consistent. Surprisingly, the
431   human annotators only reached an agreement rate of 91%, demonstrating the challenge of evaluating
      Olympiad-level problems, even for graduate-level annotators.

      5   L IMITATIONS
487
488
489
      Absence of Visual Content. Our dataset currently focuses on text-only problems, which may
      limit its effectiveness in certain areas, particularly geometry. Many geometry problems rely heav-
490
      ily on diagrams to fully convey the problem statement. Incorporating relevant images and figures
491
      could significantly enhance the dataset’s comprehensiveness and applicability, especially in visually-
492   dependent mathematical domains.
493
494   Evaluation of Proof-based Questions. Our evaluation dataset focuses on QA pairs with clear, final
495
      answers, which is well-suited to a broad range of Olympiad-level problems. However, a signifi-
      cant portion of such types of problems involve more complex proof-based questions that require
496
      detailed logical reasoning and multiple steps. While we incorporate proof-based questions in our
497
      instruction-tuning pipeline, the current evaluation pipeline lacks the ability to evaluate such ques-
498   tions effectively.
499
500   Quality Variability in Community-Generated Content. The community-driven content from the
501
      AoPS forum provides a rich source of high-quality data. Nevertheless, as with any community-
      generated content, the quality of answers and solutions can vary. While our filtering and refinement
502
      processes have successfully mitigated much of this noise, incorporating more advanced techniques
503
      in future iterations could result in better consistency and precision.

506   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
507
508
      In conclusion, this paper introduces the AoPS-Instruct dataset and LiveAoPSBench, leveraging
509
      community-driven content from the Art of Problem-Solving forum to address the challenges of lim-
510   ited training data and unreliable evaluation for LLMs solving Olympiad-level math problems. By
511   developing a scalable and automated pipeline for extracting and refining question-answer pairs, this
512   work presents a dataset containing over 650, 000 QA pairs, along with an up-to-date, contamination-
513   resistant evaluation benchmark. Our experiments demonstrate significant performance improve-
514   ments across multiple standard benchmarks for models fine-tuned on the AoPS-Instruct, highlight-
515   ing enhanced mathematical reasoning capabilities. Furthermore, the observed performance decline
516   of various LLMs on LiveAoPSBench underscores the importance of continuously updating evalua-
517   tion sets to mitigate the risks of data contamination.
518   For future work, there are several promising directions to explore. First, while this paper focuses on
519   the AoPS forum, the pipeline developed is not limited to this domain. It is generalizable and can
520   be applied to other online forums or different subject areas, enabling the creation of high-quality
521   datasets for various fields, such as physics, computer science, or even non-technical disciplines. Ex-
522   panding this pipeline to other knowledge-intensive communities could further improve the training
523   and evaluation of LLM across disciplines. Additionally, the quality of the dataset can be signifi-
524
      cantly improved by incorporating more advanced LLMs into the pipeline. Leveraging state-of-the-
      art models for question extraction, answer detection, and solution rewriting would result in more
525
      accurate and detailed data, ultimately enhancing the effectiveness of the fine-tuned models. Lastly,
526
      the current pipeline focuses on question-answer pairs with clear final answers, but a significant por-
527   tion of Olympiad-level problems involves proof-based questions that require a deeper evaluation of
528   logical reasoning, argument structure, and intermediate steps. Future work could include adapting
529   the pipeline to accommodate these proof-based problems, potentially using another advanced LLM
530   as a judge (Li et al., 2023), or incorporating formalization methods to better assess these complex
531   solutions.

764         2. Putting them together we get 15 * 20 / 2 = 25 * AD / 2. So AD = 15 * 20 / 25 = 12. Because DE
765         perpendicular to AC and DF perpendicular to AB, AEDF is a rectangle, which means that EF = AD,

            EF = 12. The answer is A.
