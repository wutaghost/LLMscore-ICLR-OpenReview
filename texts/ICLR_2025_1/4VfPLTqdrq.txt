009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Crowd localization plays a crucial role in visual scene understanding towards pre-
012            dicting each pedestrian location in a crowd, thus being applicable to various down-
013            stream tasks. However, existing approaches suffer from significant performance
014            degradation due to differences in head scale distributions (scale shift) between
015            training and testing data, a challenge known as domain generalization (DG). This
016            paper aims to comprehend the nature of scale shift within the context of domain
017            generalization for crowd localization models. To this end, we address three key
018            questions: (i) how to quantify the scale shift influence on DG task, (ii) why does
019            this influence occur, (iii) how to mitigate the influence. Specifically, we first es-
020
               tablish a benchmark, ScaleBench, and reproduce 20 advanced DG algorithms, to
               quantify the influence. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate the limi-
021
               tations of existing algorithms and highlight the under-explored nature of this issue.
022
               To further understand its behind reason, we provide a rigorous theoretical analysis
023            on scale shift. Building on this analysis, we further propose a simple yet effec-
024            tive algorithm called Semantic Hook to mitigate the influence of scale shift on
025            DG, which also serves as a case study revealing three significant insights for fu-
026            ture research. Our results emphasize the importance of this novel and applicable
027            research direction, which we term Scale Shift Domain Generalization.

      Crowd localization (Liu et al., 2019; Gao et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2022; Han et al.,
      2023; Chen et al., 2024) aims to accurately identify the positions of individuals, particularly in dense
034
      and diverse population scenarios. It provides quantity of applicable utilities for downstream tasks.
035
      For example, pinpointing the exact location of each individual within a crowd can improve public
036   surveillance (Li et al., 2013), facilitate event management (Mundhenk et al., 2016), and assist in
037   urban planning (Marsden et al., 2018). Moreover, the frameworks for crowd localization are appli-
038   cable to dense cell (Morelli et al., 2021) and pathology detection (Lagogiannis et al., 2023), thereby
039   advancing clinical diagnosis. Hence, previous researchers have developed a variety of supervised
040   crowd localization algorithms.
041
      However, the generalization performance of these fully-supervised models often fall short when
042   exposed to unseen data distributions, a challenge commonly referred to as domain shift (Wang et al.,
043   2022). Over the years, the community has made substantial efforts to address various forms of
044   domain shifts, such as dataset shifts (Du et al., 2023) (e.g., from SHHA (Zhang et al., 2016) to
045   QNRF (Idrees et al., 2018)), scene shifts (Wang et al., 2019; Gong et al., 2022) (e.g., from street
046   to stadium), and weather shifts (Peng & Chan, 2024) (e.g., from sunny to snowy). It is widely
047   accepted that such domain shifts between the training (source) and testing (target) domains can lead
048   to performance degradation in crowd analysis models. Recently, Ma et al. (2021) have identified
049   that the head scale distribution of crowd datasets significantly influences the performance of crowd
050
      analysis models when crossing datasets evaluation. However, it is still unexplored how scale shift
      affects the performance under domain generalization (Wang et al., 2022) scenario.
051
052   Hence, we conduct realistic experiments to reveal that the generalization performance of state-of-
053   the-art (sota) crowd locators degrade significantly when scale shift occurs across domain. Specifi-
      cally, as shown by Table 1, we firstly divide existing datasets into two domains (like Tiny and Big)

      Despite recognizing the impact of scale shifts on domain generalization, this issue has not been
      sufficiently addressed in the literature. Previous work mainly concentrate on how to capture differ-
075
      ent scales in a fully-supervised paradigm (Han et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a). As for the scale
076
      shift under cross dataset evaluation, SDNet (Ma et al., 2021), it focuses on “domain adaptation”2 ,
077   in which the target domain is accessible during training. Our task “domain generalization” assumes
078   the whole target domain should be unseen during training, which is more pertinent to the deploy-
079   ment of crowd models in open-set environments. Furthermore, much of the existing research on
080   cross-domain crowd analysis (Du et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023; Peng & Chan,
081   2024) overlooks a crucial aspect: there is no assurance of performance retention on the source do-
082   main. In real applications where crowd locators may operate in open-set scenarios, the target scales
083   remain uncertain. Thus, it is essential to maintain performance on both out-of-distribution and in-
084   distribution data. Therefore, it is critical to answer: How can we effectively generalize crowd
085
      localization models to unseen scales while preserving performance on seen scales?
086   In this paper, we present as far as we know the FIRST study on scale shift domain generalization in
087   crowd localization. Our research addresses three key questions: 1) Influence: How to quantify the
088   influence of scale shift on the domain generalization performance of crowd localization? 2) Analy-
089   sis: Why does this influence occur? 3) Mitigation: What strategies can be employed to mitigate this
090   influence? We provide a comprehensive analysis to answer these questions.
091
092   • Influence: ScaleBench as a benchmark to quantify scale shift and its influence. In Sec. 2,
093
        we establish a scale benchmark dataset “ScaleBench” with 17,138 images to officially quantify
        scale shift and its influence on domain generalization with crowd localization tasks. Specifically,
094
        we manually annotate over 1.5 million bounding boxes for datasets (SHHA (Zhang et al., 2016),
095
        SHHB (Zhang et al., 2016), and QNRF (Idrees et al., 2018)) and integrate with originally anno-
096     tated datasets (SHRGBD (Lian et al., 2019), JHU (Sindagi et al., 2020), and NWPU (Wang et al.,
097     2020b)). Furthermore, we propose an innovative domain partitioning method to categorize the
098     images in ScaleBench into four distinct domains based on progressive scale distributions. This
099     benchmark is then utilized to evaluate domain generalization ability under scale shift conditions.
100     Then, we designed a PyTorch codebase and conducted a comparative experiments of 20 state-of-
101     the-art domain generalization algorithms3 , most of which exhibits even worse performance than
102     baseline, thus reveals the under-studied nature of this issue.

      • Analysis: Scale Shift as Mixed Shifts in Diversity and Correlation. In Sec. 3.1, we investigate
109     the reasons behind the unsatisfactory performance of domain generalization models and find that
110     scale shift affects domain generalization by causing the model to learn a spurious association
111     between scale and target. By employing established definitions of domain shifts, which include
112     diversity and correlation shifts (Ye et al., 2022), we prove that scale shift embodies a combination
113     of both. This elucidates why existing domain generalization algorithms struggle with scale shifts.

115     we introduce an algorithm, Semantic Hook, designed to strengthen the association between se-
116     mantic features and task predictions. Using Semantic Hook as a case study, our extensive analysis
117     provides three key insights for future research on scale shift domain generalization: 1) Enhancing
118     the connection between final predictions and semantic features while minimizing scale feature
119
        influence. 2) Traditional image interpolation methods, while useful, have limited efficacy. 3) In-
        creasing training data yields marginal benefits if the data maintain a consistent scale distribution.

      To study the scale shift problem, it is crucial to have bounding box information for each human
148
      head, as these bounding boxes include size information essential for exploring scale variance. How-
149
      ever, we cannot always obtain such information from temporal mainstream datasets. Earlier but
150   still widely-used datasets such as SHHA (Zhang et al., 2016), SHHB (Zhang et al., 2016), and

152   center of each head. Fortunately, more recent datasets, including SHRGBD (Lian et al., 2019),

186   2.2.2    C HALLENGE 2: C ONTINUAL S CALE D ISTRIBUTION PARTITION
187
      While we currently have access to a rich repository of data, the next challenge lies in effectively
188   partitioning this data into domains that accurately reflect scale shifts. The simplest approach is to
189   collate objects within a dataset, derive a scale distribution, and then apply various scale thresholds
190   for partitioning. However, this paradigm faces two significant challenges: 1) the presence of varying
191   scale ranges within a single image; and 2) the complexities involved in selecting appropriate scale
192   thresholds. Specifically, the original images in existing datasets often exhibit intrinsic scale varia-
193   tion, meaning that each image encompasses objects spanning multiple scale levels. Consequently,
194   assigning such images to different domains does not guarantee sufficient scale differentiation (Wang
195   et al., 2022) among them. Moreover, the choice of scale thresholds directly impacts the number of
196
      samples in each domain, with improper selection leading to imbalances in domain representation.
197   To address these challenges, we propose a novel controllable domain scale partitioning module,
198   along with an evaluation pipeline designed to leverage it. We shall introduce these in details below.
199   Controllable Domain Scale Partition To achieve scale controllable domain partition, we first
200   propose an Image-Level Scale Distribution Regularization, that aims to eliminate intra-image scale
201   variance by dividing an image into intra-scale consistent patches. Then, we set these patches as
202   our new images, and propose a Domain Partition by Manipulating Scale Distribution to group those
203
      patches into several domains. Let us elaborate on the processes within our proposed framework.
204   1) Image-Level Scale Distribution Regularization. As aforementioned, the significant scale variance
205   present within individual images complicates the assignment of these images to scale-aware do-
206   mains. We attribute this challenge to the high resolutions of original images collected from prior
207   research; for example, some images in the NWPU dataset are more than 10, 0002 pixels. To miti-
208   gate this, we propose segmenting images into patches according to scales. This reduces the extensive
209
      image-level scale variation and enables better regularization of the sample-wise scale distribution.
      Importantly, this patch division does not affect the subsequent training process, as temporal loca-
210
      tors (Liang et al., 2022; Han et al., 2023) operate by cropping images into patches for training.
211

      3.1       D OMAIN S CALE S HIFT
289
290   To better understand domain scale shift, we first need to answer:Why does domain scale shift affects
291   the generalization of crowd locators? Crowd images are composed of numerous independent indi-
292   viduals z, each defined by various attributes, including semantic features s (such as skin color), scale
293   c, and other characteristics. Therefore, when we feed training sample pairs (x, y) into a crowd lo-
294   cator, our learning process is modeled as the conditional distribution p(y|x). Additionally, the input
295   distribution p(x) can be decomposed into a joint distribution of these various attributes, represented
296
      as p(s, c, . . . ). According to the chain rule, we can express this relationship as follows:

358   features from fE (x + ϵ) to reduce the loss. Given that ϵ predominantly influences semantic infor-

      4.3     E MPIRICAL A NALYSIS
457
458   Q1: Can scale shift be alleviated by increasing in-distribution data? To address this, we isolate
459   the train, validation, and test sets within each domain in Table 3. By considering all possible domain
460   combinations, we aim to obtain an impartial assessment of generalization. Our analysis begins with
461   single to single generalization, which serves as the baseline case. We find that larger average domain
462   scales correlate with poorer performance, indicating that greater scale shifts diminish generalization.
463   When we increase the in-distribution data through multi-source scenarios, we observe that domains
464   farther from the target domain exert less influence on performance. For instance, the performance
465   results for TN to S (77.92%) and TNB to S (77.94%) show that the improvement from adding the
466
      additional domain B is minimal. A similar trend is evident in other cases as well. Thus, we conclude
      that in-distribution data offers limited help in alleviating scale shift generalization.
467
468
      Q2: Can scale distribution be treated as one of the major at-
469
      tributes in representing crowd images? To address this ques-
470
      tion, we design a novel experimental pipeline. When an attribute is
471   a primary factor in representing crowd images, we can select a small
472   core set by sampling identically and independently (IID) from the
473   corresponding attribute distribution. A model trained on this core
474   set should achieve comparable or even better performance than one
475   trained on the entire dataset.
476
      To this end, we IID sample images according to the scale distribu-
477   tion and split them into training, validation, and test sets, ensuring
478   the in-distribution (In-D) property among them. As shown in Fig-
479   ure 4, we observe an intriguing phenomenon: when scale attributes
                                                                                                          Figure 4: Less is more.
480   are In-D, we only need 30% of the original dataset to achieve com-
481   parable performance. This further emphasizes that increasing the
482   amount of In-D data provides limited benefits for generalization.
483
484   Q3: How does image interpolation influence the scale shift? Theoretically, as discussed in
485   Sec. 3.1, we show that image interpolation can mitigate the shift term of p(c|y, s, ...), but it does not

532
      We presented Scale Shift Domain Generalization with the realm of crowd localization, a new and
533
      applicable research direction. In this paper, we built a benchmark on this task called ScaleBench.
534   Extensive experiments on ScaleBench revealed the limitations of existing domain generalization
535   algorithms in addressing scale shift. Through our analysis, we demonstrated scale shift as a joint
536   shift between diversity and correlation shift. Building upon this property, we proposed an algorithm
537   called Semantic Hook to mitigate the issue, and conducted extensive analysis to derive three sig-
538   nificant insights for future works. We believe this work serves as a catalyst for greater scholarly
539   attention toward the essential yet challenging task of crowd localization under scale shifts, and we
      hope it inspires further investigations and advancements in this field.

999    B    D ETAILS FOR BASELINE C ROWD L OCALIZATION M ODEL
1000
1001   We illustrate the pipeline of our baseline crowd localization method in Figure 7. Given an image
1002   x ∈ RH×W ×3 , IIM (Gao et al., 2020), composed of an encoder fE , threshold learner fT and
                                                              H   W
1003   decoder fD , first embeds x into a feature fE (x) ∈ R 8 × 8 ×D with a feature dimension of D. Then,
1004   fE (x) is fed with threshold learner fT and decoder fD . Later, the decoder firstly transfers this
1005   image embedding into a sigmoid σ processed confidence map σ{fD [fE (x)]} ∈ [0, 1], where the
1006   fD [fE (x)] has a resolution of (H, W, 1), and each value within the map indicates the probability
1007   of the corresponding pixel value in the current location belongs to the human head are. Then, this
1008   fD [fE (x)] is also fed into the threshold along with the aforementioned fE (x) to obtain a threshold
1009   map T (x) ∈ [0, 1], which also has a resolution of (H, W, 1). Then, the final prediction can be
1010   obtained by comparing the values by I{fD [fE (x)] ≥ T (x)}, where I denotes the indicator function.
1011   With this predicted binary map, we can obtain the predicted areas mask. Then, the locations of these
1012   foreground areas can be extracted by graphical operation, where the center of each area is treated as
1013   the predicted human heads’ location.
1014
       However, in our task, to make the model concise enough to promise its adaptability, we remove
1015   the processes concerning the learnable threshold map, in which we directly obtain a confidence
1016   from encoder and decoder, then obtain the final binary map via a global and fixed threshold of 0.5.
1017   Experiments in the main text showcases this threshold is able to generalize well under the InD data.

1051   Correlation Alignment for Domain Adaptation (CORAL) (Sun & Saenko, 2016): CORAL mini-

1055   Domain Adversarial Neural Network (DANN) (Ganin et al., 2016): Based on ERM, DANN has a
1056   domain discriminator which aims to enhance the discrimination of predicted feature over domains.

       Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) (Li et al., 2018): In MMD, it tries to minimize the maximum
1059
       mean discrepancy among source distributions. Empirically, to obtain the feature distribution, we use
1060
       a Gaussian kernel to transfer it into reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS).
1061
1062   Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) (Arjovsky et al., 2019): IRM seeks to find representations that
1063   are invariant across different environments by minimizing a combination of the empirical risk and a
1064
       penalty term that measures the divergence of optimal predictors across environments. The idea is to
       make the representation good for all environments simultaneously, which is hypothesized to lead to
1065
       better OOD generalization.
1066
1067   Manifold Mixup (Mixup-F) (Verma et al., 2019): Mixup-F is a technique that extends the idea of
1068   Mixup-Ito the hidden representations within a neural network. It generates virtual training examples
1069   by combining hidden representations of different training examples along with their corresponding
1070
       labels. This regularizes the neural network to favor simple linear behaviors in-between training
       examples, which can lead to better generalization.
1071
1072   Mixup (Yan et al., 2020): Mixup-Itrains a model on convex combinations of pairs of examples and
1073   their labels. By doing this, it encourages the model to behave linearly in-between training examples,
1074   which can help to regularize the model and can potentially improve the OOD performance by making
1075   the model less certain on interpolations between training domains.
1076   Sharpness Aware Minimization (SAM) (Foret et al., 2021): SAM seeks to improve model gen-
1077   eralization by focusing on the sharpness of the loss landscape. By minimizing the worst-case loss
1078   within a neighborhood around the parameters, SAM aims to find parameters that lie in flatter regions
1079   of the loss landscape, under the assumption that flatter minima correlate with better generalization,
       especially in OOD scenarios.

1080
       Variance Training Risks (VREx) (Krueger et al., 2021): VREx is a method that minimizes the
1081   variance of the empirical risk across different environments. The intuition is that by finding a model
1082   that has stable performance across various source domains, it will likely perform well on unseen
1083   target domains.
1084
       Spectral Decoupling (SD) (Pezeshki et al., 2021): SD addresses the overfitting to spurious correla-
1085
       tions by decoupling the spectral components of the feature representations. It does so by regularizing
1086
       the spectral norm of the weights, which encourages the model to rely less on features that are highly
1087   predictive on the training data but may not generalize well to OOD data.
1088
1089   Style-Agnostic Networks (SagNets) (Nam et al., 2021): SagNets are designed to disentangle con-
1090
       tent and style information in the neural representations to improve OOD generalization. The network
       learns to separate style-related features from content-related features, and during inference, it relies
1091
       more on content features, which are presumed to be more stable across different domains.
1092
1093   Invariant Representation Learning (IRL) (Chevalley et al., 2022): It bridges the gap between
1094   causal reasoning and representation learning. And it establishes a foundation for understanding
1095   invariance in the face of style variations.
1096   Information Bottleneck (IB) (Li et al., 2022): The IB principle aims at finding a representation
1097   that preserves as much information as possible about the target variable while compressing the input
1098   data, effectively reducing its complexity. This is achieved by minimizing a trade-off between the
1099   mutual information of the representation and the target and the mutual information of the input and
1100   the representation. In OOD settings, this can lead to learning more robust features that are less
1101   sensitive to variations not relevant to the prediction task.
1102   Exact Feature Distribution Matching (EFDM) (Zhang et al., 2022): The EFDM approach is de-
1103   signed to address the limitations of traditional feature distribution matching methods in the context
1104   of Arbitrary Style Transfer (AST) and Domain Generalization (DG) tasks. These tasks are pred-
1105   icated on the idea that matching the feature distributions between different domains or styles can
1106   improve the performance of visual learning models.
1107   DomainDrop (Guo et al., 2023): The DomainDrop approach is an innovative method designed to
1108   improve domain generalization, which is the ability of deep neural network models to perform well
1109   on unseen test datasets that may have different distributions from the training (source) datasets. The
1110   central challenge being addressed is the performance degradation that occurs due to domain shifts,
1111   meaning differences between the data distributions of the source and target domains.
1112   SAGM (Wang et al., 2023b): SAGM is an optimization method designed to enhance the domain
1113   generalization (DG) capabilities of machine learning models. The main goal of DG is to train
1114   models on a source domain in such a way that they can perform well when applied to unseen target
1115   domains.
1116
       GAM (Zhang et al., 2023b): GAM is an optimization approach that seeks to enhance the gener-
1117
       alization of deep learning models by targeting minima with uniformly small curvature across all
1118   directions in the loss landscape. The motivation behind GAM stems from the observed benefits of
1119   training models to find flat minima—regions of the parameter space where the loss function varies
1120   slowly with parameter changes, which are associated with better generalization to unseen data.

1125   D.1   S CALE B ENCH G ENERATION
1126
       In our study, we began by aggregating a comprehensive collection of images. Subsequently, we
1127
       extracted relevant information on the scale and coordinates of each instance within these images.
1128   This data was instrumental in fitting a two-dimensional Gaussian mixture model. Specifically, we
1129   normalized the scales by dividing by the maximum scale value and normalized the vertical coordi-
1130   nates by the height of the image. The normalized scale and coordinate data were then combined and
1131   inputted into an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to optimize the parameters of a Gaus-
1132   sian mixture distribution. We preset the number of Gaussian components to five for each image, a
1133   number intentionally set to include some redundancy. Following this, we segmented the images into
       patches based on their respective sub-Gaussian distributions. An initial filtering step was applied


                                                         21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       to these patches, eliminating any with a height less than 100 pixels. Moreover, we implemented a
1135   variance restriction on the scale of the patches, discarding those with a scale variance greater than
1136   twice the mean scale. Once we had obtained a set of clean patches, we moved on to the domain
1137   partitioning phase. During this stage, we generated a scale distribution for each patch and employed
1138   a greedy search algorithm to identify the optimal scale boundary. This boundary was used to divide
1139   the complete scale distribution into five discrete regions. The first four regions were designated for
1140   the formation of the ScaleBench, while the fifth region was excluded from further analysis due to its
1141   nonconformity with the established criteria.

1144   D.2    L EAVE -O NE -O UT G ENERALIZATION
1145
1146   In our leave-one-out generalization experiments, we conducted a series of four distinct trials. For
1147   each trial, one particular domain was designated as the ’target’ while the remaining domains collec-
1148   tively formed the ’source’ domain. During the training process, we implemented a random rescaling
1149   of the input images to vary their size within a range of 0.8 to 1.2 times their original resolution. The
1150   images were then randomly cropped to a standard size of 512 × 512 pixels. For images with a height
1151
       smaller than 512 pixels, we employed padding to increase their size to 513 pixels to ensure consis-
       tency in input dimensions. Additionally, to augment the dataset and promote model robustness, we
1152
       included a random horizontal flip for each image.
1153
1154   For algorithms that did not feature a bespoke optimizer, we utilized the Adam optimizer to fine-
1155   tune the model parameters. We initiated the optimization with a learning rate of 1 × 10−5 , which
1156   was systematically reduced following each training step at a decay rate of 0.99 to allow for precise
1157
       adjustments as the model converged. When it came to sampling during training, we tailored our ap-
       proach to the architecture of each neural network. Specifically, we sampled 8 images for each source
1158
       domain when training with ResNet-18, 6 images for HRNet, and 4 images for ViT-B. This strategy
1159
       ensured that each network received an appropriate number of images from the source domains to
1160   effectively learn and generalize across the distinct datasets.

1163   D.3    M ULTI -S OURCE G ENERALIZATION
1164
1165
       The experimental setup for multi-source generalization mirrors that of the leave-one-out general-
1166
       ization approach in many aspects. However, a key distinction lies in the segregation of the dataset
1167   within each domain into three subsets: training (train-set), validation (val-set), and testing (test-set).
1168   When a domain is designated as the source domain, both its train-set and val-set are employed for
1169   model training and validation, respectively. This allows the model to learn from and tune its parame-
1170   ters based on a diverse range of examples and feedback within the source domain. In contrast, when
1171   a domain assumes the role of the target domain, its test-set is exclusively utilized. The performance
1172   of the model is then evaluated based on how well it generalizes to this unseen data. This structured
1173   approach ensures a clear delineation between the data used for model development and the data used
1174   for testing, thereby providing a rigorous assessment of the model’s generalization capabilities across
1175
       different domains.

1676   To harness the capabilities of vision transformers for our task, we began by extracting the image
1677   encoder from the selected vision transformer models. The encoder serves as a feature extractor, cap-
1678   turing the intricate patterns and high-level representations within the images. Recognizing the need
1679   to reconstruct the spatial detail lost during the encoding process, we complemented the encoder with
1680   a series of transposed convolutional modules. These modules, often referred to as deconvolutional
1681   layers, function in a manner inverse to that of standard convolutional layers. By employing learn-
1682   able filters and strides, they progressively upsample the lower-resolution feature maps output by
1683   the encoder, thereby regaining the original spatial resolution of the input images. This architecture,
1684
       combining the discriminative power of vision transformer encoders with the spatial recovery ability
       of transposed convolutional modules, forms a robust foundation for our model. However, to fully
1685
       exploit this structure, we tailored the fine-tuning stage to optimally adjust the pre-trained weights to
1686
       our specific application. Fine-tuning was performed with a bifurcated learning rate strategy aimed at
1687   balancing stability and adaptability. For the decoder, we adopted a learning rate of 1 × 10−5 , which
1688   is relatively higher to encourage the model to learn the nuances of upsampling and reconstruction
1689   more rapidly. This rate allows the decoder to adapt to the task of restoring image details without
1690   major restrictions. In contrast, for the encoder—which already possesses a wealth of pre-trained
1691   knowledge—a much lower learning rate of 1 × 10−8 was chosen. This conservative learning rate
1692   ensures that the valuable encoded representations are retained and only subtly modified, preventing
1693   the overwriting of useful features developed through pre-training on large and diverse datasets. By
1694   employing this dual learning rate approach, we strike a delicate balance: we maintain the integrity
1695
       of the encoder’s pre-trained features while allowing the decoder to evolve and specialize for the task
       at hand. This fine-tuning methodology is designed to bring the entire model in line with the specific
1696
       requirements of our application, thus enabling the production of high-fidelity results in recovering
1697
       the original image resolution.

1699   To undertake a comprehensive evaluation of the generalization capabilities of various pre-trained
1700   models under conditions of scale shift, we must establish an experimental framework that presents
1701
       models with a range of object scales that change dynamically.
1702   This framework is essential for simulating scenarios akin to real-world applications where object
1703   sizes can differ significantly from those seen during the training phase. The experimental setup is as
1704   follows:
1705
1706         1 Creation of a Varied Scale Dataset: Our first step is to assemble a dataset that is reflective of
1707           diverse object scales. This dataset is composed of image patches, each containing objects
1708
               of different sizes. By covering a broad spectrum of scales, the dataset ensures that the scale
               distribution is continuous and representative of potential real-world variations.
1709
1710         2 Discretization into Scale Bins: With the dataset in hand, we segment the continuous scale
1711           distribution into discrete intervals called scale bins. Each bin corresponds to a specific
1712           range of object scales and effectively represents a mini-dataset or ’domain.’ This segmen-
1713           tation allows us to handle the scale variation in a structured manner, facilitating separate
1714           analysis and modeling for each bin.
1715
             3 Expansion of Domain Variety: This binning approach stands in contrast to our primary
1716           method, referred to as ScaleBench, which limited the domain count to four. By increasing
1717           the number of bins, we correspondingly increase the number of distinct domains, thereby
1718           enriching the diversity of scale shifts that we can analyze. This methodological shift en-
1719           ables a more detailed investigation into how models respond to subtle changes in scale,
1720           beyond the coarse groupings used previously.
1721
             4 Domain Partitioning for Training and Testing: We then strategically divide the scale bins
1722
               into two groups based on a predetermined scale threshold. The bins below this threshold are
1723
               designated for training the models and selecting the optimal model configurations (modes).
1724           Conversely, the bins that exceed this threshold are reserved for testing. This division is
1725           critical for evaluating OOD generalization: during training, models are exposed only to a
1726           restricted range of scales, while during testing, they encounter scales that they have not
1727           been trained on—mirroring the challenges models face when deployed in the real world.

2073
2074   Recognizing that annotating bounding boxes in congested and complex scenes can be tedious, we
2075

2076
       through human input. The final two rounds aimed at further refining the manually annotated boxes.

2078   To facilitate this process, we divided our team into three sub-teams: Team A (20 members), Team
2079   B (15 members), and Team C (4 members). Through this collaborative approach, we successfully
2080   provided more than 1.5 million bounding boxes for the over 2,700 images.

2082   H.2     U NIFIED E VALUATION M ETRIC FOR C ROWD L OCALIZATION
2083
       In the domain of crowd localization, accurately evaluating performance is crucial. Typically, this
2084
       evaluation involves establishing a point-to-point correspondence between the predicted coordinates
2085   and the actual ground-truth positions through the construction of a bipartite graph. Subsequently,
2086   distances are computed between paired points, and a prediction is deemed correct if this distance
2087   falls below a predetermined threshold. Nonetheless, the choice of threshold is pivotal, greatly im-
2088   pacting the perceived precision of predictions. A threshold that is excessively lenient may yield
2089   results that are overly generalized, while an overly strict threshold might result in an underesti-
2090   mation of the model’s predictive capabilities. In practice, for datasets that provide bounding box
2091   annotations, such as NWPU-Crowd (Wang et al., 2020b) and JHU-Crowd++ (Sindagi et al., 2020),
2092   the threshold is often pragmatically set to the length of the diagonal of these boxes. However, ear-
2093
       lier datasets like SHHA (Zhang et al., 2016), SHHB (Zhang et al., 2016), and QNRF (Idrees et al.,
       2018) do not offer such annotations, thereby introducing an element of subjectivity into the evalu-
2094
       ation process concerning the localization threshold. In our work, we address this inconsistency by
2095
       contributing bounding box annotations for the SHHA, SHHB, and QNRF datasets. This enhance-
2096   ment enables us to standardize the evaluation procedure by setting the matching threshold to the
2097   diagonal length of the bounding box. Furthermore, we advocate for subsequent research in this area
2098   to utilize these annotations, fostering a more objective and uniform assessment of methodological
2099   performance across the SHHA, SHHB, and QNRF datasets.
