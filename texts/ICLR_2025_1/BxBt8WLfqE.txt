011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            A methodology for informed machine learning is presented and its effectiveness is
014            shown through numerical experiments with physics-informed learning problems.
015            The methodology has three main distinguishing features. Firstly, prior information
016            is introduced in the training problem through hard constraints rather than through
017
               the typical modern practice of using soft constraints (i.e., regularization terms).
               Secondly, the methodology does not employ penalty-based (e.g., augmented La-
018
               grangian) methods since the use of such methods results in an overall methodology
019
               that is similar to a soft-constrained approach. Rather, the methodology is based
020            on a recently proposed stochastic-gradient-based algorithm that maintains compu-
021            tationally efficiency while handling constraints with a Newton-based technique.
022            Thirdly, a new projection-based variant of the well-known Adam optimization
023            methodology is proposed for settings with hard constraints. Numerical experiments
024            on a set of physics-informed learning problems show that, when compared with a
025            soft-constraint approach, the proposed methodology can be easier to tune, lead to
026            accurate predictions more quickly, and lead to better final prediction accuracy.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In this paper, we propose a methodology for informed supervised machine learning and demonstrate
032   its effectiveness on a set of challenging test problems. The methodology involves incorporating prior
033   knowledge into the learning process through hard constraints that are imposed during training only.
034
      Both of these highlighted aspects are critical for its effectiveness. Our methodology’s use of hard
      constraints is in contrast to previously proposed methodologies that incorporate prior knowledge
035
      through either (a) soft constraints (Zhu et al., 2019) (i.e., through regularization/penalty terms in
036
      the objective function) or (b) designing the prediction function to incorporate knowledge directly
037   (Chalapathi et al., 2024; Négiar et al., 2023) (e.g., through neural network layers for which a
038   forward pass requires solving a set of equations or even an optimization problem). By imposing
039   such constraints during training only, one can avoid having the trained network require expensive
040   operations for each forward pass. Another key feature of our proposed methodology is that we do
041   not solve the hard-constrained training problem with a penalty-based (e.g., augmented Lagrangian)
042   method. This feature is also critical for the effectiveness of our methodology. Ours is not the
043   first article to propose the use of hard constraints for informed learning; see, e.g., Márquez-Neila
044   et al. (2017). However, some other approaches for training with hard constraints that have been
045
      proposed use such penalty-based methods, even though this ultimately means that the behavior of the
      training algorithm is similar to employing an unconstrained training algorithm to a soft-constrained
046
      formulation; see, e.g., Lu et al. (2021). Our intuition is that hard constraints guide the neural network
047
      to prioritize mapping the PDE solution, e.g. in physics-informed machine learning, even only at
048   certain inputs, enabling faster and more efficient training. For our methodology to solve the hard-
049   constrained problems, we propose a variant of the stochastic-gradient-based algorithm from Berahas
050   et al. (2021) that handles the constraints with a Newton-based technique; see also Berahas et al.
051   (2023). An additional important feature of our proposed methodology is a new projection-based
052   variant of the well-known Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) optimization routine. (Our framework is easily
053   extended to other diagonal-scaling methods, such as Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011) and RMSprop
      (Dauphin et al., 2015; Tieleman & Hinton, 2012). We simply demonstrate our framework using Adam

      due to its popularity in the supervised learning literature (Rathore et al., 2024).) By decomposing the
055   stochastic algorithm’s step computation routine and exploiting the fact that the Adam scaling matrix
056   is diagonal and positive definite, the per-iteration cost of our algorithm is comparable to that of an
057   Adam-based method employed in a soft-constraint/regularization approach.
058
      As a case study for demonstrating the effectiveness of our proposed methodology, we consider a set
059
      of physics-informed learning problems. Through straightforward formulations of such problems, we
060
      show that our stochastic algorithmic framework with projected Adam (P-Adam) scaling outperforms
061   alternative approaches involving only soft constraints or a non-projection-based Adam routine. In
062   particular, we show that our approach can (a) yield good solutions more quickly, (b) yield more
063   accurate predictions after training is completed, and (c) require less hyperparameter tuning.

065   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS AND L IMITATIONS
066
067   We propose a methodology for informed supervised machine learning that (a) incorporates prior
068   knowledge through hard constraints, (b) employs a computationally efficient method that handles the
069   objective with a stochastic-gradient-based technique and handles the constraints with a Newton-based
070   technique such that the overall per-iteration cost is comparable to that of alternative soft-constrained
071   approaches, and (c) involves a new projection-based variant of Adam. We show that our method
072
      yields superior performance on a test set of physics-informed learning problems.
073   Our proposed algorithm is based on a method that enjoys state-of-the-art convergence and complexity
074   guarantees. We do not show that these guarantees extend to the setting when our P-Adam routine
075   is employed, although our experiments show that the routine is robust. In addition, our approach
076   has a higher per-iteration cost when compared to a method that may be employed to minimize a
077   soft-constraint-based (unconstrained) training problem. However, our experiments show that, within
078
      the same computational time budget, our approach can offer better trained models. Also, the fact
      that—through our methodology—one might avoid having to employ a model that requires expensive
079
      operations with every forward pass can justify this additional per-iteration cost during model training.

170   2.2   A NEW PROJECTION - BASED VARIANT OF A DAM
171
172   The practical performance of stochastic-gradient-based methods for (unconstrained) training can
173
      be improved significantly with adaptive scaling. Approaches of this type include Adagrad (Duchi
      et al., 2011), RMSProp (Dauphin et al., 2015; Tieleman & Hinton, 2012), and Adam (Kingma &
174
      Ba, 2015), the popular variants of which involve only diagonal scaling matrices. One approach for
175
      employing, say, an Adam-based scheme in the context of a Newton-based algorithm for constrained
176   optimization is to choose the matrix Hk in (2) as such a positive definite diagonal scaling matrix and
177   to replace the first component on the right-hand side of (2) with the running average of gradients that
178   is employed in Adam for unconstrained training/optimization. Such an approach was proposed and
179   tested in Márquez-Neila et al. (2017).
180
      We propose a new projection-based variant of Adam for constrained optimization, presented in
181
      Algorithm 2. It is similar to that proposed in Márquez-Neila et al. (2017), but takes into account the
182   fact that, in the setting of a Newton-based method for constrained optimization, the component of a
183   stochastic-gradient estimate that lies in the range space of the constraint derivative does not affect
184   the search direction. Therefore, the idea proposed here projects-out this component for the running
185   averages of gradient values. In other words, our Algorithm2 mainly differs from Márquez-Neila et al.
186   (2017) in that Algorithm2 utilizes the momentum of the component of the stochastic gradient in the
187   null space of ∇c(wk )T rather than the entire stochastic gradient, which is the case in Márquez-Neila
188   et al. (2017). This is a nontrivial change. In our experiments, this distinction demonstrates the
189   superior performance of our method compared to that of Márquez-Neila et al. (2017).
190   A Newton-based step for the constraints and a steepest-descent-type step for the objective can
191   be computed at wk by solving (2) with Hk = I. By the fundamental theorem of linear algebra,
192   let sk = vk + uk , where vk ∈ Range(∇c(wk )) and uk ∈ Null(∇c(wk )T ). The second row
193   of (2) implies vk = −∇c(wk )(∇c(wk )T ∇c(wk ))−1 c(wk ), which is unaffected by gk . On the other
194   hand, with the columns of an orthogonal matrix Zk spanning the null space of ∇c(wk )T , one finds
195   uk = −Zk (ZkT Zk )−1 ZkT gk is the orthogonal projection of gk onto Null(∇c(wk )T ).
196   The discussion in the prior paragraph shows that the solution component sk in (2) with Hk replaced
197   by I is the same when the stochastic gradient gk is replaced by its orthogonal projection onto the null
198   space of ∇c(wk )T , which is to say that this projection of gk is what matters for the search direction
199   component. This suggests that an Adam-based approach can be employed where the scaling matrix
200   and right-hand side vectors are computed based on the projection of gk , rather than on gk itself. Since
201   it is not computationally tractable to compute a null-space basis matrix Zk for all k ∈ N, one can
202
      instead employ the projection operator as shown in Algorithm 2 below.
203   Algorithm 2 offers various alternatives as well. For example, a projection-based variant of Adagrad
204   is obtained when, instead, pbk ← gk and qbk ← qbk−1 + (gk ◦ gk ). The major computation cost of
205   Algorithm 2 per iteration is from Line 3 and Line 8. For Line 3, the cost of (∇c(wk )T ∇c(wk ))−1 is
206   O(m2 d + m3 ). Hence the cost of computing ḡk is O(m2 d + m3 ). The cost of computing sk in Line 8
207   is also O(m2 d + m3 ), as shown next. Therefore, the overall cost of Algorithm 2 is O(m2 d + m3 ).
208
      When the number of rows of ∇c(wk )T (i.e., m) is small, the overall cost per iteration is proportional
      to that of computing H −1 g with a diagonal and positive definite H, as is required for an Adam-based
209
      method for the unconstrained setting.

255   3    P HYSICS - INFORMED LEARNING PROBLEMS
256
257   Our stochastic-gradient-based algorithm with P-Adam scaling for solving hard-constrained problems
258   can be employed in numerous informed-learning contexts (e.g., fair learning (Curtis et al., 2023b;
259   Donini et al., 2018; Komiyama et al., 2018; Zafar et al., 2017a;b; 2019)). For this work, we tested
260   our approach on a few physics-informed learning problems. We emphasize that our goal here is not
261
      to test huge-scale, state-of-the-art techniques for physics-informed learning. Rather, we take a few
      physics-informed learning test problems and train relatively straightforward neural networks in order
262
      to demonstrate the relative performance of our proposed algorithm with a soft-constrained approach
263
      with Adam scaling (Kingma & Ba, 2015) and a hard-constrained approach with Adam (not P-Adam)
264   scaling (Márquez-Neila et al., 2017). The relative performance of the algorithms would be similar if
265   we were to train much more sophisticated and large-scale neural networks that are being developed
266   in state-of-the-art physics-informed learning. For more on physics-informed learning we direct the
267   reader to, e.g., Cuomo et al. (2022); Karniadakis et al. (2021); Lagaris et al. (1998); Raissi et al.
268   (2019); Takamoto et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2021; 2023). The work (Chen et al., 2024) lies in the
269   physics-informed learning with hard constraints, but is restrict to the hard constraints that the PDE
      inputs and solutions are linearly related, whereas our method handles general nonlinear constraints.

270
      They enforce feasibility via projection, while we allow infeasible iterates, using projection only for
271   momentum. Thus, we do not compare our method with theirs.

273   Let us now provide an overview of the setting of physics-informed learning that we consider in
274
      our experiments. A parametric partial differential equation (PDE) can be written generically as
      F(ϕ, u) = 0, where (Φ, U, V) is a triplet of Banach spaces, F : Φ × U → V is a differential operator,
275
      ϕ ∈ Φ represents PDE parameters, and u ∈ U denotes a solution of the PDE corresponding to ϕ. The
276
      aim is to train a model to learn a mapping from the PDE parameters to a corresponding solution. Let
277   (an approximation of) such a mapping be denoted as G : Φ × Rn × Rd → U, the inputs to which are
278   PDE parameters, a vector encoding information about the domain of the PDE solution about which
279   one aims to make a prediction (e.g., temporal and/or spatial coordinates), and, say, neural-network
280   model parameters, and the output is a solution value predicted by the neural network model.
281
      For training a model to solve the PDE with potentially no known solution values (see Karniadakis
282
      et al. (2021)), one can consider a set of training inputs {(ϕi , yi )}i∈S1 and minimize the average
283   PDE residual over the training inputs. Assuming that, in addition, one has access to observed
284   and/or computed solution data in the form of tuples {(ϕi , yi , ui )}i∈S2 , one can also aim to minimize
285   the differences between known and predicted solution values. Mathematically, these aims can be
286   expressed as finding w to minimize
287                   1 X                                       1 X
288                          ∥F(ϕi , G(ϕi , yi , w))∥22 and/or             ∥ui − G(ϕi , yi , w)∥22 .       (4)
                    |S1 |                                      |S2 |
289                      i∈S1                                        i∈S2

290   Note that the ϕi and/or yi elements in {(ϕi , yi )}i∈S1 may be the same or different from those in
291   {(ϕi , yi , ui )}i∈S2 . Additional terms may also be used for training, e.g., pertaining to initial and/or
292   boundary conditions, or pertaining to partial physics information. For example, in §4.2, we train
293   a model for which it is known that a mass-balance equation should hold, so our training problem
294   involves residuals for the known mass-balance equation, even though this only defines the physics
295
      partially. Overall, if one combines all learning aims into a single objective function—say, with a
      linear combination involving weights for the different objective terms—then one is employing a
296
      soft-constrained approach to learning. We contend that a more effective approach can be to take
297
      at least a subset of terms and impose them as hard constraints during training. For example, with
298   respect to the aims in (4), one might impose constraints such as F(ϕi , G(ϕi , yi , w)) = 0 for some
299   i ∈ S1 and/or ui = G(ϕi , yi , w) for some i ∈ S2 . Our experiments show the benefits of this idea.

305   constrained approach with Adam scaling (Adam(unc) for “unconstrained”) (Karniadakis et al.,
306   2021) and a hard-constrained approach with (projection-less) Adam scaling (Adam(con)) (Márquez-
307   Neila et al., 2017). We consider four test problems. A few of them—namely, our 1D spring, 1D
308   Burgers’ equation, and 2D Darcy flow problems—have been seen in the literature; see Li et al. (2021);
309   Négiar et al. (2023). We also consider a problem from chemical engineering, a modified version of a
310   reaction network proposed in Gupta et al. (2016). To ensure a fair comparison, for a test problem,
311   P-Adam(con), Adam(unc) and Adam(con) use the same objective function, which is usually
312   the data-fitting loss plus PDE residual, while the hard-constrained methods P-Adam(con) and
313
      Adam(con) impose additional constraints: the PDE residuals are zero at some input data points.
      Further details are provided in each problem’s subsection. A GitHub repository containing the
314
      implementations of each of the algorithms and our test problems can be found at [to be inserted in
315
      non-anonymized version]. The software uses PyTorch (BSD-3 license). For all algorithms and all
316   experiments, the Adam parameters β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, and µ = 10−7 were used; see Algorithm 2.
317   Our numerical experiments were performed using Google CoLaboratoryTM V100 GPU platforms.
318   We estimate that it would require about one week to reproduce all of our experimental results. We
319   discuss a comparison of the running times of the three methods in Appendix C.

489   et al., 2023; Takamoto et al., 2023). With respect to the spatial domain [0, 1]2 , a forcing function f ,

491
492                   −∇ · (ν(x)∇u(x)) = f (x), x ∈ (0, 1)2 ;        u(x) = 0, x ∈ ∂[0, 1]2 .
493
494   Our aim was to train a neural network with the known PDE and boundary condition over various
495   diffusion coefficients such that, for any x ∈ [0, 1]2 and ν, it could be used to predict u(x).
496
      We used the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) architecture imported from the neuralop library (Ko-

500   dimension 16 × 16 (corresponding to u(x)). The training problems involve three objective terms:
501   PDE-residual (weighted by 10−2 ), boundary-residual (weighted by 10−2 ), and data-fitting (weighted
502   by 1) terms. Training data was generated by solving the PDE over 1000 ν values, the values and
503   corresponding solutions of which were obtained by neuralop using default settings. For each ν
504   value, the PDE-residual and boundary-residual terms involved all relevant generated training points,

528   5     C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
529
530   We proposed a method for informed learning that is stochastic-gradient-based, handles hard con-
531   straints, and employs a novel projection-based Adam diagonal scaling. The method’s per-iteration
532   cost is comparable to an unconstrained (soft-constrained) approach that also uses diagonal scaling.
533   Numerical experiments reveal practical benefits of the proposed scheme, which we conjecture would
534   also be witnessed when training larger and more sophisticated neural networks for informed learning.
535   Future work includes proving that the theoretical convergence guarantees of the stochastic SQP
536   method extend when our P-Adam(con) strategy is used. The analysis could be based on conver-
537   gence guarantees of the Adam method that have been established in recent years (e.g., in Zhang
538   et al. (2022)). The recent two stepsize SQP method in O’Neill (2024) is also a way to accumulate
539   the momentum of a step only in the null space of the constraint Jacobian. Comparisons of the
      performance of that method with P-Adam(con) would also be relevant for future work.

779   C     RUNNING TIME
780
781
      We ran the three methods for all problems with full batch and learning rate 5 × 10−4 for a fixed
782
      number of epochs (iterations) on a Colab L4 GPU python 3 node. Running time statistics are shown
783   in the Table 1.
784
785   We have several observations from the table. First, Adam(con) and P-Adam(con) exhibit
786
      similar running times. This is expected since the primary difference between the two methods is
      that P-Adam(con) requires projecting the stochastic gradient onto Range(∇c(wk )), which is not
787
      more computationally expensive since both methods need to compute the projection of c(wk ) onto
788
      Range(∇c(wk )) when computing the v component of the step. Second, for problems with either 3
789   or 10 constraints, the running time of Adam(con) and P-Adam(con) is approximately double
790   that of Adam(unc). For the problem with 50 constraints, the running time is roughly three times
791   that of Adam(unc). This is also expected, as Adam(con) and P-Adam(con) need to solve two
792   linear systems when computing the v and u steps, with the size of the linear system matrix being the
793   number of constraints by the number of trainable parameters. However, it is worth noting that the
794   computation time for Adam(con) and P-Adam(con) could be improved by using a more efficient
795   linear system solver, such as minres. Currently, we are using torch.linalg.solve(A, b)
796   from PyTorch. While PyTorch does not support the minres solver, other libraries, such as SciPy,
797
      do. Nevertheless, improving the efficiency of solving linear systems is not our focus in this work,
      so we opted for the straightforward approach available in PyTorch. Still, even though our proposed
798
      method has a higher per-iteration cost, one can see through all of the experiments in the paper and
799
      these appendices that, in many cases, our proposed method yields a better trained model if one were
800   to have a computational time budget.

837   E    E FFECTS ON DIFFERENT NUMBER OF HARD CONSTRAINTS
838
839   One may wonder about the effects of changing the number of hard constraints in our method. For
840   example, when we use a selection of ODE/PDE residuals as hard constraints, we can define a hard
841   constraint for each ODE/PDE input by using the residual at that input. In this case, the number of
842   hard constraints equals the number of selected inputs. Alternatively, we can aggregate the residuals
843   from multiple inputs to define a single hard constraint.
844
      In this section, we test the performance of our method when varying the number of hard constraints
845   and when define hard constraints by aggregating over multiple inputs. As an example, we use the
846   1D Spring problem in the mini-batch setting with a step size of 0.0005. The results are shown in
847   Figure 13.
848
      Our results show that: (1) Increasing the number of hard constraints by selecting more inputs improves
849
      the training loss performance, as seen when comparing the green and purple curves. However, this
850
      comes at the cost of increased computational effort.(2) Using the same number of hard constraints but
851   aggregating the residuals over more inputs does not significantly change the computational cost. It
852   may lead to faster convergence, but the training loss after convergence may not improve, as observed
853   when comparing the green and red curves.
