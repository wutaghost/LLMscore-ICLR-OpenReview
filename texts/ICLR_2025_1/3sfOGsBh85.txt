011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Brain signal to speech synthesis offers a new way of speech communication,
014            enabling innovative services and applications. With high temporal and spatial
015
               resolution, invasive brain sensing such as stereotactic electroencephalography
               (sEEG) becomes one of the promising solutions to decode complex brain dynamics.
016
               However, such data are hard to come by. In this paper, we introduce a bilingual
017
               brain-to-speech synthesis (CerebroVoice) dataset: the first publicly accessible
018            sEEG recordings curated for bilingual brain-to-speech synthesis. Specifically, the
019            CerebroVoice dataset comprises sEEG signals recorded while the speakers are
020            reading Mandarin Chinese words, English words, and Mandarin Chinese digits. We
021            establish benchmarks for two tasks on the CerebroVoice dataset: speech synthesis
022            and voice activity detection (VAD). For the speech synthesis task, the objective is to
023            reconstruct the speech uttered by the participants based on their sEEG recordings.
024            We propose a novel framework, Mixture of Bilingual Synergy Experts (MoBSE),
025            which uses a language-aware dynamic organization of low-rank expert weights to
026
               enhance the efficiency of language-specific decoding tasks. The proposed MoBSE
               framework achieves significant performance improvements over current state-of-
027
               the-art methods, producing more natural and intelligible reconstructed speech. The
028
               VAD task aims to determine whether the speaker is actively speaking. In this
029            benchmark, we adopt three established architectures and provide comprehensive
030            evaluation metrics to assess their performance. Our findings indicate that low-
031            frequency signals consistently outperform high-gamma activity across all metrics,
032            suggesting that low-frequency filtering is more effective for VAD tasks. This
033            finding provides valuable insights for advancing brain-computer interfaces in
034            clinical applications. The CerebroVoice dataset and benchmarks are publicly
035            available on Zenodo and GitHub for research purposes.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
039
      Recent advancements in brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) have opened a new frontier in human-
040
      computer interaction: speech synthesis directly from neural signals (1; 2; 3). Such systems hold
041
      significant potential to provide a natural means of communication for individuals with speech loss (4).
042   Although surface EEG is a widely used non-invasive technique, it primarily captures cortical activity
043   and lacks the spatial resolution needed to probe deeper brain regions, which are essential for speech
044   production (5; 6). Given the complex nature of speech, brain-to-speech synthesis relies on the
045   high-resolution and high signal-to-noise ratio, intracranial electroencephalography (iEEG) to capture
046   intricate neural correlates of speech production (6; 7; 8).
047
      The iEEG signal, also referred to as electrocorticography (ECoG) when using subdural grid electrodes
048   or stereotactic EEG (sEEG) when using depth electrodes, has attracted interests across diverse
049   domains of human neuroscience (9; 10). Many efforts have been made to ECoG-based brain-to-
050   speech synthesis and achieved promising outcomes (6; 7; 11). On the other hand, sEEG has several
051   unique advantages for brain-to-speech synthesis. Firstly, the implantation of sEEG electrode shafts
052   into the brain involves smaller incisions, potentially with fewer complications (12). This offers a
053   safer alternative for long-term brain activity monitoring (13; 14). Additionally, sEEG electrodes are
      placed directly within the brain tissue, allowing for more precise localization of functional areas (15).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Therefore, sEEG typically provides higher spatial resolution than ECoG. Furthermore, while ECoG
055   offers high-density coverage of specific regions, sEEG provides sparse sampling across multiple
056   geometric regions. This characteristic presents significant potential for speech synthesis that involves
057   processes in deep brain regions or spatially disparate, bilateral areas (16; 17; 18; 3). Recent progress
058   has also validated the feasibility and effectiveness of sEEG-based speech synthesis (19; 20; 21)
059
      However, a major challenge is that iEEG signals are typically only available in clinical settings,
060
      limiting data collection due to the clinical environment and the underlying pathological conditions of
061   participants. Thus, publicly available datasets, especially sEEG-speech parallel data, are extremely
062   rare. This motivates the need for high-quality sEEG-speech parallel datasets.
063
064
      The contributions of this work are as follows. we introduce the CerebroVoice dataset, the first publicly
      accessible sEEG recordings curated for bilingual brain-to-speech synthesis. The dataset includes
065
      sEEG signals recorded while speakers read Mandarin Chinese words, English words, and Mandarin
066
      Chinese digits. We establish benchmarks for two tasks: speech synthesis and VAD. For speech synthe-
067   sis, we propose the Mixture of Bilingual Synergy Experts (MoBSE) framework, which dynamically
068   organizes low-rank expert weights for more effective language-specific decoding. MoBSE shows
069   significant performance improvements over current state-of-the-art methods, producing more natural
070   and intelligible speech. For VAD, we reproduce three classic EEG-based architectures and pro-
071   vide comprehensive evaluation metrics, finding that low-frequency signals outperform high-gamma
072   activity, suggesting low-frequency filtering is better suited for VAD tasks.
073
074   2    R ELATED W ORK
075
076
      Considerable progress has been made in iEEG (sEEG and ECoG) based brain-to-speech synthesis
077
      in recent years. Martin et al. (22) decoded spectro-temporal features of speech from brain activity
078   using ECoG, and Mugler et al. (23) further demonstrated that the full set of American English
079   phonemes can be decoded from ECoG. In (11), Moses et al. explored real-time decoding of perceived
080   and produced speech from high-density ECoG activity during a question-and-answer dialogue task.
081   Angrick et al. (24) explored the use of deep neural networks (3D convolutional neural networks) for
082   reconstructing speech from ECoG recordings. Moses et al. (4) investigated the long-term stability of
083   ECoG recording and its performance in decoding speech over an extensive 81-week recording period
084   in a paralyzed patient with anarthria.
085   More recently, Metzger et al. (6) have further improved the performance of speech decoding using
086   ECoG collected over 13 days. Building on this study, Feng et al. (25) further conducted similar work
087   in Mandarin Chinese. Despite much progress, the datasets for these studies are not publicly available.
088   The absence of publicly released datasets hinders reproducibility and collaborative research efforts in
089   brain-to-speech synthesis.
090   Similarly, publicly available sEEG-speech datasets remain scarce, as summarized in Table 1. Angrick
091   et al. (8) released a 15-minute sEEG-speech dataset from one single Dutch-speaking epilepsy patient,
092   while Kohler et al. (26) published a similar dataset of three epilepsy patients, with 10 to 20 minutes
093   each. Verwoert et al. (13) also released a dataset of 10 Dutch-speaking epilepsy patients, however,
094   each only contributed 5 minutes of data. The above sEEG data are not adequate for machine learning
095   studies. To address this, a recent dataset release offers 3 hours of sEEG-speech data per subject (27).
096   However, most prior brain-to-speech synthesis research has focused on monolingual tasks, with little
097   exploration of bilingual speakers. The development of an iEEG-based encoder for bilingual speech
098
      synthesis is highly desirable (28). This gap underscores the necessity of an sEEG dataset specifically
      designed for bilingual speech synthesis.
099
100   Additionally, there is limited research on VAD using sEEG, with no publicly available datasets
101   specifically tailored for this task (29; 30). Consequently, we established a benchmark and compared
102   three classical baseline models to evaluate their performance.
103   Addressing the research need, we propose a CerebroVoice dataset, comprising sEEG recordings
104   captured when the participant read aloud Mandarin Chinese words, English words, and Mandarin
105   Chinese digits. Two patients, both implanted with depth electrodes to identify epileptic foci and
106   plan potential resections, were recruited for this study. As shown in Table 1, each participant’s data
107   recording duration was about 75 minutes. This CerebroVoice dataset represents the first bilingual
      iEEG-speech dataset encompassing both tonal (Mandarin Chinese) and non-tonal (English) languages.

122   3.1      PARTICIPANTS
123
124   Two patients with epilepsy undergoing neurosurgical treatment were enrolled as the listening and
125
      speaking subjects in the data collection. They are referred to as the participants. One participant
      (Subject 1) was a 25-year-old male native Mandarin Chinese speaker with basic English conversation
126
      skills. The other participant (Subject 2) was a 30-year-old female native Mandarin Chinese speaker
127
      with limited English proficiency.
128
129   The study was conducted in accordance with the principles embodied in the Declaration of Helsinki
130   and approved by the Ethics Committee of the South China Hospital of Shenzhen University
131
      (HNLS20231229003-A). Both patients gave written informed consent to participate in the study.
      Data collection was conducted under the supervision of experienced doctors to ensure the comfort
132
      and safety of the participants. During the recording process, patients were required not to enter any
133
      personal identification information. Therefore, this dataset does not contain the identity information
134   of actual users.

      3.2      N EURAL R ECORDINGS
137
138   Both participants were implanted with sEEG electrode shafts to identify epileptogenic foci and all
139   the locations of sEEG electrodes were determined based on each patient’s specific epilepsy treatment
140   plan. 13 electrode shafts were implanted in each subject. Each shaft contains 8-16 electrode contacts,
141   resulting in a total of 176 and 185 electrode contacts for Subjects 1 and 2, respectively. To accurately
142   determine the positions of contacts, we used an open-source MATLAB package LeGUI (31), in
143   which the processing is performed based on Statistical Parametric Mapping toolbox (SPM12) (32).
144
      Fig in appendix illustrates three views of the depth electrode locations for each participant, where
      dots of the same color represent electrodes belonging to the same shaft. Notably, all electrodes in
145
      Subject 1 were implanted within the right hemisphere, while those in Subject 2 were located in the
146
      left hemisphere.

      3.3      DATA ACQUISITION
149
150   The participants underwent implantation of platinum-iridium sEEG electrode shafts (Sinovation (Bei-
151   jing) Medical Technology SDE-10/12/16, China), featuring a diameter of 0.8 mm and an inter-contact
152   distance of 3.5 mm. Each electrode shaft contained between 10 and 16 electrode contacts. Notably,
153   the placement of all electrodes was determined based on the patients’ therapeutic requirements. sEEG
154   signals were recorded at a sampling rate of 1000 Hz or 500 Hz (Nihon Kohden EEG 1200, Tokyo,
155   Japan), and auditory data was simultaneously collected. Specifically, audio recordings were captured
156   with a JABRA speakerphone using OBS Studio software at 48 kHz.
157   As depicted in Fig. 1, a computer was placed in front of the participants, serving as the control
158   center. It delivered the audio stimuli via a speaker, and recorded the participant’s speech. During
159   recording, the computer screen shows a blank screen so as not to distract the participants. Both the
160   participants’ sEEG signals and audio signals were recorded. To ensure synchronization between the
161   auditory stimuli and sEEG responses, we employed a Python-scripted tool to play audio stimuli and
      simultaneously mark the corresponding sEEG responses.

179   3.4   E XPERIMENT P ROTOCOL
180
      During our experiment, participants were presented with auditory stimuli across three different
181
      categories: 30 categories of Mandarin Chinese words, 10 categories of Mandarin Chinese digits
182   (1-10), and 10 categories of English words. The duration designated for listening and repeating
183   was set at 5 seconds for both Mandarin Chinese and English words, while for Mandarin Chinese
184   digits, it was set to 4 seconds. Each participant completed 8 rounds of experiments, with each round
185   consisting of 30 English words, 60 Mandarin Chinese digits, and 110 Mandarin Chinese words. At
186   the beginning of each round, a participant is given a 5-second interval to get ready, where a prompt
187   "Please listen to the audio attentively and repeat loudly what you will hear" is played, that is followed
188   by a “ding" sound to signal the start of the attended speech content. After each word was played, the
189   participants were expected to recite the speech content within 1.5 seconds, and then remain relaxed
190
      until the next "ding" sounded.
191   To avoid fatigue, the participants took a 5 to 10-minute break between two rounds. Additionally,
192   several familiarization trials were conducted to ensure that the subjects understood the experimen-
193   tal procedures before recording. Following data collection, we assessed the quality of the audio
194   recordings, manually removing recordings with mispronunciations or pauses. First, we employed
195   a pre-trained Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) model to transcribe the speech into text. We
196
      then compared this transcription with the ground truth text to calculate the Word Error Rate (WER).
      For samples where the WER was not 100%, a manual review was conducted to determine whether
197
      discrepancies were due to reading errors or ASR system inaccuracies. As a result, the CerebroVoice
198
      dataset comprises 72.94 minutes of data for Subject 1 and 76.49 minutes for Subject 2.

202   4.1   DATA L OADING
203
204   The CerebroVoice dataset is publicly available for research use (https://zenodo.org/
205
      records/13332808). To simplify the use of the data, we have preprocessed the sEEG sig-

      4.2   N EURAL S IGNAL P REPROCESSING
210
211   First, we excluded electrodes identified in epileptologists’ reports as showing abnormal epileptiform
212   discharges (33). Specifically, 62 electrodes were removed from Subject 1 (114 left) and 27 electrodes
213   from Subject 2 (158 left). Subsequently, bipolar referencing was applied to the remaining sEEG
214   signals (27). Previous studies have highlighted the critical role of high-gamma frequency (HGA) and
215   low-frequency signal (LFS) features in synthesizing speech from brain signals (8; 34; 6). Accordingly,
      we followed the preprocessing methods used in previous research to extract the LFS and HGA


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      frequency bands (6). Additionally, we tested broadband signals (BBS), which combine both LFS
217   and HGA sEEG features, to provide a comprehensive perspective and evaluate their combined
218   contributions to speech synthesis performance. Specifically, to compute HGA, we first band-passed
219   the signals in the high-gamma frequency range (70–150 Hz), then calculated the analytic amplitude of
220   these signals, and finally downsampled them to 200 Hz. For LFS, we applied a low-pass anti-aliasing
221   filter with a cutoff frequency of 100 Hz before downsampling the signals to 200 Hz. Lastly, we
222   normalized the extracted HGA and LFS signals from each sEEG electrode within each 1.5-second
223   window.
224
225
      4.3     AUDIO S IGNAL P REPROCESSING
226
227
      We used LibROSA, a commonly adopted Python library for audio processing (35), to downsample
228
      the audio signals to 16 kHz and extract the mel-spectrograms. To capture the temporal dynamics of
229   the audio signal, a window length of 64 milliseconds and a hop length of 20 milliseconds were set.
230   Additionally, we set the number of bins in the mel-spectrogram to 80, aiming to capture sufficiently
231   detailed frequency information to describe the participants’ speech signals (36).
232
233
234   4.4     DATA P REPARATION FOR VOICE ACTIVITY D ETECTION
235
236   We implemented VAD using the Mel-Filter Bank and Energy-based VAD methods. The Mel-
237   Filter Bank transforms the audio signal into mel-scaled spectrograms, while the Energy-based VAD
238
      processes the log-energy of Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs) to detect speech activity.
      Key parameters include a window length of 0.064 seconds and a window shift of 0.02 seconds, which
239
      define the audio segmentation into overlapping frames.

244   5.1     BASELINE M ETHODS FOR S PEECH S YNTHESIS

246   5.1.1    BASELINE A RCHITECTURES
247
248   sEEG-based brain-to-speech study is still at its early stage. We propose an sEEG to mel-spectrogram
249   conversion model based on FastSpeech2, which is a state-of-the-art text-to-speech synthesis frame-
250   work with an encoder-decoder structure. The model architecture is shown in Fig. 2.
251   In the original FastSpeech2, text embeddings are used as input to the encoder. For our sEEG-based
252   speech synthesis task, we replaced these text embeddings with embeddings derived from sEEG
253   signals. Specifically, we transformed 1.5-second sEEG signals into a 2D data format with dimensions
254   (75, C), where 75 represents the time dimension and C represents the channel dimension. This
255   transformation is analogous to the mel-spectrogram features, which have dimensions of (75, 80) in
256   which 80 is the dimension of features and each frame lasts 0.02-second, ensuring alignment with the
257
      temporal structure of the speech.
258   The FastSpeech2-based model first maps high-dimensional sEEG signals to a lower-dimensional
259   space through an embedding layer. Subsequently, these embedded signals are further processed by
260   positional encoding to obtain the positional information of the time series. The encoder extracts deep
261   features, and the decoder decodes based on these features to ultimately output the mel-spectrogram.
262   We will elaborate the process in detail next.
263
264   5.1.2    E NCODER
265
266   The encoder is implemented in a Transformer architecture which follows that in the FastSpeech2
267   model, utilizing six feedforward Transformer (FFT) blocks (37). These FFT blocks, through the
268   self-attention mechanism and position-wise feedforward networks, enhance the model’s ability to
269   capture long-distance dependencies. Each FFT block contains a self-attention layer and a feedforward
      network layer that can effectively encode the temporal characteristics of the sEEG signal.

      The decoder is implemented with a single one-dimensional convolutional layer to directly transform

      the encoded high dimensional features into mel-spectrogram features. The final output dimension

      5.1.4          WAVEFORM D ECODER
295
296   Since the advent of WaveNet (38) in 2016, neural vocoders have played a crucial role in reconstructing
297   highly natural speech, capable of converting a mel-spectrogram frame into high quality speech
298   waveform. In this study, we used the HiFi-GAN vocoder (39), which consists of a generator and two
299   discriminators: multi-scale and multi-period discriminators. This vocoder is pretrained in advance.
300
301   5.1.5          T RAINING D ETAILS
302
303   We adopt the positional encoding scheme as in FastSpeech2. The introduction of positional encoding
304   enables the model to more effectively capture and understand the specificity of different channels
305   in the sEEG signal, as well as the temporal information at different moments of the sequence. It
306   is expected that this encoding helps distinguish the unique physiological signals carried by each
307
      channel, at the same time, identifies the characteristics of the signal as it changes over time, which is
      crucial for accurately parsing the temporal structure of sEEG signals.
308
309   In the training process, we adapted the training methodology to fit our task requirements. The Adam
310   optimizer was utilized with hyperparameters β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.98. The model was trained using
311   a batch size of 16 and a learning rate of 0.001. The L1 loss function was adopted to measure the
312   difference between the predicted and ground-truth mel-spectrograms.
313
314   5.2      M IXTURE OF B ILINGUAL S YNERGY E XPERTS
315
316   As illustrated in Figure. 2 (d), this module is designed for the mixture of bilingual synergy experts
317   within the feed-forward network (FFN) of FasterSpeech2. It is specifically tailored for the task
318   of bilingual stereo-electroencephalography (sEEG)-based speech decoding, enhancing the model’s
319   ability to process and decode bilingual information from sEEG signals.
320   The input to this module is a feature tensor x ∈ RB×T ×D , where B represents the batch size, T
321   denotes the temporal dimension, and D is the feature dimension. The features encapsulate temporal
322   sEEG information from two languages. To effectively decode the bilingual information, we employ a
323   mixture of experts framework, where each expert is specialized in extracting features specific to one
      language’s sEEG signals.


                                                                 6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      For each task label, indicating whether the decoding task is for Mandarin or English and represented
325   as a one-hot encoded vector t, we perform a linear transformation. The transformed vector is
326   fused with the input features x and passed through a linear layer, followed by global average
327   pooling (GAP) over the temporal dimension to obtain the input g for the gating network: g =
328   GAP(Linear(x + Linear(t))), where x denotes the feature tensor, t represents the one-hot encoded
329   task label, Linear signifies a linear layer, and GAP signifies global average pooling over the temporal
330   dimension.
331   The gating network, parameterized by a MLP, converts the bilingual fused features into weights w
332   for each expert: w = GatingNetwork(g). The final output y is then obtained by combining the
333                                                             PN
      weights with the outputs from each low-rank expert: y = i=1 wi · LowRankExperti (x), where
334   i represents the i-th expert in the mixture of experts framework. LowRankExpert comprises a
335   dimension reduction and an expansion linear layer.
336
      We chose to use 8 experts in the MoBSE framework based on results from ablation studies, which
337
      tested configurations with 4, 6, 8, 10, and 12 experts, the configuration with 8 experts achieved
338
      the best overall performance,striking a balance between effective language-specific decoding and
339   minimizing redundancy or overfitting.
340
341   This architecture ensures that the respective language experts can process the corresponding sEEG
342
      information with high effectiveness. The mechanism guarantees accurate decoding of bilingual sEEG
      features, leveraging the unique strengths of language-specific experts. This innovative approach
343
      significantly enhances the model’s adaptability and performance in bilingual speech decoding tasks,
344
      positioning it as a robust solution for future research and application in the field of neural decoding.

348
349   We evaluate the performance of various speech synthesis models using the CerebroVoice dataset. We
350
      employ metrics such as Pearson Correlation Coefficient (PCC) (40), Mel Cepstral Distortion (MCD)
      (40), Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) (40), and Short-Time Objective Intelligibility (STOI) (41) to
351
      assess the effectiveness of each model. Specifically, we compare BrainTalker (40), FastSpeech 2 (42),

      Shaft CNN (19), Hybrid CNN-LSTM (43), Dynamic GCN-LSTM (44), and our proposed Mixture of
353   Bilingual Synergy Experts (MoBSE) framework.

355
356   5.4   VOICE ACTIVITY D ETECTION M ETHODS FOR C OMPARISON
357
358
      We utilized three classical baseline methods for VAD: EEGNet (45), STANet (46), and EEG-
      ChannelNet (47). EEGNet is designed for EEG data, using depthwise separable convolutions
359
      to capture spatial features efficiently. STANet (Spatial-Temporal Attention Network) employs at-
360
      tention mechanisms to model spatial and temporal dependencies, improving detection robustness.
361   EEG-ChannelNet uses channel attention to selectively aggregate information from different EEG
362   channels.

521
522   In this study, the placements of sEEG electrodes were determined solely based on the patient’s
523
      clinical needs. Hence, there was significant inter-individual variability in terms of brain regions. This
      variability is undesirable because it makes it difficult to compare results across participating subjects,
524
      and generalize to new subjects. To establish the broader applicability of the findings, we are looking
525
      into scaling up the data collection effort towards a larger cohort of participating subjects.

528   8    C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we introduced CerebroVoice, the first publicly accessible sEEG dataset for bilingual
531
      brain-to-speech synthesis and VAD. Contributed by two bilingual participants, this dataset supports
      the study of how spoken languages, word categories, frequency bands, and decoding models impact
532
      decoding accuracy. We validated the dataset’s quality through benchmarks for speech synthesis
533
      and VAD tasks. Our proposed Mixture of Bilingual Synergy Experts (MoBSE) model significantly
534   outperformed the FastSpeech2 baseline in speech synthesis, producing more natural and intelligible
535   speech. For VAD, low-frequency signals proved superior to high-gamma activity, providing valuable
536   insights for brain-computer interface applications. This study offers essential data and theoretical
537   support for the research community, promoting interdisciplinary integration between neuroscience
538   and artificial intelligence. The success of sEEG-based brain-to-speech synthesis and VAD tasks not
539   only enhances our understanding of the human brain but also supports the development of innovative
      communication and diagnostic technologies.

 9   In our study, subjects were exposed to auditory stimuli from three different classifications: 30
10   categoriess of Chinese Mandarin words, 10 categoriess of Chinese Mandarin digits, and 10 categories

12   words was allocated 5 seconds, whereas for Chinese Mandarin digits, this phase lasted 4 seconds.

18   word, subjects were required to repeat the speech content within 1.5 seconds and then stay relaxed
19   until the next "ding" was heard. The data collection timeline for each round is depicted in Figure. 1.

39   2     Dataset Structure

40   Our dataset collected 3200 samples from 3 volunteers, and then reserved 3069 samples, including
41   1493 samples from the first participant and 1576 samples from the second p articipant. Our data
42   includes 27 folders. The outermost three folders are classified into BBS, HGA, and LFS to represent
43   different frequency bands. The middle three folders are classified into Chinese Mandarin, English,
44   and digits according to the type of words. It is essential to note that within each frequency band, we
45   extracted samples from the initial pool of 3069, giving us a total of 9207 distinct samples across the
46   full spectrum of frequency bands. This additional extraction process has allowed us to delve deeper
47   into the data and create a comprehensive and detailed dataset.
48   As illustrated in Figure. 3, the innermost three folders are training set, validation set, and test set. In
49   order to facilitate data users to view the basic information of each sample, we use a unified format to
50   name the files of the training set, validation set, and test set, namely roundID_wordID_wordName,
51   where round ID represents the round of experiments, word id represents the number of words read by
52   the participant in this round of experiments, and word name represents the content of the words read
53   by the participant. For ease of use, we provide the preprocessed sEEG signal and mel-spectrogram,
54   both stored in npy format. It contains the following data:

66   3   Societal Impact

67   As we point out in Section 7 of the paper, we publish a sEEG-speech dataset that is specifically
68   designed for the study of decoding speech from brain signals. The broad applicability of this dataset
69   is crucial for explaining and predicting the neural mechanisms of human language. We not only
70   confirm the quality and completeness of this dataset, but also verify the feasibility of sEEG-based
71   brain-to-speech synthesis. This brain-to-speech synthesis technology provides new research paths at
72   the intersection of neuroscience and artificial intelligence, especially in decoding spoken language,
73   vocabulary categories, frequency bands, and the influence of decoding models.

                                                                  3
74    Although our innovative research and the application of sEEG-speech datasets have demonstrated
75    their obvious advantages, we need to point out some of the negative social impacts they may have.
76    A major problem is that when not all EEG signals can be accurately decoded into understandable
77    speech, this may limit the expression of the patient’s true intentions to some extent. Medical staff
78    often need to combine the patient’s facial expressions and physiological reactions to more accurately
79    understand their true intentions.
80    In addition, this technology may have an impact on patients’ right to make their own decisions, as
81    they may feel pressured to accept the technology, even though they may have their own concerns.
82    Therefore, we are actively promoting the introduction of more relevant policies to respect and protect
83    patients’ right to choose whether to use this technology. We hope that such policies can help ensure
84    the rights and interests of every individual, while providing an important reference for the use of
85    similar technologies in the future.

97    6     Implementation Details

98    6.1   Experimental Parameter

99    In our experiments, to ensure uniformity and fairness across all experimental setups, we applied
100   identical hyperparameter configurations for all comparison tests. Each model was trained over 300
101   epochs to guarantee convergence in every experiment. Specifically, we set the batch size to 16 and
102   chose an initial learning rate of 0.0625. Utilizing the Adam optimizer with betas parameters of 0.9
103   and 0.98 allowed us to regulate the exponential moving average of both the gradient and its squared
104   form, aiming to achieve a balance between training stability and speed. Additionally, we implemented
105   a gradient clipping threshold of 1.0 to effectively mitigate the risk of gradient explosion. Additionally,
106   we implemented a warm-up strategy to stabilize the training process.

107   6.2   Evaluation Metrics

108   PCC (Pearson Correlation Coefficient) is a statistical indicator used to measure the strength and
109   direction of the linear relationship between two variables. PCC is the most commonly used metric in
110   the field of sEEG-based speech decoding[1–4]. The value range of this indicator is between -1 and 1,
111   where:

112          • If PCC is equal to 1, it means that the two variables are completely positively correlated,
113            that is, when one variable increases, the other variable also increases, and the relationship
114            between the two is linear.

                                                          4
115          • If PCC is equal to -1, it means that the two variables are completely negatively correlated,
116            that is, when one variable increases, the other variable decreases, which is also a linear
117            relationship.
118          • If PCC is equal to 0, it means that there is no linear relationship between the two variables.
