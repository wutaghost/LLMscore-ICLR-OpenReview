                                                   A BSTRACT
013
014
015
               Current video mirror detection models demonstrate satisfactory performance by
               analyzing different attributes of mirrors and incorporating temporal information.
016
               However, these models still struggle to detect mirrors in complex and dynamic
017
               scenarios. A simple yet critical visual cue is that objects reflected in a mirror appear
018            to be farther away than the mirror itself. Motivated by this observation, we propose
019            to explicitly analyze the Depth of Mirror (DOM) within a video to effectively
020            localize mirrors - DOM refers to distinct perceived distances that make mirror
021            regions appear farther away from their surroundings. Specifically, we devise a novel
022            framework called FTM-Net, which contains two main contributions: a Pattern-
023            Compensated DOM estimation strategy and a Dual-Granularity Affinity module.
024            The Pattern-Compensated DOM estimation strategy uses multiple visual mirror
025            patterns to refine the DOM, enhancing the accuracy of mirror localization in a single
026
               image. Furthermore, the Dual-Granularity Affinity module can effectively detect
               mirrors in video sequences by tracking and integrating DOM changes across frames.
027
               Experimental results on a benchmark dataset show that our model significantly
028
               outperforms 18 state-of-the-art methods in the video mirror detection task.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Mirrors are commonly found in our daily lives; however, their presence can have a significant negative
034
      impact on various applications, such as drone tracking (Chen et al., 2017b), robot navigation (Gul
035
      et al., 2019), and so on. Accurate mirror detection is crucial for avoiding potential safety issues in
036   these applications. Therefore, the development of mirror detection models is essential to precisely
037   detect mirrors and to provide critical mirror information for other tasks. As shown in Figure 1 and
038   according to optical principles (Born & Wolf, 2013; Mei et al., 2021), objects reflected in a mirror
039   appear to be farther away than the mirror itself. Motivated by this observation, we introduce a new
040   concept, the Depth of Mirror (DOM), which identifies areas of the image that appear farther away
041   from their surroundings and are likely to be mirrors. We argue that accurately analyzing DOM is
042   crucial for effective mirror detection.
043   While the existing literature has primarily focused on single-image mirror detection by exploring
044   the attributes of mirrors themselves (Lin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Yang et al., 2019), these
045   methods struggle with video mirror detection tasks due to the absence of temporal information across
046   video frames. Recently, VMD-Net (Lin et al., 2023) achieved promising video mirror detection
047   results by utilizing both intra-frame and inter-frame correspondences to model temporal information.
048   However, merely modeling temporal information without considering mirror information proves
049   to be insufficient, as mirrors reflect actual objects, complicating the localization of mirror areas in
050
      dynamic scenes. This limitation underscores the necessity for a comprehensive approach that not
      only models temporal dynamics but also integrates these with DOM changes to thoroughly address
051
      the challenges of video mirror detection.
052
053   To this end, we introduce a novel method named FTM-Net (Farther Than Mirror), which features a
      Pattern-Compensated DOM estimation strategy and a Dual-granularity Affinity module for video

092   Image Mirror Detection. Detecting mirrors in an image often works as the first step in various
093
      computer vision applications, including drone tracking and robot navigation. Numerous methods
      have been developed for mirror segmentation tasks. For example, MirrorNet (Yang et al., 2019)
094
      introduced the first mirror detection dataset and network, utilizing contextual contrasted information
095
      for accurate detection. PMDNet (Lin et al., 2020) presents a more challenging benchmark for mirror
096   detection tasks by leveraging multi-scale mirror edge features to enhance perception. More recently,
097   VCNet (Tan et al., 2022) has further improved mirror detection performance by exploiting chirality
098   cues and implicit correspondences. Mei et al. (Mei et al., 2021) propose the first RGB-D mirror
099   segmentation dataset and utilize depth information to assist in mirror detection. However, this method
100   requires additional devices to simultaneously capture the RGB image and depth map. Furthermore,
101   these methods still fall short when applied to video mirror detection, as they do not consider the
102   temporal relationships between adjacent frames.
103   Video Shadow/Saliency/Mirror Detection. Many video-based methods have been proposed to
104   explore temporal information. Video shadow detection task has been extensively studied in recent
105   years. For instance, Scotch-Soda (Liu et al., 2023c) presents a new type of trajectory attention
106   and employs a contrastive loss to help the model learn more robust representations of shadows.
107   Additionally, Li et al. (Li et al., 2019) propose a motion-guided attention mechanism by incorporating
      optical flow to enhance appearance features for video saliency detection. Despite these advancements

      In this section, we first introduce a novel Pattern-Compensated Depth of Mirror (DOM) estimation
165   strategy. Next, we provide a detailed explanation of our novel Dual-Granularity Affinity module.
166   Finally, we describe the overall workflow of our FTM-Net.

      3.1   PATTERN -C OMPENSATED DOM E STIMATION
169
170   DepthAnything for DOM Estimation. To capture the DOM, which identifies areas of the image that
171   are far from the viewer and likely to contain mirrors, we use DepthAnything as the DOM estimation
172   network. DepthAnything, including an encoder and a decoder, is used without any training process.
173   The parameters of DepthAnything are frozen and used to generate the DOM in a zero-shot manner.
174   Pattern Decoder for Enhanced DOM. Since DepthAnything is used with a frozen state during the
175   training stage, the output DOM may inadvertently include some non-mirror regions that are also
176   physically farther than the mirror itself; as shown in Case 3 of Figure 1. Therefore, relying solely on
177   DepthAnything to estimate DOM is inadequate for accurately detecting mirrors. To overcome this
178   limitation, we introduce a mirror-pattern decoder that outputs multiple potential patterns specifically
179   for mirrors. This decoder shares a similar architecture with the DepthAnything decoder but differs
180   in two key aspects. First, instead of outputting a single channel as the DepthAnything decoder,
181
      the mirror-pattern decoder outputs multiple channels, each representing a different mirror pattern.
      Second, unlike the frozen state of the DepthAnything model, this decoder is actively trained to learn
182
      these mirror patterns. The output mirror patterns are then concatenated with the initial DOM map to
183
      produce a refined DOM, enhancing mirror detection accuracy.

193   pattern-wise affinity. This module enhances the representation of temporal relationships by capturing
194   not only pixel relationships but also the broader visual patterns that define object behavior in video

      sequences, as depicted in Figure 2.

      Dataset. We evaluate our method on the VMD-D dataset, which is the first large-scale video
      mirror detection dataset consisting of 269 videos in 14,988 image frames with corresponding precise
253
      annotations from diverse scenes. We follow the same data splitting setting of (Lin et al., 2023) to
254
      divide the entire VMD-D dataset into a training set with 143 videos (7835 images) and a testing set
255   with 126 videos (7152 images). The frame rate is 30 FPS for all videos and the images in each video
256   are with a high resolution of 1920 أ— 1080.
257
258
      Evaluation Metrics. Following previous works (Lin et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2020), we
      adopt intersection over union (IoU), pixel accuracy (Accuracy), F-measure (Fخ² ), and mean absolute
259
      error (MAE) to evaluate our method.
260
261   Implementation Details. Our model is implemented in PyTorch 2.0.1-cuda11.7 and trained on four
262   NVIDIA 4090 GPUs (24G memory for each one) with a batch size of 8. The segmentation model
263   used in our method is SegFormer (Xie et al., 2021), which is initialized using the weights from the
264
      Mit-B2 model pre-trained on the ADE20K dataset (Zhou et al., 2017; 2019). We use pre-trained
      DepthAnything-S (Yang et al., 2024) as our depth estimation network. The remaining parameters
265
      are initialized using the Xavier (Glorot & Bengio, 2010) method. During training, we resize all
266
      video frames to 512 أ— 512 and use a random horizontal flip for data augmentation. We use an
267   Adam (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) optimizer along with a poly learning rate scheduler (an initial
268   learning rate of 1e-3 and a weight decay of 3e-5) and run a total of 15 epochs for all experiments and
269   ablation studies. For inference, we do not apply any post-processing techniques and only resize the
      resolution of input frames to 512 أ— 512.
