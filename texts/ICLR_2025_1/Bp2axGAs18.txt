009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-agent systems, powered by large language models, have shown great abil-
012            ities across various tasks due to the collaboration of expert agents, each focusing
013            on a specific domain. However, when agents are deployed separately, there is a
014            risk that malicious users may introduce malicious agents who generate incorrect
015            or irrelevant results that are too stealthy to be identified by other non-specialized
016            agents. Therefore, this paper investigates two essential questions: (1) What is the
017            resilience of various multi-agent system structures (e.g., A→B→C, A↔B↔C)
018
               under malicious agents, on different downstream tasks? (2) How can we increase
               system resilience to defend against malicious agents? To simulate malicious
019
               agents, we devise two methods, AUTOT RANSFORM and AUTO I NJECT, to trans-
020
               form any agent into a malicious one while preserving its functional integrity. We
021            run comprehensive experiments on four downstream multi-agent systems tasks,
022            namely code generation, math problems, translation, and text evaluation. Results
023            suggest that the “hierarchical” multi-agent structure, i.e., A→(B↔C), exhibits su-
024            perior resilience with the lowest performance drop of 23.6%, compared to 46.4%
025            and 49.8% of other two structures. Additionally, we show the promise of im-
026            proving multi-agent system resilience by demonstrating that two defense meth-
027            ods, introducing a mechanism for each agent to challenge others’ outputs, or an
028            additional agent to review and correct messages, can enhance system resilience.
029
               Our code and data are available in the supplementary materials and will be made
               publicly available upon publication.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Multi-agent collaboration has further boosted Large Language Models’ (LLMs) already impressive
035
      performance across various downstream tasks, including code generation (Liu et al., 2023; Lee et al.,
036   2024), math problem solving (Lu et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024), and text translation (Jiao et al.,
037   2023; Wu et al., 2024). In such multi-agent systems, improvements are achieved by decomposing
038   complex tasks into smaller, specialized sub-tasks handled by expert agents in a role-specific man-
039   ner (Chen et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024).
040
      However, the decentralized nature of multi-agent systems leaves them vulnerable to faulty or mali-
041
      cious agents, which could undermine or destroy collaboration. Consider a scenario where companies
042   specializing in different areas produce expert agents, the lack of centralized control means that the
043   multi-agent system may contain agents from various sources, some of which could be faulty or ma-
044   licious. In a multi-agent coding system like Camel (Li et al., 2023), a malicious coding agent could
045   produce buggy code, causing severe errors or harmful outputs when executed by another agent.
046
      This paper studies the resilience of multi-agent systems against malicious agents, specifically their
047
      ability to recover from errors. Our focus extends beyond the transformation of agents into malicious
048   ones for various tasks to their macro-level impact on collaborative dynamics, particularly how their
049   presence leads to an overall performance decline in different systems. Recent studies (Zhang et al.,
050   2024; Tian et al., 2023; Amayuelas et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2024) have increasingly focused on safety
051   issues within multi-agent systems. However, these studies mainly investigate attacks on agents
052   to induce toxicity in their outputs or misinformation spread among all agents. While they assess
053   malicious agent behavior against safety benchmarks like AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023), they overlook
      the disruption of collaboration in solving general tasks and the impact of varying system structures.

072   To simulate the behaviors of malicious agents to study system resilience, we devise two approaches
073   to corrupting benign ones, namely AUTOT RANSFORM and AUTO I NJECT. AUTOT RANSFORM
074   transforms a given agent’s profile into a malicious version that retains original functionalities while
075   introducing stealthy errors. AUTO I NJECT is designed to directly and automatically inject errors into
076   messages spread among agents. The two methods offer automate introduction of errors in multi-
077
      agent systems without requiring manual modifications.
078   To study the impact of different structures on system resilience, we select six multi-agent collabo-
079   ration systems representing three classical real-world structures: Linear (Hong et al., 2024; Dong
080   et al., 2024), Flat (Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024c), and Hierarchical (Chen et al., 2024; Liang
081   et al., 2024). We evaluate the performance of these systems across four tasks: code generation (Chen
082   et al., 2021), math problem solving (Liang et al., 2024), translation (He et al., 2020), and text evalu-
083
      ation (Wang et al., 2024a), as shown in Fig. 1. In our results, we find that the hierarchical structure
      exhibits the least performance degradation at 23.6%, aligning with its prevalence in human societal
084
      organizational structures (Mihm et al., 2010). Code generation, as a relatively objective task, is most
085
      affected by malicious agents, experiencing a performance drop of 39.6%. Additionally, increasing
086   the ratio of erroneous messages and using semantic errors results in a greater performance drop than
087   increasing the number of errors per message and using syntactic errors. We also analyze the impact
088   of roles and rounds on system resilience, which appears minimal.
089
      As an initial investigation into approaches for enhancing system resilience and defending against
090
      malicious agents, we introduce two strategies, each related to a specific error introduction method.
091
      The “Challenger” method adds to each agent’s profile the ability to challenge received messages,
092   mirroring AUTOT RANSFORM which rewrites agents’ profiles to make them malicious. The “In-
093   spector” agent reviews and corrects messages, mirroring AUTO I NJECT which intercepts and injects
094   errors into messages. We apply these defense methods to the two weaker systems: Self-collab with a
095   linear structure and Camel with a flat structure. Our results demonstrate that both methods enhance
096   system resilience, recovering up to 87.9% of performance lost due to malicious agents.
097
      The contribution of this paper can be summarized as follows:
098
099
100
      • We explore the under-explored scenario where malicious agents exist and disrupt the collabora-
        tion, and are the first to examine how different structures of multi-agent systems affect resilience.
101
102
103   • We implement AUTOT RANSFORM and AUTO I NJECT to automatically introduce malicious agents,
104     and design defense methods, the Inspector and the Challenger.
105
106   • We conduct extensive experiments involving six multi-agent systems across three system struc-
107     tures, applied to four common downstream tasks. Various factors that may influence resilience are
        analyzed, offering detailed insights into designing resilient multi-agent systems.

      2     P RELIMINARIES
109
110
      A Management Science Perspective on Multi-Agent Systems Humans have developed various
111
      modes of collaboration due to their social nature (Yang & Zhang, 2019; Alexy, 2022), which also
112   influences how different studies design the structures of multi-agent systems. In this paper, we select
113   three categories originating from management science: (1) Linear (Yang & Zhang, 2019): Agents
114   engage in one-way communication, e.g., A→B→C. (2) Flat (Alexy, 2022): Agents exclusively
115   use mutual communication, e.g., A↔B↔C. (3) Hierarchical (Mihm et al., 2010): This system
116   incorporates both one-way and mutual communications, e.g., A→(B↔C), distinguishing it from (1)
117   which is a purely linear model. These structures align with Zhang et al. (2024)’s categorization of
118   Hierarchical, Joint, and Hierarchical + Joint, based on agent interactions. An introduction to various
119   multi-agent systems is provided in §6.
120
121   System Resilience In human collaboration, the capacity to mitigate mistakes or intentional dis-
122   ruptions within a team and maintain functionality despite individual failures is usually referred to as
123
      “resilience” (Alliger et al., 2015; Boin & Van Eeten, 2013; Hartwig et al., 2020). Resilience reflects
      the ability to handle internal errors, maintaining overall operation without being affected by a single
124
      failure. LLM-based multi-agent systems face safety issues where malicious agents produce errors
125
      too stealthy to be found by other agents but can cause undesired consequences. Therefore, holding
126   this same ability as human collaboration becomes critical.
127
128
129   3     M ETHODOLOGY: I NTRODUCING E RRORS
130
131   We offer two methods for introducing errors in multi-agent systems: AUTOT RANSFORM converts
132   agents into malicious entities that generate errors autonomously, while AUTO I NJECT directly in-
133   troduces errors into messages. In this section, we first discuss the rationale behind designing the
134
      autonomous transformation agent in §3.1. Next, we introduce the method for directly injecting
      errors into messages within multi-agent systems in §3.2. These two methods are designed to be
135
      general-purpose, applicable to any agent profiles and downstream tasks. For presentation clarity,
136
      we use “message” to refer to intermediate outputs between agents, and “result” to denote the final
137   output from the last agent.
138
139
      3.1   AUTOT RANSFORM : M ALICIOUS AGENT T RANSFORMATION
140
141   AUTOT RANSFORM is an LLM-based approach that takes any agent’s profile as input and outputs a
142   profile of a malicious agent performing the same functions but introducing stealthy errors. Drawing
143   inspiration from how we manually convert an agent into malicious one, the design of AUTOT RANS -
144   FORM follows three key steps: (1) To ensure applicability to any target agent and downstream tasks,
145   AUTOT RANSFORM first analyzes the input agent profile and extract the assigned task. This step
146   helps to extract the task and identify potential ways to produce erroneous outputs. (2) Based on the
147   task analysis, AUTOT RANSFORM lists all possible methods to inject errors, emphasizing the need
148   for stealth to avoid detection by other agents. (3) AUTOT RANSFORM then rewrites the agent’s pro-
149
      file with these error-injection methods, ensuring that the original functionalities of the agent remain
      unchanged. An example of using AUTOT RANSFORM to alter an agent’s profile is shown in Fig. 2c.
150
      The complete prompt is provided in §B.3 in the appendix.

      3.2   AUTO I NJECT : D IRECT E RROR I NJECTION
154
      While AUTOT RANSFORM can conveniently generate malicious agents, it is hard to ensure these
155
      agents introduce a specific number and type of errors due to the inherent randomness of the gen-
156   eration process. For example, “injecting syntax errors in 20% lines of the generated code” cannot
157   be guaranteed by the malicious agents. However, precise error generation is crucial for analyzing
158   the impact of various factors on system resilience. To address this, we introduce AUTO I NJECT, an
159   approach that takes the outputs of other agents and intentionally injects specific errors. This ap-
160   proach allows for exact control over the proportion of erroneous messages, the specific errors within
161   a message, and the types of errors introduced. We start by discussing two key factors in our study:
      error rate and error type.

197   Error Rate In this paper, we examine two aspects of error injection in multi-agent collaboration
198   systems: Macro Perspective: We control the ratio of erroneous messages produced by a malicious
199   agent in all its messages, which is a practical way to obscure its identity while facilitating stealthy
200   errors. We denote this probability that a message is intentionally flawed as Pm . Micro Perspective:
201   We manage the degree of error within each faulty message. For instance, in code generation tasks,
202
      we can adjust the number of errors per line of code. The proportion of a message that is erroneous
      is denoted by Pe .
203
204
      Error Type In tasks that demand formality, rigor, and logic, such as code generation, two types
205
      of errors can be identified. Syntactic Errors include mistakes that violate logical or factual cor-
206
      rectness within a given context. Semantic Errors pertain to issues that, while logically sound and
207   syntactically correct, are either irrelevant or fail to accurately execute the intended instruction.
208
209   AUTO I NJECT is designed for specific tasks and agents. First, we assign the task, agent, error rates
210
      (Pm and Pe ), and error type. AUTO I NJECT then selects messages1 from the agent with a probability
      of Pm and injects errors into Pe of the total lines or sentences in the selected message. Errors are
211
      introduced automatically using LLMs, which receive the task introduction, error type, and the spe-
212
      cific line or sentence. The LLMs produce erroneous lines or sentences, which replace the originals.
213   An example of using AUTO I NJECT to modify an agent’s output into erroneous is shown in Fig. 2d.
214   Prompts for different tasks are detailed in §B.4 the appendix.

227
228   Downstream Tasks We evaluate general-purpose task-solving abilities using common tasks:
229
      • Code Generation: HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021) contains 164 hand-written programming
230
        problems to assess LLMs’ ability to synthesize correct and functional Python code. Accuracy
231     (Pass@1) is used for evaluation.
232
      • Math Problem Solving: CIAR (Liang et al., 2024) presents 50 questions with hidden traps to
233
        evaluate LLMs’ Counter-Intuitive Arithmetic Reasoning abilities, requiring multi-step reasoning.
234
        Accuracy is used for evaluation.
235
236
      • Translation: CommonMT (He et al., 2020) consists of paired sentences to test models’ handling
        of three types of commonsense reasoning, especially in ambiguous contexts. We randomly sam-
237
        pled 100 sentences from the most challenging type, Lexical, for our evaluation, using BLEURT-
238
        20 (Sellam et al., 2020; Pu et al., 2021) for evaluation, following the practice in Liang et al. (2024).
239
240
      • Text Evaluation: FairEval (Wang et al., 2024a) includes 80 human-annotated “win/tie/lose” out-
        comes comparing responses from ChatGPT and Vicuna-13B, aiming to determine if the model’s
241
        preferences align with human judgments. Accuracy is used for evaluation.
242
243
      Multi-Agent Systems We consider three types of system architectures mentioned in §2:
244
245   • Linear: MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2024) employs Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) to create
246     an efficient workflow in a software company setting, utilizing five agents for code generation.
247     Self-collaboration (Dong et al., 2024) designs three roles, namely analyzers, coders, and testers,
248     implemented using 2-5 agents on code generation task.
249   • Flat: Camel (Li et al., 2023) presents a framework where a “User” agent iteratively refines outputs
250     from an “Assistant” agent, applicable across various tasks. SPP (Wang et al., 2024c) uses Solo-
251     Performance-Prompting to engage a single model into three personas for coding tasks.
252   • Hierarchical: MAD (Liang et al., 2024) introduces a Multi-Agent Debate framework with two de-
253     baters and one judge to promote divergent thinking in LLMs for various tasks. AgentVerse (Chen
254     et al., 2024) employs a dynamic recruitment process, selecting agents for multi-round collabora-
255     tion as needed, utilizing four agents for our selected tasks.
256
257   Not all systems are designed to support the four tasks studied in this paper. Therefore, we modified
258   the prompts of some systems to adapt to our selected tasks. The modified prompts are detailed
259
      in §B.1 of the Appendix. We use GPT-3.5 and GPT-4o as the backbone with a temperature of
      zero for main experiments (RQ1 and RQ2) while using GPT-3.5 for the remaining. Our findings
260
      are consistent with the GPT-4o results; see §A.1 in the appendix for details, as they are omitted
261
      here due to space constraints. We introduce one malicious agent at a time to avoid interference
262   and facilitate essential analysis. Non-malicious agents remain unaware of the malicious agent’s
263   presence, reflecting a realistic information-asymmetric scenario (Zhou et al., 2024a).

267   The hierarchical structure has a higher resilience than other two, exhibiting the smallest accu-
268   racy drop. Fig. 3a illustrates the impact of AUTOT RANSFORM and AUTO I NJECT on various multi-
269   agent system types across different downstream tasks. System resilience, ranked from strongest to
      weakest, is: hierarchical, flat, and linear. The hierarchical architecture experiences relative accuracy

      drops of 23.6% and 22.6% for AUTOT RANSFORM and AUTO I NJECT, respectively. We attribute
271   this resilience to the presence of a higher-level agent (e.g., the evaluator in MAD), which is always
272   presented with various versions of the answer by multiple agents performing the same sub-task,
273   increasing the likelihood of error recovery from a single agent. The flat structure shows similar

283   weakness where they become less effective as the context lengthens, especially where conflict exists
284   in instructions. For our malicious agents, they gradually lose track of the task to produce errors,
285
      prioritizing new instructions from other agents to correct errors in the message. (2) AUTO I NJECT
      consistently introduces errors, whereas AUTOT RANSFORM does not always ensure error genera-
286
      tion. Despite being transformed into malicious agents, they sometimes fail to generate errors due to

301   4.3     RQ2: I MPACT OF D OWNSTREAM TASKS
302
303
      Tasks requiring rigor and formalization, such as code generation and math, are more sensitive
      to agent errors and exhibit lower resilience compared to translation and text evaluation. Code
304
      generation and math demand greater objectivity than the more subjective tasks of translation and
305
      text evaluation. Fig. 3b illustrates the impact of AUTOT RANSFORM and AUTO I NJECT across dif-
306   ferent downstream tasks. We also present the performance of single-agent using GPT-3.5 with the
307   prompts listed in §B.2, for a clearer comparison. The results indicate several conclusions: (1) Multi-
308   agent systems can outperform single-agent settings, but their performance may decline to similar or
309   worse levels when affected by malicious agents. (2) Objective tasks benefit more from multi-agent
310   collaboration, while subjective tasks gain less. Additionally, errors in subjective tasks are often
311   overlooked by other agents due to the lack of rigorous correctness standards. (3) In terms of system
312   resilience, tasks ranked from least to most vulnerable are: code generation, math, translation, and
313   text evaluation. Even minor errors in the first two tasks, particularly in code generation, significantly
314
      affect rigor and formalization. Conversely, the latter two tasks are less sensitive to minor variations
      in a single agent’s output. (4) AUTOT RANSFORM and AUTO I NJECT perform similarly across most
315
      tasks, except in code generation.
316
317   Injecting errors can surprisingly improve performance on downstream tasks. We find that cer-
318   tain multi-agent collaboration systems, such as MAD, Camel, and AgentVerse, benefit from delib-
319   erately injected errors rather than being hindered by them. Fig. 4 shows the performance changes of
320
      MAD with AUTO I NJECT. Additionally, Camel’s text evaluation performance increases from 43.8%
      to 49.5%, while AgentVerse’s translation performance also rises from 43.8% to 49.5%.
321

      4.5     RQ4: I MPACT OF E RROR T YPES
371
      Semantic errors cause a greater performance drop than syntactic errors. Fig. 5b presents the
372   performance decline caused by syntactic and semantic errors across six systems, including the aver-
373   age. Most systems handle syntactic errors more effectively than semantic errors. This likely stems
374   from LLMs excelling at identifying syntactic errors due to their extensive training on code corpora,
375   where such errors differ from the training data distribution. In contrast, semantic errors resemble

423

456   5        I MPROVING S YSTEM R ESILIENCE
457
458   Based on our experimental observations and findings, we propose two strategies for improving re-
459   silience in multi-agent collaboration systems, defending against malicious agents.
460
461   Defense Methods The core idea behind our defense methods involves adding a correction mech-
462   anism within the system. We explore two variants, the “Challenger” and the “Inspector.” The
463   “Challenger,” akin to our AUTOT RANSFORM, is an additional description of functionalities added
464   in agent profiles. This method addresses the limitation that many agents can only execute assigned
465   tasks and may not address certain problems they encounter, although they usually have the knowl-
466
      edge to. By empowering agents to challenge the results of others, we enhance their problem-solving
      capabilities. This is because most current multi-agent systems use the same LLM as the backbone
467
      for all agents, indicating their underlying ability to partially solve tasks outside their specialization.
468
469   In contrast, the “Inspector,” similar to our AUTO I NJECT, is an additional agent that intercepts all
470   messages spread among agents, checks for errors, and corrects them. This method draws inspi-
471   ration from the “Police” agent in Zhang et al. (2024). Detailed prompts for the “Challenger” and
472
      “Inspector” methods can be found in §B.5 and §B.6, respectively, in the appendix.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
493
494   6.1   M ULTI -AGENT S YSTEMS
495
496   LLMs enhance multi-agent systems through their exceptional capability for role-play (Wang et al.,
497   2024b). Despite utilizing a same architecture, like GPT-3.5, distinct tasks benefit from tailored
498   in-context role-playing prompts (Min et al., 2022). Besides the six frameworks selected in this
499   study, researchers have been exploring multi-agent collaboration in downstream tasks or simulated
500   communities. ChatEval (Chan et al., 2024) is a multi-agent debate system for evaluating LLM-
501
      generated text, providing a human-like evaluation process. ChatDev (Qian et al., 2024) uses a linear
      structure of several roles to address code generation tasks. AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023) offers a generic
502
      framework for building diverse applications with multiple LLM agents. AutoAgents (Chen et al.,
503
      2023) enables dynamic generation of agents’ profiles and cooperation, evaluated on open-ended QA
504   and creative writing tasks. Zhou et al. (2023) support planning, memory, tool usage, multi-agent
505   communication, and fine-grained symbolic control for multi-agent or human-agent collaboration.
506   Additionally, there are studies simulating daily life or conversations (Park et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,
507   2024b), and multi-agent competition (Huang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2023). These
508   frameworks are not selected either because they are not task-oriented (e.g., simulated society or
509   competitions) or their system design overlaps with those chosen for this study.
510
511   6.2   S AFETY I SSUES IN M ULTI -AGENT S YSTEMS
512
513
      PsySafe (Zhang et al., 2024) is a framework that integrates attack, evaluation, and defense mecha-
      nisms using psychological manipulation involving negative personalities. EG (Evil Geniuses) (Tian
514
      et al., 2023) is an attack method that automatically generates prompts related to agents’ original
515
      roles, similar to our AUTOT RANSFORM. While PsySafe and EG are applied to different multi-agent
516   systems such as Camel and MetaGPT, they do not examine the impact of adversaries on downstream
517   tasks like code generation or translation. Agent Smith (Gu et al., 2024) showed that malicious be-
518   haviors can spread among agents, using multi-agent interaction and memory storage. Amayuelas
519   et al. (2024) investigates how an adversary in multi-agent debate can disrupt collaboration in tasks
520   including MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding) (Hendrycks et al., 2021), Truth-
521   fulQA (Lin et al., 2022), MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022), and LegalBench (Guha et al., 2023), finding
522   that the adversary’s persuasion skill is crucial for a successful attack. Ju et al. (2024) proposes a
523   two-stage attack strategy to create an adversary that spreads counterfactual and toxic knowledge
524
      in a simulated multi-agent chat environment. This method can effectively break collaboration in
      MMLU. Unlike our study, Amayuelas et al. (2024) and Ju et al. (2024) do not explore how different
525
      system architectures are affected by these adversaries.
526
527
528   7     C ONCLUSION
529
530   This paper investigates the resilience of three multi-agent collaboration systems—linear, flat, and
531
      hierarchical—against malicious agents that produce erroneous or misleading outputs. Six systems
      are selected and evaluated on four downstream tasks, including code generation, math problem solv-
532
      ing, translation, and text evaluation. We design AUTOT RANSFORM and AUTO I NJECT to introduce
533
      errors into the multi-agent collaboration. Results indicate that the hierarchical system demonstrates
534   the strongest resilience, with the lowest performance drops of 23.6% and 22.6% for the two error
535   introduction methods. However, some systems can benefit from the intentionally introduced errors,
536   further improving performance. Objective tasks, such as code generation and math, are more sig-
537   nificantly affected by errors. Additionally, the frequency of erroneous messages impacts resilience
538   more than the number of errors within a single message. Moreover, systems show greater resilience
539   to syntactic errors than to semantic errors. Finally, we recommend designing hierarchical multi-
      agent systems, which reflects a prevalent collaboration mode in real-world human society.

      All six multi-agent collaboration systems selected in this study support only some of the downstream
867   tasks in their original design. Therefore, we extend three scalable systems—Camel, MAD, and
868   AgentVerse—to adapt to all four downstream tasks. The first three systems provide a high-level,
869   non-task-oriented design for task division, while the other three systems are deeply intertwined with
870   code generation tasks. Using Camel as an example of adapting systems to other tasks: For translation
871   and math, we improve system performance by adding “step by step” instructions in prompts. For
872   instance, in translation, it correctly interprets “拉下水 (pull into water)” to its correct meaning
873   of “engaging in wrongdoing” in Chinese. In math, a single agent calculates “Average Speed=
874   (1 + 3)/2 = 1m/s,” whereas Camel’s multi-agent system correctly computes “average speed=
875
      (1 + 3)/2 = 2m/s.” The detailed instructions likely reduce the occurrence of “seemingly” correct
      answers and increase accuracy in these specific cases.

1214   C      L IMITATIONS
1215
1216   There are several limitations in this study. First, due to budget constraints, we explore only GPT-3.5
1217   and GPT-4o. Since our primary goal is to fairly evaluate different multi-agent systems’ resilience
1218   against malicious agents, we believe the results would not significantly differ from other models.
1219   The second limitation is the selection of multi-agent systems and downstream tasks, which cannot
1220   be comprehensive. We mitigate this by selecting representative systems from three well-established
1221   human collaboration modes (Yang & Zhang, 2019; Alexy, 2022; Mihm et al., 2010) and using
1222   four commonly-used datasets for benchmarking the abilities of multi-agent systems (Liang et al.,
1223
       2024; Chen et al., 2021). The final limitation concerns the analysis, where latent variables affecting
       system resilience might be unidentified. To minimize this risk, we examine system architectures,
1224
       downstream tasks, error rates, error types, agent roles, and the number of agents’ communications.
1225
       To the best of our knowledge, no additional factors influencing system resilience are found.

1228   D      E THICS S TATEMENTS AND B ROADER I MPACTS
1229
1230   The two error introduction methods developed in this study, AUTOT RANSFORM and AUTO I NJECT,
1231   could potentially pollute benign agents and result in negative social impacts. To mitigate this risk,
1232
       we have proposed effective defense mechanisms against them. We would like to emphasize that the
       goal of proposing these methodologies is to study and improve the behavior of LLM-based multi-
1233
       agent systems. We strongly oppose any malicious use of these methods to achieve negative ends.
