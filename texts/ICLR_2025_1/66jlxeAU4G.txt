009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The recently proposed Generalized Referring Expression Segmentation (GRES)
012            and Comprehension (GREC) tasks extend the traditional RES/REC paradigm by
013            incorporating multi-target and non-target scenarios. However, the existing ap-
014            proaches focus on these tasks individually, leaving the unified generalized multi-
015            task visual grounding unexplored. Moreover, current GRES methods are limited
016            to global segmentation, lacking fine-grained instance-level awareness. To address
017            these gaps, this paper introduces a novel Instance-aware Generalized multi-task
018
               Visual Grounding (IGVG) framework. IGVG is the first to integrate GREC and
               GRES, establishing a consistent correspondence between detection and segmenta-
019
               tion via query guidance. Additionally, IGVG introduces instance-level awareness,
020
               enabling precise and fine-grained instance recognition. Furthermore, we present a
021            Point-guided Instance-aware Perception Head (PIPH), which employs attention-
022            based query generation to identify coarse reference points. These points guide
023            the correspondence between queries, objects, and instances, enhancing the direc-
024            tivity and interpretability of the queries. Experimental results on the gRefCOCO
025            (GREC/GRES), Ref-ZOM, and R-RefCOCO/+/g benchmarks demonstrate that
026            IGVG outperforms state-of-the-art methods.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Classic Referring Expression Perception (REP) tasks primarily include Referring Expression Com-
032   prehension (REC) (Yu et al., 2018a; Yang et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2023) and Referring Expression
033
      Segmentation (RES) (Liu et al., 2023e; Shang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). The goal of REC
      is to locate a specific target based on textual descriptions, while RES aims to achieve more fine-
034
      grained, pixel-level localization. Both REC and RES share a common characteristic, i.e., one-to-one
035
      correspondence between textual description and target object. Recently, the generalized REP tasks,
036   such as Generalized REC (GREC) (He et al., 2023) and RES (GRES) (Liu et al., 2023a), have been
037   proposed to extend the applicability of classic methods by involving multiple/non-target scenarios.
038
039
      Furthermore, numerous studies have focused on multi-task learning, which aims to tackle detection
      and segmentation with a unified architecture. MCN (Luo et al., 2020) is one of the first approaches to
040
      combine REC and RES while investigating consistency constraints. Subsequent works (Li & Sigal,
041
      2021; Zhu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023d) have primarily concentrated on harnessing the comple-
042   mentary strengths across multiple tasks. However, the effectiveness of joint multi-task learning in
043   generalized scenarios has yet to be explored and validated. In this paper, we heuristically construct a
044   generalized multi-task visual grounding framework that simplifies task complexity while achieving
045   complementary predictions. As illustrated in Fig. 1(c), we adopt a straightforward architecture in
046   which two independent decoders for detection and segmentation are jointly trained to build a bridge
047   between GREC and GRES tasks.
048   As illustrated in Fig. 1(b), most existing GRES methods (Liu et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024;
049   Xia et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024) merge all target masks into a single global mask, effectively
050   treating the task as semantic segmentation that classifies all targets as a unified foreground against the
051   background. These methods exhibit certain limitations. On the one hand, the models lack instance-
052   level perception during training, resulting in the loss of fine-grained supervision. On the other
053   hand, their predictions fail to capture instance-level awareness, yielding only coarse foreground
      masks, which are inadequate for scenarios requiring detailed instance perception. In this paper, we

104   Traditional Referring Expression Comprehension/Segmentation. In conventional REC, one sen-
105   tence corresponds to a single target bounding box. Early two-stage methods (Yu et al., 2018a;b;
106   Hong et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021) tackled this problem by first generating candi-
107   date proposals and then matching the referring expression to the proposals. Later, one-stage meth-
      ods (Zhou et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2020) adopted a dense anchor strategy to enable

108
      efficient inference. In recent years, many Transformer-based methods (Ye et al., 2022; Zhu et al.,
109   2022; Deng et al., 2021; Su et al., 2023a) have been proposed to effectively capture cross-modal
110   relationships. RES, on the other hand, is a task where one sentence corresponds to a set of pixels.
111   Classical approaches (Hu et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2020; Feng et al., 2021) typi-
112   cally rely on convolution-based operations for cross-modal fusion to generate segmentation masks.
113   To address the limitation of insufficient visual-language relation modeling in previous works, recent
114   studies (Yang et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023b;e; Kim et al., 2022) have employed ad-
115   vanced attention-based mechanisms (Vaswani et al., 2017) to enhance the multimodal interaction. In
116   particular, SimVG (Dai et al., 2024) improves referential understanding by decoupling multimodal
117
      fusion from downstream tasks to upstream pre-training. Building on this, our work focuses on gen-
      eralized scenarios and introduces an adaptively point-guided multi-task visual grounding approach.
118
119   Generalized Referring Expression Comprehension/Segmentation. Recently, to mitigate the in-
120   flexibility of REC with one-to-one pairing, ReLA (Liu et al., 2023a) introduced the GRES task,
121   which expanded the scope to include both empty-target and multiple-target scenarios. Furthermore,
122   GREC (He et al., 2023) extended GRES from segmentation to detection tasks. Similarly, DMMI (Hu
123
      et al., 2023) introduced a new benchmark and baseline for beyond-single-target segmentation, and
      RefSegformer (Wu et al., 2024) equipped transformer-based models with empty-target sentence
124
      discrimination, achieving robust segmentation performance. Nonetheless, all these methods pre-
125
      dict a global mask that combines all instances, directly overlooking the importance of fine-grained
126   instance-level information. In contrast, the proposed approach reuses instance annotations by in-
127   corporating instance-level in addition to global semantics supervision. This not only enhances the
128   model’s instance-aware capability but also ensures consistent predictions between target and in-
129   stances.
130
      Multi-Task Visual Grounding (MTVG). MTVG aims to localize and segment referring expres-
131
      sions using a single integrated model. Some Transformer-based methods (Luo et al., 2020; Li &
132   Sigal, 2021; Su et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024) have pursued more comprehensive multimodal
133   modeling approaches to improve the performance of multi-task visual grounding. SeqTR (Zhu et al.,
134   2022) and PolyFormer (Liu et al., 2023d) utilized a sequential transformer model that processes vi-
135   sual and textual data in a unified manner, sequentially refining predictions to enhance multi-task
136   visual grounding performance. Recently, LLM-based methods (Peng et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2024;
137   Xia et al., 2024; Rasheed et al., 2024) harnessed the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) to
138   enforce rule-based serialization of predictions, effectively integrating the REC and RIS tasks within
139   a unified framework. However, multi-task visual grounding in generalized scenarios remains under-
140
      explored. To bridge this gap, this paper pioneeringly presents a solution that combines GREC and
      GRES in a single framework.

182   3.1            T HE P OINT- GUIDED I NSTANCE - AWARE P ERCEPTION H EAD (PIPH)
183
184   As shown in the light blue part of Fig. 2, the core idea of PIPH is to adaptively filter prior reference
185   points through the Attention-guided Query Generation module (AQG), guiding subsequent object
186   detection and instance segmentation. This not only enhances the interpretability of the queries but
187   also achieves finer-grained perception. Specifically, an attention-based query selector is first used
188
      to select the query set Qinit ∈ RNq ×C and the initial prior point set Pr ∈ RNq ×2 . These are then
      passed through a deformable DETR decoder (Zhu et al., 2020) to dynamically query contextual
189
      information from multi-scale image features, generating refined queries Qd ∈ RNq ×C that capture
190
      different target contextual semantics.
191
192   Subsequently, the model computes the cost between all targets through the prior points, predicted
193   boxes, and confidence scores. The optimal assignment between queries and targets Mq2b is deter-
194
      mined using the Hungarian algorithm (Carion et al., 2020). On the one hand, the model directly
      computes the DETR object detection loss. On the other hand, Qd is multiplied with the global
195                                                                           ′   ′

196
      segmentation result Sglobal to obtain a response mask Qs ∈ RNq ×H ×W for each query within
      the global context. Using Mq2b , we pass this information to the instance mask matching process,
197
      constructing the pairing relationships Mq2i . This ensures the consistency between predicted boxes
198
      and masks for each query with respect to their corresponding targets. Last, the instance segmen-
199   tation loss, Lins−seg , supervises both the positive and negative sample masks to reinforce accurate
200   instance segmentation.

      3.1.2                                       T HE I NSTANCE - AWARE P OINT- GUIDED M ATCHER
277
278   The core idea of the matching process is to guide the query-to-target matching relationship through
279   reference points. This process can be divided into two main components: 1) Using reference points
280   to guide the matching between Qd and targets. 2) Establishing the matching relationship between
281   Qs and instances based on the correspondence between boxes and instances. This strategy ensures
282   that the target and instance associated with each query remain consistent.
283   Point-guided Target Matcher. Compared to traditional Hungarian matching in DETR, we intro-
284   duce an additional weighting term in the cost matrix that accounts for the distance between the point
285   and the center of the target box. This modification establishs a more direct association between the
286   initial reference points and the target objects. The cost is defined as:

289
290   Query-Instance Matcher. After applying the point-guided target matcher, we obtain the query-to-
291   object matching relationship Mq2b . The query-instance matcher then propagates Mq2b to establish
292   the query-to-instance matching relationship Mq2i , given the one-to-one correspondence between
293   objects and instances. In essence, Qd contains the target’s positional information, while Sglobal
294   provides global semantic information. The dot product between Qd and Sglobal effectively captures
295   the process where queries use feature similarity comparisons to generate instance-aware semantic
296   masks. Thus, Qs can be interpreted as an instance-level semantic query.

298   3.2                             T RAINING O BJECTIVES
299
300   The training objective includes four components. 1) Detection: The detection branch employs a
301   loss function Ldetr similar to DETR, incorporating the L1, Cross-Entropy, and GIoU loss functions
302   to handle the detection task. 2) Global Segmentation: This branch uses the BCE and Dice loss
303   functions, similar to those used in (Liu et al., 2023e), to quantify the difference between the ground
304   truth and predicted global masks: Mgt and Sglobal . 3) Instance Segmentation: The instance seg-
305   mentation branch also utilizes the same BCE and Dice losses as the global segmentation branch, but
306
      applies additional weighting to balance positive and negative samples. 4) The Non-Target Branch:
      This branch is responsible for binary classification and employs the BCE loss to distinguish between
307
      target and non-target regions. The total loss function is defined as:
308

      4.3.1    E FFECTIVENESS OF T HE C ORE M ODULES
401   The core issues discussed in this paper include: 1) the impact of multi-task joint learning on gen-
402   eralized visual grounding; 2) the influence of fine-grained instance-aware perception; and 3) the
403   effectiveness of the proposed Point-guided Instance-aware Perception Head. As shown in Tab. 5,
404   multi-task joint supervision positively contributes to task complementarity in generalized scenarios,
405   leading to performance improvements in both the GREC and GRES benchmarks. Specifically, the
406   F1score is increased by 1.2% and gIoU is improved by 2.3%. After incorporating an instance-level
407   segmentation branch to enable query-guided instance perception, the F1score is improved by 1.3%
408   and gIoU is increased by 3.2%. Finally, introducing point priors to guide both instance and object
409
      predictions for all queries resulted in a further improvements of 3.0% in F1 score and 2.3% in gIoU.
410
411   4.3.2    T HE P OINT- GUIDED I NSTANCE - AWARE P ERCEPTION H EAD
412
      Analysis of Attention-guided Query Generation. First, the Score Text Selector (STS) chooses Nq
413
      highly responsive tokens from the Nt tokens of Ft , thereby reducing the computational cost of multi-
414   head cross attention. As observed in Tab. 6, the introduction of STS results in almost no accuracy
415   loss. The Dist-Score Point Selector (DSPS) selects Nq prior reference points covering different
416   instances based on attention responses. In contrast, the baseline uses a strategy of selecting the Top
417   Nq points, and DSPS improves F1score by 2.1% and gIoU by 1.2%. Lastly, we designed Inject Text
418   Query (ITQ) to assist in learning the attention map. This not only helps to optimize the attention
419   map but also injects text information into the initial query, resulting in a +3.0% improvement in
420   N-acc and a +0.7% increase in gIoU.
421   Analysis of Different Decoders. As shown in Tab. 7, our baseline method uses the original
422   DETR (Carion et al., 2020) decoder. By introducing the Deformable DETR decoder, we observe an
423   improvement of 1.3% in F1score and 0.5% in gIoU. Furthermore, we design a multi-scale feature
424   map using SimFPN, and by employing a hierarchical multi-scale Deformable DETR decoder, N-acc
425   increases by 1.9%. Finally, using the points filtered by AQG as the initial reference points for the
426   Deformable DETR yields a further improvement of 0.5% in F1score and 0.9% in gIoU.

479
      This paper presents the Instance-aware Generalized Multi-task Visual Grounding (IGVG) frame-
480   work, which, for the first time, unifies the GREC and GRES tasks while exploring the feasibility
481   of instance-aware perception in GRES. Additionally, we propose a novel Point-guided Instance-
482   aware Perception Head (PIPH) that adaptively selects prior reference points through attention maps,
483   incorporating spatial priors into queries to enhance instance-specific targeting. Furthermore, by es-
484   tablishing associations between queries, objects, and instances, we achieve consistent predictions for
485   points, boxes, and masks. Lastly, our IGVG framework significantly outperforms existing methods
      across gRefCOCO (GREC/GRES), Ref-ZOM, and R-RefCOCO/+/g datasets.
