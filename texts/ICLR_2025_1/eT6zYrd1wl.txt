                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Rewiring the input graph of graph neural networks (GNNs) has been proposed as
011            a pre-processing step to address issues like over-squashing and over-smoothing.
012
               However, most existing techniques rely solely on topology-based modifications,
               neglecting performance-critical node label information. To fill this gap, we propose
013
               SoLAR (Surrogate Label Aware Rewiring), a method that rewires the graph based
014
               on predicted node labels from a surrogate model. We prove its effectiveness in
015            a theoretically tractable setting highlighting two key mechanisms that enable its
016            success. The first is a denoising effect, while the second is a novel knowledge
017            distillation-inspired process, where information from a surrogate model is encoded
018            into the graph structure. Extensive experiments demonstrate consistent improve-
019            ments of SoLAR across various datasets. Notably, the best surrogate models arise
020            from iterative SoLAR, and reusing the same model class is a competitive strategy.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025
026   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as powerful tools for analyzing and learning from
027
      graph-structured data, which is ubiquitous in real-world applications. Many complex systems can
      be naturally represented as graphs, and GNNs excel at capturing the relational information inherent
028
      in these data.We focus in this work on node classification, a fundamental task in graph learning
029
      which has numerous practical applications ranging from user profiling and interest prediction in
030   social networks (Purificato et al., 2023), warning potentially infected nodes during a pandemic (Tomy
031   et al., 2022; Burkholz & Quackenbush, 2021) to predicting protein functions within protein-protein
032   interaction networks (Jha et al., 2022), to give a few examples.
033
      Despite their success, GNNs face challenges like over-smoothing (Li et al., 2019; Oono & Suzuki,
034
      2020) and over-squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2021; Topping et al., 2022), which limit their effectiveness
035
      on complex graph structures. Several studies argue that the input graph plays a crucial role during
036   training, influencing the predictions even when the graph structure is uninformative (Bechler-Speicher
037   et al., 2024). This suggests that graph rewiring as a pre-processing step to obtain a suitable compu-
038   tational structure holds great promise, and recent studies have proposed different rewiring criteria
039   (Nguyen et al., 2023; Jamadandi et al., 2024) —mostly based on topological modifications such as
040   the spectral gap, overlooking the importance of node label information.
041
      But which edges should be rewired? According to Yang et al. (2024), GNN training dynamics tend
042   to align with the structure of the graph, suggesting that an optimal input graph would closely align
043   with the label distribution. In line with these findings, we study a theoretical setting in which GNN
044   accuracy explicitly depends on the homophily level of the learning task and thus the tendency of
045   connected nodes to share similar labels. The empirical strong correlation between homophily and
046   message-passing GNN performance (Ma et al., 2022) has also motivated other efforts to improve
047   homophily. As homophily cannot be measured without test labels, it is typically promoted during
048   training (Jiang et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2022), or substituted by similarity measures (Bi et al., 2024).
049   In this work, we therefore ask the following questions: Can a better input graph be discovered such
050   that it aligns more closely with the test labels without having access to them? Can this improve
051   performance over the original graph, even when the modifications are dictated by the original
052   predictions? We further study how a model can encode and transmit additional information by
053   modifying its input graph, so that a second model can use it to outperform the first model. Moreover,
      a similar mechanism should be effective for both homophilic and heterophilic graphs.

054
      To positively answer these questions, we propose SoLAR (Surrogate Label Aware Rewiring), a
055   method that rewires the input graph to increase the predicted homophily based on a surrogate model
056   —that is, by adding same-prediction edges and/or deleting different-label edges—, which is then used
057   as the message-passing structure for a second model. We prove that rewiring based on predicted
058   homophily can improve true homophily, and therefore accuracy. We show this to be true empirically
059   and theoretically for both homophilic and heterophilic graphs. In addition, we demonstrate that the
060   modified input graph can transfer extra information from the first model to the second one, ultimately
061   leading to improved overall performance. This could be interpreted as a novel form of knowledge
062   distillation, where information from a teacher model is encoded in the input graph of a student model.
063
      Yet, as our theory suggests, SoLAR rewiring goes beyond knowledge distillation, as the resulting
      model can learn more than the combination of the surrogate and the original model. We evaluate
064
      SoLAR on various GNN benchmarks with different model combinations, both in a one-shot approach
065
      and an iterative prediction-pruning process, and find performance boosts that align with our theory.
066
067   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
068
      1. We propose SoLAR, a predicted-label aware rewiring mechanism that leverages a surrogate
069
         GNN model’s predictions to modify the input graph of a second model. Iterative SoLAR, which
070
         alternates between model training and graph rewiring cycles, yields further performance boosts.
071
072
      2. We develop a theoretical framework to show that rewiring based on predicted homophily will
         increase the true homophily and, consequently, improve GNN accuracy. Our analysis, grounded
073
         in mean field theory, finds this holds for both homophilic and heterophilic graphs.
074
075   3. SoLAR can be interpreted as a form of knowledge distillation, where information from the
076
         surrogate (teacher) model is encoded into the input graph to enable a subsequent (student) model
         to outperform the surrogate’s performance. This mechanism offers a novel approach to information
077
         transfer between GNNs, namely through graph rewiring. Our theory highlights another mechanism
078
         that explains why, beyond knowledge distillation, the resulting GNN model can perform better
079      than a combination of the original and the surrogate model.
080
      4. We provide comprehensive experimental validation for SoLAR on a diverse set of benchmark
081
         datasets for both homophilic and heterophilic graphs. Our results show consistent improvements
082
         over existing baselines for graph rewiring.

      1.1   R ELATED WORK
085
086   Graph rewiring. Real-world graphs often contain noise or sub-optimal connections, leading to
087   challenges such as over-squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2021; Topping et al., 2022; Giovanni et al., 2023),
088   where bottlenecks cause an exponential amount of information to be squashed and potentially lost,
089   and over-smoothing (Li et al., 2019; NT & Maehara, 2019; Oono & Suzuki, 2020; Zhou et al.,
090   2021; Keriven, 2022), where nodes become more indistinguishable as the depth of the network
091   increases. Graph rewiring is regarded as a standard strategy for addressing these challenges. Our
092   work focuses on modifying the graph’s edges prior to training, which can be done using a variety
093
      of criteria. For example, some methods aim to maximize the spectral gap to improve connectivity
      (Karhadkar et al., 2023) or to maximize other measures to mitigate over-squashing (Nguyen et al.,
094
      2023). While primarily edge additions have been considered, deletions also achieve competitive
095
      results, in particular on heterophilic graphs, where nodes tend to be connected to nodes with different
096   labels (Jamadandi et al., 2024). Even though GNNs should learn to cut ties with neighbors when
097   it aids their performance, in practice they often face difficulties in doing so (Mustafa et al., 2023;
098   Mustafa & Burkholz, 2024), which explains why edge deletions can also help.
099
      Promoting homophily based on soft labels. The optimization of homophily by means of soft label
100
      predictions has been empirically explored in the context of self-training (Li et al., 2018), where the
101
      training set is repeatedly enlarged based on the confidence of the pseudo-labels and the same model is
102   trained. A variant of this approach (Nagarajan & Raghunathan, 2023) enhances the observed tendency
103   of a graph, making homophilic graphs more homophilic and heterophilic graphs more heterophilic.
104   Contrarily, our proposed approach follows a different principle: firstly, it is more general as it allows
105   for different kinds of surrogate and student models and thus proposes a novel variant of knowledge
106   distillation. Furthermore, the training data remains the same as we only use the predictions of the
107   surrogate model to modify the edge structure. Our approach can also be repeated for a flexible number
      of iterations, and promotes homophily in both homophilic and heterophilic settings.

      Knowledge distillation. Although we highlight that one of the key mechanisms for SoLAR’s effec-
124   tiveness is knowledge distillation, there are notable differences between our strategy and approaches
125   related to knowledge distillation and pre-training. We do not have a separate objective function

129   surrogate (teacher) model encodes its predictions in the input graph of another (student) model. Yet,
130   the performance-boosting mechanism of SoLAR goes beyond the information transfer or knowledge
131   distillation effect, as the resulting model can achieve a performance greater than the combination of
132
      the surrogate and the original model.

136   Basic setup and notation. In message-passing Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Gori et al., 2005;
137   Scarselli et al., 2009), nodes exchange and aggregate information from their neighbors over multiple
138   iterations, where each iteration corresponds to a graph neural network layer. This process enables
139   GNNs to learn node representations that are used to solve tasks at the node or graph level. A typical
140
      setting for node classification is transductive or semi-supervised learning, where the input graph
      —and thus all nodes and edges— remains fixed throughout training and testing. More formally, let
141
      G = (V, E) be an undirected and unweighted graph with |V| nodes and |E| edges. The adjacency
142
      matrix A ∈ R|V|×|V| encodes the graph topology. The goal in transductive node classification is to
143
      correctly classify the labels of the nodes in the test set by learning from nodes in the training set as
144   well as their neighbors, whose labels are not necessarily known (but usually their features). That
145   is, given a set of nodes Vtrain with available labels Ytrain , we need to predict the labels of nodes
146   Vtest = V\Vtrain . To solve the task, we train any message-passing GNN model that operates on
147   node features X and on the adjacency matrix A of the input graph, or, more commonly, on the degree
148   normalized adjacency matrix with added self-loops: Â = D̃−1/2 (A + I)D̃−1/2 , where D denotes a
149   diagonal matrix that carries the degrees di of nodes i ∈ V and I the identity matrix.
150
      GNN architectures. In the message-passing paradigm, each layer of the network obtains node
151
      representations as a learnt function of the previous layer’s representation and the node’s aggregated
152   neighbourhood. While several types of aggregation schemes are possible, we center our study on
153   mean aggregation models, but SoLAR applies to general GNNs. Our main baseline is the Graph
154   Convolutional Neural Network (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Chen et al., 2021), where node
155   representations in Layer l take the following form:

      SoLAR. To increase the homophily of a learning task, we propose SoLAR, which uses predictions
      made by a surrogate GNN model as proxy labels for rewiring the input graph of a second model, as
172
      illustrated in Figure 1. Specifically, the process works in three stages. In the first stage, we instantiate
173
      the surrogate GNN model fsurrogate (G, Θ) and train it to convergence obtaining a set of predicted
174   labels. In the second stage, we use the predicted labels, Ysurrogate , to rewire the graph by either
175   deleting (predicted) inter-class edges and/or adding (predicted) intra-class edges to obtain a rewired
176   graph Ĝ = (V, Ê). We use the predictions only on the test and validation sets, as we already have
177   access to the ground truth labels on the train set. In the last stage, we instantiate a second ‘training’
178   or ‘student’ GNN model ftrain (Ĝ, Θ), which operates on the rewired graph. The above outlined
179   rewiring can either be applied in a one-shot way, or iteratively, where the model from the previous
180   round becomes the surrogate model of the next round.

      3     C ONCEPTUAL ANALYSIS
184
185   At first glance, it is not apparent why SoLAR should improve the performance of a model beyond a
186
      knowledge distillation mechanism, where a surrogate model has access to information that comple-
      ments another model. If we rewired the input graph based on a surrogate model M and then retrained
187
      the same model M on the rewired graph, would we not simply enhance our original findings and,
188
      for instance, increase our certainty but not gain additional information? Against this intuition, our
189   conceptual analysis of the next sections negates this question and highlights a simple mechanism for
190   how SoLAR can still obtain performance gains in this setting. In fact, we show that M does not even
191   need to be retrained on the rewired graph. Only deleting edges according to SoLAR can improve the
192   node classification task.
193

194
      pn of the n nodes are assigned to class c = c1 and (1 − p)n to class c = c2 . For simplicity, we
195   consider 1-dimensional independently, normally distributed features as visualized in Figure 2(b), i.e.,

      3.2   M EAN FIELD ANALYSIS
274
275   The mean field limit, also known as branching process approximation, is a common tool in complex
276   network science and theoretical physics to obtain theoretically tractable insights (Dorogovtsev et al.,
277   2008; Gleeson & Cahalane, 2007; Burkholz & Schweitzer, 2018) and emerges in the large graph
278   limit of the configuration model (Molloy & Reed, 1995; Newman et al., 2001), where the graph
279   structure is characterized by a degree distribution pD (and, potentially, degree-degree correlations
280
      of connected nodes, which we do not need here). The graph neural tangent kernel (Du et al., 2019)
      can be considered as a special case of similar assumptions. Most importantly, in the limit n → ∞,
281
      the graph structure becomes locally tree-like, which allows us to treat the states of neighbors as
282
      independent, and often also results in a good approximation of sparse, finite graphs in practice.

      5     D ISCUSSION
522
      Both our theoretical analysis (§3) and extensive experiments (§4) have established that our proposed
523
      graph rewiring strategy significantly boosts GNNs. SoLAR not only distills knowledge but also
524   obtains models that can outperform the combination of the surrogate and initial model. It works
525   seamlessly for both homophilic and heterophilic settings, contrary to methods which use non-robust
526   feature similarity measures (Huang et al., 2020; Bi et al., 2024) or require expensive k-hop rewiring
527   during training (Gutteridge et al., 2023). Different from methods that rely purely on topological
528   characteristics, our approach optimizes homophily (cf. §C), a critical predictor of GNN performance.
529   However, the impact of rewiring largely depends on the quality of the surrogate. If the predicted
530   labels are too noisy, they might amplify issues that were already present in the initial model. In such
531   scenarios, it could be interesting to take features and the uncertainty of predictions into account.
532
      Furthermore, SoLAR rewiring does not explicitly address over-squashing and over-smoothing, which
      relate to problems with trainability and information propagation. Combining SoLAR with topological
533
      considerations could be another direction for future extensions.

949   C    E FFECT ON HOMOPHILY
950
951   Graph neural networks provably perform better on homophilic graphs and some good-heterophilic
952   graphs (Ma et al., 2022). We investigate the effect our one-shot rewiring strategy (GCN☼GCN)
953   has on Edge label informativeness (ELI) and adjusted homophily score proposed in (Platonov et al.,
954   2023a) and report the Normalized Mutual Information between the node ground truth labels and
955   community membership labels after performing modularity maximization (Clauset et al., 2004) on
956   the rewired graph in Figure 5. Evidently, our rewiring strategy improves the homophily score, as
957   well as the edge label informativeness (denoted by ELI), which is also found to have high correlation
958
      to GNN performance (Platonov et al., 2023a). We also better align the node ground truth labels to
      community labels, as we delete inter-community edges (denoted by NMI).
959
960   We also visualize a T-SNE plot in Figure 6 of the node embeddings after training on the original graph
961   and the rewired graph (GCN☼GCN) on Cora and Squirrel datasets. From the figure, we can see that
962   the classes are more separable in the embedding space on the rewired graph, the class separability is
963   more evident in a homophilic graph like Cora (Figure 6(b) than in a heterophilic graph like Squirrel
964
      (6(d)), highlighting the fact that GNNs are usually more useful in homophilic settings and if the
      surrogate model gives noisy labels for rewiring, the performance on the downstream is also affected.
