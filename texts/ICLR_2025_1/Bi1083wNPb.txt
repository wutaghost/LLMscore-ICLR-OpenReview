011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite their considerable success in multiple fields, studying 3D molecular struc-
014            tures of varying sizes presents a significant challenge in machine learning, particu-
015            larly in drug discovery, as existing methods often struggle to accurately capture
016
               complex geometric relationships and tend to be less effective at generalizing across
               diverse molecular environments. To address these limitations, we propose a novel
017
               Equivariant Graph Self-Attention Transformer, namely EG-SAT, which effectively
018
               leverages both geometric and relational features of molecular data while maintain-
019            ing equivariance under Euclidean transformations. This approach enables the model
020            to capture molecular geometry through higher-order representations, enhancing its
021            ability to understand intricate spatial relationships and atomic interactions. By ef-
022            fectively modeling the radial and angular distributions of neighboring atoms within
023            a specified cutoff distance using Atom-Centered Symmetry Functions (ACSFs),
024            EG-SAT leads to a more nuanced and comprehensive understanding of molecular
025            interactions. We validate our model on the QM9 and MD17 datasets, demonstrating
026            that EG-SAT achieves state-of-the-art performance in predicting most quantum
027
               mechanical properties, thus showcasing its effectiveness and robustness in this
               domain.

033
      Geometric deep learning has gained prominence as a powerful approach that leverages the inherent
      symmetries of specific learning tasks by embedding geometric priors (Satorras et al., 2021; Bronstein
034
      et al., 2021; Chmiela et al., 2018). By embedding these priors, models are endowed with a significant
035
      inductive bias, which narrows the scope of learnable functions and leads to enhanced performance.
036   Classic examples include Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (LeCun et al., 1995), which are
037   designed to maintain in each layer equivariance to translations, and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)
038   (Kipf & Welling, 2016), which are inherently invariant to node permutations. These models, by
039   exploiting symmetry groups, have been instrumental across a range of applications.
040
      Among these, GNNs have seen extensive success in modeling molecular structures, from small
041
      molecules for quantum chemistry predictions (Gilmer et al., 2017; Schütt et al., 2017) to larger
042   macromolecular structures like proteins (Ingraham et al., 2019; Fout et al., 2017). Their effectiveness
043   lies in their ability to represent molecules as graphs, where atoms are treated as nodes and the edges
044   are defined by either chemical bonds or spatial proximity. GNNs commonly capture molecular
045   geometry using rotationally invariant features such as pairwise distances to model local interactions.
046   However, while this approach has been widely adopted, it lacks the ability to encode directional
047   information, which is critical for fully capturing the spatial arrangement and interactions between
048   atoms.
049   To address these shortcomings, the inclusion of more comprehensive geometric features is necessary.
050   GNNs (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Hou et al., 2020) excel at representing atomic systems due to their
051   natural fit with 3D graph representations, where atoms are defined by their Cartesian coordinates.
052   Incorporating 3D molecular data, including bond lengths and angles, is crucial for improving model
053   performance (Schütt et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2019; Gasteiger et al., 2020b). However, purely
      rotationally invariant representations can blur distinctions between different structures, causing the

173   2.1   S YMMETRY AND G ROUP R EPRESENTATIONS
174
175
      A critical aspect of geometric graphs is their symmetry properties. These are captured by the group of
      symmetries Π, which preserves the structural relationships in the graph. Formally, an n-dimensional
176
      representation of a group Π is a mapping ρ : Π → Rn×n , where each group element π ∈ Π is
177
      associated with an invertible matrix ρ(π). This mapping satisfies the condition ρ(πω) = ρ(π)ρ(ω)
178   for all π, ω ∈ Π. When ρ(π) is orthogonal for all π ∈ Π, the representation is called orthogonal.
179
180   Group representations enable us to define two key properties:
181
             • Invariance: A function f : X → Y is Π-invariant if f (ρ(π)x) = f (x) for all π ∈ Π,
182
               meaning the output remains unchanged under the group action.
183
184
             • Equivariance: A function f : X → Y is Π-equivariant if f (ρ(π)x) = ρ(π)f (x) for all
               π ∈ Π, meaning the group action on the input results in a corresponding transformation of
185
               the output.

246   3.1   I NVARIANT G EOMETRIC GNN S
247
248   Recent advancements in geometric graph models have introduced novel methods to achieve equivari-
249
      ance, with many focusing on scalarization rather than relying solely on group representation theory.
      In scalarization, geometric vectors are first transformed into invariant scalar quantities, which are
250
      then passed through multiple layers of multilayer perceptrons (MLPs). These scalars control the
251
      magnitude of the original vectors, which are subsequently reintroduced in their original directions
252   to ensure equivariance. This method was pioneered in models like SchNet (Schütt et al., 2018) and
253   DimeNet (Gasteiger et al., 2020b), though they applied it in a purely invariant manner. Building on
254   these models, SphereNet (Liu et al., 2022) extended scalarized message passing by incorporating
255   angular and torsional information, enabling the model to differentiate molecular chirality while
256   maintaining overall invariance. Similarly, Radial Field (Köhler et al., 2020) introduced an equivariant
257   version of this scalarization approach, although it focused exclusively on geometric vectors without
258   considering node features.
259   Despite the relative simplicity of scalarization techniques, their effectiveness has been theoretically
260   supported. Villar et al. (Villar et al., 2021) demonstrated that methods based on inner products could
261   universally achieve equivariance. Expanding upon this foundation, GemNet (Klicpera et al., 2021)
262   integrated richer geometric information, such as dihedral angles, into the message-passing framework,
263   advancing the principles initially laid out by DimeNet. The key idea remains: multiplying an invariant
264   scalar by an equivariant vector still results in an equivariant vector.
265   Several additional approaches to equivariant message passing have emerged, following these scalar-
266   ization principles. For example, PaiNN (Schütt et al., 2021) and the Equivariant Transformer (Thölke
267   & De Fabritiis, 2022a) enhance the invariant SchNet by using radial basis functions to project in-
268   teratomic distances. These methods iteratively update both vector and scalar features during the
269   message-passing process. GVPGNN (Jing et al., 2020), on the other hand, offers a stronger theoretical
      framework for achieving universal equivariance in message passing.

298   3.2   I RREDUCIBLE R EPRESENTATIONS
299
300   Equivariant neural networks are designed to process geometric tensors, such as type-L vectors, to
301   maintain equivariance (Thomas et al., 2018; Miller et al., 2020; Batzner et al., 2022; Brandstetter
302   et al., 2022; Thölke & De Fabritiis, 2022b). A key technique employed is the use of geometric
303   functions derived from spherical harmonics and irreducible representations (irreps), that networks
304
      behave equivariantly in 3D Euclidean space.
305   Different approaches within this framework vary in how they implement equivariant operations,
306   particularly in their message-passing mechanisms. Both TFN (Thomas et al., 2018) and NequIP
307   (Batzner et al., 2022) use linear message-passing strategies, where equivariant message updates
308   are achieved through convolutional layers. NequIP further enhances the equivariant architecture
309   by introducing gating mechanisms that modulate the messages in a structured manner, improving
310
      model performance. In contrast, SEGNN (Brandstetter et al., 2022) deviates by employing non-
      linear message-passing mechanisms on irreducible representations, incorporating a similar gating
311
      mechanism, but extending beyond linear messages to achieve more expressive power. Another
312
      prominent architecture, the SE(3)-Transformer (Fuchs et al., 2020), builds on the dot-product attention
313   mechanism, applying it to type-L vectors in an equivariant manner. Subsequent models (Thölke &
314   De Fabritiis, 2022b; Le et al., 2022a) refined these Transformers, tailoring them specifically to work
315   with type-0 and type-1 vectors while maintaining the attention-based framework.
316
      Central to these architectures is the ability to update higher-order spherical tensor features in an
317
      equivariant manner (Thomas et al., 2018; Batatia et al., 2022; Fuchs et al., 2020; Brandstetter et al.,
318

      3.3   S ELF -ATTENTION T RANFORMERS
338
339   In a self-attention module (Vaswani, 2017), each node with associated features is mapped into a query
340   and a set of key-value pairs to produce an output. The output is calculated as a weighted sum of the
341   values, where the weights are based on the similarity between the query and the corresponding key.
342   These modules are relatively easy to implement and offer significant design flexibility (Baek et al.,
343   2021; Jumper et al., 2021), which has led to their broad application across various domains, including
344   language modeling (Vaswani, 2017; Devlin, 2018) and graph-based tasks (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020;
345   Veličković et al., 2018).
346   Recently, SE(3)- and E(3)-equivariant self-attention modules have been introduced. In (Fuchs
347   et al., 2020), constructs SE(3)-equivariant query, key, and value representations using irreducible
348   representations. In E(3)-equivariant models, the L2-norm of the 3D coordinate vector differences
349   between nodes is frequently used as an invariant feature. Some methods generate queries, keys, and
350   values from scalar features and combine the query-key similarity with invariant features through
351   addition (Maziarka et al., 2020) or multiplication (Morehead et al., 2022; Le et al., 2022b). These
352   operations enhance the model’s ability to handle geometric transformations effectively.
353   Additionally, some models adopt a more generalized self-attention approach by calculating attention
354   weights directly from both scalar features and invariant properties. This technique, as seen in works
355   such as (Satorras et al., 2021; Köhler et al., 2020; Schütt et al., 2017), provides greater flexibility
356   in capturing complex interactions between nodes, leading to improved performance in graph-based
357   tasks. By leveraging both scalar and invariant features in the attention mechanism, these models
358   can more effectively encode the geometric and topological structure of the data, resulting in more
359
      powerful representations.

361   4     E QUIVARIANT G RAPH S ELF -ATTENTION T RANSFORMER (EG-SAT)
362
363   We introduce a new class of Atom-Centered Symmetry Functions (ACSFs) (Schutt et al., 2018),
364   referred to as Attention-based Atom-Centered Symmetry Functions (AACSFs), which maintain
365   roto-translational invariance. In addition, we propose a novel Equivariant Graph Self-Attention
366   Transformer (EG-SAT). We provide a theoretical analysis of how EG-SAT can be designed to achieve
367
      equivariance, ensuring that it respects the necessary symmetry properties for accurate modeling of
      3D molecular structures. This design enables EG-SAT to effectively capture higher-order interactions
368
      and complex spatial relationships among atoms while preserving the model’s ability to generalize
369
      across different molecular environments.

373   The primary limitations of Atom-Centered Symmetry Functions (ACSFs) become evident when
374   dealing with molecules that exhibit diverse chemical elements. First, ACSFs do not scale efficiently
375   with element diversity; they require an increasing number of radial and angular functions to represent
376   all possible element pairs and triples. As the number of elements in a system increases, the number of
377   necessary symmetry functions grows exponentially, complicating the descriptor vector size (Gastegger
      et al., 2018). This leads to a significant rise in computational overhead for both model training and

      6   C ONCLUSION
521
      In conclusion, our work introduces a novel framework for modeling 3D molecular structures with
522
      improved scalability, accuracy, and geometric representation. By developing Attention-based Atom-
523
      Centered Symmetry Functions (AACSFs) and the Equivariant Graph Self-Attention Transformer
524   (EG-SAT), we provide a robust solution to the limitations of traditional methods. The integration
525   of element-specific attention mechanisms and gating functions allows EG-SAT to capture higher-
526   order atomic interactions and intricate spatial relationships more effectively than existing models.
527   Our approach addresses the scalability issues associated with ACSFs, offering an efficient way to
528   handle diverse chemical elements while preserving the necessary symmetry properties for molecular
529   modeling. Validation on the QM9 and MD17 datasets demonstrates the better performance of EG-
530   SAT, making it a valuable tool for applications in quantum chemistry and drug discovery. This work
531   paves the way for more accurate and scalable models in the study of molecular interactions and
532
      quantum mechanical properties.
