                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               We propose a novel approach for learning interchangeable tokens in language
012
               models to obtain an extendable vocabulary that can generalize to new tokens. Our
013            method addresses alpha-equivalence, the principle that renaming bound variables
014            preserves semantics. This property arises in many formal languages such as tem-
015            poral logics, where all proposition symbols represent the same concept but remain
016            distinct. To handle such tokens, we develop a dual-part embedding approach. The
017            first part is shared across all interchangeable tokens, enforcing that they represent
018            the same core concept. The second part is randomly generated for each token,
019            enabling distinguishability. As a baseline, we consider a simpler approach that
020            uses alpha-renaming for data augmentation. We also present alpha-covariance,
021
               a metric for measuring robustness against alpha-conversions. When evaluated in
               a Transformer encoder-decoder model for solving linear temporal logic formulae
022
               and copying with extendable vocabulary, our method demonstrates promising gen-
023
               eralization capabilities as well as a favorable inductive bias for alpha-equivalence.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Following the deep learning revolution that affected numerous application areas (Dargan et al.,
029
      2020), recent literature shows that deep learning based approaches also perform well in neurosym-
030   bolic reasoning tasks, such as theorem proving (Han et al., 2021) and mathematical reasoning (Rabe
031   et al., 2020). The formal reasoning capabilities of these models were once doubted, but Liu et al.
032   (2023) demonstrated the ability of Transformer models (Vaswani et al., 2017) to learn shortcuts
033   to automata. Of particular interest is the generalization ability of such models to unseen, out-of-
034   distribution data (Sanh et al., 2021), enhancing their appeal for logical reasoning (Abbe et al., 2023).
035
      Another application area is linear-time temporal logic (LTL), which is heavily utilized by the formal
036   verification community (Clarke et al., 2018; Baier & Katoen, 2008) for reasoning about how logical
037   propositions change over time (Pnueli, 1977). Through the use of temporal operators, LTL formulae
038   can specify, for example, that a proposition p must hold at all time steps (Gp), or at least one time
039   step (Fp). LTL formulae operate on traces, which describe how the propositions change over time.
040
      Solving a given LTL formula involves finding a satisfying trace, and it proved essential for gener-
041
      ating examples for system specifications in the literature. This field was dominated by the methods
042   that use classical algorithms, such as spot (Duret-Lutz et al., 2022) and aalta (Li et al., 2014).
043   However, following the success of Transformer models on end-to-end symbolic integration (Lample
044   & Charton, 2019), Hahn et al. (2021) attacked the LTL solving problem using the same approach.
045   Their capability to generalize to longer formulae is especially noteworthy, and it was made possible
046   thanks to tree-positional encoding (Shiv & Quirk, 2019).
047
      However, generalization to longer formula lengths is not the only concern. In particular, each LTL
048   formula features a set of atomic propositions (henceforth APs), and it’s desirable for the model to
049   generalize to more APs. But the architecture of the model does not even accept new APs that are not
050   seen during training, despite the fact that all APs represent semantically equivalent concepts while
051   being distinguishable from each other. This situation arises in many other application areas, such as
052   mathematical expressions and lambda calculus (alp, 1984), where renaming the bound variables does
053   not change the meaning. This phenomenon is described as alpha-equivalence. Alpha-conversion (or
      alpha-renaming) refers to the process of creating alpha-equivalent input-output pairs.

054
      In this paper, we propose a novel approach for representing interchangeable tokens in neural network
055   models. To summarize, our method constructs some part of the token embeddings on-the-fly instead
056   of learning all of them during training. The token embeddings for interchangeable tokens consist
057   of two parts: a learnable part and a randomized part. The learnable part is shared across all inter-
058   changeable tokens, and the model must depend on the randomized part to differentiate these tokens.
059   We use the weight tying technique (Press & Wolf, 2016) to share the same token embeddings with
060   the final projection matrix, which calculates the logits (i.e., next-token probabilities before softmax).
061   We use our embedding method in a Transformer encoder-decoder model and evaluate it on two
062   tasks: copying with an extendable vocabulary and solving LTL formulae. For the second task, we
063   use datasets generated using spot (Duret-Lutz et al., 2022), a library for LTL manipulation and
064   model checking using conventional algorithms. As a baseline, we consider a simpler approach
065   that uses alpha-renaming for data augmentation during training to expose the model to a larger
066   vocabulary, which is also new in the literature to the best of our knowledge. Overall, our method
067   demonstrates generalization capabilities to larger vocabulary sizes, and also combines well with
068   positional encodings that exhibit length generalization. We also experiment with dataset perturbation
069
      to show that our method introduces a helpful inductive bias for alpha-equivalence. Finally, we
      present alpha-covariance, a metric for measuring robustness against alpha-conversions.

074   Language modeling and formal reasoning The transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017),
075   now ubiquitous in modern deep learning, was initially proposed as a generative model to trans-
076   late between natural languages autoregressively. This led to many successful attempts to frame
077   formal reasoning tasks as language modeling problems, such as symbolic integration (Lample &
078   Charton, 2019), symbolic regression (Kamienny et al., 2022; Vastl et al., 2022), LTL solving (Hahn
079   et al., 2021), and many more. Further developments shifted the field towards large language models
080   (LLMs), e.g., by prompting a model pre-trained on a gigantic scale (Frieder et al., 2023), by en-
081   hancing the prompt with retrieved references for proof generation (Welleck et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
082
      2023), by training an LLM on a specialized dataset for mathematics (Azerbayev et al., 2023). How-
      ever, the reasoning abilities of LLMs were questioned by (Tang et al., 2023), who showed LLMs
083
      struggle with symbolic reasoning when semantics are decoupled, and by others (Wu et al., 2023).
084
085
086
      Extensible vocabulary Efforts to create an extensible vocabulary for neural networks are scarce in
      the broader machine learning community, let alone the formal reasoning literature. Morazzoni et al.
087
      (2023) exploited dictionary definitions to create extensible word embeddings. Wei et al. (2016) pro-
088
      posed a vocabulary-extensible sign language recognition framework by using a component based
089   approach, where each sign gesture is recognized based on common components such as hand shape,
090   orientation, axis, rotation, and trajectory. These studies depend on either external information (dic-
091   tionary definitions) or properties specific to an application area (components of hand gesture); they
092   do not attempt to design an extensible vocabulary for interchangeable tokens, which has been ne-
093   glected by the literature alongside the concept of alpha-equivalence.

      3    P ROBLEM D EFINITION
097
098   In a language modeling problem, the goal is to predict the next token in the output sequence given
099
      the input and the past output. (See Appendix A.1 for more details on language models.) Let V
      denote the set of all unique tokens, i.e., the vocabulary of a language modeling problem. We assume
100
      that Vi is the set of interchangeable tokens and Vn = V\Vi is the set of non-interchangeable tokens.
101
      The core idea behind alpha-equivalence is that renaming interchangeable tokens between each other
102   in both input and output preserves meaning. Let f : V → V be a bijection such that f (x) = x for
103   all x ∈ Vn , i.e., f arbitrarily renames the interchangeable tokens between each other in one-to-one
104   correspondence and preserves the rest of the tokens. We apply f to each token in a given pair of input
105   a ∈ V∗ and output b ∈ V∗ strings, obtaining a′ = (f (a1 ), f (a2 ), . . .) and b′ = (f (b1 ), f (b2 ), . . .).
106   We call this operation alpha-conversion or alpha-renaming. The set of interchangeable tokens Vi
107   must be defined such that a′ and b′ form a valid input-output pair semantically equivalent to (a, b)
      for all possible f .


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Our task is to design an embedding method that—alongside being resilient to alpha-renaming by
109   construction—can support a new vocabulary V′ = V′i ∪ Vn where Vi ⊂ V′i after training on V.
110   In other words, the model should be able to operate on a larger vocabulary than the one seen dur-
111   ing training, as long as the newly introduced tokens belong to the class of interchangeable tokens.
112   Although we don’t impose any restrictions about the size of V′ in this problem definition, the maxi-
113   mum size of V′ in practice may change as a function of the number of embedding dimensions. Thus,
114   while setting the hyperparameters, the expected size of V′ must be considered.
115
116   Example In the LTL solving problem (Appendix A.2), the set of non-interchangeable tokens Vn
117   includes the operators, constants, delimiter tokens (“;”, “{”, “}”), and any special tokens such as
118   the end token. The set of interchangeable tokens equals to the set of atomic propositions (APs):
119
      Vi = P . Assuming P = {a, b}, the formula-trace pair (“&aXb”, “a;b;{1}”) is alpha-equivalent
      to (“&bXa”, “b;a;{1}”). Further, assume that the augmented set of interchangeable tokens is
120
      V′i = P ′ = {a, b, c, d}. Now, the aforementioned pair can also be equivalently represented as
121
      (“&cXd”, “c;d;{1}”). The augmented vocabulary allows the expression of formula-trace pairs
122   that feature up to 4 APs instead of 2. For example, (“&&abX&cd”, “&ab;&cd;{1}”) cannot be
123   expressed using P = {a, b}. Our goal is to create a model that can handle such inputs despite being
124   trained on the limited vocabulary V = Vn ∪ P .

      During training, the embedding matrix must be reconstructed in each forward pass with resampled
163   random vectors β1 to βm . Resampling βi for 1 ≤ i ≤ m during training prevents the model from
164   adapting to the idiosyncracies of a particular random generation and forces it to distinguish between
165   interchangeable tokens regardless of the contents of βi . During inference, it’s created once at the
166   start and remains the same since the autoregressive generation involves multiple forward passes on
167   the same input.

      4.2   R ANDOM EMBEDDING GENERATION
177   This section will explain how the distinguishing part of the interchangeable token embeddings,
178   βi , 1 ≤ i ≤ m, are created. To this end, we developed 3 methods to generate random vectors.
179   Table 1 provides a summary at a glance. The first method simply samples the standard normal dis-
180   tribution for each dimension. The second one uses the neighboring grid points around the origin,
181   which correspond to the 8 directions in 2D. For each interchangeable token, a unique vector in this
182   set is sampled. The last method is similar, but its set consists of the vertices of a hypercube centered
183   around the origin, i.e., diagonal direction vectors.

198   In the normal distribution method, we don’t have any additional constraints to ensure distinguisha-

      bility between vectors. However, in other two methods, we need make sure that each interchangeable
      token gets assigned to a unique vector since the sampling set is finite. To achieve this quickly and

      space-efficiently, we define a mapping from integers to possible vectors. The unique vectors are
201
      generated by sampling m unique random integers (which can be calculated efficiently using the
202   reservoir sampling technique), and then using the defined mapping to convert these integers to the
203   vectors. This strategy avoids materializing the whole set of possible vectors. In the hypercube ver-
204   tices method, we map the binary digits of an integer in [0, 2dβ ) to {−1, 1}. Although “Neighboring
205   Points" is simply the ternary version of the same idea, avoiding the zero vector requires special care.
206   The zero vector maps to the integer iz = (3dβ − 1)/2. Therefore, we define our domain as the
207   integers in [0, 3dβ − 1) and add 1 to the integer i before converting it if i ≥ iz .
208   Integer mapping approach for generating unique vectors works well for up to 32 dimensions, after
209   which the limits of integer representation become an issue for reservoir sampling. Therefore, in
210   such cases, we simply disable the uniqueness check because the size of the sampling set grows
211   exponentially, rendering the probability of drawing the same sample negligible.

213   4.3   P ROJECTION
214
215   Weight tying In a traditional language modeling setting, since both the embedding and projec-
      tion matrices are entirely composed of learnable parameters, it’s not necessary to share them, even


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      though there are many advantages of weight tying (Press & Wolf, 2016). However, we construct the
217   embedding matrix manually in our method, which makes weight tying a requirement. Furthermore,
218   since we perform our experiments on an encoder-decoder architecture in this paper, we utilize a
219   three-way weight tying approach, whereby the embedding matrices of encoder and decoder are tied
220   in addition to the final projection matrix. Three-way weight tying is particularly appropriate for our
221   problem domain since many tokens are shared between the LTL formulae and traces.
222
223   Feature normalization Given the output of the last layer before the final projection v (henceforth
224   called feature vector), instead of directly applying the final projection as in U v, we apply L2 normal-
225   ization to the feature vector v before passing it through the final projection: U ff n (v). This matrix
226   multiplication constitutes taking a dot product with each row. Since a · b = |a||b|cos(θ) where θ
227
      is the angle between a and b, normalizing both the embeddings and the feature vector leaves only
      the cosine term to determine the logits. This forces the model to distinguish between tokens based
228
      solely on the directions, which may improve the gradient flow.
229
230
      Cosine loss If we normalize both the embeddings and the feature vector, the only thing that deter-
231
      mines each logit is the cosine of the angle between the feature vector and the embedding. Applying
232   the softmax loss to such logits is known as cosine loss in the literature. Although cosine-based loss
233   functions were successful in face recognition (Ranjan et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2017), it proved
234   sensitive to hyperparameter settings in these losses. To avoid this problem, we use AdaCos loss
235   function (Zhang et al., 2019) that scales the logits adaptively throughout training.
236
      To adapt the AdaCos loss function to our use case, we make the following modifications: Since the
237
      language modeling problem involves a sequence length dimension in addition to the batch dimen-
238   sion, we combine these two dimensions while ignoring the padding tokens, effectively treating both
239   dimensions as batch dimensions. However, since this change greatly increases the number of batch
240   dimensions, it can lead to numerical issues, even with the log-sum-exp trick. Therefore, we clip the
241   scale value calculated by AdaCos to a maximum of 100 to avoid numerical issues.

414
415   Given a vocabulary of n AP tokens and an LTL formula-trace pair containing k APs, it’s possible to
416   write nPk = n!/(n − k)! alpha-equivalent pairs. Since these are semantically equivalent, we expect
417   the model’s predictions to be the same after undoing the alpha-conversions for all of them. As there
418   is no metric to quantify this in the literature to the best of our knowledge, we develop a new metric.
419   Let (x, y) be an input-output pair for the model, and let P = {(x1 , y 1 ), . . . , (xn , y n )} be n input-
420   output pairs alpha-equivalent to (x, y). We define αi as the alpha-conversion function for the ith
421   input-output pair such that αi (x) = xi and αi (y) = y i . To compute the alpha-covariance of a
422   model with respect to P, we generate predictions for each input in P, obtaining the prediction ŷ i
423   for each xi . We define a set that contains the predictions with alpha-conversion undone: U =
424   {αi−1 (ŷ i ) | 1 ≤ i ≤ n}. Note that if we defined this set for the ground truth outputs in P, we
425   would get {y} since αi−1 (y i ) = y holds for each y i by definition. The model’s sensitivity to
426   alpha-conversions could be quantified by simply |U|, but this value may be hard to interpret since it
427
      depends on |P|. To normalize this value intuitively, we define the alpha-covariance of a model with

529   The primary difference between machine learning and numerical optimization is the intention to
530   generalize to out-of-distribution samples, for which the network architecture and its inductive biases
531

532

      A.1   P RELIMINARY: L ANGUAGE MODELS
670
671   The autoregressive language modeling or sequence modeling in a broader sense—whose goal is
672   to predict the next token given the past tokens—was revolutionized by the transformer architec-
673   ture (Vaswani et al., 2017), replacing the step-by-step processing of recurrent neural networks
674   (RNNs) with a parallelizable attention mechanism. At its core lies the attention mechanism, which
675   computes three vectors—query, key, and value—from input embeddings. This mechanism allows
676   the model to weigh the importance of different tokens, enabling it to capture long-range depen-
677   dencies efficiently. In self-attention, these vectors come from the same sequence, while in cross-
678   attention, key and value vectors come from a different sequence, as in encoder-decoder setups. The
679
      transformer consists of an encoder with self-attention and feed-forward layers, and a decoder that
      adds cross-attention to incorporate the encoder’s output. Since attention lacks an inherent sense
680
      of token order, positional encodings are added to input embeddings to provide sequence structure.
681
      During training, attention masking ensures causality in predictions, preventing future tokens from
682   being considered when predicting the next one.
683
684
      A.2   T EMPORAL LOGIC OVERVIEW
685
686   Linear Temporal Logic (LTL) extends propositional logic by introducing the ability to reason about
687   the evolution of propositions over time (Pnueli, 1977). The syntax of LTL, defined over a finite
688   set of atomic propositions P , is as follows: The syntax of LTL, defined over a finite set of atomic
689   propositions P , is given in Equation 3, where T represents True, p ∈ P an atomic proposition, ¬
690   the negation operator, ∧ the conjunction operator, X and U the temporal operators next and until
691   respectively.
692
693                                 ϕ := T | p | ¬ϕ | ϕ1 ∧ ϕ2 | Xϕ | ϕ1 Uϕ2                            (3)
694
695   Specifically:
696
697          • Xϕ holds at time t if and only if ϕ holds at the next time step, i.e., at time t + 1.
698          • ϕ1 Uϕ2 means that ϕ2 must hold at some future time t2 , and ϕ1 holds at every time step t
699            from the current time t1 up to but not necessarily including t2 .
700
701   For instance, the formula XXa specifies that a must hold at the third time step. Similarly, the
      formula TUa requires that a holds at some point in the future. Finally, as a more complex example,


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      the formula Xb ∧ aUc asserts that b holds at the second time step, c holds at some future time, and
703   a holds at all preceding time steps.
704
705   An LTL formula is evaluated over a trace, which represents a sequence of truth values for atomic
706
      propositions over time. In this work, as in DeepLTL (Hahn et al., 2021), we consider symbolic traces
      of infinite length. These traces are expressed in what is known as a lasso form, denoted uv ω , where
707
      u is a finite prefix, and v is a finite sequence that repeats indefinitely.
708
709   A symbolic trace represents all traces that satisfy the propositional formulae at the respective time
710   steps. For example, the symbolic trace a, a ∧ ¬b, (c)ω describes all traces in which a holds at the
711   first two time steps, b does not hold at the second time step, and c holds at every step from the third
712
      onward. This symbolic trace satisfies the formulae TUc and X¬b ∧ aUc, but it violates the formula
      XXb since b is not guaranteed to hold at the third time step. Symbolic traces, such as this one,
713
      can be underspecified, meaning that certain propositions (e.g., a and b) may take arbitrary values at
714
      some time steps.
715
716   The LTL solving problem involves identifying a symbolic trace in lasso form uv ω that satisfies a
717   given input formula ϕ. We approach this as an autoregressive language modeling task: given an
718
      LTL formula and a partially generated symbolic trace, the model predicts the probabilities for the
      next token in the trace.
719
720   For compatibility with the dataset from DeepLTL (Hahn et al., 2021), both our traces and formulae
721   are represented in Polish (prefix) notation, where operators precede their operands. For instance,
722   a ∧ b is written as \&ab, which avoids the need for parentheses to resolve ambiguities.
723   As described earlier, we assume that traces are infinite and represented in lasso form uv ω . Along-
724   side atomic propositions, constants (True:1 and False:0), and logical operators, we use special
725   symbols in the notation: “;” is a position delimiter, and “{” and “}” enclose the repeating period v.
726   For example, the string “a;&ab;{b}” represents the symbolic trace a, a ∧ b, (b)ω .
