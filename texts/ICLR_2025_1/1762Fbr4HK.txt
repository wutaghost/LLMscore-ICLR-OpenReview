009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A significant challenge in many fields of science and engineering is making sense
012            of time-dependent measurement data by recovering governing equations in the
013            form of differential equations. We focus on finding parsimonious ordinary differen-
014            tial equation (ODE) models for nonlinear, noisy, and non-autonomous dynamical
015            systems and propose a machine learning method for data-driven system identifica-
016            tion. While many methods tackle noisy and limited data, non-stationarity – where
017            differential equation parameters change over time – has received less attention.
018            Our method, dynamic SINDy, combines variational inference with SINDy (sparse
019
               identification of nonlinear dynamics) to model time-varying coefficients of sparse
               ODEs. This framework allows for uncertainty quantification of ODE coefficients,
020
               expanding on previous methods for autonomous systems. These coefficients are
021
               then interpreted as latent variables and added to the system to obtain an autonomous
022            dynamical model. We validate our approach using synthetic data, including nonlin-
023            ear oscillators and the Lorenz system, and apply it to neuronal activity data from
024            C. elegans. Dynamic SINDy uncovers a global nonlinear model, showing it can
025            handle real, noisy, and chaotic datasets. We aim to apply our method to a variety of
026            problems, specifically dynamic systems with complex time-dependent parameters.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Many fields of science and engineering now benefit from unprecedented amounts of data due to
032
      increased efforts and technological breakthroughs in data collection. The challenge is to use these
      measurements to expand our understanding of dynamical systems in areas like climate science,
033
      neuroscience, ecology, finance, and epidemiology. Machine learning methods, such as neural
034
      networks, are widely used for data-driven modeling, offering high prediction accuracy but limited
035   interpretability. In contrast, traditional techniques that identify ordinary and partial differential
036   equations (ODEs and PDEs) provide interpretable and generalizable insights into the system’s
037   underlying physics. While neural networks may lose accuracy as conditions change, in many systems
038   the governing differential equations remain reliable. The key question is whether we can combine the
039   strengths of deep learning with the clarity and simplicity of data-driven differential equation models.
040
041   A key challenge in data-driven system identification is that many systems exhibit nonlinear
042   behavior, such as switching between dynamical regimes (30; 17; 21; 34). These "hybrid systems"
043
      (33), where continuous dynamics shift at discrete events, are more challenging to define and simulate
      than classical systems with smooth vector fields (1; 5). Standard methods often assume that the
044
      data comes from a system governed by a fixed set of equations and terms, but time-varying hidden
045
      variables can further hinder identification of the system’s underlying dynamics. This motivates our
046   focus on non-autonomous (or non-stationary) systems, where sudden shifts or hidden continuous
047   dynamics complicate accurate modeling and prediction.
048
049   We introduce dynamic SINDy, a data-driven method for finding non-autonomous dynamic
050   systems with switching or continuously-varying latent variables. These systems are described by:
051
                                                  ẋ = f (x(t), t)                                       (1)
052
053   where x is vector-valued. A simple such example is ẋ = A(t)x. Importantly, we focus on systems
      where the time-varying component and the main variables of interest x are separable (e.g., ẋ =

      f (x, t) = sin(t)x, but not ẋ = f (x, t) = sin(tx). Another assumption is that if multiple trajectories
055   of the system are available, these all display the same underlying switching or hidden variable
056   dynamics.
057
058
      Dynamic SINDy combines the interpretability of differential equations with the power of deep
      learning. It uses a deep generative model to uncover sparse governing equations directly from
059

060

061   The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 introduces key concepts, including SINDy, variational

069
070   2.1   S YSTEM IDENTIFICATION OF NON - LINEAR DYNAMICAL SYSTEMS (SINDY )
071
072   SINDy (Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics) (43) is a data-driven method that uses
073   sparse regression on a library of nonlinear candidate functions to match data snapshots with their
074   derivatives, revealing the governing equations. The method assumes that only a few key terms
075   explain the system’s dynamics. More specifically, consider x(t) ∈ Rd governed by the ODE:
076
      ẋ(t) = f (x(t)). Given m snapshots of the system X = [x(t1 ), x(t2 ), ..., x(tm )]T and the esti-
077   mated time derivatives Ẋ = [ẋ(t1 ), ẋ(t2 ), ..., ẋ(tm )]T , we construct a library of candidate func-
078   tions Θ(X) = [1, X, X2 , ..., Xp , sin(X), cos(X), ...]. We then solve a sparse regression problem,
079   Ẋ = Θ(X)Ξ, to identify the optimal coefficients Ξ and to reduce the number of terms, enforcing
080   parsimony. A sparsity-promoting regularization function R is added to the final loss to yield:
081
                                      Ξ̂ = argminΞ (Ẋ − Θ(X)Ξ)2 + R(Ξ)                                    (2)
082
083   Several innovations have followed the original formulation of SINDy (42; 18; 32; 14). For instance,
084   integral and weak formulations (47; 20) have enhanced the algorithm’s robustness to noise. Of
085   relevance to our study, SINDy’s generalization to non-autonomous dynamical systems has been
086
      previously explored using group sparsity norms (42) or clustering algorithms (33).
087
088   2.2   (DYNAMIC ) VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODERS FOR SYSTEM IDENTIFICATION
089
      The Variational Autoencoder (VAE) (35; 11) combines neural network-based autoencoders with
090
      variational inference for probabilistic modeling and data generation. Unlike standard autoencoders,
091   VAEs stand out due to two key features: (i) VAEs encode input data X as a distribution in the
092   latent space, allowing the decoder to generate new data by sampling from this distribution; and
093   (ii) a regularization term ensures the latent space resembles a standard (e.g., normal) distribution,
094   making it continuous (nearby points generate similar outputs) and complete (all points produce
095   meaningful data). Further mathematical details can be found in Supplementary Material (SM)
096   Section 1.1. A related method of interest is HyperSINDy (28). It combines VAEs with SINDy to
097   discover differential equations from data. The VAE approximates the probability distribution of
098   equation coefficients, so that once trained, HyperSINDy generates accurate stochastic dynamics and
099
      quantifies uncertainty, making it a powerful tool for model discovery.
100
      In order to adapt the VAE/SINDy framework to non-autonomous systems, we would like
101
      to implement generative architectures that capture the temporal dependencies in sequential data.
102   Dynamic VAEs (DVAEs) is an approach that extends VAEs to handle time series data (24). A number
103   of DVAE architectures are described that use recurrent neural networks or state-space models to
104   address both latent and temporal relationships (36; 37; 13; 22; 23; 2; 26; 39; 46). We specifically use
105   timeVAE (10), which has shown strong performance in generating time series data by processing
106   entire sequences with dense and convolutional layers to capture correlations. Our approach is flexible,
107   allowing the VAE architecture to be swapped for other models better suited to the data or system
      under study (SM Sec. 1.2).

      2.3   OTHER MACHINE LEARNING METHODS FOR NON - AUTONOMOUS DYNAMICAL SYSTEMS
109
110   Traditionally, methods for handling hybrid or switching systems often involve dividing time or
111   space into segments (16). For instance, reduced-order models for nonlinear systems segment
112   time intervals into smaller windows, then build a local, reduced approximation space for each
113   segment (25; 6; 27). Clustering methods are also employed for modeling, particularly in complex
114   fluid flows, where clusters represent states that can transition via a Markov model (12; 3) or via
115   dynamic mode decomposition with control (31; 4). Through data-informed geometry learning,
116
      authors in (48) reconstruct the relevant “normal forms", which are prototypical realizations of the
      dynamics, providing bifurcation diagram and insights about the parameters even for non-autonomous
117
      systems. Yet another method (49) applies Koopman operator theory using DMD algorithms to find
118
      time-dependent eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes in linear non-autonomous systems.
119
120   We compare dynamic SINDy with two existing methods (Section 4.6). First, we look at a
121   method (recurrent SLDS) (41) that extends switching linear dynamical systems (SLDS) (15; 9) by
122   generating transitions through changes in a continuous latent state and external inputs, rather than
123   relying on a discrete Markov model for switching states. This model breaks the data into simpler
124   segments and is interpretable, generative, and efficiently fitted using modular Bayesian inference.
125   Second, we examine a method from (15; 9) that uses group-sparse penalization for model selection
126   and parameter estimation. This method assumes shared sparsity across parameters by applying
127
      group-sparsity regularization to smaller time windows in the data, identifying the system for each
      segment, and then combining the results.

190   4.1   S YSTEM IDENTIFICATION OF NON - AUTONOMOUS HARMONIC OSCILLATORS
191
192   We begin by identifying noisy, non-autonomous dynamical systems using a simple toy model – a
193   non-autonomous harmonic oscillator with time-varying ODE coefficients (Eq. 3, Figure 1). First, we
194   vary the coefficient A(t) in a switch-like fashion (Figure 2(a)-(c)). The system behaves like a classic
195
      harmonic oscillator, but with a frequency switch. The inferred coefficients (Figure 2, Suppl. Fig. 4)
      and the reconstructed trajectories (Suppl. Fig. 6) align well with the true values. These trajectories
196
      are generated during testing, with z sampled from a standard normal distribution.
197
198   When varying both A(t) and B(t) as sinusoids with different frequencies, the resulting tra-
199   jectories generally capture their oscillations, though some higher error and a large outlier appear
200   toward the end (Figure 2(d), Suppl. Fig. 3). We also successfully reproduce coefficients composed of
201   multiple frequencies (a finite Fourier series) in Figures 2(e)-(f). In (f), some error occurs in the first
202   half because the system approaches a fixed point where the derivative is nearly zero. In such cases,
203   system identification becomes difficult, as multiple solutions can produce the same dynamics.
204
205   4.2   U NCERTAINTY QUANTIFICATION IN NON - AUTONOMOUS HARMONIC OSCILLATORS
206
207
      We use VAEs to quantify uncertainty by estimating the standard deviation of the coefficients over time.
      Therefore we generate multiple trajectories by sampling z from a standard normal distribution during
208
      testing. Figures 3(a)-(c) show examples of trajectories from networks trained on noisy data with two
209
      noise levels: low (0.01) and high (0.5) standard deviations. As expected, trajectories vary more under
210   high noise than low noise. Our results show that the estimated standard deviation generally follows
211   the true coefficient variations. First, we compute the standard deviation across generated samples at
212   each time point and average these deviations (Figure 3 B(a)). Second, we subtract a smooth mean
213   from the trajectory samples and calculate the standard deviation over time (Figure 3 B(b)). Both
214   methods demonstrate that standard deviation aligns with the ground truth, particularly for switch
215   signal coefficients, but is less clear for Fourier series coefficients. Further work is needed to improve
      standard deviation estimation, considering the VAE architecture and hyperparameters.

364   4.5   DYNAMICS IN THE NEMATODE C. ELEGANS DURING LOCOMOTION BEHAVIOR
365
366   Modern neuroscientific data is noisy, nonlinear, and incomplete, with recordings from hundreds or
367   thousands of neurons, yet many network features and neurons remain unmeasured. This makes it a
368   challenging test for dynamic SINDy’s ability to model such complex systems. To demonstrate our
369
      method’s potential, we use a dataset of C. elegans neural activity (Sec. 3.1, (38)). Unlike previous

370

371
      of the neural data: two stable fixed points representing forward and reversal behaviors, transitions
372   between them, and variability in those transitions, reflecting real neural dynamics.

      We apply dynamic SINDy to minimize the error between the model’s derivatives (ẋ(t), ẏ(t)) and
404   the dominant PC derivatives from the data. Following sections 4.1 and 4.3, we identify the sparsity
405   pattern of the SINDy coefficients, enforcing ẋ = y. The method highlights the terms 1, x, y, x2 , x3
406   for describing ẏ and calculates their time-varying coefficients (SM Sec. 3.1, 3.2). To further simplify
407   the model, we set coefficients for all variables to be constant, except the flexible term u which we
408   can also reduce to a time series of switches (Figure 6D, see SM Sec. 3.2.1) without meaningfully
409   affecting global dynamics. Converting u into a switching signal simplifies this term, helping to
410   regularize the model and improve interpretability. This approach aligns with previous studies
411   showing bistability and sudden transitions in behavior.
412
      Our approach identifies a cubic function for the differential equation model: ẍ = f (x, β) + u(t) =
413
      0.002 · x3 + 0.0087x2 − 0.22 · y + 0.05 · x + ui , where ui alternates between u0 = −0.266 and
414
      u1 = 0.044 (see SM Sec. 3.1 for details). Each time u switches, the cubic function shifts, altering the
415   fixed point that the trajectory converges to. This switching signal u enables the transitions between
416   the two fixed points, which correspond to forward and reversal behaviors. Overall, the reconstructed
417   data captures key features like fixed points and transitions (Figures 6E-F, 6G). By labeling the
418   trajectory based on behaviors, we align the inferred dynamics with the training data (Figure 6H).
419
420   The reconstructions are accurate regardless of whether we use the processed switching term
421   u (Figure 6 D) or the original time series u (Figure 6 C). However, u alone does not adequately
422   explain the data; removing other terms leads to poor fits or instability. By systematically eliminating
423
      different terms, we find that all are essential for capturing the dynamics. When we initialize the
      inferred ODE system from different starting points and use u from training, the resulting dynamics
424
      qualitatively match the data. This suggests that our method effectively avoids overfitting. Unlike
425
      Morrison et. al., which relies on selecting a model based on human-labeled behavioral states,
426   dynamic SINDy is fully data-driven and does not require labeled data to partition the phase space
427   (29). Additionally, unlike Fieseler et. al., our model accommodates nonlinear dynamics with
428   two stable fixed points (8). A key advantage of our ODE model is the potential for biologically
429   interpretable parameters (see (29). SM Sec. 3.3 offers a more comprehensive discussion of the
430   benefits of our framework, comparing our global nonlinear switching model to previous studies. In
431   summary, we have demonstrated that dynamic SINDy can do automatic data-driven model discovery,
      generating a nonlinear model with minimal input from the data scientist.

      4.6   DYNAMIC SINDY AND OTHER METHODS FOR SYSTEM IDENTIFICATION
457
458   We begin by comparing dynamic SINDy with switching linear dynamic systems (SLDS) (15; 9)
459   and its extension, rSLDS (41). SLDS uses a discrete latent variable, zt , to partition the state space
460   between switches (see SM Sec. 4.1), simplifying complex nonlinear dynamics into more manageable
461   linear segments. The rLDS extension allows switches to depend on continuous latent states and
462   external inputs using logistic regression (41). We evaluate how well SLDS/rSLDS identifies
463   switching signals in the dynamical systems studied so far, specifically inferring where the latent
464   variable z changes for switching to occur. We use coefficients based on sigmoids and two types
465   of switching signals (refer to the "ground truth" in Figure 7A). Running SLDS or rLDS generates
466
      samples of the latent variable z that segment the training trajectory, enabling us to compare this
      segmentation with the actual ground truth switches.
467
468
      We find that for a sigmoidally varying coefficient, SLDS identifies the switch fairly well
469   (Figure 7A (a), (b)), as shown by the colored trajectories and the insets comparing the zt time series
470   to the ground truth; although for Lorenz dynamics, the predicted change in the latent state zt is
471   slightly delayed relative to the actual switch (Figure 7A (b)). However, SLDS struggles when there
472   are multiple state switches in the time series (Figures 7A (c) and (d)). For the harmonic oscillator,
473   rSLDS produces a model with too many switches and is more complex than the ground truth. For
474   Lorenz dynamics, both SLDS and rSLDS switch periodically whenever the dynamics change between
475   attractors, but this periodicity does not match the true switches defined by the coefficients. To address
476   these challenges, we added time as a new dimension to the dataset, represented as a simple feature
477
      vector [1, 2, . . . , T ], where T is the total number of time steps. The goal was for SLDS/rSLDS to
      recognize that the switches are time-dependent rather than state-dependent. However, this addition
478
      did not improve the performance of SLDS or rSLDS.
479
480   Another method for identifying non-autonomous systems, discussed in references (42; 19)
481   and SM Sec. 4.2, involves dividing the trajectory into smaller time windows and applying SINDy
482   to each segment while enforcing a consistent sparsity pattern across all windows. We tested this
483   approach on two toy datasets, using SINDy coefficients modeled as sigmoids, sinusoidal functions,
484   and a Fourier series with seven frequencies. Without the group sparsity regularization, the sparsity
485   patterns varied across the windows, highlighting the importance of group sparsity for achieving a
      coherent solution. The group sparsity approach worked well when the coefficients were sigmoid


                                                         9

486
      functions with varying smoothness (Figure 7 B (a) and (c)). However, it struggled with sinusoidal
487   and Fourier series coefficients, particularly in the Lorenz system (7 B(b) and (d)). In cases of
488   misidentified coefficients, the algorithm also generated incorrect sparsity patterns. We conclude that
489   neither SLDS or rSLDS, nor the group sparsity method are as effective as our method in identifying
490   non-autonomous dynamical systems from data.

524   5        C ONCLUSION
525
526   We have developed dynamic SINDy, an extension of SINDy designed for data-driven identification of
527   noisy, nonlinear, and non-autonomous dynamical systems, as well as for discovering latent variables.
528   We demonstrated the effectiveness of dynamic SINDy on both benchmark synthetic datasets and a
529   real, noisy, chaotic dataset of neuronal activity from C. elegans.
530
531
      However, our method has some limitations. First, the DVAE architecture has many hyper-
      parameters to tune, and results may not be robust to these settings, especially in noisy datasets.
532
      A systematic approach for hyperparameter tuning and addressing multiple solutions is necessary.
533
      To prevent overfitting, we should encourage simpler time series through regularization. Although
534   we included a simple approximation of total variation term in our loss function, realistic datasets
535   might require more sophisticated regularization. Future research should explore different DVAE
536   architectures to evaluate their accuracy in reproducing dynamics and their ability to quantify
537   uncertainty. It would also be valuable to apply our method to experiments with different trial
538   dynamics and types of noise than those studied here (e.g., varying switching points across trials).
539   Lastly, we aim to apply dynamic SINDy to realistic data from fields like biology, physics, and
      engineering to uncover hidden dynamics and fully utilize its potential for discovering latent variables.
