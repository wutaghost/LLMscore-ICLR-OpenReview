009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Training large models with millions or even billions of parameters from scratch
012            incurs substantial computational costs. Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)
013            methods, particularly Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), address this challenge by
014            adapting only a reduced number of parameters to specific tasks with gradient-
015            based optimizers. In this paper, we cast PEFT as an optimal filtering/state estima-
016            tion problem and present Low-Rank Kalman Optimizer (LoKO) to estimate the
017            optimal trainable parameters in an online manner. We leverage the low-rank de-
018
               composition in LoRA to significantly reduce matrix sizes in Kalman iterations and
               further capitalize on a diagonal approximation of the covariance matrix to effec-
019
               tively decrease computational complexity from quadratic to linear in the number
020
               of trainable parameters. Moreover, we discovered that the initialization of the co-
021            variance matrix within the Kalman algorithm and the accurate estimation of the
022            observation noise covariance are the keys in this formulation, and we propose ro-
023            bust approaches that work well across a vast range of well-established computer
024            vision and language models. Our results show that LoKO converges with fewer
025            iterations and yields better performance models compared to commonly used op-
026            timizers with LoRA in both image classifications and language tasks. Our study
027            opens up the possibility of leveraging the Kalman filter as an effective optimizer
028            for the online fine-tuning of large models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The widespread adoption of deep neural networks, particularly large models, across various fields
      is mainly driven by the pre-training on extensive datasets followed by task-specific fine-tuning (Han
034
      et al., 2024). In recent years, the concept of online fine-tuning has attracted significant attention
035
      across diverse domains, ranging from robotics (Yang et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2022; Julian et al.,
036   2020), reinforcement learning (Zheng et al., 2022; Nakamoto et al., 2024), computer vision (Gao
037   et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2020), and natural language processing (Fan et al., 2024). Online fine-
038   tuning refers to the process of continually updating a pre-trained model’s parameters as new data in a
039   temporal stream of data becomes available, typically during deployment or in real-time applications.
040   As online full fine-tuning requires substantial computational resources and can negatively impact the
041   generalization capabilities(Han et al., 2024), Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques
042   freeze the majority of the model parameters and selectively update a smaller subset. Among PEFT
043   approaches, the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) technique has recently been widely recognized due
044
      to its efficient adaptation with low computational overhead (Han et al., 2024). Many extensions to
      LoRA have also been proposed to improve its learning capacity and training stability (Liu et al.,
045
      2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Renduchintala et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2024; Valipour et al., 2022).
046
047   In these PEFT approaches, stochastic gradient descent (SGD) has been the dominant method for the
048   parameter optimization. The adaptive first-order optimizers, such as Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014)
049   and its variants, have demonstrated superior performance compared to traditional SGD ones(Ruder,
050
      2016), as evidenced by their widespread adoption in LoRA extensions. Despite the simplicity and
      efficacy of these gradient-based optimizers, they exclusively rely on first-order derivatives, which
051
      may result in (sub-)optimal convergence and inefficient optimization (Reddi et al., 2019).
052
053   In contrast, many previous works (Singhal & Wu, 1988; 1989; Puskorius & Feldkamp, 1991;
      Williams, 1992; Heimes, 1998; Rivals & Personnaz, 1998) showed that recursive Extended Kalman

      Filter (EKF) algorithm – a method for state estimation of nonlinear systems using a data stream
055   introduced by Kalman & Bucy (1961) – can optimize relatively small models with performance
056   surpassing the gradient-based counterparts. This achievement remained obscure until when Ol-
057   livier (2018) theoretically demonstrated that EKF is effectively equivalent to online natural gradient
058   descent. This offers a new perspective on advanced optimization: instead of relying on complex
059   techniques, we can recursively infer the optimal parameters as state estimation. However, despite
060   the commendable performance of the EKF in training neural models, its practicality has been ham-
061   pered, especially with the advent of large models. Pariticularly, the EKF algorithm involves several
062   sequential operations, including Linearization, Prediction, and Update steps, all of which entail sig-
063
      nificant computational overhead. The size of the crucial covariance matrix in EKF can even grow
      quadratically with the number of model parameters involved in training.
064
065   In this paper, we leverage the low-rank decomposition technique from LoRA to reduce the number
066   of trainable parameters in specific layers. We show that the EKF algorithm can be particularly
067   useful for fine-tuning as fine-tuning only involves a small portion of model parameters. We intro-
068   duce LoKO, a Kalman-based training algorithm, as an alternative to advanced optimizers for online
069   fine-tuning of large models. Particularly, our contributions are:
070
071          • Based on the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) method, introduced by Hu et al. (2021), which
072            significantly reduces the number of trainable parameters, we demonstrated its compatibil-
073            ity with the Kalman filter. Also, we showed that this combination offers faster performance
074            than traditional optimizers in online fine-tuning scenarios.
075          • We employ a diagonal approximation for the covariance matrix P , a common approach to
076            reduce the computational overhead from quadratic to linear. By integrating this with the
077            exponential moving average (EMA) for estimating the matrix R and incorporating it into
078            the LoRA framework, we achieve improved performance without additional computational
079            cost.
080
081
             • We conduct various experiments to demonstrate LoKO’s performance in online fine-tuning
082
               classification tasks across computer vision and language modeling domains.
083          • To the best of our knowledge, this is the first successful attempt to fine-tune large and com-
084            plex models, including transformers with millions of parameters, using the Kalman filter
085            algorithm.
086
087   In summary, LoKO shows outstanding performance on computer vision and language modeling
088   benchmarks: MNIST (LeCun et al., 1998), CIFAR-10/100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), ImageNet100
089
      (Vinyals et al., 2016), SST-2 (Socher et al., 2013), COLA (Warstadt, 2019), MRPC (Dolan & Brock-
      ett, 2005). This paper contributes to ongoing efforts to develop a more efficient and fast optimization
090
      algorithm for online fine-tuning of increasingly more complex large models.

093   2   R ELATED WORK
094
095   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT): Traditional full fine-tuning typically demands sig-
096   nificant computational resources and may damage the model’s generalization ability, occasionally
097   leading to catastrophic forgetting (Han et al., 2024). In contrast, Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
098   (PEFT) efficiently freezes a portion of the parameters while updating a reduced number of trainable
099
      ones to mitigate these issues. Three primary approaches for PEFT are commonly used: plug-in
      adapters, parameter freezing and model reparameterization. Plug-in adapters refer to the techniques
100
      of introducing an extra trainable adapter module to the pre-trained model, as demonstrated in works
101
      such as (Chen et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024). Parameter freezing is to freeze selected model pa-
102   rameters and update only a targeted subset, like BitFit (Zaken et al., 2021), Child-Tuning (Xu et al.,
103   2021), IA3 (Liu et al., 2022), and FISH Mask (Sung et al., 2021). Model reparameterization, as the
104   name suggests, reparameterizes model parameters using typically the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)
105   technique, which adds low-rank weight matrices as trainable parameters(Hu et al., 2021). Multiple
106   extensions to LoRA have been proposed to improve learning capacity and training stability. For
107   instance, DoRA (Liu et al., 2024) decomposes pre-trained weights into magnitude and direction
      components to minimize the number of trainable parameters more efficiently. AdaLoRA(Zhang


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2023) utilizes singular value decomposition (SVD) to dynamically allocate the parameter
109   budget based on importance scoring. Tied-LoRA(Renduchintala et al., 2023) leverages weight tying
110   and selective training to reduce the number of trainable parameters further. To optimize the memory
111   efficiency, QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024), QA-LoRA (Xu et al., 2023), and LoftQ (Li et al., 2023)
112   address the issue of memory usage by quantization technique. DyLoRA (Valipour et al., 2022) is a
113   dynamic search-free LoRA to avoid exhaustive search for the most optimal rank. However, all these
114   extensions utilize the Adam optimizer or its variants like AdamW in parameter optimization.
115
116   Kalman Filter for Optimizing Neural Networks: The idea of using the Kalman filter as param-
117   eter optimization in deep learning comes from Singhal & Wu (1988), which showed that the process
118   of training neural networks can be conceptualized as tackling a system identification challenge for a
119   nonlinear dynamic system, and thus Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) can be used to train neural net-
120   work parameters. The superior performance of the Kalman-based training algorithm compared to
121
      the traditional backpropagation technique drew attention to exploring the relationship between these
      two classical algorithms (Ruck et al., 1992; Ollivier, 2018). To make the Kalman filter more scalable,
122
      several studies have addressed the computational complexity associated with this training algorithm,
123
      notably using matrix partitioning techniques (Shah & Palmieri, 1990; Shah et al., 1992; Puskorius &
124   Feldkamp, 1991), and a low-dimensional (block-)diagonal approximation of the covariance matrix
125   (Murtuza & Chorian, 1994). Only recently, Ollivier (2018; 2019) demonstrated that training with
126   a Kalman filter is, in fact, equivalent to an online natural gradient descent. This finding renewed
127   interest in this training method once again. Various studies have since addressed the scalability
128   challenges of the EKF optimizer. For example, Chang et al. (2022) introduced a diagonal Gaus-
129   sian approximation, while Chang et al. (2023) proposed a low-rank plus diagonal decomposition of
130   the posterior precision matrix. Hennig et al. (2024) developed a matrix-free iterative algorithm to
131   further enhance efficiency. In the context of factorization models, Gómez-Uribe & Karrer (2021)
132
      introduced a decoupled EKF (DEKF). Furthermore, EKF has been applied to several specialized ar-
      eas, such as continual learning (Titsias et al., 2023), test-time adaptation (Schirmer et al., 2024), and
133
      reinforcement learning (Shashua & Mannor, 2019; 2020; Shashua & Mannor; Totaro & Jonsson,
134
      2021; Shih & Liang, 2024). Other notable work includes loss adaptivity in the Kalman optimiza-
135   tion algorithm (Davtyan et al., 2022), the Bayesian online natural gradient (Jones et al., 2024), and
136   approaches for handling nonstationary data in online learning (Jones et al., 2022b;a). However, the
137   practical implementation of these methods remained infeasible for large models.

172   3.2   L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION (L O RA)
173
174   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) is a technique designed to efficiently fine-tune large
175   pre-trained neural networks by reducing the number of trainable parameters. Instead of updating
176   the entire pre-trained weight matrix, LoRA introduces a low-rank decomposition that captures the
177   essential changes needed for fine-tuning. Consider a layer in a neural network with a pre-trained
178   weight matrix W0 ∈ Rd×q , where d and q represent the dimensions of the weight matrix. The
179
      output of this layer can be expressed as:
180                                             z = W0 x + ∆W x                                               (4)
181
182   Here, x is the input vector, and ∆W represents the adjustment to the weights during fine-tuning.
183   LoRA modifies this by introducing two smaller matrices, A ∈ Rr×q and B ∈ Rd×r , where r is the
184
      chosen rank with r ≪ min(d, q). The update to the weight matrix is then expressed as:
185                                              z = W0 x + BAx                                               (5)
186
187   At the beginning of training, the matrix A is initialized with a random Gaussian distribution
188   N (0, σ 2 I), and matrix B is initialized to zero. Using this low-rank decomposition, LoRA reduces
189
      the number of trainable parameters from d × q to r × (d + q), where r is much smaller than d and
      q. This technique can be applied on certain layers of a neural network model, h(x, θ), resulting
190
191
      in a re-parameterized version, hLoRA (x, θ̃), with a reduced number of trainable parameters. This
      reduction enables efficient fine-tuning of large models, making it feasible to apply the Kalman filter.

196   4.1   K ALMAN F ORMULATION FOR L O RA
197
198   Consider a pre-trained model in which certain layers are scaled down using the LoRA method. The
199   modified model is parameterized by a reduced set of trainable parameters θ̃k ∈ Rñ , where ñ ≪ n:
200
201
                                               ŷk = hLoRA (xk , θ̃k ).                                       (6)
202   Here, xk ∈ Rp denotes the model input and ŷk ∈ Rm represents the predicted output in the k th
203   observed data. Let us adopt yk as the true output. The ŷk represents the predicted values for the
204   regression tasks and the predicted probabilities for the classification tasks. In both scenarios, the
205   predicted output ŷk can be interpreted as the mean parameter of a Gaussian distribution over the
206   actual output yk . This relationship can be expressed as white noise as vk ∼ N (0, Rk ), where
207   Rk = Cov(yk |ŷk ). More broadly, ŷk serves as the mean parameter for an exponential family
208   distribution over T (yk ), where T (·) denotes the sufficient statistics for the exponential family. In this
209
      case, Rk = Cov(T (yk )|ŷk ) denotes the covariance matrix of the exponential family distribution.
210   Several approximations for the matrix Rk have been proposed in the literature. One common ap-
211   proach is to approximate it as the identity matrix, Rk ≈ I, as shown in (Puskorius & Feldkamp,
212   1991; Murtuza & Chorian, 1994). Other formulations include Rk = I · e−k/50 (Singhal & Wu,
213   1988; 1989), and a more recent approximation, Rk = diag(ŷk ) − ŷk ŷk⊤ (Ollivier, 2018; Chang
214   et al., 2022). To obtain a more precise approximation of Rk , we employ an Exponential Moving
215   Average (EMA) approach based on the definition of the covariance matrix for estimating the matrix
      Rk . We make the simplifying assumption that θ̃k ≈ θ̃k|k−1 , allowing us to compute the covariance

224   LoKO estimates the (sub-)optimal values of LoRA matrices A and B in real-time data streams
225   through the Kalman algorithm. To formulate the online fine-tuning problem within the Kalman fil-
226   tering framework, we assume there are no feedback loops in machine learning model, for example, a
227
      feed-forward neural network and transformers. This assumption enables us to consider the trainable

229   process model (or transition function) can be modeled as an identity function f (xk , θ̃k−1 ) = θ̃k−1
230

233
      of θ̃k .
234   Although the low-rank decomposition by LoRA offers a significant reduction in parameter size com-
235   pared to the original parameter space n, the size of the covariance matrix P scales quadratically with
236   the number of trainable parameters ñ. For large models such as deep neural networks, characterized
237   by high-dimensional trainable parameters, the computational cost of implementing the Kalman al-
238   gorithm becomes prohibitively expensive due to its ñ2 complexity. To address this challenge, one
239
      strategy involves decoupling the update phase of the Kalman filtering algorithm into smaller par-
      titions, as outlined by Puskorius & Feldkamp (1991), which, however, may be infeasible for large
240

241

245                                             E[p̂] = diag(P ).                                        (8)

284   4.2   I NITIALIZATION OF P & A PPROXIMATION OF R
285
286   Initialization of p̂0 : Our findings demonstrate that the initialization of p̂0 plays a crucial role in
287   the performance of the filter as a training algorithm. High values of p̂0 indicate high uncertainty or
288
      lack of confidence in the initial learning parameters, which can result in the Kalman filter making
      large corrections and experiencing potential instability and divergence. In contrast, initialization
289
      p̂0 with very low values suggests high confidence in the initial learning parameters. In this case,
290
      the filter will heavily trust the initial model parameters. If these initial parameters are inaccurate,
291   this can lead to slow updates or even no updates at all, as the filter gives insufficient weight to new
292   measurements. Proper initialization of p̂0 balances these extremes, ensuring that the filter can adapt
293   appropriately to new data while maintaining stability and accuracy throughout the training process.
294   Therefore, it is essential to establish both upper and lower bounds for p̂0 . To ensure the initialization
295   of p̂0 = [p1 , p2 , ...pi , ..., pn ] yields a positive definite diagonal matrix, we examined two methods
296   for initialization of p̂0 :
297
298          • Method 1: Setting a constant positive value: pi = c ∀i.
299          • Method 2: Assigning random positive values drawn from a uniform distribution: pi ∼
300            U (0, upper bound) ∀i.
301
302   Our experiments show that precisely estimating the Rk matrix greatly influences the bounds of p̂0 .
303
      The more accurate the estimation of Rk , the broader the acceptable range for the initial p̂0 .

      6   C ONCLUSION
525
526
527   In this study, we present the Low-Rank Kalman Optimizer (LoKO), a Kalman-based training
528   algorithm. LoKO offers a comparative alternative to advanced gradient-based optimizers for
529   online fine-tuning scenarios. By leveraging the low-rank adaptation technique, and a diagonal
530   approximation of the covariance matrix and incorporating a novel observation noise estimation
531   technique based on EMA, we reformulate the EKF algorithm accordingly. Empirical evaluations
532
      on the benchmark of computer vision and language models, including MNIST, CIFAR-10/100,
      ImageNet100, SST-2, COLA, and MRPC demonstrate that LoKO consistently achieves superiority
533
      over (or at least comparability with) commonly used optimizers, showcasing its potential for
534
      efficient online fine-tuning of large models. Although this paper focuses on online fine-tuning in
535
      computer vision and language models for classification tasks, further evaluations are necessary
536
      across a broader range of domains, such as reinforcement learning. We leave these explorations for
537
      future work.

      C     C OMPUTATIONAL A NALYSIS
880
881   C.1   C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY
882
883   To analyze the computational complexity of our proposed algorithm, let n represent the number
884   of parameters, ñ the number of trainable parameters (ñ ≪ n), and m the number of model outputs.
885   The computational complexities of each step in the LoKO algorithm compared to the vanilla Kalman
886   filter are as follows:
887
888
889   Prediction: The computational complexity of both LoKO and the vanilla Kalman filter for the
890   Prediction step is O(1).
891
892
893
      Pre-Updating: For LoKO, estimating the observation noise covariance using equation 7 has a
894   complexity of O(m2 ). When using equation 12, the complexity increases to O(m2 ñ). In contrast,
895   for the vanilla Kalman filter, the complexity for equation 7 is O(m2 ), while for equation 12 it is
896   O(m2 n + n2 m).
897
898
      Updating: The computational complexity for LoKO when calculating the Kalman gain ( equa-
899
      tion 11a) is O(m3 + m2 ñ), while for the vanilla Kalman filter (using equation 3a), it will be
900
901
      O(m3 + m2 n + n2 m). For updating parameters (equation 11b), LoKO has a complexity of O(mñ),
902
      compared to O(mn) for the vanilla Kalman filter. And finally, the complexity of covariance updat-
903
      ing (equation 11c) in LoKO is O(ñ), while for the vanilla Kalman filter (using equation 3c), it will
904   be O(n2 m).
905   Thus, the total computational complexity in the worst-case scenario, for LoKO is O(m3 + m2 ñ),
906   and for the vanilla Kalman filter will be O(m3 + m2 n + n2 m). In cases where the number of pa-
907   rameters is significantly larger than the output size, the dominant term for LoKO is O(m2 ñ), while
908   for the vanilla Kalman filter, it will be O(n2 m). Therefore, LoKO reduces the computational com-
909   plexity from quadratic to linear in the number of parameters as well as decreasing the number of
910   trainable parameters (ñ ≪ n).
