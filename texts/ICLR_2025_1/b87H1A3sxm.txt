                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
               It is known that deep neural networks (DNNs) are vulnerable to imperceptible
012
               adversarial attacks, and this fact raises concerns about their safety and reliabil-
013            ity in real-world applications. In this paper, we aim to boost the robustness of
014            a DNN against white-box adversarial attacks by defining three new information
015            quantities—robust conditional mutual information (CMI), robust separation, and
016            robust normalized CMI (NCMI)—which can serve as robust performance met-
017            rics for the DNN. We then utilize these concepts to introduce a novel training
018            method that constrains the robust CMI and increases the robust separation si-
019            multaneously. Our experimental results demonstrate that our method consistently
020            enhances model robustness against C&W and AutoAttack on CIFAR and Tiny-
021
               ImageNet datasets with and without additional synthetic data. Specifically, it
               is shown that our approach improves the robust accuracy of a DNN by up to
022
               2.66% on CIFAR datasets and 3.49% on Tiny-ImageNet in the case of PGD at-
023
               tack and 1.70% on CIFAR datasets and 1.63% on Tiny-ImageNet in the case of
024            AutoAttack, in comparison with the state-of-the-art training methods in the lit-
025            erature. Our implementation is publicly available at https://github.com/
026            ICLR2025-Robust-NCMI/ICLR2025-Robust-NCMI.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Despite the remarkable success of deep neural networks (DNNs) in computer vision (Krizhevsky
032   et al., 2012; He et al., 2016a) and natural language processing (Vaswani et al., 2017; Devlin et al.,
033   2019), DNNs are found to be vulnerable to adversarial attacks (Szegedy et al., 2013; Goodfellow
034   et al., 2015). These attacks generate adversarial sample instances by adding slight perturbations, im-
035   perceptible to human eyes, to the original benign sample instances to deceive the underlying DNN.
036   This raises concern to apply deep learning (DL) models to safety-critical domains like autonomous
037   driving and medical diagnosis (Kurakin et al., 2018; Finlayson et al., 2019).
038   A simple, yet effective method to train robust DNNs against adversarial attacks is adversarial training
039   (AT) (Goodfellow et al., 2015; Madry et al., 2018). In AT, the model is trained not on benign
040   sample instances, but on adversarial ones. This process is formulated as a min-max optimization
041   problem: the inner maximization focuses on generating adversarial sample instances, while the outer
042   minimization aims to reduce the adversarial loss associated with these attacked sample instances.
043   Following Madry et al. (2018), a significant body of work has focused on improving model robust-
044   ness, primarily through four approaches: (i) modifying or adding additional regularization terms to
045   the loss function (Zhang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020), (ii) altering model archi-
046   tecture (Xie et al., 2019), (iii) applying data augmentation techniques (Rebuffi et al., 2021), and (iv)
047   utilizing strategies like early stopping and weight averaging (Rice et al., 2020; Gowal et al., 2020).
048   Even with these enhancements of AT, however, the underlying vulnerability of DNNs against adver-
049   sarial attacks remains unresolved: data sample instances which are near the decision boundary of
050
      DNNs, are in general more susceptible to perturbation and likely to cause misclassification (Zhang
      et al., 2021).
051
052   Now regard a classification DNN as a mathematical mapping from raw data x ∈ Rd to a probability
053   distribution f (x) over the set of class labels, predicting an output label ŷ with probability f (x)[ŷ] in
      response to input x. To mitigate the issue above, we are inspired by the work Yang et al. (2023) and

055   properties in the output probability distribution space. Specifically, for each sample instance x, let
056   x′ denote its adversarial instance, and refer to f (x′ ) as an adversarial output probability distribution
057   for x. In the output probability distribution space, the set of adversarial distributions f (x′ ) for all
058   sample instances x with the same ground truth label y forms a cluster, which is referred to as an ad-
059   versarial cluster corresponding to y hereafter. Following Yang et al. (2023), the concentration of this
060   adversarial cluster can be measured by the conditional mutual information (CMI) I(X ′ ; Ŷ ′ |Y = y),
061   where X ′ is an adversarial sample corresponding to the benign sample X, Ŷ ′ is the random label
062   predicted by the DNN in response to the input X ′ with probability f (X ′ )[Ŷ ′ ], and Y is the ground
063   truth label of X. Here X, Y , X ′ , and Ŷ ′ are all random variables. Averaging over all labels y with
064   respect to the distribution PY (y) of Y , the CMI I(X ′ ; Ŷ ′ |Y ) then measures the average concentra-
065   tion across all adversarial clusters from a given attack method. If for each label y, the centroid of
066   the adversarial cluster corresponding to y is close to the one hot probability vector corresponding to
067   y, then the smaller I(X ′ ; Ŷ ′ |Y ) is, the less likelihood the attack method has to succeed.

082   From the perspective of information theory (or information geometry), the robustness of the DNN
083   x → f (x) can also be gauged by its robust CMI, robust separation, and robust NCMI. To enhance

134   As an AT defense mechanism, vanilla AT (Madry et al., 2018) showed great success by training a
135

139   boundaries (Ding et al., 2020; Rade & Moosavi-Dezfooli, 2022; Xu et al., 2023b) or by assigning
140   greater weights to vulnerable examples near such boundaries (Liu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021).
141   Despite all above methods achieved good robust performance, they considered each attacked data
142   point individually, and overlooked to view adversarial samples with the same label as a cluster.
143
      In our approach, we argue that robustness can be further enhanced by constraining adversarial exam-
144   ples from a cluster perspective, ensuring that the worst-case perturbed samples within a class remain
145   as close to the centroid of their corresponding adversarial cluster as possible. Furthermore, since
146   previous research demonstrated that additional labelled or unlabelled data (Schmidt et al., 2018;
147   Carmon et al., 2019; Uesato et al., 2019) as well as synthetic data created by generative models
148   (Gowal et al., 2021; Rebuffi et al., 2021; Sehwag et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023) can largely boost
149   robust performance, we evaluate our method on datasets both with and without synthetic data, and
150   present our methodology and results in the following sections.

532   6      C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we have introduced three new information quantities—robust CMI, separation and
535   NCMI—to gauge the robust performance of DNNs. Based on these robust performance metrics, we
536   have developed a new generic adversarial training framework and alternating learning algorithms
537   to jointly minimize robust NCMI and the conventional adversarial training objective functions. Ex-
538   tensive experimental results show that our method consistently improves model robustness against
539   various white-box attacks when combined with existing adversarial loss functions, demonstrating
      the ‘plug-and-play’ nature and effectiveness of our method in the field of AT.

1211    In this subsection, we visualize the effect of our adversarial CMI and adversarial separation under
1212    different constraining levels when the underlying DNN architecture is PreAct-ResNet-18 (He et al.,
1213    2016b). We first randomly picked 3 classes from the CIFAR-10 validation set, then focused on
1214
        a subset of validation samples with these labels perturbed by C&W∞ attack (Carlini & Wagner,
        2017) in 40 steps. Each sample from this subset is fed into the DNN, and only the three logits
1215
        corresponding to the selected 3 labels are retained. These logits are subsequently converted into a
1216
        3-dimensional probability vector using the softmax operation. After the steps mentioned above, we
1217    further projected all obtained 3-dimensional probability vectors from the samples in the constructed
1218    validation subset into a 2-dimensional simplex to visualize the concentration and separation effect
1219    of our trained robust DNNs in Fig. 7 and 8.
1220
        We compare the effect of varing constraining levels on adversarial CMI and separation, utilizing
1221
        vanilla AT (Madry et al., 2018) as the base adversarial loss function, in Fig. 7 and 8, respectively.
1222
        With different constraining levels on adversarial CMI, one can observe that a larger value of α en-
1223    force each adversarial cluster more compact around its centroid, while different clusters are closer
1224    to each other at the same time. We suggest that this creates a trade-off, as a stronger constraining
1225    level on intra-class concentration (adversarial CMI) might weaken the regularization effect on robust
1226    accuracy. This also justifies the necessity of constraining on adversarial separation, which further
1227    separates perturbed samples with different labels apart. As shown in Fig. 8, a stronger constrain-
1228    ing level on adversarial separation (larger β) enlarge the margins between each pair of adversarial
1229    clusters, and further push them to the corresponding corner point with a negligible affect on concen-
1230    tration within each adversarial cluster.
1231    In general, since our method enforces data points from the same class to be more concentrated
1232    around the centroid of corresponding cluster and different clusters to be more separated from each
1233    other, the overlapping areas in the simplex among different classes presented above are smaller than
1234    that of the baseline method (top-left subplots of Fig. 7 with title ‘α = 0, β = 0’), especially when
1235    α = 1 and β is set to 2 or 5 (bottom row of Fig. 8). As such, there are less data points near
1236    the decision boundaries and susceptible against adversarial attacks under our proposed framework
1237
        comparing with baseline, thereby enhances the robustness of a model.

1533   In Yang et al. (2023), the authors mentioned that in benign image classification task, training a DNN
1534   with constraining NCMI on benign samples leads to improved robustness compared to training with
1535   only CE loss. Their experimental results indicate that models trained with CMIC-DL framework are
1536   more robust against FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2015) and PGD attacks (Madry et al., 2018) under
1537
       various levels of perturbation budgets than standard DNNs trained with CE loss in MNIST dataset
       (LeCun et al., 1998). In our experiments, we also observed that constraining NCMI on benign
1538
       samples alongside TRADES (Zhang et al., 2019) enhances DNNs’ robustness on CIFAR-{10, 100}
1539
       datasets, both with and without additional synthetic data. We mostly followed the experimental
1540   setting of Yang et al. (2023), where we additionally sample 8 instances per class (64 in original
1541   CIFAR-10) in each mini-batch and use such sampled instances to update centroid of each benign
1542   cluster per mini-batch with a WA factor τQ = 0.999 in original CIFAR-100. In experiments on
1543   CIFAR datasets with an additional 1 million synthetic images, we increased the sample size for
1544   centroid updates in each mini-batch by a factor of 4, aligning with the batch size, which was 4 times
1545   compared to the original CIFAR datasets. We also adjusted the WA factor τQ to 0.99.
1546   Indicated in Table 11, constraining NCMI over benign samples also enhances adversarial robust-
1547   ness of a DNN in comparison with models trained with standard TRADES as objective function.
1548   To interpret the effectiveness of constraining NCMI over benign samples, we argue that the output
1549   probability distributions of a DNN become more compact within each benign sample cluster and
1550   better separated among different clusters comparing with the counterpart trained without constrain-
1551   ing on benign NCMI. This approach makes it harder for adversarial attacks to create perturbations
1552   that would shift a sample into a cluster with a different label in output probability space, thereby im-
1553   proving robust performance. However, we found that constraining NCMI on benign samples cannot
