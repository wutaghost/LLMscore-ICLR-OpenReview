                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               Large linear systems are ubiquitous in modern computational science and engi-
015
               neering. The main recipe for solving them is the use of Krylov subspace iterative
016
               methods with well-designed preconditioners. Deep learning models can be used as
017            nonlinear preconditioners during the iteration of linear solvers such as the conju-
018            gate gradient (CG) method. Neural network models require an enormous number
019            of parameters to approximate well in this setup. Another approach is to take ad-
020            vantage of small graph neural networks (GNNs) to construct preconditioners with
021            predefined sparsity patterns. Recently, GNNs have been shown to be a promis-
022            ing tool for designing preconditioners to reduce the overall computational cost of
023            iterative methods by constructing them more efficiently than with classical lin-
024            ear algebra techniques. However, preconditioners designed with these approaches
025
               cannot outperform those designed with classical methods in terms of the number
               of iterations in CG. In our work, we recall well-established preconditioners from
026
               linear algebra and use them as a starting point for training the GNN to obtain pre-
027
               conditioners that reduce the condition number of the system more significantly.
028            Numerical experiments show that our approach outperforms both classical and
029            neural network-based methods for an important class of parametric partial differ-
030            ential equations. We also provide a heuristic justification for the loss function used
031            and show that preconditioners obtained by learning with this loss function reduce
032            the condition number in a more desirable way for CG.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Modern computational science and engineering problems are often based on parametric partial dif-
038   ferential equations (PDEs). The lack of analytical solutions for realistic engineering problems (heat
039   transfer, fluid flow, structural mechanics, etc.) leads researchers to exploit advances in numerical
040   analysis. The basic numerical methods for solving PDEs, such as finite element, finite difference,
041
      finite volume and meshless methods (e.g., smoothed particle hydrodynamics), result in a system of
      linear equations Ax = b, A ∈ Rn×n , x ∈ Rn , and b ∈ Rn . These systems are usually sparse,
042
      i.e. the number of non-zero elements is ≪ n2 . Furthermore, some classes of parametric PDEs
043
      are characterized by a very large dimension of the parametric space and by a high variation of the
044   parameters for a given sample.
045
046   Typically, the application of parametric PDEs produces large linear systems, often with entries of
047
      varying scale, and therefore poses significant computational challenges. Projection Krylov subspace
      iterative methods are widely used to solve such systems. They rely on finding an optimal solution in
048
      a subspace constructed as follows: Kr (A, b) = span{b, Ab, A2 b, . . . , Ar−1 }..
049
050   The conjugate gradient (CG) method is used to solve large sparse systems with symmetric and pos-
051   itive definite (SPD) matrices (Saad, 2003; Axelsson, 1996). CG has a well-established convergence
052   theory and convergence
                        p      guarantees for any symmetric matrix. However, the convergence rate of CG
053   is determined by κ(A). The condition number κ(A) of an SPD matrix       A, defined in 2-norm, is a
      ratio between the maximum and minimum eigenvalues κ(A) = |λmax | |λmin |.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Real-world applications with non-smooth high-contrast coefficient functions and high-dimensional
055   linear systems separate eigenvalues and results into ill-conditioned problems. Decades of research in
056   numerical linear algebra have been devoted to constructing preconditioners P for ill-conditioned A
057   to improve the condition number in the form (for left-preconditioned systems) κ(P −1 A) ≪ κ(A).
058
      The well-designed preconditioner should tend to approximate A, be easily invertible and be sparse
059
      or admit an efficient matrix-vector product. The construction of a preconditioner is typically a trade-
060
      off between the quality of the approximation and the cost of storage/inversion of the preconditioner
061   (Saad, 2003).
062
063   In this paper, we propose a novel neural method for preconditioner design called PreCorrector (short
064
      for Preconditioner Corrector). Preconditioners constructed with PreCorrector have better effect on
      the spectrum than classical preconditioners by learning correction for the latter. Our contributions
065
      are as follows:
066
067
            1. We propose a novel scheme for preconditioner design based on learning correction for
068
               well-established preconditioners from linear algebra with the GNN.
069
070         2. We propose an understanding of the loss function used with emphasis on low frequencies.
071            We also provide experimental justification for the understanding of learning with such an
072
               objective.
073         3. We propose a novel dataset generation approach with a measurable complexity metric that
074            addresses real-world problems.
075
            4. We provide extensive experiments with varying matrix sizes and dataset complexities to
076
               demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach and loss function over classical pre-
077            conditioners.

080   2   N EURAL DESIGN OF PRECONDITIONER
081
082   Related work While there are a dozen different preconditioners in linear algebra, for example
083   (Saad, 2003; Axelsson, 1996): block Jacobi preconditioner, Gauss-Seidel preconditioner, sparse ap-
084   proximate inverse preconditioner, algebraic multigrid methods, etc., the choice of preconditioner
085   depends on the specific problem, and practitioners often rely on a combination of theoretical un-
086   derstanding and numerical experimentation to select the most effective preconditioner. Even a brief
087
      description of all of them is beyond the scope of a single research paper. One can refer to the related
      literature for more details
088
089   The growing popularity of neural operators for learning mappings between infinite dimensional
090   spaces (e.g., (Hao et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2024; Raonic et al., 2024)) is also present in recent work
091   on using neural networks to speed up iterative solvers. The FCG-NO (Rudikov et al., 2024) approach
092   combines neural operators with the conjugate gradient method to act as a nonlinear preconditioner
093
      for the flexible conjugate gradient method (Notay, 2000). This method uses a proven convergence
      bound as a training loss. A novel class of hybrid preconditioners (Kopaničáková & Karniadakis,
094
      2024) combines DeepONet with standard iterative methods to solve parametric linear systems. This
095
      framework uses DeepONet for low-frequency error components and conventional methods for high-
096   frequency components. The HINTS (Zhang et al., 2022) method integrates traditional relaxation
097   techniques with DeepONet. It targets different spectral regions, ensuring a uniform convergence
098   rate. It is also possible to use convolutional neural networks to speed up multigrid method (Azulay
099   & Treister, 2022; Li et al., 2024), which require materialization of sparse matrices into dense format.
100   However, these approaches can suffer from the curse of dimensionality when applied to large linear
101   systems and can be too expensive to apply at each iteration step.
102   The authors of (Li et al., 2023; Häusner et al., 2023) present a novel approach to preconditioner
103   design using GNNs that aim to approximate the matrix factorization and use it as a preconditioner.
104   These approaches use shallow GNNs and typically require a single inference before the iteration
105   process. GNNs take the initial left hand side matrix and right hand side vector as input and construct
106   preconditioners in the form of a Cholesky decomposition. However, these GNNs cannot produce
107   preconditioners that have a better effect on the condition number of the solving system than their
      classical analogues.

      Problem statement We consider systems of linear algebraic equations from the discretization of
109   differential operators Ax = b formed with a symmetric positive definite (SPD) matrix A ≻ 0. One
110   can use Gaussian elimination of complexity O(n3 ) to solve small linear systems, but not real-world
111   problems, which produce large and ill-conditioned systems.
112
113   Preconditioned linear systems Before solving initial systems by iterative methods, we want to
114   obtain a preconditioner P such that the preconditioned linear system P −1 Ax = P −1 b has a lower
115   condition number than the initial system. If one knows the sparsity pattern of A, then possible
116   options are incomplete LU decompositions (ILU) (Saad, 2003): (i) with p-level of fill-in denoted
117   as ILU(p) and (ii) ILU decomposition with threshold with p-level of fill-in denoted as ILUt(p).
118   Additional information about these preconditioners can be found in the Appendix A.7.
119   In this paper we focus on the SPD matrices so instead of ILU, ILU(p) and ILUt(p) we use the
120   incomplete Choletsky factorization IC, IC(p) and ICt(p). Further, we will form the preconditioners
121   in the form of Choletsky decomposition (Trefethen & Bau, 2022) P = LL⊤ with sparse L obtained
122   by different methods.
123
124   Preconditioners with neural networks Our utlimate goal is to find such a decomposition that
125   κ((L(θ)L(θ)⊤ )−1 A) ≪ κ((LL⊤ )−1 A) ≪ κ(A), where L is the classical numerical IC decompo-
126
      sition and L(θ) = F(A) is an approximate decomposition with some function F. Several papers (Li
      et al., 2023; Häusner et al., 2023) suggest using GNN as a function F to minimize certain loss func-
127
      tion:

168   3.1   G RAPH NEURAL NETWORK WITH PRESERVING SPARSITY PATTERN
169
      Following the idea of (Li et al., 2023), we use the message-passing GNN architecture (Zhou et al.,
170
      2020) to preserve the sparsity pattern and predict the lower triangular matrix to create a precondi-
171
      tioner in a form of IC decomposition.
172
173   The duality between sparse matrices and graphs is used to obtain vertices and edges, such as Ax =
174   b → G = (V, E), where ai,j = ei,j ∈ E, bi = vi ∈ V. The original GNN architecture from (Li et al.,
175
      2023):
176
177
            1. First step is to use node and edge encoders to increase their dimensionality with multi-layer
               perceptrons (MLPs): vi = MLPv (vi ), ei,j = MLPe (ei,j ).
178
179         2. Then the encoded graph is processed with T rounds of message passing (Brandstetter et al.,
180            2022) (t = 1, . . . , T ) to transfer information between vertices
                                                                           P        and edges. During a single
181            round, we update vertices with vi,t+1 = MLPmp,v (vi,t , j ei,j,t vj,t ), and then update the
182            edges with ei,j,t+1 = MLPmp,e (ei,j,t , vi,t+1 vj,t+1 ), for i ̸= j.
183
            3. Next step is to decode the lower triangular matrix while preserving the information in the
184            upper triangular part of the matrix. To do this we average the bidirectional edges, decode
185

               them with MLP and then zero out the upper triangular part: ei,j,T = ei,j,T + ej,i,T /2
186            and Li,j|i≤j = MLPdecod (ei,j,T ), Li,j|i>j = 0.
187
188         4. After all round of message passing the diagonal of the decomposition inherited as the diag-
189            p from original matrix to ensure SPD property in resulting decomposition diag(L(θ)) :=
               onal
190              diag(A).
191         5. Finally, assemble the preconditioner in a form of Choletsky decomposition P                 :=
192            L(θ)L(θ)⊤ .
193
194   In our experiments, we observe that training GNN from scratch can be unstable and results in pre-
195   conditioners that have weaker effect on the spectrum than their classical analogues. Moreover, with
196   growing matrix size, the very first step of training, when GNN is initialized with random weights,
197   overflows loss since the residual with random P is huge. We propose to solve both problems by
198   learning corrections to classical preconditioners.

200   3.2   P RE C ORRECTOR
201
202   Instead of passing left hand side matrix A as input to GNN in (1), we propose: (i) to pass L from the
203   IC decomposition to the GNN and (ii) to train GNN to predict a correction for this decomposition
204   (Figure 1):
205
206
                                         L(θ) = L + α · GNN(θ, L, b).                                     (6)
207
208
      The correction coefficient α is also a learning parameter that is updated during training. At the
209
      beginning of training, we set α = 0 to ensure that the first gradient updates come from pure IC
210   factorization. Since we already start with a good initial guess, we observed that pinning the diagonal
211   is redundant and limits the training of the PreCorrector. Moreover, GNN in (6) takes as input the
212   lower-triangular matrix L from IC instead of A, so we are not anchored to a single specific sparse
213   pattern of A and we can: (i) omit half of the graph and speed up the training process and (ii) use
214   different sparsity patterns to obtain even better preconditioners. In Experiment section, we show that
215   the proposed approach with input L from IC(0) and ICt(1) produces better preconditioners compared
      to classical IC(0) and ICt(1) and previous preconditioners using neural networks.

259   The diffusion equation is chosen because it occurs frequently in many engineering applications,

262

264   The condition number of a linear system depends on both the grid size and the contrast, but usually
265   in scientific machine learning research, high contrast is not taken into account. In Section 5.1 we
266
      demonstrate that the previous approach can handle growing matrix size with constant coefficients
      quite well, but faces problems with growing contrast in the coefficients.
267

269   complexity of the diffusion equation with discontinuous coefficients, we can measure the contrast in

293   Metrics The main comparison of preconditioners designed with different algorithms are made by
294
      comparing total time including preconditioner construction time and the number of CG iterations
      to achieve a given tolerance. For construction time, we report averaged values over 200 runs of
295
      preconditioner construction and for CG time and iterations we report averaged values over the test
296
      set as well as standard deviations for the average values. Construction time for PreCorrector is
297   reported including construction time of classical preconditioners.
298
299   The main idea behind using GNNs to construct preconditioners is to preserve the sparsity pattern.
300
      Therefore, the algorithmic complexity of using preconditioners (matrix-vector product) is the same
      when using preconditioners with the same sparsity pattern. This allows a fair evaluation of the
301
      quality of neural preconditioners with the same sparsity pattern only in terms of the number of
302
      CG iterations. Furthermore, all approaches to IC decomposition with the same sparsity pattern,
303   including the classical ones, compete with each other in terms of construction time, effect on the
304   spectrum (i.e., number of CG iterations) and generalization ability.
305

444   5.2   L OSS FUNCTION
445
446   The equivalence of the losses (3) and (5) allows to avoid explicit inverse materialization and provides
447   maximum complexity of the matrix-vector product in the loss during training. Recall that A comes
448   from the 5-point finite difference discretization of the diffusion equation (Section 4). A tends to a
449
      diagonal matrix with n → ∞ and we can assume that A is a diagonal matrix for sufficiently large
      linear systems. Minimizing a matrix product between the preconditioner and A−1 in (3) makes the
450
      eigenvalues tend to 1.
451
452   As mentioned in Section 2, one should focus on approximating the low frequency components. The

454   can be observed in Table 5. Moreover, the gap between the extreme eigenvalues is covered by the
455
      increase in the minimum eigenvalue, which supports the hypothesis of low frequency cancellation.

479   6     D ISCUSSION
480
481   In our work, we propose a neural design of preconditioners for the CG iterative method that can
482   outperform analogous classical preconditioners with the same sparsity pattern of the ILU family in
483   terms of both effect on the spectrum and total time-to-solution. Using the classical preconditioners
484   as a starting point and learning corrections for them, we achieve stable and fast training convergence
485   that can handle parametric PDEs with contrast coefficients. We also propose a complexity metric to
      measure the complexity of PDEs with random coefficients.

518
519   7   L IMITATIONS
520
521   The limitations of the proposed approach are as follows:
522
523
           1. Theoretical study of the loss function used. We provide only a heuristic understanding with
524           experimental justification for the loss function. A theoretical analysis of the loss function
525           is the subject of future research.
526
527        2. The target objective in norms other than ∥·∥F may provide a tighter bound on the spectrum.
528           Investigating the possible use of target values in other norms is a logical next step.
529        3. Experiments on other meshes and sparsity patterns of the resulting left hand side matrices
530           A. Generalization of the PreCorrector to transformation in the space of sparse matrices
531           with general sparsity patterns.
532
533        4. While the PreCorrector has only been tested on systems with SPD matrices from the dis-
534
              cretization of elliptic equations, further work will require generalization to irregular grids,
              non-symmetric problems, hyperbolic PDEs, nonlinear problems, to other iterative solvers
535
              such as GMRES and BiCGSTAB and modification of the preconditioner design accord-
536
              ingly.
537
538        5. The forcing term f (x) is sampled from the standard normal distribution, but the case of
539           complex forcing terms needs to be studied separately as it can also affect the complexity of
              solving parametric PDEs.

802   A.5   S CALABILITY
803
804   The PreCorrector is a combination of several MLPs with message-passing connections. When grow-
805   ing non-zero elements nnz (i.e., growing a matrix size) with the same sparsity pattern, the complexity
806   of the PreCorrector forward call grows linearly. The algorithm for IC(0) has O(nnz). We expect a
807   small increase in computational cost for larger systems, since with matrix grows we are only inter-
808   ested in the growth of non-zero elements in the matrix. The growth of non-zero elements is much less
809   severe than the growth of a matrix size due to the nature of matrices – discretization of PDEs. The
      PreCorrector works directly on the edges and nodes of the graph and can be easily data-parallelized.

855   A.7   I NCOMPLETE LU FACTORIZATION
856
857   Full LU decomposition (Trefethen & Bau, 2022) for a square non-singular matrix defined as the
858   product of the lower and upper triangular matrices B = LB UB . In general, these matrices have no
859
      restriction on the position and number of elements within their triangular structure and can even be
      dense for a sparse matrix B. On the other hand, an ILU is an approximate LU factorization:
860
861
862                                                    A ≈ LU,                                       (11)
863
      where LU − A satisfies certain constraints.


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Zero fill-in ILU, denoted ILU(0), is an approximate LU factorization A ≈ L0 U0 in such a way
865   that L0 has exactly the same sparsity pattern as the lower part of A and U0 has exactly the same
866   sparsity pattern as the upper part of A. For the ILU(p) decomposition the level of fill-in is defined
867   hierarchically. The product of the factors of the ILU(0) decomposition produces a new matrix B with
868   a larger number of non-zero elements. The factors of the ILU(1) factorization have the same sparsity
869   patterns as lower and upper parts of the sparsity pattern of the matrix B. With this recursion one gets
870   a pattern of the ILU(p) factorization with p-level of fill-in. ILU(0) is a typical choice to precondition
871   iterative solvers and relies only on the levels of fill-in, e.g. sparsity patterns (Saad, 2003). One can
872   obtain better approximation with ILU by using incomplete factorizations with thresholding.
873   One such technique is the ILU factorization with thresholding (ILUt(p)). The parameter p defines the
874   number of additional non-zeros allowed per column in the resulting factorization. For the ILUt(p)
875   decomposition, the algorithm is more complex and involves both dropping values by some prede-
876   fined threshold and controlling the number of possible non-zero values in the factorization. In the
877   case of ILUt(p), the value p represents additional non-zero values allowed in the factorization per
878   row. The thresholding algorithm provides a more flexible and effective way to approximate the in-
879
      verse of a matrix, especially for realistic problems where the numerical values of the matrix elements
      are important.
880
881   The complexity of solving sparse linear systems with matrices in the form of the Choletsky decom-
882   position defined by the number of non-zero elements O(nnz). This value also defines the storage
883   complexity and the complexity of preconditioner construction.
