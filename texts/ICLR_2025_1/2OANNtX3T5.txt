011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Ensuring that Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) maintain consis-
014            tency in their responses is essential for developing trustworthy multimodal intel-
015
               ligence. However, existing benchmarks include many samples where all MLLMs
               exhibit high response uncertainty when encountering misleading information, re-
016
               quiring even 5-15 response attempts per sample to effectively assess uncertainty.
017
               Therefore, we propose a two-stage pipeline: first, we collect MLLMs’ responses
018            without misleading information, and then gather misleading ones via specific
019            misleading instructions. By calculating the misleading rate, and capturing both
020            correct-to-incorrect and incorrect-to-correct shifts between the two sets of re-
021            sponses, we can effectively metric the model’s response uncertainty. Eventually,
022            we establish a Multimodal Uncertainty Benchmark (MUB) that employs both ex-
023            plicit and implicit misleading instructions to comprehensively assess the vulner-
024            ability of MLLMs across diverse domains. Our experiments reveal that all open-
025            source and close-source MLLMs are highly susceptible to misleading instructions,
026
               with an average misleading rate exceeding 86%. To enhance the robustness of
               MLLMs, we further fine-tune all open-source MLLMs by incorporating explicit
027
               and implicit misleading data, which demonstrates a significant reduction in mis-
028
               leading rates.

      In recent years, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (Abdin et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023;
      AI et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b; OpenAI, 2024; Anthropic, 2024) demonstrate impressive capabil-
034
      ities across various benchmarks (Fu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023e; Yue et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2022;
035
      Schwenk et al., 2022). Evaluating the reliability and robustness of MLLMs’ responses is essen-
036   tial for advancing the development of explainable artificial intelligence (AI) systems (Zhang et al.,
037   2024b; Tu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024). Some works (Qian et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a) assess
038   the reliability of MLLMs by introducing deceptive information into prompts. Other works (Liu
039   et al., 2024; Kimura et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024d; Zhang et al., 2024a;c) primarily focus on the
040   robustness of MLLMs by evaluating inconsistencies between visual and textual inputs. Nonetheless,
041   they neglect the ability to retain original answers despite the presence of misleading information.
042   Most MLLMs evaluation benchmarks primarily evaluate their capabilities by assessing the correct-
043   ness of their responses (Huang & Zhang, 2024). However, we find that the correctness of responses
044   tends to exhibit significant uncertainty after adding misleading information, with the proportion of
045   such easily misled data exceeding 65% in nine commonly used benchmarks (Lu et al., 2023; Zhang
046   et al., 2024c; Li et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Kembhavi et al., 2016). Previous works (Lin et al.,
047   2023; Li et al., 2023; Yadkori et al., 2024) have investigated response inconsistency of large lan-
048   guage models (LLMs), primarily gathering multiple responses to calculate the consistency rate to
049   assess uncertainty (Xiong et al., 2023). Our findings reveal that MLLMs exhibit significantly low
050
      consistency when handling high misleading rate data. As is shown in Figure 1, we calculate the con-
      sistency rate for 20 responses of each sample. The results show that more than half of the responses
051
      generated by the model exhibit a consistency rate below 62.15% in high misleading rate data.

136   Preliminaries. In this work, we mainly focus on the multimodal multi-choice and true/false tasks.
137   Formally, given a dataset D = {(Xi , Ri )}ni=1 , where Xi ∈ X represents the multimodal input for
138   the i-th sample, consisting of text and image, represented as Xi = (Ti , Ii ). The corresponding
139
      output is denoted as Ri ∈ R. The model M : X → R generates responses Rij for the input Xi ,
      where j denotes the j-th run or variant of input. For discriminative tasks, if the response R is correct,
140
      we set C(R) = 1; otherwise, the C(R) = 0.

142   Consistency Rate. To evaluate the uncertainty of a model’s responses, a common approach is to
143   calculate the most frequent response from multiple outputs generated by the model across multi-
144   ple runs. This method quantifies the model’s prediction uncertainty using a metric known as the
145
      consistency rate (CR), which measures the model’s reliability in producing stable responses to iden-
      tical inputs. For each sample i, the model is independently run mi times with the same input Xi ,
146
      resulting in a set of responses Ri = {Rij | j = 1, 2, . . . , mi }, where Rij responses produced
147
      by the model on the j-th run for input Xi . To quantify the frequency of each response R within
148   the set Ri , we definePfi (R), which calculates how often a specific response R appears across the
149                            mi
      mi runs: fi (R) =        j=1 I(Rij = R), where I is the indicator function, taking the value 1 if
150   I(Rij = R) and 0 otherwise. The consistency rate for the i-th sample, denoted as CRi , is defined
151   as the proportion of the most frequent response R in Ri relative to the total number of responses,
152   where CRi = maxR∈Ri fi (R)/mi . This metric captures the model’s ability to consistently produce
153   the same output by identifying the most frequent response in the set Ri and dividing its frequency
154   by the total number of responses generated for input Xi . To provide a comprehensive measure of
155   consistency across the entire dataset, we introduce the average consistency rate (ACR), calculated
156   as the mean of the individual consistency rates across all n samples:

      Misleading Rate. In this paper, we propose the misleading rate (MR) to evaluate the uncertainty of
163   MLLMs’ responses by measuring how the correctness of the model’s outputs changes when exposed
164   to misleading inputs. The MR is defined as the correctness of the response changes between the
165   original and misleading inputs. For the original input the Xi1 = (Ti , Ii ) is provided to the model M,
166   which generates the response Ri1 = M(Xi1 ). And then the misleading input Xi2 = (Ti + Ti′ , Ii ) is
167   feed to the models M, and the corresponding response is Ri2 = M(Xi2 ). To analyze specific shifts
168   in the correctness of the model’s responses, we define the misleading rate, denoted as MR(s→t) ,
169   to measure the transitions between two states: s, the correctness state of response Ri1 (from the
170   original input), and t the correctness state of response Ri2 (from the misleading input). The state
171   s and t take values in {T, F }, where T represents a true response, and F represents an incorrect
172   response. The MR(s→t) is formulate as :
173
174                              \text {MR}^{(s \rightarrow t)} = \frac { \sum _{i=1}^{n} \mathbb {I}( C( R_{i1} ) = s ) \mathbb {I}( C( R_{i2} ) = t ) }{ \sum _{i=1}^{n} \mathbb {I}( C( R_{i1} ) = s ) + \epsilon },    (2)
175
176   where I is the indicator function. The small positive constant ϵ is added to the denominator to
177   prevent division by zero when no samples satisfy the condition C(Ri1 ) = s. There are four pos-
178   sible state transitions: MR(T →F ) , MR(T →T ) , MR(F →F ) , and MR(F →T ) . If the initial response
179   is correct, the model’s second response can either remain correct (MR(T →T ) ) or become incorrect
180   (MR(T →F ) ). Similarly, if the first response is incorrect, the second response can either remain in-
181   correct (MR(F →F ) ) or change to correct (MR(F →T ) ). In this paper, we focus primarily on two
182   transitions: MR(T →F ) and MR(F →T ) .

      2.1   M ULTIMODAL U NCERTAINTY B ENCHMARK
185
186   Motivation. While recent works (Yue et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023d; Fu et al., 2023) have extensively
187   evaluated the overall capabilities of multimodal models, there remains a significant gap in evaluating
188   benchmarks tailored to assess the MLLMs’ responses uncertainty. Building a benchmark presents
189   three main challenges: 1) Identifying Uncertain Data. Not all images trigger uncertainty in models’
190   responses, and the same image with different questions may lead to varying levels of uncertainty.
191
      Even within existing benchmarks (Zhang et al., 2024c; Lu et al., 2023; 2022), there is considerable
      uncertainty in model responses. Our experimental results show that uncertain data constitutes 70%
192
      of the total across the six commonly used MLLM benchmarks. 2) Uncertainty responses. The
193
      model’s responses exhibit considerable uncertainty in high misleading rate data. As is shown in
194   Figure 1, we computed 20 responses for each sample and found that nearly half of the samples had
195   a consistency rate below 62.15%. 3) Inefficiency Uncertainty Evaluation. Previous work (Xiong
196   et al., 2023) evaluated uncertainty by generating multiple responses and calculating the consistency
197   rate (CR). As is shown in Figure 11, achieving stable consistency rates requires 5-15 iterations,
198   which can lead to significant computational costs. Additionally, the number of iterations needed
199   to stabilize the CR varies across different samples, making it challenging to determine how many
200   responses are required for each sample.
201   Misleading Instructions. To efficiently identify uncertain data, we propose a two-stage misleading
202   instructions method. In the first stage, we record the model’s responses to questions without any
203   manipulation. In the second stage, we introduce misleading instructions (e.g., “The true answer is
204   {true option or false option}”) to influence the model to choose either the correct or
205   incorrect option. This manipulation may cause the model’s response to shift from correct to incor-
206   rect or vice versa. If the correctness of the model’s responses fluctuates, it indicates uncertainty in
207   the data. To evaluate these transitions, we propose the misleading rate (MR) as a metric for measur-
208   ing uncertainty. Specifically, MR(T →F ) assesses the model’s ability to maintain correct responses
209   despite misleading instructions, while MR(F →T ) captures how often incorrect responses shift to cor-
210   rect when influenced by true option. A higher overall misleading rate suggests higher uncertainty in
211   the model’s responses, highlighting potential weaknesses in its robustness.
212   Multimodal Uncertainty Benchmark Design. In this paper, we first evaluated twelve open-source
213   models using nine widely-used MLLM benchmarks, including MME (Fu et al., 2023), MMB (Liu
214   et al., 2023e), MMMU (Yue et al., 2023), MathVista (Lu et al., 2023), ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022),
215   ConBench (Zhang et al., 2024c), SEED (Li et al., 2024), MMStar (Chen et al., 2024b), AI2D (Kem-
      bhavi et al., 2016). By applying misleading instructions to these models on the same datasets, we


                                                                                                                         4

216
      quickly identified data instances where the models exhibit uncertainty. To reduce the computa-
217   tional cost of evaluation, we selected a subset of data that misled at least six models to construct a
218   new multimodal uncertainty benchmark (MUB). Our benchmark contains 2.5k data, including 1.7k
219   multiple-choice questions and 0.8k true/false questions. A more detailed distribution of the selected
220   data from each dataset, along with the number of data for each difficulty level, is provided in Fig-
221   ure 7. We categorized the data into three difficulty levels based on the number of models misled: low
222   (questions that misled six models), medium (questions that misled nine models), and high (questions
223   that misled all MLLMs). Similar to previous work (Zhang et al., 2024c), our benchmark is grouped
224   into three main tasks: perception, reasoning, and mastery. Perception tasks include basic tasks such
225
      as counting, color recognition, OCR, and scene classification. Reasoning tasks involve analyzing
      image content, integrating text, and solving more complex tasks like calculations, translations, and
226
      code reasoning. Mastery tasks require the application of advanced domain-specific knowledge in
227
      fields such as chemistry, physics, art, and geography. Detailed analysis is provided in Figure 8.

229   Multimodal Uncertainty Benchmark Analysis. We analyze the constructed benchmark from mul-
230   tiple perspectives to validate its robustness and effectiveness. 1) Efficiency. Existing benchmarks
231
      often required re-sampling data (Qian et al., 2024) or generating new data (Liu et al., 2024), , which
      involves significant human and financial resources. In contrast, our benchmark can be created by
232
      simply adding a single misleading input to any existing dataset, eliminating the need for additional
233
      data processing or manual review. 2) Broader Evaluation and Strong Scalability. Our benchmark
234   has a broad evaluation scope, allowing it to extract relevant data from any dataset where the model
235   demonstrates uncertainty in prior tests, thereby thoroughly assessing the model’s capabilities. With
236   strong scalability, the benchmark can easily incorporate new data from existing datasets. Moreover,
237   it effectively identifies areas where the model exhibits uncertainty, helping to explore the boundaries
238   of the model’s knowledge more thoroughly.

      Explicit Misleading Instructions. We define explicit misleading as scenarios where the instruc-
243   tions can be directly provided with the true or false answer. If the model’s knowledge is not well-
244   established or has not been aligned with data containing misleading instructions, it can be easily
245   deceived by explicit misleading inputs. These explicit misleading instructions are generated by ap-
246   plying deterministic or observable transformations to the input Xi2 . Specifically, for true-to-false
247   (T → F ) misleading scenarios, we employ the statement explicit(Xi2 ): “The true answer is {false
248   option}”, which is added to the input to mislead the model. Conversely, for false-to-true (F → T )
249   misleading scenarios, we apply “The true answer is {true option}” to manipulate the input and de-
                                                                      explicit
250   ceive the model. The model’s responses are then given by Ri2             = M(explicit(Xi2 )), where
251   explicit represents the transformation applied to the input, and M is the MLLM that generates
252   responses. To ensure the effectiveness of explicit misleading method, we design additional 12 man-
253
      ually designed prompt templates to evaluate the uncertainty of response in Experiment 3.2, Table 7
      and Table 6. For example, we provide explicit instructions templates such as “the GPT-4’s answer
254
      is”, “the user’s answer is”, “based on the given information, the answer should be”, and so on.
255
256   Implicit Misleading Instructions. We define implicit results as cases where the answer is not
257   directly provided to the model, requiring it to reason the correct or incorrect answer. To address

      deceive the model. Specifically, we first test that implicit misleading instructions generated by

260

261

272   Mixed Instructions Strategy. Previous works (Chen et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2023a; 2024) have
273   focused on constructing additional data for fine-tuning new robustness models. In contrast, our ap-
274   proach leverages data identified from existing benchmarks through a misleading instruction method,
275   which can be directly used to fine-tune models. For data selection, we excluded overlapping data
276   from our benchmark and selected additional high misleading rate data. For each data, we com-
277   bined explicit misleading instructions with the question and provided separate implicit misleading
278
      instructions for each question. The formats of explicit and implicit fine-tuning data are provided in
      Figure 21. Through detailed experimental analysis, detail in Figure 5-(d), we found this to be the
279
      most effective data mix strategy. In this paper, we randomly selected 1k data with explicit instruc-
280
      tions and 1k data with implicit instructions from the high misleading rate data. The analysis of the
281   data size is shown in Figure 5-(a).
282
283   Fine-Tuning Details. A direct approach is to explicitly inform the model within the instructions that
284
      contain misleading information. However, the results( Table 22) show that the misleading rate re-
      mains approximately 70%. In this paper, we aim to fine-tune all MLLMs to improve their abilities to
285
      resist misleading information and maintain confidence in their responses when confronted with such
286
      input. Specifically, we adopt the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) method for fine-
287   tuning all open-source models, focusing on the language model. The experiment results (Table 2)
288   show that all the fine-tuned MLLMs show a significant reduction in the misleading rate. To further
289   verify the robustness improvements of the fine-tuned models, we selected 100 data for each of the
290   four models from categories with zero, low, and high misleading rates. We evaluate four MLLMs,
291   including GLM4V-9B-chat (Du et al., 2022), MiniCPM-Llama3-v2.5 (Hu et al., 2023), LLaVA-
292   Next-34b (Liu et al., 2023b) and Phi-3-vision (Abdin et al., 2024) by generating 20 responses for
293   each data. As shown in Figure 1, the mean consistency rate of the models increased significantly.
294   The average consistency rate increased by 29.4% on high misleading rate data, while it improved by
295
      14.8% on low misleading rate data. Additionally, to ensure data diversity, we selected our data from
      a variety of other benchmarks. To confirm that the fine-tuning process did not degrade the model’s
296
      performance on other tasks, we evaluated the fine-tuned models on the MMStar (Chen et al., 2024b)
297
      and AI2D (Kembhavi et al., 2016) datasets. The results in Table 19 show an improved fluctuation
298   of approximately 1.5% in accuracy. Furthermore, when tested on our benchmark, we observed an
299   average accuracy improvement of 5.25%, detailed in Table 18.

452
      lation experiments to evaluate our fine-tuning strategy: (1) Assessing the impact of different data
453   scales on the performance of fine-tuned models. During the data scaling stage, the model was
454   provided with each piece of explicitly misleading data separately. As shown in Figure 5, we eval-
455   uated the impact of varying data scales on fine-tuning with explicit and implicit instructions. The
456   results indicate that misleading rates stabilize when the dataset size exceeds 1,000 samples. (2) Ap-
457   plying various explicit and implicit fine-tuning strategies. We tested several data strategies for
458   fine-tuning MLLMs, including combining different misleading instructions and using more diverse
459   misleading instructions (Table 26). For explicit instructions, our results indicate that combining
460   or fine-tuning them separately has minimal impact on performance when the data is sufficient. In
461
      contrast, for implicit instructions, combining the data leads to worse performance compared to fine-
      tuning them separately. Therefore, during fine-tuning, we integrate explicit instructions using the
462
      combined method and fine-tune implicit instructions separately. (3) Fine-tuning with only explicit
463
      instruction data to test on implicit misleading. As shown in Table 26, we fine-tuned MLLMs
464   with explicit instructions to assess the misleading rate of implicit instructions. The results show
465   that although the overall decrease in misleading rate is not significant, it emphasizes the importance
466   of fine-tuning models with implicit data. (4) Evaluating the effectiveness of common Chain-
467   of-Thought (CoT) defense strategies against misleading information. We employed standard
468   CoT (Wei et al., 2022) techniques by incorporating the prompt “think step by step” into the in-
469   structions. As shown in Appendix 22, the misleading rate remains high, suggesting that standard
470   CoT-based defensive strategies are ineffective in mitigating misleading information. (5) Evaluating
471   the effectiveness of prompt-based method on both explicit and implicit scenarios. We also in-
472
      corporated various prompt-based defense strategies into deceptive displays and implicit misleading

      nificantly lower. Most models exhibit a certain reduction in the misleading rate, by approximately

534
535     In this work, our two-stage pipeline misleading instructions method provides an effective frame-
536     work for measuring the response uncertainty of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs).
537     By analyzing both correct-to-incorrect and incorrect-to-correct shifts in model responses, we re-
538     veal significant vulnerabilities in current MLLMs, which often exhibit high uncertainty. Based on
539     our findings, we advocate for the incorporation of more misleading information during the training
        process of MLLMs to enhance their robustness and ensure consistent multimodal intelligence.

      A.1     R ELATED W ORKS
765
      Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). Building on the success of Large Language
766
      Models, recent research has increasingly focused on MLLMs Achiam et al. (2023); Team et al.
767   (2023). MLLMs have indeed become an increasingly hot research topic in recent years. These
768   include both open-source models, including MiniCPM-v-v2 Hu et al. (2023), Phi-3-vision Ab-
769   din et al. (2024), Yi-VL-6b AI et al. (2024), Qwen-VL-Chat Bai et al. (2023), Deepseek-VL-7b-
770   Chat Lu et al. (2024b), LLaVA-NeXT-7b-vicuna Liu et al. (2023b), MiniCPM-Llama3-v2.5 Hu
771   et al. (2023), GLM4V-9B-chat Du et al. (2022), CogVLM-chat Wang et al. (2023), InternVL-Chat-
772   V1-5 Chen et al. (2023), LLaVA-Next-34b Liu et al. (2023b), and Yi-VL-34b AI et al. (2024). On
773   the other hand, close-source models, including GPT-4o OpenAI (2024), Gemini-Pro Team et al.
774   (2023), Claude3-Opus-V Anthropic (2024), and Glm-4V Du et al. (2022).
775   Uncertainty of MLLMs. Uncertainty estimation in the responses of LLMs has been extensively
776   explored in recent research (Xiong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Yadkori et al., 2024).
777   Studies have shown that hallucinations contribute significantly to uncertainty in model outputs (Zhou
778   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). Concurrently, evaluations of MLLMs under inconsistencies between
779   visual and textual inputs have been conducted to assess their robustness (Liu et al., 2024; Kimura
780   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024d; Zhang et al., 2024a;c). Other works have focused on enhancing the
781
      trustworthiness (Gong et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Yu et al., 2024b; Tu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024a)
      and robustness (Zhang et al., 2024c; Liu et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024c) of MLLMs. However,
782
      previous studies have not assessed MLLMs’ response uncertainty when encountering misleading
783
      information. In this work, we address this gap by analyzing and quantifying MLLM uncertainty
784   under these conditions, offering insights into their real-world reliability.
785
786   Adversarial prompts. Previous studies have primarily focused on attacking LLMs and MLLMs
787
      by appending adversarial suffixes to prompts or design misleading questions, effectively perform-
      ing jailbreak attacks (Zou et al., 2023; Paulus et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023).
788
      Other works have evaluated the reliability of MLLMs in resisting deceptive information embed-
789
      ded within prompts (Qian et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a), such as in MAD-Bench (Qian et al., 2024)
790   and AVIBench (Zhang et al., 2024a), which assess models’ robustness against adversarial visual
791   instructions. Additionally, the MMR dataset (Liu et al., 2024) reveals that MLLMs are fragile to
792   leading questions despite understanding visual content. Unlike these approaches, our work focuses
793   on the response uncertainty of MLLMs by introducing misleading information into the original ques-
794   tion without the need to design new specific deceptive questions or visual inputs, offering greater
795   flexibility.
