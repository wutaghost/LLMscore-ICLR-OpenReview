011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Cryogenic electron tomography (cryo-ET) can produce detailed 3D images called
014            tomograms of cellular environments. An essential step of cryo-ET reconstruction
015            and analysis is to find all instances of a protein in tomograms, a task known as
016
               particle picking. Due to the low signal-to-noise ratio, artifacts, and vast diversity
               in proteins, particle picking is a challenging 3D object detection problem. Ex-
017
               isting approaches are either slow or limited to picking a few particles of interest,
018
               which requires large annotated and difficult to obtain training datasets. In this
019            work, we propose ProPicker, a fast and universal particle picker that can detect
020            particles beyond those in the training set. Our promptable design allows for se-
021            lectively detecting a specific protein in the volume based on an input prompt. Our
022            experiments demonstrate that through a favorable trade-off between performance
023            and speed, ProPicker can achieve performance close to or on par with state-of-the-
024            art universal pickers, while being up to an order of magnitude faster. Moreover,
025            ProPicker can be efficiently adapted to new proteins through fine-tuning with a
026            few annotated samples.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Cryo-electron tomography (cryo-ET) is very popular due to its unique capabilities of imaging bio-
032
      logical macromolecules in their native environments (Turk & Baumeister, 2020; Hylton & Swulius,
      2021). An ambitious goal of cryo-ET is to obtain an ‘atlas’ of the cell with all of its constituent
033
      macromolecules mapped in their native environment. This would revolutionize our understanding
034
      of essential protein interactions and has the potential to provide breakthroughs in modern medicine
035   spanning cell biology to drug discovery (Bodakuntla et al., 2023).
036
037   In this paper, we focus on particle picking, which is the task of finding all instances of a particle of
038
      interest in 3D volumes, called tomograms, obtained with cryo-ET. Particle picking is an essential
      step in cryo-ET reconstruction and analysis.
039
040   Particle picking is a challenging 3D object detection problem, for various reasons. First, due to the
041   fundamental limitations of data acquisition in cryo-ET, tomograms have a very low signal-to-noise
042   ratio and exhibit strong artifacts. Moreover, tomograms are often large (200 × 1000 × 1000 voxels
043   and up), and cryo-ET datasets can consist of more than a hundred tomograms, which makes their
044
      analysis computationally demanding (Genthe et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023). Finally, due to the
      significant diversity in protein types within the cell, there is a vast array of unique object classes to
045
      be detected, many of which only differ subtly, rendering differentiation challenging. For instance,
046
      the human body alone is estimated to contain more than 20,000 unique proteins (Li & Buck, 2021).
047
048   Given these challenges, a particle picking method should be accurate, fast, and universal, i.e., should
049   be able to pick any particle of interest. Existing methods for particle picking are either slow or not
050
      universal, that is they are limited to picking a few (and fixed) particles of interest.
051   In this paper, we propose ProPicker, a Promptable particle Picker that can rapidly detect any type of
052   protein selected by a versatile prompting mechanism. ProPicker is inspired by the Segment Anything
053   Model (SAM) (Ravi et al., 2024; Kirillov et al., 2023) and CLIPSeg Lüddecke & Ecker (2022). For
      fast particle picking, ProPicker leverages an efficient 3D segmentation network to segment particles

      synthetic data for training and evaluation. We also demonstrate the applicability of ProPicker to

082
083         1. We propose ProPicker, a novel particle picker that offers a favorable trade-off between pick-
084            ing performance and speed. ProPicker can accurately pick a variety of different proteins
085            while simultaneously being up to 10× faster at single particle picking than the state-of-the-
086            art.
087         2. ProPicker is universal in that it can pick new particles that the model has not encountered
088            during training. For cases where ProPicker’s out-of-the-box picking performance on novel
089            particles is not satisfactory, we demonstrate that fine-tuning on little data can increase per-
090            formance.
091         3. We provide experiments which indicate that ProPicker can generalize to unseen real-world
092            tomograms, even though we train the model exclusively on synthetic data.
093
094   Overall, our findings demonstrate that ProPicker is a fast, high-performance and universal particle
095   picker that is based on the contemporary foundation model paradigm. As such, we expect major
096   further improvements in both performance and generalization in the near future when larger and
097   more diverse datasets for cryo-ET become available.

099   2   BACKGROUND & R ELATED W ORK
100
101   Deep learning methods have already revolutionized particle picking from 2D micrographs in the
102   context of single particle cryo-EM (Wang et al., 2016; Bepler et al., 2019; Wagner et al., 2019).
103   They are also on the rise for picking particles in 3D tomograms produced with cryo-ET. However,
104   especially in cryo-ET, traditional methods still play an important role.
105
106   Particle Picking with Template Matching. Template Matching (TM) is the most widely used
107   method for particle picking in cryo-ET (Bohm et al., 2000; Cruz-León et al., 2024). TM-based
      methods compare a template of the particle to be picked to candidate sub-tomograms extracted from


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      a 3D sliding window. This approach is universal, as it can pick any particle as long as a template
109   is available. However, TM is computationally demanding (up to several hours per tomogram), as
110   the stride of the sliding window needs to be small for accurate picking (Genthe et al., 2023; Maurer
111   et al., 2024).
112
      Building upon classical template-based approaches, the recent TomoTwin method (Rice et al., 2023)
113
      utilizes a learned convolutional encoder to map both template and sub-tomogram into a structured
114
      latent space, where similarity is evaluated. The training of the encoder follows a deep metric learning
115   approach such that latent representations of particles of the same class have high cosine similarity,
116   while those of different classes have low cosine similarity. TomoTwin is state-of-the-art among
117   universal particle pickers, can be faster than classical TM, and is more convenient to use.
118
119   Particle Picking with Deep Learning-Based Object Detection. Particle pickers using deep
120   learning-based object detection often outperform TM in terms of performance and picking speed
121   (Gubins et al., 2020; Genthe et al., 2023). Many such pickers, including our ProPicker method,
122   use a convolutional network to segment particles of interest belonging to one or more classes and
123   produce candidate particle locations by clustering the predicted segmentation masks. Examples for
124   such methods include DeepFinder (Moebel et al., 2021), DeePiCt (De Teresa-Trueba et al., 2023),
125   and DeepETPicker (Liu et al., 2024).
126   Deep learning-based object detection approaches for particle picking are typically significantly faster
127   than TM-like methods (Gubins et al., 2020), but current variants are not universal: they are trained
128   on datasets containing a fixed set of particles of interest and trained models are limited to picking
129   particles seen during training.
130   As a first step towards a universal segmentation-based picker, Zhao et al. (2024) have recently
131   proposed CryoSAM. CryoSAM takes user-specified center coordinates of target proteins as input.
132   Leveraging DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023), it extracts features around these points and generates
133   new prompts via feature matching with other parts of the tomogram. The general 2D segmentation
134   model SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) then segments particles iteratively, slice by slice, using the newly
135   generated prompts.
136
137   Relation of ProPicker to Existing Particle Pickers. ProPicker shares the setup of picking parti-
138   cles based on a single observation with TomoTwin and CryoSAM. In contrast to TomoTwin, ProP-
139   icker does not rely on slow template matching, but is based on fast 3D segmentation. CryoSAM
140   is segmentation-based but, the techniques used differ significantly from ProPicker. CryoSAM does
141
      not involve any training or fine-tuning on cryo-ET specific data but relies on 2D models trained in
      the general domain on mostly natural images. ProPicker on the other hand uses 3D segmentation
142
      and has been trained on domain-specific cryo-ET data. We compare the performance and speed of
143
      TomoTwin, CryoSAM and ProPicker in Section 4.

146   3     P RO P ICKER : P ROMPTABLE S EGMENTATION FOR PARTICLE P ICKING
147
148   After stating the problem we consider in most parts of this work, we describe ProPicker (Figure 1).
149
150   Problem Statement We are given a set of tomograms {x1 , x2 , ...}, represented as 3D voxel ar-
151   rays, i.e., xi ∈ Rn×n×n . Assume that the tomograms contain multiple instances of a type of particle
152   in which we are interested, and whose size is known. The individual instances can appear at arbi-
153   trary orientations. We consider the task of finding the set C ∗ = {c∗1 , c∗2 , ...} containing the centers
154
      c∗i ∈ R3 , i = 1, 2, ... of all instances of the particle of interest in all of the tomograms.
155
156   3.1    P RO P ICKER ARCHITECTURE
157
158
      ProPicker takes a tomogram x, and a prompt, a 3D voxel array p ∈ Rm×m×m representing the
      particle of interest as input. The output is a set C containing predicted particle centers.
159
160   In this work, a prompt p is a small sub-tomogram, i.e., a part of a larger tomogram, of shape
161   37 × 37 × 37 containing one instance of the particle of interest. The prompt sub-tomogram is
      extracted from one of the tomograms to which we wish to apply particle picking. However, other


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      representations that uniquely identify particles, such as amino-acid sequences or atomic coordinates
163   could also be used as prompts.
164
165   ProPicker consists of a prompt encoder and a segmentation model, which we detail in the following.
166
167   The prompt encoder. The prompt encoder εp : Rm×m×m → Rd extracts a salient feature vector
168   zp ∈ Rd that encodes information required for efficiently detecting the particle in tomograms. We
169   focus on prompts in voxel-space and use the TomoTwin encoder (Rice et al., 2023) as the prompt
170   encoder due to its robustness and good performance in template-based particle classification (see
171
      Section 2). Its input is a sub-tomogram that includes the particle of interest, and it outputs a concise
      (d = 32) representation zp of the particle that we use to condition the segmentation model.
172
173
174   The segmentation model. Given an input volume x ∈ Rn×n×n and prompt p, our promptable
175
      segmentation model S : Rn×n×n × Rd → Rn×n×n can be conditioned on the input prompt that
      steers the output map y ∈ {0, 1}n×n×n to the desired particle class, that is y = S(x; zp ), where
176
      zp = ε (p) . The model output y is the voxel-wise prediction of the model with respect to the
177
      absence/presence of the particle described in the input prompt. promWe use a well-established
178   convolutional 3D U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015), which is an encoder-decoder architecture (see
179   Figure 1), as our segmentation model. The U-Net’s encoder consists of 5 spatial downsampling
180   layers, and the corresponding decoder has 5 spatial upsampling layers. In total, the U-Net has 124
181   million trainable parameters. More details on the segmentation model and how we condition it on
182   the encoded prompt can be found in Appendix B.
183
184   3.2   PARTICLE P ICKING P IPELINE WITH P RO P ICKER
185
186   Next, we describe the steps of picking a single particle type in a tomogram using ProPicker.
187
188   1. Prompt extraction. First, we obtain a representation of the particle of interest in the tomogram.
189   We focus on voxel-space prompting, and therefore manually extract a sub-tomogram that includes
190   an instance of the particle of interest to be used as a prompt.
191
192   2. Particle segmentation. Next, we embed the extracted prompt and segment the tomogram using
193   ProPicker. As tomograms are typically very large, we segment the volume using a strided 3D sliding
194   window approach. Specifically, we slide a moderately sized window across the tomogram to extract
195   sub-tomograms, and segment each sub-tomogram individually. We obtain a full-sized segmenta-
196   tion mask for the tomogram by combining the sub-tomogram level masks, averaging overlapping
197   regions. An important hyperparameter of particle segmentation is the stride s of the sliding window,
198
      which controls the overlap between segmented sub-volumes. Template-based pickers rely on high
      overlap (low s), as these techniques need the window to be centered around the target particle. Our
199
      segmentation-based approach is less sensitive to the amount of overlap, and thus performance can
200
      be maintained with larger strides. Due to the 3D nature of the problem, a factor N reduction in
201   stride results in N 3 times decrease in compute. Note that it is also possible to use strategies other
202   than the sliding window to process the tomogram, which may potentially yield higher speed and/or
203   improved segmentation masks (Rumberger et al., 2021).
204
205   3. Finding particle center coordinates. We propose two strategies to map the segmentation
206   output y to particle center coordinates:
207
208          • Clustering-based picking (ProPicker-C): We detect clusters in the segmentation map by
209            finding connected components. The centroid of each cluster is a predicted particle center.
210            The precision of this approach can be improved by leveraging prior information about the
211            target particle size by excluding clusters that are too small or too large.
212          • TM-based picking (ProPicker-TM): We apply a TM-based picker to the input tomogram
213            over regions where our segmentation mask predicts the presence of a particle.
214
215   Our default variant, ProPicker-C, is fast as it directly predicts the particle centers from the segmen-
      tation map using lightweight connected component finding.


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      The performance of ProPicker-TM depends on the TM procedure which produces the actual picks.
217   Therefore, ProPicker-TM is particularly useful when the goal is to speed-up an existing TM pipeline
218   in case the latter is known to have good performance. As the segmentation masks produced with
219   ProPicker are typically very sparse in the volume (in our experiments, up to 98% of the tomograms
220   were masked out), ProPicker-TM can yield significant speedups for TM.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
224
225   In this Section, we demonstrate that ProPicker is effective at picking particles. We consider two
226
      different setups, divided into Section 4.1 and Section 4.2.
227   In Section 4.1, we consider picking all copies of a particle based on a single observation (prompt)
228   of the particle. As baselines, we consider TomoTwin (Rice et al., 2022) and CryoSAM (Zhao et al.,
229   2024). However, we find that TomoTwin performs better than CryoSAM and that CryoSAM is un-
230   able to handle diverse, crowded tomograms, and is less robust to noise (see Section 4.1.2). Therefore
231   the state-of-the-art TomoTwin is our main baseline. We do not compare to non-universal methods
232
      or methods that require any data from the target tomograms for training, as these do not match the
      setup. We compare to such methods in Section 4.2.
233
234   In Section 4.2, we discuss fine-tuning ProPicker as a means to improve performance and to adapt
235   to new particles. This setup is similar to that of non-universal deep-learning-based pickers, out of
236   which we choose the popular and powerful DeepFinder method (Moebel et al., 2021) as baseline.
237   Before we discuss our experiments, we describe the training of ProPicker and our evaluation metrics.
238
239   Training Dataset of ProPicker. We train ProPicker on realistically simulated tomograms from
240   Rice et al. (2023) and Gubins et al. (2020), which have also been used to train TomoTwin. Our train-
241   ing set contains the majority of TomoTwin’s training data, and consists of 78 tomograms containing
242   a total of 113 unique protein types, as well as gold fiducial markers, vesicles and filaments. Each
243   tomogram contains around 1500 protein instances, each belonging to a set of 10 − 13 unique protein
244   types. We train on sub-tomograms of size 64 × 64 × 64 extracted from all tomograms with a 3D
245   sliding window with stride 32.
246
247   Training ProPicker. To reduce computational cost, we keep the prompt encoder frozen during
248   training (see Section 3.1). We train the segmentation model with the Adam optimizer (Kingma &
249   Ba, 2015) with fixed learning rate 0.01. For each gradient step, we first randomly sample a batch of
250   8 sub-tomograms. For each sub-tomogram, we also randomly sample 10 prompts, one for each of
251
      the 10 unique proteins the sub-tomogram may contain (see ”Training Dataset”). We pass each sub-
      tomogram and its corresponding prompts through the conditional segmentation model. This yields
252
      a total of 80 = 8 · 10 predicted segmentation masks. Finally, we compute the average voxel-wise
253
      binary cross entropy between the model outputs and the 80 single-class target masks as loss.
254
255
      Evaluation Metrics. We measure picking performance with F1 score, and report best-case per-
256
      formance with thresholds optimized on test data for all methods following common practice (Rice
257
      et al., 2023), unless stated otherwise.

503   5    D ISCUSSION & C ONCLUSION
504
505   In this work, we propose ProPicker, a particle picking method for cryo-ET that leverages a prompt-
506   able segmentation model for rapid and accurate detection of proteins. The core of ProPicker is an
507   efficient 3D segmentation model capable of selectively detecting particles in tomograms based on a
508   prompt, i.e., a concise representation of the particle of interest.
509   Depending on the particle to be picked, ProPicker can achieve state-of-the-art picking performance,
510   in some cases even if the particle was not seen during training, while at the same time offering
511   significant speedups compared to the popular state-of-the-art template matching baseline TomoTwin.
512
      Through evaluation on two real-world tomograms, we demonstrated (see Section 4.1.3) that ProP-
513
      icker can generalize to real-world data. In our experiments, we also encountered real-world tomo-
514   grams and particles for which ProPicker was unable to produce good results. This is not surprising
515   because, due to a lack of large and diverse annotated real-world datasets, we rely exclusively on
516   synthetic data for training. Such issues related to generalization and robustness are not exclusive to
517   ProPicker (Bandyopadhyay et al., 2022): Huang et al. (2024) reported significant drops in perfor-
518   mance when applying deep learning-based particle pickers, among them DeepFinder (Moebel et al.,
519   2021)) and TomoTwin, to tomograms whose characteristics are too different from the training data.
520   Note that a concrete example of such a case can be found in Section 4.2, where we showed that both
521   ProPicker and TomoTwin struggle to pick particles that have not been seen during training and that
522
      are very dissimilar from all other particles in the training dataset.
523   However, the fact that ProPicker is indeed able to perform well on both unseen synthetic particles
524   (Section 4.1.2) and real-world tomograms datasets highlights the great potential of the method. It
525   is widely accepted that training on larger and more diverse datasets improves the robustness of
526   deep learning models (Radford et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2022; Lin & Heckel, 2024). Therefore,
527   a promising direction for future work is to collect large datasets of tomograms with ground truth
528
      particle annotations for a variety of particles. Large scale efforts to do so have already been initiated,
      see for example the CryoET Data Portal (Ermel et al., 2024) and (Ishemgulova et al., 2023). Once
529
      such datasets become available, incorporating them into the training sets of universal particle pickers
530
      like TomoTwin and ProPicker is likely to increase their robustness and performance.
531
532   ProPicker is a step towards a fast foundational particle picking model for cryo-ET. We expect particle
533   picking to profit significantly from adopting the foundation model paradigm where large pre-trained
534
      models give good picking results out-of-the-box or are fine-tuned on little data, as demonstrated for
      ProPicker in Section 4.2.

      A     S UB -T OMOGRAM AVERAGING
706
      Sub-tomogram averaging is a central application of cryo-ET and one of the main motivations for
707
      particle picking. The goal of sub-tomogram averaging is to get high-resolution models of parti-
708
      cles like proteins based on data from tomograms. The idea is to align and average sub-tomograms
709   that show the same particle, which suppresses the strong noise and artifacts present throughout the
710   tomograms and can yield high-resolution models of the particle (Wan & Briggs, 2016). For this
711   procedure, particle picking is an essential component and bottleneck both in terms of time demands
712   and accuracy.
713
714
      B     A RCHITECTURAL DETAILS
715
716   B.1   P ROMPTABLE SEGMENTATION MODEL
717
718   We use a well-established convolutional 3D U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) as our segmentation
719   model. The U-Net’s encoder matches the architecture of the prompt encoder, and consists of 5 spatial
720   downsampling/upsampling layers with corresponding skip connections. We condition the decoder
721   on the encoded prompt zp , using a feature-wise linear modulation (FiLM) approach (Perez et al.,
722   2018) (see Appendix B.2). Conditioning only the decoder enhances our technique with additional
723
      efficiency, as the encoder features can be re-used and decoded through multiple prompts in case one
      wishes to pick more than one type of particle. As is common in segmentation tasks, we activate
724
      the output of the U-Net with a sigmoid function to ensure the voxel values are between 0 and 1.
725
      At inference, voxels have to be binarized according to a user-defined threshold for clustering via
726   connected component detection.
727
728   B.2   P ROMPT C ONDITIONING
729
730   We condition each of the decoder’s 5 spatial upsampling layers with FiLM (Perez et al., 2018),
731   which we implement as follows: Let C be the number of channels (features) of an intermediate
732   3D feature map after upsampling. First, we multiply the encoded prompt zp ∈ R32 with two
733   (learnable) matrices A, B ∈ RC×32 . Finally, we map each channel k ∈ {1, ..., C}, with an affine
734   transformation with slope (Azp )k ∈ R and intercept (Bzp )k ∈ R, which gives the conditioned
735   feature map. We use one separate pair of matrices (A, B) for each of the 5 upsampling layers. We
736
      use one FiLM conditioning after each up-sampling layer, so there are 5 pairs of matrices (A, B) in
      total.
737
738
739   C     D EPENDENCE OF P ICKING P ERFORMANCE ON THE P ROMPT
740
741   The user has to manually extract the prompt from a tomogram at inference, and the tomogram
742   typically contains many instances of the particle of interest. Thus, a natural question is how the
743   performance of ProPicker depends on the concrete choice of the prompt. We investigate this prompt-
744
      dependence on two synthetic tomograms both containing 10 unique particles which have been all
      seen during training. The two tomograms are part of the test set described in Section 4.1.1. As the
745
      ground truth location of all particles is known for the synthetic tomograms, we randomly sampled
746
      10 prompts for each particle and evaluated the best-case picking F1 score.
747
748   As can be seen in Figure A1, for all but few particles, the picking performance of ProPicker-C
749   does not vary much with the concrete choice of the prompt. For some particles like 5ahu and 6tps
750
      (both in Tomogram 1), there are outlier prompts which yield significantly lower picking performance
      compared to the rest. We therefore recommend to try a few prompts for each particle.

      E                             F INE - TUNING ON SEEN PARTICLES
801
802   Finetuning of ProPicker can also improve performance on data seen during training. We consider
803   the synthetic SHREC 2021 dataset (Gubins et al., 2020) which is part of ProPicker’s training dataset.
804   The SHREC 2021 dataset is a popular synthetic benchmark for non-universal particle picking meth-
805   ods which target picking a fixed set of particles of interest. It consists of 10 synthetic tomograms
806   each containing 12 unique proteins. We train one model for each particle (12 models in total)
807   on 8 tomograms. We use the 9th tomogram for early stopping and optimizing the hyperparame-
808   ters (thresholds, maximal and minimal cluster sizes) of cluster-based picking. The 10th tomogram
809   serves as test tomogram. The test tomogram was part of neither the training dataset nor the fine
      tuning dataset of ProPicker.

862   Both real-world experiments presented in Section 4.1.3 are on tomograms containing mostly ribo-
863   somes, which have a large molecular weight and are, therefore, comparably easy to pick. Here, we
      show that ProPicker is able to pick particles other than ribosomes in real-world tomograms based

864
      on a single prompt as well. We also show that ProPicker is able to distinguish particles in a diverse
865   real-world tomogram.
866
867   We consider a tomogram from the DS-10440 dataset generated by Peck et al. (2024), which we
868
      downloaded from the Cryo ET Data Portal (Ermel et al., 2024). Among other particles, the dataset
      contains expert annotations for cytosolic ribosomes and apoferritin. Apoferritin has a molecular
869
      weight of 450 kilodalton which is 10.47% of that of the ribosomes in the dataset (4.3 megadalton),
870
      and is also smaller in size than the ribosomes (Peck et al., 2024). Our goal is to pick all instances of
871   the ribosomes and the apoferritin in the same tomogram based on a single prompt.
872
873   As can be seen in Figure A4, the segmentation model of ProPicker is able to detect both the ribo-
874
      somes (ice blue) and the apoferritin (pink) based on a single prompt each. Using ProPicker-TM,
      i.e. using TomoTwin to pick particles in the respective segmentaiton masks, we obtained a best case
875
      F1 picking score of 0.69 for the ribosomes and 0.71 for the apoferritin. The cluster-based picking
876
      approach of ProPicker-C gave a best-case F1 picking score of 0.5 and 0.53 respectively. The worse
877   performance of cluster-based picking is likely due to crowded parts (see, e.g. the zoomed-in region),
878   where several instances of the same particle appear close together, which makes clustering challeng-
879   ing compared to peak-finding, which is used in TomoTwin-based picking. TomoTwin on its own,
880   i.e. applied to the entire tomogram, performs similarly to ProPicker-TM.
