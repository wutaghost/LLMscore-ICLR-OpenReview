                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            In-context learning (ICL) is a powerful technique for getting language models to
011            perform complex tasks with no training updates. Prior work has established strong
012            correlations between the number of in-context examples provided and the accuracy
013            of the model’s predictions. In this paper, we seek to explain this correlation by
014
               showing that ICL approximates a Bayesian learner. This perspective gives rise to a
               family of novel Bayesian scaling laws for ICL. In experiments with GPT-2 models
015
               of different sizes, our scaling laws match existing scaling laws in accuracy while
016
               also offering interpretable terms for task priors, learning efficiency, and per-example
017            probabilities. To illustrate the analytic power that such interpretable scaling laws
018            provide, we report on controlled synthetic dataset experiments designed to inform
019            real-world studies of safety alignment. In our experimental protocol, we use SFT
020            to suppress an unwanted existing model capability and then use ICL to try to bring
021            that capability back (many-shot jailbreaking). We then experiment on real-world
022            instruction-tuned LLMs using capabilities benchmarks as well as a new many-shot
023            jailbreaking dataset. In all cases, Bayesian scaling laws accurately predict the
024            conditions under which ICL will cause the suppressed behavior to reemerge, which
025
               sheds light on the ineffectiveness of post-training at increasing LLM safety.
026

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      Large language models (LLMs) can infer how to perform a task given only demonstrations and
030   without additional training updates. This capability is known as in-context learning (ICL; Brown
031   et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2022). Under ICL, task performance generally increases with the number of
032   demonstrations, though the precise relationship between these two quantities is unclear. We call this
033   relationship the ICL curve and seek to model it. Being able to predict the shape of the ICL curve
034   would help us decide whether to do many-shot ICL (Agarwal et al., 2024) after testing only few-shot
035   performance, predict potential alignment failures under many-shot jailbreaking (Anil et al., 2024),
036   and decide how much fine-tuning we need in order to suppress ICL of undesirable behaviours.
037   The learning algorithm underlying ICL has been characterised as Bayesian by Xie et al. (2022) and
038   many later works (§2). Drawing on this line of research, we use Bayes’ theorem to derive a family of
039   Bayesian scaling laws for ICL (§3) which model the ICL curve of an ideal Bayesian learner.
040
      To evaluate the performance of our Bayesian laws, we model the ICL curve for gpt2 models trained
041
      on simple synthetic data following Xie et al. (2022) as well as real-world LLMs tested on standard
042   benchmarks (§4.1). Compared to the power laws proposed by Anil et al. (2024), our Bayesian laws
043   achieve comparable error rates on both interpolation and extrapolation of the ICL curve, while also
044   providing interpretable parameters for the prior over tasks, the efficiency of ICL, and per-example
045   probabilities under different tasks. In our second set of experiments (§4.2), we present a case
046   study using our Bayesian laws to model how post-training affects ICL of favoured and disfavoured
047   behaviours. On toy models, we find that smaller amounts of post-training strongly change the prior
048   over tasks but not the model’s knowledge of each task, and the amount of post-training needed to
049   suppress ICL of disfavoured tasks increases with scale.
050   Finally, we present experiments on real-world LLMs ranging from 1B to 405B parameters (§5).
051   Our laws accurately predict the ICL behaviour of several models on both capabilities and safety
052   benchmarks and a new many-shot jailbreaking dataset we introduce. We then compare Llama 3.1 8B
053   Base and Instruct using one of our Bayesian scaling laws (§5.2) and find that alignment merely
      reduces the prior probability of harmful behaviour but not its learnability under ICL. Our work thus


                                                         1
054
      introduces a tool for interpreting the task knowledge of LLMs using purely behavioural observations,
055
      which we hope is valuable for improving LLM alignment.

      2     R ELATED WORK
058
059
      Understanding in-context learning. LMs trained from scratch on controlled synthetic data have
060   been variously claimed to approximate Bayesian learners (Xie et al., 2022; Hahn & Goyal, 2023;
061   Zhang et al., 2023; Jiang, 2023; Wies et al., 2023), gradient descent (von Oswald et al., 2023; Ahn
062   et al., 2023), or differing learning algorithms depending on the task, model scale, and training
063   progress (Akyürek et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Falck et al.,
064   2024). Neverthless, no work has attempted to directly model the ICL curve on the basis of claims
065   about the learning algorithm underlying ICL. In this work, we test the claims that LMs are Bayesian
066   learners by deriving an expression for the ICL curve under Bayesian assumptions and seeing how
067   well it models actual ICL behaviour.
068   Scaling laws. Researchers have sought to characterise how LM loss and performance relates to
069   model architecture, model scale, data scale, and training hyperparameters in order to predict and
070   optimise training runs (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022). LM scaling laws may also
071   take into account data complexity (Pandey, 2024) or use more expressive formulations for better
072   extrapolation (Alabdulmohsin et al., 2022; Caballero et al., 2023). Power laws seem ubiquitous in
073   describing LM behaviour and have recently been adopted to model the ICL curve under different
074   model and data settings (Anil et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024); we use these power laws as baselines.
075   The ineffectiveness of post-training. Much work has found that post-training, even when applied at
076   scale, only changes LLM behaviour in ways that are superficial and easy to bypass (Qi et al., 2024;
077   Zou et al., 2023; Shayegani et al., 2024; Carlini et al., 2023; Geiping et al., 2024; Jain et al., 2024;
078   Prakash et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024a; Lee et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024a; Schwinn et al., 2024;
079   Sheshadri et al., 2024).
080
      Concerningly, ICL enables re-learning of behaviours that were suppressed with fine-tuning (Wei
081   et al., 2024b; Xhonneux et al., 2024; Anil et al., 2024; Anwar et al., 2024). Under a Bayesian view of
082   post-training, it is possible that task priors are only reweighted while task knowledge is unchanged;
083   our Bayesian scaling laws can test this hypothesis.
084
085   3     A BAYESIAN LAW FOR IN - CONTEXT LEARNING
086
087   As discussed in §2, there are many competing hypotheses about how ICL is learned and implemented
088   in LMs. When training LMs on a variety of simple algorithmic tasks (e.g. linear regression, HMM
089   next-emission prediction), many works find that ICL approximates a Bayesian learner (Xie et al.,
090   2022, inter alia).
091   If ICL is indeed Bayesian, we should be able to use Bayesian assumptions to exactly predict how
092   prediction accuracy relates to number of in-context examples. This observation leads us to state some
093   key assumptions necessary to frame ICL as Bayesian. Next, we use repeated application of Bayes’
094   theorem to model how ICL updates the task prior after encountering each new in-context example
095   (§3.1). Finally, we simplify our model to reduce parameter count and add an efficiency coefficient
096   K to take into account the effect of example length and informativeness (§3.2). This results in a
097   family of Bayesian scaling laws. We close the section by setting up some baselines and metrics for
098
      our experiments (§3.3).

312
313   Now that we have confirmed that the Bayesian law is an accurate model of ICL behaviour, we can
314
      interpret the learned parameters of the Bayesian fits. We plot some interesting parameters of the

315
316     • The prior (ρ) distributions are somewhat noisy but roughly uniform, agreeing with the uniform

318     • ICL efficiency (K) roughly increases with model depth i.e. larger models have faster ICL, and

          ICL.

       4.3.1                DPO ( EVENTUALLY ) BREAKS THE ICL CURVE
417
418   We show some key results in Figure 5. Unlike SFT, DPO suppresses the prior of the disfavoured
419   HMMs beyond the ability of ICL to recover. DPO training requirements are also much less sensitive
420   to model size than SFT. However, with enough DPO training, the probability of the preferred output
421   (HMM 0) also declines and the ICL curve eventually collapses. As a result, none of the scaling laws
422   model the ICL curve well after some amount of DPO training.We do observe that larger models
423   require slightly more DPO training to suppress the negative distribution, but not as starkly as for SFT.
424   The collapse of the positive distribution is a known failure mode of DPO, which occurs because it
425   maximises the relative difference between the probabilities of the positive and negative distributions
426   (Pal et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024; D’Oosterlinck et al., 2024). Overall, DPO impacts more of the
427   model’s knowledge about tasks than SFT.

470   5.1                      E XPERIMENT 4: BAYESIAN SCALING LAWS ARE COMPETITIVE ON REAL - WORLD LLM S
471
472   Data. Our datasets include both capabilities and safety evaluations, including 2 multiple-choice
473
      reasoning benchmarks, 3 binary-choice personality evaluations from Perez et al. (2022), and a new
      many-shot jailbreaking dataset that we created using HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024). More details
474
      are in appendix E.2.
475
476   Method. We experiment on 7 instruction-tuned LLMs from the Gemma and Llama families, with
477   parameter counts spanning from 1B to 405B parameters; see appendix E.1 for details. For each
478   dataset and model pair, we construct 50 many-shot prompts adhering to each model’s chat template.
479   We use as many shots as possible, filling the context window. We run the LLM on each of these
480
      many-shot prompts and, for each shot, store the next-token prediction probability of the relevant
      portion of the response. We find that many LLMs suffer degradation near the end of their context
481
      window, so we only use the data from the starting 90% of the context window.
482
483   Results. As before, we fit each of the scaling laws to the ICL curves and evaluate the quality of
484   the fits by comparing the NRMSE of the predictions. We report overall results across all models in
485   Table 2; we find that most comparisons between the scaling laws are not statistically significant, so
      again the Bayesian laws are not worse than alternatives.

      5.2   E XPERIMENT 5: C OMPARING L LAMA 3.1 8B BASE AND I NSTRUCT
487
488   In our final experiment, we compare the parameters of the Bayesian (scoring-wise) law on Llama 3.1
489   8B Base and Instruct on all of the real-world tasks. The Base model was not used in the previous
490   experiment. We report raw probabilities as well as the posterior probabilities for the task computed
491   by the scaling law in Figure 6. We find that the instruction-tuning of this model does reduce the prior
492   probability of unsafe behaviours (harmbench and the 3 persona evals) but fails to prevent many-shot
493   jailbreaking.
494   Our scaling law shows that the posterior eventually saturates even if instruction-tuning reduces the
495   prior. Along with our synthetic experiments with SFT and DPO in a low-data setting, this is additional
496   evidence for the claim that real-world instruction-tuning merely modifies the prior over tasks and not
497   task knowledge. This may be because the compute allocated to instruction-tuning is is still too small
498   compared to that for pretraining.

500   6     D ISCUSSION
501
502   In-context learning, like most of the noteworthy properties of large language models, is something
503   that we don’t quite understand. This paper emerged from our attempt to reconcile the existing
504   literature that attempts to ascribe a Bayesian basis for the emergence of ICL with the empirical
505
      science of scaling laws. We did find that Bayesian scaling laws are competitive with non-theoretical
      (and relatively unconstrained) scaling laws at modelling ICL behaviour in both toy and real settings.
506
507   Real-world applications. The Bayesian approach seems to perform better at extrapolating model
508   behaviour from a few shots. This can be useful for predicting multi-turn safety failures before they
509   happen or whether additional inference-time computation will deliver worthwhile gains.
510
      Interpretability. An additional advantage of our approach is that the parameters of the scaling laws
511   mean something and so can shed light on the internal workings of LLMs without needing to fully
512   open the black box. E.g. studying both the prior over tasks and how ICL affects their posterior
513   is valuable for interpreting the effects of alignment on real-world LLMs. Future work could also
514   mechanistically interpret how Bayesian ICL is performed (e.g. localise the prior in activation space).
515
516
      Are LLMs Bayesian? In this work we attempt to elucidate model behaviour without reference to
      model internals. We believe that our results show that a Bayesian interpretation of ICL is compatible
517
      with real LLM behaviour, but due to non-Bayesian laws being (generally) equally good fits, we do
518
      not claim to have proven that LLMs are Bayesian learners. We note that previous works claiming that
519   LLMs are theoretically Bayesian prove their claims on toy models that vastly simplify the complexity
520   of natural language and web-scale pretraining data;2 it’s possible that actual web-scale Bayesian
521   reasoning is beyond the capacity of current LLMs, but they still may behave approximately Bayesian,
522   explaining the success of our scaling law.
523
524   7     C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we combined two questions to make progress at understanding ICL: (1) what scaling
527   law best describes ICL, and (2) is ICL Bayesian? We showed that Bayesian assumptions naturally
528   lead to a scaling law for ICL, and that Bayesian scaling laws are a great fit for both ICL behaviour
529   by small LMs trained on controlled synthetic data, as well as LLMs trained on natural language.
530
      Using a Bayesian formulation gave us interpretable parameters for the prior, learning efficiency, and
      task-conditional probabilities, which can help us understand how model behaviour changes under
531
      alignment. We use these to show how ICL ability varies at different model scales, understand how
532
      finetuning harms knowledge of disfavoured distributions, and compare base and instruction-tuned
533   LLMs. We are confident that further progress on understanding ICL is possible through the empirical
534   science of scaling laws.

936   B        O UR CHOICES FOR BASELINES
937
938   Our inclusion of the power law and the bounded power law stem from their use in Anil et al. (2024).
939   We note that their justification for fitting a power law to the ICL curve is predicated on (1) the ubiquity
940   of power laws in describing language model behaviour in general, particularly during training;3 and
941
      (2) a few toy derivations which show how the attention mechanism could implement ICL in a way
      that results in a power law shape for the ICL curve.4
942
943   As for the bounded power law, Anil et al. (2024) propose it in Appendix H.1 of the paper, but do
944   not provide theoretical justification for it as they did for the power law. The key advantage of the
945   bounded power law, they point out, is that “it asymptotes to constant values for both limits n → 0
946   and n → ∞” (where n is the number of ICL examples).
947   When reading this justification, we couldn’t help but recall the canonical example of a function the
948   asymptotes in both directions: the logistic function. If we apply a log transform to the input variable,
949   the logistic asymptotes to constant values for n → 0 and n → ∞, just like the bounded power law.
950
      We also note that since laws that asymptote towards both limits (such as the bounded power law, our
951   log-logistic baseline, and our Bayesian scaling laws) are empirically better fits for ICL behaviour
952   on real-world LLMs, the toy model of ICL that Anil et al. (2024) propose must not capture the real
953   mechanism underlying ICL, since it only predicts power law fits (which assymptote only as n → ∞).

1010   range (−∞, 0] using the softplus activation. For the sampling-wise and scoring-wise variants, we
1011   find it appropriate to ensure γi > βi , so to compute βi we sum its underlying parameter with the
1012   underlying parameters for γi , forcing it to always be smaller. This slightly harms performance but
1013   leads to more interpretable fits.
1014

       E.2   DATASETS
1081
1082   We compute ICL curves on the following datasets:
1083
1084     • CREAK (Onoe et al., 2021) tests commonsense reasoning using entity knowledge. The model
1085
           must respond with “true” or “false” given a statement.
1086     • Persona (Perez et al., 2022) evals test whether a model adopts a particular persona by asking
1087       personality quiz-type questions with “yes” and “no” responses. We evaluate on the machiavel-
1088       lianism, narcissism, and psychopathy subsets. We jointly model positive and negative personality
1089       traits when fitting our Bayesian law, which gives us a prior over both e.g. psychopathic and
1090       non-psychopathic responses.
1091     • HarmBench (ours) is a new many-shot jailbreaking dataset that we generated, which follows
1092       the recipe from Anil et al. (2024). We take prompts from HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024)
1093       which safety-tuned models are supposed to refuse, and generate non-refusal responses to them
1094       using WizardLM-13B-Uncensored (Hartford, 2023), an instruction-following LLM that has not
1095       been safety-tuned. We force-decode the prefix of the uncensored response with ‘Here is how’.
1096       Instead of using an expensive refusal classifier model, we simply compute the probability of this
1097       prefix for our ICL curves.
1098     • LogiQA (Liu et al., 2020) is a multiple-choice machine reading comprehension dataset which
1099       requires logical reasoning to answer. We use the prompt format from the EleutherAI Eval
1100       Harness (Gao et al., 2024).
