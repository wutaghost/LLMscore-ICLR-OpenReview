011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The task of co-optimizing the body and behaviour of agents has been a long-
014            standing problem in the fields of evolutionary robotics and embodied AI. Previous
015            work has largely focused on the development of learning methods exploiting mas-
016            sive parallelization of agent evaluations with large population sizes, a paradigm
017            which is applicable to simulated agents but cannot be transferred to the real world
018
               due to the assoicated costs with the production of embodiments and robots. Fur-
               thermore, recent data-efficient approaches utilizing reinforcement learning can
019
               suffer from distributional shifts in transition dynamics as well as in state and action
020
               spaces when experiencing new body morphologies. In this work, we propose a new
021            co-adaptation method combining reinforcement learning and State-Aligned Self-
022            Imitation Learning to co-optimize embodiment and behavioural policies withing a
023            handful of design iterations. We show that the integration of a self-imitation signal
024            improves the data-efficiency of the co-adaptation process as well as the behavioural
025            recovery when adapting morphological parameters.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Finding an optimal combination of body and morphology of agents has been a long-standing research
031   problem, finding its roots in the community of evolutionary robotics (Lipson & Pollack, 2000; Clune
032   et al., 2013; Doncieux et al., 2015). Originally, research in this area largely focused on the use and
033   development of evolutionary or genetic algorithms adapting body and control parameters at the same
034   time (Lipson & Pollack, 2000; Watson et al., 2002; Bongard, 2011; Buason et al., 2005; Kempen &
035   Eiben, 2022). This was and is largely inspired by observations made about the evolutionary principles
036   governing the adaptation of animal species in nature bringing forth animals with unique morphological
037   features and behaviours, such as Carparachne aureoflava, a spider capable of “wheeling” down sand
038   dunes to escape predators (Harvey & Zukoff, 2011; Western et al., 2023). More recent research (Hale
039
      et al., 2019; Luck et al., 2019) has presented evidence of the benefits of considering the different
      time-scales on which co-adaptation of body and behaviour occurs in the real world: adaptation of the
040
      body is costly and time-consuming, as it involves growing appendices, organs and tissue in nature;
041
      likewise in robotics, where even fast manufacturing methods like 3D-printing and casting require a
042   considerable amount of work-hours and material. However, adaptation of behaviour occurs at much
043   faster time-scales, enabled by fast and inexpensive changes to neurons in the brain or changes to
044   control parameters and artificial neural network weights in robots.
045
      Recent years have brought forward several works considering the use of reinforcement learning (RL)
046
      methods for the problem of co-adapting robots (Chen et al., 2021; Pigozzi et al., 2023; Sun et al.,
047
      2023; Luck et al., 2019), usually with a fast behavioural adaptation process and slower morphology
048   adaptation. This allowed to develop methods capable of being deployed in principle on real-world
049   robotics due to their data-efficiency. However, data-efficient co-adaptation processes can suffer
050   considerably from the problem of distributional shift inherent to the co-adaptation problem setting.
051   Every new agent morphology the algorithms experiences brings with it changes to the transition
052   distribution, as well as to the semantics of state and action spaces. For example, changes to the
053   orientation of a robot leg lead to changes between the mapping of motor actions and of orientation
      and movement of the robot leg. This can be detrimental to the co-adaptation process, as changes to


                                                         1

054
      the morphology can lead to catastrophic forgetting due to policy actions causing different motion
055   patterns between individual designs.
056
057   We propose a novel co-adaptation methodology tackling the aforementioned problems by combining
058
      reward-driven reinforcement learning and self-imitation learning utilizing Wasserstein distances for
      data-efficient adaptation of body and behaviour of agents. The idea of our approach is to not only
059
      force the reinforcement learning algorithm to adapt body and behaviour for maximizing an objective
060
      function such as forward velocity, but also to encourage the imitation of the agent’s ’ancestors’ and
061   their previous behaviours to increase learning stability and accelerate the co-adaptation progress.
062
063   In this paper1 , we present the following contributions:
064
      (C1) An extension of State-Alignment Imitation Learning (SAIL) (Liu et al., 2019) for mismatching
      morphologies to State-Aligned Self-Imitation Learning for the problem of co-adapting the morphol-
065
      ogy and behaviour of agents.
066
      (C2) A novel co-adaptation method, Co-Adaptation with Self-Imitation Learning (CoSIL), uti-
067   lizing State-Aligned Self-Imitation Learning to optimize an agent’s morphology and behaviour
068   data-efficiently on fewer design iterations.
069   (C3) We demonstrate in an empirical study the benefits and limitations of CoSIL by evaluating its
070   performance versus a non-self-imitating baseline in a range of locomotion tasks.

      Reinforcement Learning (RL): In a reinforcement learning setting, problems are formulated as a
075
      Markov decision process (MDP) ⟨S, A, r, p⟩. We consider an environment-agent interaction fully
076   described by a set of possible states S ∈ Rm , a set of possible actions taken by the agent in a given
077   state A ∈ Rn , a reward function r : S × A 7→ R and a transition function p : S × A × R × S 7→ [0, 1].
078   The transition function defines the dynamics of the environment by providing a probability p(s′ |s, a)
079   of each next state given the current state and the chosen action. In order to train an agent for a given
080   task, we model the desired behaviour as a reward function and use an optimization procedure to
081   design a policy π(a|s) ∈ [0, 1] which approximates the optimal action a to take in any given state s
082   as a probability distribution over A to maximize the cumulative rewards.
083
084   Multi-Body Reinforcement Learning: In multi-body reinforcement learning, we consider an
085
      extension to the classic Markov Decision Process (MDP) suitable for modelling the fact that both
      behaviour and morphological parameters are adapted. The Multi-Body MDP (MB-MDP) consists of
086
      (S, A, Ξ, r, p(st+1 |st , at , ξ), p(s0 |ξ)) with state space S ∈ Rs and action space A ∈ Ra . Notably, in
087
      a MB-MDP the set Ξ models the morphological parameter space, containing individual instances
088   of agent morphologies ξ ∈ Ξ. Throughout this paper, we will without a loss of generality consider
089   Ξ ∈ Rd for d continuous design parameters, such as limp lengths or width/size of agent body elements.
090   As changes to the physics of the agent morphology impact its dynamics, the transition function
091   p(st+1 |st , at , ξ) depends on the current morphology parameter ξ. The reward function r(st , at , ξ)
092   may also implicitly depend on ξ via the transition function, or explicitly if the manufacturing costs
093   are taken into account, for example. The objective is to find a policy πθ (st , ξ) = at which maximizes
094   the finite-horizon expected discounted reward

175   3.3      I MITATION R EWARD AND E NVIRONMENTAL R EWARD
176
177   CoSIL makes use of two reward functions: rIL for the self-imitation reward, and rRL for the environ-
178   ment reward we aim to maximize as the main objective. While rRL is a fixed objective given by the
179   environment, rIL is a learned function which rewards the agent for a behavioural policy π minimizing
180   Eq. (8), given a demonstration dataset τ E . Multiple choices exist for the imitation learning method
181
      used to learn rIL . Candidates include the Adversarial Inverse Reinforcement Learning (AIRL) reward
182                            rIL (ϕ(st ), ϕ(st+1 )) = log(ρ(ϕ(st ))) − log(1 − ρ(ϕ(st ))),                           (9)
183
      where ρ is a discriminator which differentiates between agent states and expert states, as well as
184   State-Aligned Imitation Learning (SAIL) using the Wasserstein distance with reward function
185
186                               rIL (ϕ(st ), ϕ(st+1 )) = ρ(ϕ(st+1 )) − Es∼τ E [ρ(ϕ(s))] ,                           (10)
187   where ρ is a learned discriminator function (i.e., a neural network) modelling the Kantorovich’s
188   potential, assigning higher values to states similar to those seen in the expert dataset τ E . Further
189   details about the training procedure to learn these reward functions can be found in (Fu et al., 2018)
190   for AIRL, as well as (Liu et al., 2019) for SAIL. In this paper, we will consider mainly the SAIL
191   reward in Eq. (10), as previous work has shown it performs better in this task setting (Rajani et al.,
192   2023).

456   5     R ELATED W ORK
457
458   Evolutionary Robotics: Designing robot hardware with evolutionary principles has been a long-
459   standing research effort. Seminal work by (Lipson & Pollack, 2000) explored using genetic algorithms
460   to co-adapt a simple controller architecture of agents trying to crouch forward as fast as possible.
461   Similarly, earlier works by (Sims, 1994) used competition as a reward signal in a genetic algorithm
462   to adapt the bodies of two robots fighting against each other in a virtual arena. Approaches for
463   evolutionary robotics have been successfully applied to a number of different robotic platforms,
464   primarily in simulation (Bongard, 2013), although recent works have identified that developing
465
      methods applicable to real world evolution remains an open challenge (Doncieux et al., 2015). Recent
      work has focused primarily on the fast changeability of robotic platforms as means to allow real
466
      world evolution of robots, such as extendable legs (Nygaard et al., 2021) or modularity (Hale et al.,
467
      2019; Alattas et al., 2019), although this constrains the range of possible robot designs considerably.
468
469   Co-Adaptation with Reinforcement Learning: Recent works have increasingly sought to improve
470   data-efficiency and applicability of co-adaptation by using a reinforcement learning method as
471
      its main component. Seminal work by (Ha, 2019) introduced a policy gradient framework to
      jointly co-adapt the body and behaviour of agents in simulation with REINFORCE (Williams,
472
      1992). (Schaff et al., 2019) extended this approach by proposing a deep reinforcement learning
473
      co-adaptation algorithm. Increased data-efficiency was achieved by (Luck et al., 2019) with the
474   introduction of an off-policy deep reinforcement learning method using the Q-value function for
475   design candidate evaluations. Another recent work (Gupta et al., 2021) employed deep reinforcement
476   learning with mass-parallelization of agent populations in simulation, hence ignoring data-efficiency,
477   using evolutionary techniques to investigate the Baldwin effect and Lamarckian evolution, for
478   example.
479
      Imitation Learning: Imitation learning has been a key technique in robot learning to enable agents
480   to repeat behaviour demonstrated by humans (Fang et al., 2019; Asfour et al., 2008). Early techniques
481   such as Behaviour Cloning (Pomerleau, 1988; Bain & Sammut, 1995) use a supervised learning
482   strategy to extract motion policies replicating demonstrated behaviour. Generative Adversarial
483   Imitation Learning (GAIL) (Ho & Ermon, 2016) measures the success of an imitator using an
484   adversarial deep learning approach, employing a logistic loss to differentiate between the policies of
485   the agent and the demonstrator. Other adversarial imitation learning algorithms have been devised in
      an attempt to perform well under changing state and action space representations, as well as different


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      transition functions. Adversarial Inverse Reinforcement Learning (AIRL) (Fu et al., 2018) produces
487   disentangled rewards with respect to the environment dynamics. In contrast with the usage of the
488   Jensen–Shannon divergence (Lin, 1991) in GAIL, State Alignment-based Imitation Learning (SAIL)
489   (Liu et al., 2019) attempts to minimize the Wasserstein distance (Villani, 2009) between the state
490   distributions induced by the demonstrator and the agent’s policies. Closest to our work, (Rajani
491   et al., 2023) proposed a first approach integrating morphological agnostic imitation learning into the
492   co-adaptation process to adapt agent behaviour and design without an environmental reward and only
493   given human expert demonstrations. Similarly, for our proposed method we include an imitation
494   signal in the learning process. Crucially, however, CoSIL employs also the goal-oriented reward as
495
      primary objective for policy and design optimization, using imitation learning as secondary guidance
      to imitate the agent’s previous behavior (i.e., self-imitation).
496
497
498   6    L IMITATIONS
499
500   While we can show that CoSIL increases the performance of co-adaptation with the help of a self-
501   imitation reward, there are obvious limitations to this approach. We can argue that CoSIL increases
502   data-efficiency and achieves higher performance with less morphologies, a key advantage given that
503   the construction and manufacturing of robot prototypes in the real world is a costly and time-intensive
504
      endeavour. However, it is worth to point out that CoSIL adds a considerable computational overhead.
      In addition to multi-body reinforcement learning, CoSIL requires the costly training of discriminator
505
      networks in order to generate rewards via rIL . In our experiments, we run CoSIL as long as possible
506
      on the available cluster infrastructure for a time duration of 72 hours. Standard co-adaptation with
507   reinforcement learning (Coadapt) was capable of evaluating designs almost twice as fast than CoSIL;
508   nonetheless, the converged performance of CoSIL was still higher. Hence, as we describe in our
509   analysis about the limitations of CoSIL, one may not want to employ our proposed self-imitation
510   learning approach on problems with low task complexity or low dimensionality in the morphology
511   space as it is the case with the HalfCheetah task. Furthermore, our approach introduces another set of
512   hyper-parameters, here the weights ω and ωopt , which may have to be fine-tuned for any given task.
513   This could be alleviated in future work by introducing an automatic adaptation method.
514
515   7    C ONCLUSION
516
517   We presented a new co-adaptation method named Co-Adaptation with Self-Imitation Learning
518   (CoSIL) which introduces the idea of using a self-imitation reward within a reward-driven co-
519   adaptation framework using deep reinforcement learning for the purpose of jointly adapting the
520   morphology and behaviour of embodied agents. To achieve this, we used State-Aligned Imitation
521   Learning (SAIL) (Liu et al., 2019), introduced a method to select and match expert data from
522   previously seen morphology-policy combinations, and employed separate Q-value functions for
523   the objective and imitation rewards to increase data-efficiency when optimizing the morphology
524
      parameters. In experiments on morphology-adaptable agents in simulation, we showed that by
      imitating previously seen behaviour we can combat the distributional shift in dynamics, action
525
      and state spaces. Furthermore, we are able to demonstrate that self-imitation in combination with
526
      reward-driven co-adaptation can outperform both classical co-adaptation with rewards and pure
527   imitation learning approaches. However, CoSIL requires a larger amount of computational effort due
528   to additional deep neural network training, which makes it not preferable for simple co-adaptation
529   problems. Nevertheless, with the methodology proposed in this paper we make a further step towards
530   the useful integration of imitation learning techniques into co-adaptation techniques using deep
531   reinforcement learning. Several interesting avenues for future work are opened up by our work, such
532   as the use of quality-diversity approaches for selection of self-demonstrations, or further investigations
533   of using a self-imitation reward during design optimization.

      D.1   R ESULTS
919
920   Using the experimental setup of the Unitree robot in the Mujoco physics simulator we evaluate both
921   the proposed co-adaptation method with self-imitation learning versus the standard reward-driven
922   co-adaptation process. We performed for each methods four experiments with different seeds and
923   allowed experiments to run for approximately 250 hours. The result confirm the previous experiments,
924   that CoSIL shows better performance in more complex task and agent settings, such as humanoid and
925   the Unitree Go1 robot. The results indicate that CoSIL is more resistant against the distributional
926
      shifts in action- and state-spaces when switching between morphologies (Fig. 10b). Furthermore,
      CoSIL is able to uncover better performing combinations of morphology and behaviour than reward-
927
      only-driven co-adaptation, highlighting the increased sample- and data-efficiency achievable with
928
      self-imitation learning in a co-adaptation setting.
