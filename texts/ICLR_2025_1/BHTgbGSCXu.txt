011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013                While multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have achieved remarkable
014                success in recent advancements, their susceptibility to jailbreak attacks has come
015                to light. In such attacks, adversaries exploit carefully crafted prompts to coerce
016                models into generating harmful or undesirable content. Existing defense mech-
017
                   anisms often rely on external inference steps or safety alignment training, both
                   of which are less effective and impractical when facing sophisticated adversar-
018
                   ial perturbations in white-box scenarios. To address these challenges and bolster
019
                   MLLM robustness, we introduce S AFE MLLM1 by adopting an adversarial train-
020                ing framework that alternates between an attack step for generating adversarial
021                noise and a model updating step. At the attack step, S AFE MLLM generates ad-
022                versarial perturbations through a newly proposed contrastive embedding attack
023                (CoE-Attack), which optimizes token embeddings under a contrastive objective.
024                S AFE MLLM then updates model parameters to neutralize the perturbation ef-
025                fects while preserving model utility on benign inputs. We evaluate S AFE MLLM
026                across six MLLMs and six jailbreak methods spanning multiple modalities. Ex-
027                perimental results show that S AFE MLLM effectively defends against diverse at-
028
                   tacks, maintaining robust performance and utilities.

      1       INTRODUCTION
032
033
      Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have demonstrated remarkable success across various
      tasks (Liu et al., 2023a; Driess et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024). However, recent studies also reveal their
034
      security threats (Qi et al., 2024a; Bailey et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024) in different domains. Among
035
      these risks, a rising concern is jailbreak attacks, where attackers can bypass the safety guardrails
036   of MLLMs and prompt them to generate harmful content or illegal suggestions. There are several
037   widely used ways to defend against jailbreak attacks on MLLMs, including content filtering based
038   on post-processing (Pi et al., 2024; Gou et al., 2024; Helff et al., 2024) and safety fine-tuning (Zong
039   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024).
040
      Implementing strong content filters is required to introduce a third-party large language model
041
      (LLM) or MLLM to scan generated output and block harmful or inappropriate responses before
042   they are delivered. However, these filters are not inherently designed to function as harmful content
043   discriminators, and simply relying on their capabilities may lead to inaccurate filtering results (Cao
044   et al., 2024a). Safety fine-tuning approaches have been proposed to directly align MLLM outputs
045   with human values to alleviate these issues. These methods typically involve either fine-tuning the
046   model on an instruction-tuning dataset (Zong et al., 2024) containing toxic image and question in-
047   puts paired with safety response labels, or employing reinforcement learning from human feedback
048   (RLHF) (Chen et al., 2024). Despite these efforts, such alignment strategies can still be circum-
049   vented by carefully crafted adversarial perturbations, particularly in white-box scenarios, where the
050
      attacker has access to the model’s parameters and gradient information (Zong et al., 2024).
051   To illustrate this vulnerability, we conducted an experiment examining the susceptibility of a repre-
052   sentative safety-tuning approach, VLGuard (Zong et al., 2024), under various attacks across different

      A straightforward solution to these issues is to apply existing adversarial training techniques (Bai
085   et al., 2021), generating adversarial samples and using them to fine-tune the target model. How-
086   ever, most current adversarial training methods focus on closed-set classification tasks (Madry et al.,
087   2018; Shafahi et al., 2020), making them unsuitable for direct deployment on MLLMs, which in-
088   volve open-ended generation tasks. While some efforts have been validated on LLMs (Mazeika
089   et al., 2024; Xhonneux et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a), significant barriers remain when applying
090   these methods to MLLMs due to the multimodal nature of jailbreak attacks on MLLMs, where
091   the attacks can be executed on images, text, or both modalities, as discussed earlier. Furthermore,
092   the adversarial perturbations used in these approaches are usually generated by approximating gra-
093
      dients on discrete text (Mazeika et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a), which renders the fine-tuned model
      insufficiently robust against stronger attacks, such as noisy images with continuous values.
094
095   To overcome these challenges, we propose a novel adversarial training framework, S AFE MLLM. To
096   the best of our knowledge, this is the first framework to perform adversarial tuning on MLLMs. As
097   shown in Figure 2, S AFE MLLM adopts an adversarial training framework and includes two iterative
098   steps: generating adversarial perturbations for MLLMs and training the model on the generated
099
      perturbations. For perturbation generation in Step I, we introduce a novel contrastive embedding
      attack (CoE-Attack) strategy that injects adversarial noise at the token embedding level (i.e., Ph0 and
100
      Pt0 in Figure 2) to simulate toxic prompts across different modalities, mimicking real-world attacks.
101
      The adversarial noise is optimized with the primary objective of maximizing the model’s likelihood
102   of producing a positive affirmation. To further strengthen the attack, we incorporate a contrastive
103   loss term that minimizes the model’s probability of generating safety responses. After generating
104   these perturbations, we update the model parameters to mitigate their effects in Step II by fixing the
105   learned noise PhM and PtM from Step I. Additionally, we introduce a utility loss based on benign
106   image-question pairs to ensure that interactions with normal users remain unaffected. The fine-tuned
107   MLLM is thus able to defend against a wide range of jailbreak attacks, even when the entire model
      is publicly accessible.

126   best of our knowledge, this is the first study to use adversarial training as a defense strategy against
127   jailbreak attacks on MLLMs. We hope it can serve as a valuable reference for enhancing MLLM

136   2       R ELATED W ORK
137
138
      Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). Recent advancements in MLLMs have demon-
139   strated their remarkable capabilities in open-ended vision question answering (Li et al., 2023a; Zhu
140   et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2023). A typical MLLM architecture consists of three primary components.
141   First, a vision transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) is employed to extract visual features from the
142   input images. These features are then processed by a cross-modal adapter, which converts them into
143   image token embeddings. These adapters are commonly implemented using either linear projection
144   layers (Liu et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2024) or self-attention modules (Li et al., 2023b). Finally, the
145   image and text token embeddings are combined and sequentially fed into an LLM decoder, which
146   generates responses in an auto-regressive manner.
147   Jailbreak Attacks. Existing jailbreak attacks on MLLMs can be categorized based on the modalities
148   they exploit, such as images, text, or both. Image-based attacks (Niu et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024a)
149   attempt to bypass the model’s internal safeguards by pairing toxic queries with adversarial images.
150   These images can be optimized either to increase the likelihood of generating a positive response to
151   the harmful query (Niu et al., 2024), or by training on a small dataset of toxic text (Qi et al., 2024a).
152   Most text-based jailbreaks (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b; Chao et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023) are
153
      originally designed for LLMs. One approach is to craft semantically meaningful prompts that fool a
      targeted LLM in a black-box scenario. For example, GPTFuzzer (Yu et al., 2023) transforms human-
154
      curated templates to craft jailbreak prompts, and PAIR (Chao et al., 2023) directly utilizes another
155
      LLM to produce these prompts. Another approach is injecting non-word adversarial noise in the
156   white-box scenario. For example, GCG (Zou et al., 2023) modifies the original query by optimizing
157   an adversarial suffix, while AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024b) injects natural text segments into toxic
158   queries via a genetic algorithm. Image-text-based methods (Li et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2023; Liu
159   et al., 2023b) leverage domain transfer techniques to obscure harmful keywords by embedding them
160   into typography within images on various backgrounds, making detection more difficult. In this
161   paper, we introduce S AFE MLLM, a defense mechanism designed to mitigate all the above attack
      methods in white-box scenarios.

162
      Jailbreak Defenses. Current defense strategies for MLLMs generally fall into two categories. One
163   approach involves introducing additional modules (Helff et al., 2024; Pi et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
164   2024) at the inference stage, such as using an LLM-based detoxifier to neutralize toxic output (Pi
165   et al., 2024) or embedding an adaptive safety statement into the MLLM’s system prompts (Wang
166   et al., 2024). However, these methods are often accompanied by high computational overhead and
167   are limited by the capabilities of external resources. The second approach is to perform safety-
168   alignment fine-tuning of the target MLLM, either by fine-tuning on new datasets (Zong et al., 2024)
169   or using reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Chen et al., 2024). In contrast to
170   these methods, our proposed S AFE MLLM offers robust defenses against jailbreak attacks in white-
171
      box scenarios without requiring additional modules.

175   3.1   M ODEL OVERVIEW
176
177   Given a benign MLLM with parameters θ, our goal is to learn a robust MLLM with parameters
                                                   ∆θ ∗
178   θ ∗ . This process can be represented as θ −−−→ θ ∗ , where ∆θ ∗ denotes the finetuned parameters
179   optimized to defend against jailbreak attacks while preserving the model’s utility in standard interac-
180   tions. Note that the trainable parameters ∆θ ∗ are obtained from the cross-modal adapter and LLM
181   decoder, optimized using LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), while the parameters of the visual encoder are
182
      fixed, following existing MLLM training methods (Liu et al., 2023a; Dai et al., 2023). After tuning,
      the learned parameters θ ∗ and the corresponding gradient information will be publicly released to
183
      potential attackers. To achieve this goal, we propose S AFE MLLM, which is an adversarial tuning
184
      framework to enhance the robustness of MLLMs. As shown in Figure 2 and described in Algo-
185   rithm 1, the proposed S AFE MLLM operates in two iterative steps – generating the most substantial
186   attack perturbations (Step I) and mitigating their impact through model tuning (Step II).
187
188
      Step I – As discussed in Section 2, three types of jailbreak attacks exist to generate perturbations on
      different modalities. An ideal robust MLLM can successfully defend against any jailbreak attack.
189
      Toward this end, we propose the Contrastive Embedding Attack (CoE-Attack) strategy to generate
190
      strong attack perturbations by directly injecting perturbations into the model’s embeddings and opti-
191   mizing them via a contrastive goal. Step II – After generating the jailbreak perturbations, we update
192   the MLLM in the second step. The updating is performed by combining two objectives: the new
193   model should mitigate the harmful jailbreak effects based on the perturbed prompts while maintain-
194   ing normal performance on benign queries. Finally, we obtain the fine-tuned MLLM by repeating
195   the above steps for T iterations. Next, we will introduce the details of S AFE MLLM in each step.
196
197   3.2   S TEP I: C ONTRASTIVE E MBEDDING ATTACKS (C O E-ATTACK )
198
199   Existing jailbreak attack approaches achieve the attacks usually through introducing adversarial per-
200   turbations across different modalities, such as placing an adversarial image I′ before the malicious
201
      query xn ∈ X (Niu et al., 2024) or appending a string suffix x′ after the query (Zou et al., 2023),
      where X denotes the collection of malicious queries. However, only perturbing a specific modality
202
      may lead to a weak attack under the multimodal scenario. One straightforward approach to seeking
203
      the worst-case attack is to simultaneously optimize an adversarial image I′ and a text suffix x′ by
204   maximizing the likelihood of generating the positive affirmation cn (e.g., “Sure, here are steps for a
205   bad thing”) of the malicious query xn .
206
207
      This naive strategy will face two challenges. On the one hand, this process could be highly compu-
      tationally intensive, as the text suffix requires a greedy search over the vocabulary, while the image
208
      perturbations need to be processed through a heavy vision encoder. On the other hand, as noted in
209
      existing work (Xu et al., 2024), the probability of generating token sequences that align with nega-
210   tive responses (e.g., “As an AI language model, I cannot . . . ”) is not small enough after the attack,
211   which makes the model still output a refusal answer after the decoding strategies. To tackle these
212   challenges, we propose a novel CoE-Attack strategy, where the adversarial perturbations are injected
213   directly as token embeddings, thus reducing overall computing resources. Additionally, we further
214   introduce a contrastive loss based on a negative response rn of xn to enhance the attack strength.
215   Consequently, the proposed CoE-Attack can perform a powerful jailbreak attack without intensive
      computational consumption.

237   Data Preparation. During each training iteration i, we first sample a small corpus of malicious
238   queries Xi = {x1 , · · · , xN } from the toxic dataset X , i.e., Xi ⊂ X . For each query xn ∈ Xi ,
239   we adopt gpt-4-turbo to generate the affirmative response cn and the negative response rn
240   based on the prompt detailed in Appendix A. Here, we only collect the positive affirmation rather
241   than the full malicious responses, as designing precise harmful replies tailored to different queries
242   is inherently difficult and requires inevitable manual efforts. When generating the responses cn
243   and rn , we explicitly request gpt-4-turbo to generate them with different semantic styles and
244
      structures, allowing us to train adversarial perturbations on more diverse linguistic patterns.
245   Perturbation Initialization. Based on these responses, CoE-Attack will optimize the adversarial
246   perturbations from the token embedding level. Specifically, we first randomly initialize two pertur-
247   bation matrices Ph0 ∈ RK×C and Pt0 ∈ RK×C from word token embeddings, where K denotes the

      6                     C ONCLUSION
532   This paper introduces a novel research challenge focused on fine-tuning multimodal large language
533   models (MLLMs) to defend against a wide range of jailbreak attacks across diverse scenarios. To
534   address this challenge, we propose the S AFE MLLM framework, which leverages the CoE-Attack
535   strategy to generate adversarial embeddings and iteratively updates model parameters to simultane-
536   ously mitigate attack effects and maintain performance on benign inputs. Our extensive experiments,
537
      conducted across six MLLMs and six jailbreak attack methods, cover multiple modalities and both
      white-box and black-box settings. The results clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of S AFE MLLM
538
      in safeguarding MLLMs from various attack vectors while preserving their intended functionality
539
      and interaction capabilities.

805   et al., 2024b), FigStep (Gong et al., 2023) and MM-SafetyBench (Liu et al., 2023b).

810
      ImgJP. Given N malicious queries, the ImgJP attack method aims to optimize an adversarial im-
811   age by maximizing the probability of generating N target positive affirmations. The optimization
812   problem is solved using PGD (Madry et al., 2018). In our experiments, we follow Niu et al. (2024)
813   to perform ImgJP on AdvBench, where we train an unconstrained adversarial image on N = 25
814   questions and evaluate it on another 100 held-out prompts. We follow the official settings, using 100
815   iterations to optimize the adversarial image.
816
      VAA. Unlike the ImgJP method, VAA directly optimizes an adversarial image to maximize the
817   probability of generating a few-shot toxic corpus. Specifically, for each training iteration, VAA
818   first samples N toxic texts from the corpus as labels. Next, it only adopts the adversarial image as
819   the model’s input and optimizes the image noise by maximizing the log probability of generating
820   these toxic labels. In our experiment, we follow Qi et al. (2021) by first training an unconstrained
821   adversarial image on 66 toxic texts and then evaluating the ASR on 40 manually designed harmful
822   instructions. The image is optimized over 5000 iterations with a batch size of 8.
823   GCG. The GCG attack method compromises the victim model by appending a universal single suf-
824   fix string after the malicious queries. It employs a greedy gradient-based search strategy, selecting
825   candidate tokens with the largest negative gradient in the loss of generating target affirmative labels
826   for the malicious queries. For the attack setting, we follow Zou et al. (2023) to optimize an adver-
827   sarial text suffix consisting of 32 tokens based on 25 malicious queries extracted from AdvBench.
828   The string is optimized over 500 iterations and is tested on another 100 held-out malicious queries.
829   AutoDAN. The recently proposed AutoDAN is a sample-wise jailbreak attack method. For each
830   malicious query, it aims to generate a unique jailbreak prompt by injecting semantically meaningful
831   adversarial texts. These adversarial texts are generated by replacing synonyms in a prototype prompt
832   based on the genetic algorithm. In our experiment, we evaluate the ASR performance on the first
833   100 prompts of the AdvBench dataset. We follow Liu et al. (2024b) to optimize each adversarial
834   string over 100 iterations.
835   FigStep. FigStep is an image-text attack method that utilizes the domain transfer strategy to place
836   a malicious text question on a plain white image using typography. It then paraphrases the original
837   question into a harmless request, such as “Please answer the question shown in the image.”. FigStep
838   is a black-box attack approach, meaning it does not require access to the gradient information of
839   the victim MLLM. We evaluate this attack on the corresponding SafeBench-Tiny dataset proposed
840   by Gong et al. (2023).
841   MM-SafetyBench. MM-SafetyBench is also an image-text attack approach applied in the black-
842   box scenario. It inserts the toxic keywords extracted from the original prompt into an AI-generated
843   image, and then combines the image with a harmless request as the model inputs. We evaluate this
844   attack method on the corresponding dataset proposed by Liu et al. (2023b).

      of S AFE MLLM, and we introduce the structures of these models in the next section.

850
851   We introduce the details of all MLLMs used in our experiments, including MinGPT4-7B, MinGPT4-
852   13B, InstructBLIP-7B, InstructBLIP-13B, LLaVA-7B and LLaVA-13B. As described in Section 2,
853   all of these models are composed of a vision encoder, an LLM decoder, and a cross-modal adapter.
854   MiniGPT4-7B. For MiniGPT4-7B, it adopts the ViT-G model pre-trained from EVA-CLIP (Fang
855   et al., 2023) as the vision encoder. The encoder accepts the image with a shape of 224×224 as inputs
856   and embeds them into 64 image embedding tokens. For the cross-modal adapter, it leverages a single
857   linear projection layer. Finally, the LLM decoder is composed of the standard Llama-2-7b model.
858   MiniGPT4-13B. MiniGPT4-13B also adopts ViT-G as a vision encoder. Unlike MiniGPT4-7B,
859   MiniGPT4-13B incorporates Q-former (Li et al., 2023b) after ViT-G to further compress the image
860   embedding tokens. Here, the Q-former adopts the encoder-decoder-based transformer structure,
861   which leverages pre-trained queries to extract image representations through the cross-attention
862   mechanism. MiniGPT4-13B also uses the same cross-modal adapter as MiniGPT4-7B, which is
863   a linear projector. Finally, the LLM decoder is composed of vicuna-13b-delta-v0 (Chiang
      et al., 2023).

      InstructBLIP-7B. The vision encoder of InstructBLIP-7B is composed of the ViT-G model pre-
865   trained from EVA-CLIP (Fang et al., 2023). The extracted image representations will next interact
866   with text prompts via Q-former, which aims to extract context information. The cross-modal adapter
867   is a linear projection layer. Finally, the transformed embeddings are fed into the LLM decoder along
868   with the text prompt. Here the LLM decoder adopts vicuna-7b-v1.1.
869
      InstructBLIP-13B. The InstructBLIP-13B model shares the same structure as InstructBLIP-7B.
870
      It consists of the ViT-G model and Q-former as the vision encoder and the linear projector as the
871   cross-modal adapter. It also adopts vicuna-13b-v1.1 as the LLM decoder.

874

875
