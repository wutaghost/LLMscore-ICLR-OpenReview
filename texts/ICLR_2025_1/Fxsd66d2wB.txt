009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Constrained by the sub-optimal dataset in offline reinforcement learning (RL),
012            the offline trained agent should be online finetuned before deployment. Due to the
013            conservative offline algorithms and unbalanced state distribution in offline dataset,
014            offline to online finetuning faces severe distribution shift. This shift will disturb
015            the policy improvement during online interaction, even a performance drop. A
016            natural yet unexplored idea is whether policy improvement can be decoupled from
017            distribution shift. In this work, we propose a decoupled offline to online finetuning
018            framework using the dynamics model from model-based methods. During online
019
               interaction, only dynamics model is finetuned to overcome the distribution shift.
               Then the policy is finetuned in offline manner with finetuned dynamics and with-
020
               out further interaction. As a result, online stage only needs to deal with a simpler
021
               supervised dynamics learning, rather than the complex policy improvement with
022            the interference from distribution shift. When finetuning the policy, we adopt the
023            offline approach, which ensures the conservatism of the algorithm and fundamen-
024            tally avoids the sudden performance crashes. We conduct extensive evaluation on
025            the classical datasets of offline RL, demonstrating the effective elimination of dis-
026            tribution shift, stable and superior policy finetuning performance, and exceptional
027            interaction efficiency within our decouple offline to online finetuning framework.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   As an approach closely aligned with data-driven paradigms, offline reinforcement learning (Levine
033   et al., 2020) has ignited the enthusiasm of the community. A large number of algorithms have
034   been developed with remarkable speed, encompassing not only traditional RL algorithms that are
035   designed to overcome overestimation on out-of-distribution (OOD) state-action pairs (Kumar et al.,
036   2020; Fujimoto & Gu, 2021; Zhuang et al., 2023), but also supervised paradigms such as sequence
037   modeling (Chen et al., 2021; Zhuang et al., 2024). Due to the limitations of data quality, the policies
038   obtained from offline learning may not be optimal and are challenging to directly deploy in real-
039
      world scenarios. This has given rise to the problem of offline to online finetuning (Guo et al., 2023;
      Nakamoto et al., 2024), which aims to further improve the performance through online interaction.
040
041   Offline to online finetuning faces the challenge of distribution shift, which is caused by the un-
042   balanced state distribution in the offline dataset (Fu et al., 2020) and the inherent conservatism of
043   offline algorithms (Kumar et al., 2020). Online finetuning pursues the superior performance than of-
044   fline pretrained policy and the exploration on out-of-distribution region that may yield high return is
045
      unavoidable. Such exploration may be blind or even dangerous, such as sudden performance drops
      (Nakamoto et al., 2024; Lyu et al., 2022), unless the distribution shift has been eliminated. That
046
      is, offline to online finetuning is required to address two conflicting issues: policy optimization and
047
      distribution shift elimination. Existing methods can be broadly categorized into two classes: Some
048   algorithms (Nakamoto et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2022), less conservative in nature,
049   have been crafted in an attempt to mitigate the extent of distribution shift, yet they are powerless
050   against the inherently unbalanced distribution within the offline dataset. Other algorithms address
051   the distribution shift by imposing additional constraints (Lee et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023), making
052   the policy safer and more effective when exploring OOD regions. Regardless, the elimination of dis-
053   tribution shift and policy improvement are perpetually intertwined, with a compromise and trade-off
      that must exist. A natural question thus arises:


                                                        1

054
                 Can we decouple the elimination of distribution shift from policy improvement?
055
056   If so, we might first eliminate the distribution shift and then carry on the policy improvement. Such
057   an approach could fundamentally avoid conflicts and the associated compromises, maximizing the
058   capabilities of both distribution shift elimination algorithms and policy improvement.
059
      Within the context of model-based offline algorithms (Janner et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2020), we pro-
060   pose a framework named Decoupled Offline to Online Finetuning (DOOF). DOOF has successfully
061   decouples the elimination of distribution shift from policy improvement. Specifically, during the
062   online interaction phase, we focus solely on the elimination of distribution shift by finetuning a
063   more accurate dynamics model. Subsequently, we utilize this finetuned dynamics model to assist
064   in the finetuning of the policy in an offline mode, without the need for further interaction with the
065   environment. In this way, the online phase only needs to address a simpler supervised dynamics
066   model learning, rather than more complex policy improvement affected by the distribution shift. In
067   addition, we leverage the model uncertainty from MOPO to encourage the data collection on OOD
068
      regions where the distribution difference is significant and the dynamics prediction is inaccurate.
      The policy is finetuned through offline algorithms, which indicates that the inherent conservatism is
069
      retained and the algorithmic consistency is ensured, fundamentally avoiding sudden policy collapse.
070
      We validate our algorithm on the classic datasets from D4RL (Fu et al., 2020) and find that with
071   only 10k online interaction steps and 300k offline training steps, DOOF achieves significant perfor-
072   mance improvements. In contrast, other baseline models fail to improve but have also experienced a
073   decline in performance in some cases. Such exceptionally high interaction efficiency is attributed to
074   the simplification of the online phase and efficient exploration guided by the dynamics uncertainty.

123   2.3     O FFLINE TO O NLINE F INETUNING
124
125   In this paper, we only consider offline RL with datasets that comprise sub-optimal trajectories rather
126   than optimal ones. If optimal, naive supervised methods such as behavior cloning (Pomerleau, 1988)
127   would sufficient to learn an optimal policy, which is not the issue that offline RL aims to address.
128
      Definition 2.1 (Offline to Online Finetuning) Assume the offline dataset D is sub-optimal and the
129   offline pretrained policy πoff still sub-optimal. Offline to Online Finetuning aims to further improve
130   the performance of πoff through the interaction with environment M.
131
132   Challenges The entire state-action space (s, a) can be divided into three distinct segments based
133   on the alignment of state and action distributions with those from the offline dataset: a) in-
134   distribution (ID) state-action pairs (sD , aD ) with s ∼ D, a ∼ D, b) in-distribution (ID) state but
135   out-of-distribution (OOD) action pairs (sD , a¬D ) with s ∼ D, a ̸∼ D, c) totally out-of-distribution
136   (OOD) state-action pairs (s¬D , a) with s ̸∼ D, a ∼ A.
137   To guarantee the performance of πoff , offline algorithms enforce the conservative Q-value on ID state
138   but OOD action pairs (sD , a¬D ) to prevent choosing the OOD action a¬D given ID state sD (Zhuang
139   et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2020). Besides, the state distribution of offline dataset D is usually
140   unbalanced (Fu et al., 2020). As a result, offline to online finetuning faces severe distribution shift
141   (Lee et al., 2022). Online finetuning should simultaneously improve the policy while eliminate the
142   distribution shift. This entangled issue may lead to a sudden collapse in performance.
143
144   3       DOOF: D ECOUPLED O FFLINE TO O NLINE F INETUNING F RAMEWORK
145
146   Previous offline to online finetuning methods directly finetune πoff through online interaction (Nair
147   et al., 2020; Nakamoto et al., 2024; Beeson & Montana, 2022). These approaches aim to eliminate
148   distribution shift while simultaneously improve policy performance. However, there may be poten-
149   tial conflicts between these two objectives. Unlike previous work, our key insight lies in decoupling
150   the elimination of distribution shift from the policy improvement within the model-based frame-
151
      work. concretely, our framework first eliminates the distribution shift through the online finetuning
      of dynamics PM   c and then finetunes the policy πoff in offline manner without online interaction.
152
153   We first theoretically decouple the offline to online finetuning into two stages and then reveal the
154   relation between online dynamics finetuning and distribution shift elimination. Subsequently, we
155   develop our algorithm Dcoupled Offline to Online Finetuning (DOOF) based on the MOPO and its
156   uncertainty estimation. Last but not least, we discuss the advantages of this decoupled framework,
157
      especially on the interaction efficiency.

      3.3   D ISCUSSION AND A DVANTAGES
310
311   The online finetuning stage of DOOF is actually the supervised dynamics model learning, which is
312   significantly more straightforward than RL. To recover to the true transition dynamics on (st , at ),
313   the dynamics model PM    c only requires data on (st , at ). In contrast, RL problem naturally relies on
314   more diverse data distribution. Taking the Q-function as an example, to learn the optimal Q(st , at ),
315   it is not only data on (st , at ) is required, but also (st+1 , at+1 , st+2 , at+2 , · · · ). This is one of the
316   reasons why our framework boasts high interaction efficiency. Besides,the online buffer collected
317   by the uncertainty policy tends to include state-action pairs with large uncertainty. This data distri-
318   bution is precisely the region where the dynamics is less accurate, and also greatly contributes to the
319   elimination of distribution shift.
320   Our policy finetuning is conducted in an offline manner without online interaction. If the dynamics
321   is accurate enough, the policy is also equivalent to interacting with the environment. This is the
322   inherent advantage of a model-based framework, which can generate a broader distribution with a
323   small amount of training data. And this property further enhances the data efficiency. Moreover, the
      policy training stage remains conservative, which can prevent sudden performance drop.

      4   R ELATED W ORK
325
326   Offline reinforcement learning Mainstream offline model-free methods mainly contains two cat-
327   egories. One is policy constraint, which constrains the learned policy close to the behavior policy
328   based on different “closeness” such as batch constrained (Fujimoto et al., 2019), KL divergence (Wu
329   et al., 2019), MMD distance (Kumar et al., 2019), MSE constraint (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021) and TV
330   distance (Zhuang et al., 2023). The other is value regularization, which regularizes the value func-
331   tion from overestimation on OOD state-action pairs (Kumar et al., 2020; Kostrikov et al., 2021a; Bai
332   et al., 2022). Besides, Decision Transformer (DT) (Chen et al., 2021) directly maximizes the action
333   likelihood, which opens up a new paradigm called sequence modeling. And Reinformer (Zhuang
334
      et al., 2024) further propose the max-return sequence modeling. Based on the conservatism on dif-
      ferent components, offline model-based RL methods derived from MBPO (Janner et al., 2019) are
335
      divided into the following three categories: MOPO (Yu et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2021) and MOReL
336
      (Kidambi et al., 2020) propose to penalize the reward function by the uncertainty of the learned
337   dynamics models and MOBILE (Sun et al., 2023) theoretically conducts uncertainty quantification
338   through the inconsistency of Bellman estimations. COMBO (Yu et al., 2021) trains a conserva-
339   tive Q-function based on CQL (Kumar et al., 2020). RAMBO (Rigter et al., 2022) and ARMOR
340   (Bhardwaj et al., 2024) incorporates conservatism by modifying the transition dynamics.
341
342   Offline to online finetuning Offline to online finetuning aims to further improve the policy using
343   the offline pretrained policy as initialization. Offline to online finetuning encompasses two issues
344   to be solved: distribution shift elimination (DSE) and policy improvement (PI). The approaches to
345   dealing with the distribution shift can be roughly divided into two classes. One class tries to design
346
      less conservative algorithms (Lyu et al., 2022; Nakamoto et al., 2024; Kostrikov et al., 2021b; Wu

      6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
530
531
      In this work, we propose a decoupled model-based offline to online finetuning framework called
      DOOF. DOOF decouples the distribution shift elimination from the policy optimization in offline
532
      to online finetuning. Specifically, DOOF finetunes the dynamics model during online interaction
533
      to eliminate the distribution shift using the online buffer collected by the uncertainty guided policy.
534   And then the policy is finetuned with the help of the finetuned dynamics in offline manner without
535   further interaction. This decoupled framework not only ensures superior and stable performance but
536   also boasts exceptional interaction efficiency. Overall, model-based offline algorithms lag behind
537   model-free counterparts in terms of performance. Therefore, we intend to explore how to extend
538   this decoupled offline to online finetuning framework to model-free algorithms by incorporating an
539   additional trained dynamics model. This integration approach may be more conducive to unlocking
      the full potential of the decoupled framework.

      B     I MPLEMENT D ETAILS
763
764
      B.1   O FFLINE T RAINING
765
766   we firstly run MOPO on the environments to get offline trained dynamics model and policy. During
767   this stage, We train an ensemble of 7 dynamics models, each model in the ensemble is represented as
768   a 5-layer feedforward neural network with 400 hidden units on Adroit domain, but 4-layer feedfor-
769   ward neural network with 200 hidden units on other Gym domain. Once dynamics model training
770   finished , we use it to rollout for generating data and helping training the policy. During model
771   rollouts, we randomly pick 5 dynamics model from the 7 models. Finally, the mix batch consists
772   of 5% from an offline dataset and the rest from dynamic rollouts, which is used to train the policy
773
      optimized by SAC.
774
775   B.2   U NCERTAINTY P OLICY T RAINING
776
      We initialize the uncertainty policy as former offline pretrained policy. The uncertainty policy opti-
777   mization is based on SAC, we sample a uncertainty dataset of 10000 transitions from offline dataset
778   and modify the reward by adding the uncertainty which is determined by our online exploration co-
779   efficient and uncertainty value evaluated by offline trained dynamics model back to reward. For each
780   update, we sample a mix batch of 256 transitions where 2.5% of them is from the offline dataset,
781   2.5% of them is from the modified uncertainty dataset, and 95% is from the synthetic dataset gen-
782   erated by the offline trained dynamics model. During dynamics model rollouts, half of the obser-
783   vations required for dynamics rollout comes from offline dataset and another half comes from the
784   modified uncertainty dataset. We expect this training stage on the uncertainty policy will enhance
785
      its exploration.
786
787   B.3   O NLINE DATA C OLLECTING
788
      we just utilize the trained uncertainty policy to act in environment for collecting online interacting
789
      data which was only used for finetuning the dynamics later without any further training. It finds out
790   that very small interact steps setup on this stage can make significant impact on dynamics finetuning.
791   Actually we just collect 10 000 online transitions, which is of great help to finetune the dynamics
792   and enormously improve the policy performance.
793
794   B.4   DYNAMICS F INETUNING
795
796   After online data collecting finished, we finetune the offline trained dynamics in original offline
797   mode on both offline dataset and online collected dataset, for each update, the mix batch consists of
798   half from offline dataset and another half from online collected dataset is used to train the dynamics
799   model.
800
801   B.5   P OLICY F INETUNING
802
      The policy to finetune was initialized offline pretrained policy. Once dynamics finetuning finished,
803
      it’s used to assist in finetuning the policy in original offline mode with no need to interact with
804   environment further, which fundamentally avoid sudden policy collapse because the inherent con-
805   servatism is retained and the algorithmic consistency is ensured. while finetuning the policy, for
806   each update, we sample a mix batch size of 256 transitions consists of 2.5% from offline dataset
807   and 97.5% from the synthetic dataset which is generated by the finetuned dynamics model. During
808   dynamics models rollouts, half of the observations required to generate this synthetic dataset comes
809   from offline dataset and another half comes from the online collected dataset.
