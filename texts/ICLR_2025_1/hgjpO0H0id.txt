009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep state-space models (SSMs) have emerged as a powerful deep learning archi-
012            tecture for sequence modeling, but the theory of how these models learn long-term
013            dependencies lags the practice. To explain how parameterization and the number
014            of layers affect a model’s expressiveness, we study the properties of deep linear
015            SSMs, i.e., linearly coupled stacks of linear time-invariant systems. We show that
016
               such systems share timescales across layers, and we provide novel analysis on the
               role of linear feedforward connections in regularizing these temporal dependen-
017
               cies. In practice, SSMs can struggle with an explosion of the hidden state variance
018
               when learning long-term dependencies. We expand our theoretical understanding
019            of this problem for deep SSMs and provide new intuitions on how this problem
020            may be resolved by increasing the number of layers. Finally, we confirm our
021            theoretical results in a teacher-student framework and show the effects of model
022            parameterization on learning convergence.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Deep state space models (SSMs) have achieved impressive performance on a variety of long-range
      sequence modeling tasks. They are competitive with state-of-the-art models for natural language (Fu
028
      et al., 2023; Gu & Dao, 2023), image tasks (Nguyen et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2024), audio process-
029
      ing (Goel et al., 2022), video forecasting (Smith et al., 2024), reinforcement learning (Lu et al.,
030   2024), genetic sequence prediction (Schiff et al., 2024), and other time-series data (Zhou et al.,
031   2023; Patro & Agneeswaran, 2024). At their core, deep SSMs are remarkably simple — each layer
032   applies a linear filter to its inputs, then the outputs are nonlinearly transformed and passed to the
033   next layer. The simplicity of the architecture suggests these models may be amenable to theoreti-
034   cal treatment, and recent work has taken steps in this direction (Wang et al., 2023; Orvieto et al.,
035   2024; Cirone et al., 2024). However, the mechanisms explaining how deep SSMs learn long-range
036   dependencies is still not well understood.
037   One key question is how the number of layers (depth) and latent state size (width) affect a model’s
038   ability to learn long-range dependencies in sequential data. Learning such dependencies is challeng-
039   ing due to issues of vanishing and exploding gradients (Bengio et al., 1994) and increased sensitivity
040   to parameter changes as the model encodes long timescale dependencies (Zucchet & Orvieto, 2024).
041
      We address this question by considering the simplified setting of deep linear SSMs. Inspired by
042   prior theoretical analyses of deep linear feedforward networks (Baldi & Hornik, 1989; Saxe et al.,
043   2014; 2019), we consider deep SSMs where the nonlinearities between layers are removed. Recent
044   work has studied the learning dynamics of single layer linear SSMs and shown that the analytical
045   solutions derived from such models can provide intuition for their nonlinear counterparts (Zucchet
046   & Orvieto, 2024; Smékal et al., 2024). While a deep linear SSM can also be cast as a single layer
047   model, we show that the learning dynamics of deep linear models vary with memory expressivity.
048   Specifically, we study how the memory of a deep linear SSM, as measured by properties of its
049   impulse response, autocorrelation, and transfer functions, vary with the depth and width of the
050   model. These properties lead to predictions of how learning dynamics will vary across architectures,
051   which we test empirically in a teacher-student setting (Hardt et al., 2019; Zucchet & Orvieto, 2024).
052   We find regimes in which increasing depth may lead to faster convergence than increasing width,
053   and discuss practical extensions of our theory to deep SSMs with nonlinear transformations between
      layers.

      2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
055
056
      Deep SSMs consist of stacks of state space layers with nonlinear coupling in between. Each state
057   space layer maps an input sequence u1:T to an output sequence y1:T via a linear filter,
058
059                               xt = At xt−1 + Bt ut ,            yt = Ct xt ,                         (1)
060
      where xt ∈ RN are the latent states. The outputs yt are passed through a nonlinearity before
061
      becoming the inputs to the next layer. We consider the special case of linear time-invariant (LTI)
062   systems in which At ≡ A, Bt ≡ B, and Ct ≡ C, as in several deep SSMs (Gu et al., 2022a;
063   Smith et al., 2023). Furthermore, we focus on single-input (ut ∈ R), single-output (yt ∈ R) SSMs
064   parameterized by a real-valued, diagonal state-transition matrix A ∈ RN ×N , input vector B ∈
065   RN ×1 , and output vector C ∈ R1×N , as in S4D (Gu et al., 2022b).
066
      Following the empirical success of deep SSMs, many questions have emerged about their temporal
067
      expressivity and how they compare to other sequence modeling architectures. A growing body of
068   theoretical work has elucidated their expressive power and potential limitations. Orvieto et al. (2024)
069   and Wang & Xue (2023) have shown that state space layers with nonlinear coupling in between
070   are sufficiently expressive to approximate any sequence-to-sequence map, and Cirone et al. (2024)
071   provided an extended analysis on the expressivity benefits of input-controlled state space dynamics.
072
      Recent work has made theoretical progress toward analyzing the learning dynamics of single-layer
073
      SSMs. In this case, the problem reduces to learning the parameters of a linear dynamical system
074   with gradient descent, as studied by Hardt et al. (2019). While the linear recurrences of deep SSMs
075   make it easier to control the issue of vanishing and exploding gradients (Bengio et al., 1994), Zuc-
076   chet & Orvieto (2024) showed that even outside the exploding regime, the latent states xt become
077   increasingly sensitive to parameter changes as the magnitude of the eigenvalues λ of the dynamics
078   matrix A approach one. They refer to this problem as the curse of memory. It leads to sharp loss
079   landscapes which make gradient-based optimization particularly challenging. In practice, this chal-
080   lenge has been addressed at the layer level with careful discretization techniques, like those used in
081   the S4 and S5 architectures (Gu et al., 2022a; Smith et al., 2023), or explicit input regularization
082
      such as that used in the LRU (Orvieto et al., 2023).
083   However, the curse of memory has not been studied for deep SSMs. The nonlinearities between
084   layers render such models analytically intractable. Here, we follow previous work on analyzing deep
085   linear feedforward networks (Baldi & Hornik, 1989; Saxe et al., 2014; 2019) and study deep linear
086   SSMs. With the nonlinearity between layers removed, we can characterize the effective memory of
087   these models and its effects on learning dynamics. We show that these considerations have practical
088
      impact on parameterization choices of depth and width in deep SSMs.
089
090   3     T HEORETICAL R ESULTS
091
092   We consider three ways of formalizing the memory of a deep linear SSM: in terms of the autocorre-
093   lation of successive layers’ outputs; in terms of the group delay, which is derived from the frequency
094   response function; and in terms of the eigendecomposition of an equivalent 1-layer linear SSM. We
095   use these formalisms to study how depth and width affect learning and memory in deep SSMs.
096
097   3.1   M EMORY AND THE AUTOCORRELATION F UNCTION
098
099
      One way to characterize the memory of a state space layer is in terms of the autocorrelation func-
      tion (ACF) of its outputs. The ACF has historically been applied to time-series data to identify an
100
      appropriate statistical model for forecasting (Shumway & Stoffer, 2017). Here, we are take a dif-
101
      ferent approach and apply the ACF to interpret the outputs of a given model. Intuitively, the ACF
102   depends on two factors: the autocorrelation of the layer’s inputs and the eigenvalues of its dynam-
103   ics matrix. When the eigenvalues are close to one, the layer will exhibit long-timescale dynamics,
104   and its outputs will have strong autocorrelations. When the inputs are autocorrelated, we expect the
105   outputs to be as well. Moreover, as Zucchet & Orvieto (2024) showed, the variance of a layer’s
106   latent states and gradients scales with the autocorrelation of the inputs. Thus, understanding how
107   autocorrelation varies across layers of a deep SSM is paramount to understanding its memory and
      learning dynamics.

      We used simple simulations to test the effects of depth and width on the memory of deep SSMs. To
      have full control over the types of long-term dependencies, we train our deep SSMs in a teacher-
295
      student setting (Hardt et al., 2019; Zucchet & Orvieto, 2024). This approach also allows us to
296
      compare the results of learning when the student and teacher differ in number of layers, latent state
297   size, ablations on which components of the model are learnable, etc. The inputs to each model
298   are sampled from uncorrelated white noise, ut ∼ N (0, 1). The student is trained with the Adam
299   optimizer on the mean-squared error loss between the teacher’s outputs and its outputs.

402   4.2   D EPTH VS WIDTH IN LEARNING CONVERGENCE
403
404   Section 3.3 discussed the equivalence between K-layer, N -dimensional state space models and a
405
      single layer, KN -dimensional system. When both B and C are learned, these two representations
      should be able to express the same temporal dependencies. Here we studied if this is true in practice
406
      using the experimental teacher-student paradigm.
407
408   We trained a student model with K = 2, N = 4, as well as a student with one layer and N = 8
409   dimensional states. The teacher was a K = 3-layer deep linear SSM with latent state size of
410   N = 2. The two student models were overparameterized with respect to the teacher, rendering
411
      the task solvable. Figure 3B shows the exact parameterization of the teacher model and the results
      of the training runs. Both students were able to learn this task, as expected given their equivalent
412
      representational capacity.
413
414   However, the empirical results also suggested that two students learn differently. The left panel in
415   Figure 3B shows two training runs of the single-layer student, a failed run and a successful run. The
416   result appeared to be influenced by the initialization of the feed-forward projections B and C. In
417
      the failed run, these parameters were initialized with values of O(1) for both students, following
      standard practice Gu et al. (2022c). In that regime, however, the eigenvalues of A diverged to
418
      regions outside the edge of stability. By sufficiently amplifying the initialization of B and C (to
419
      O(50), found empirically), we found that the eigenvalues of A remained stable throughout learning.
420   Generally, we found that the single-layer student’s learning convergence was more sensitive to the
421   initial values of the feed-forward projections than the multi-layer parameterization.

      5   D ISCUSSION
439
      We analyzed the role of depth and width in learning long-term dependencies with deep state space
440
      models. By considering three measures of memory, we developed a new theoretical understanding
441
      of how deep SSMs encode long timescales and how parameterization in latent state size and the
442   number of layers can affect learning. We showed that across the layers of a deep linear state space
443   model, the autocorrelation function diverges as the eigenvalues of a latent dynamics matrix A go
444   to one, which in turn causes the variance of the latent states to diverge. These findings extend our
445   understanding of the challenge of learning long-term dependencies with recurrent models, known as
446   the curse of memory (Zucchet & Orvieto, 2024).
447
      On the other hand, we found that depth might help to alleviate the curse of memory by allowing
448   diagonal state space models to share timescales across layers. This allows deep SSMs to capture
449   global long-term dependencies with small eigenvalues of the dynamics matrices A(l) at each layer
450   l. This analysis used the notion of group delay as a proxy for memory in deep SSMs. Using
451   the same theoretical framework, we analyzed the role of width in diagonal state space models in
452   capturing temporal dependencies. This revealed the role of feedforward projections B and C in
453   the group delay of the system and suggested how these parameters may regularize the eigenvalues
454   of A in practice. We validated our theoretical results in a synthetic teacher-student setting. Our
455   empirical study of this task revealed regimes in which depth may reduce the model’s sensitivity to
456   parameter initialization and improve learning of long-range dependencies. This suggests there is still
457
      a theoretical gap to be filled in fully explaining the role of depth and width in learning convergence
      and initialization sensitivity. We plan to address these open questions in future work.
