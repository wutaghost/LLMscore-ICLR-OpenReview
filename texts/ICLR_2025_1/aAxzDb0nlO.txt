                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Prioritized experience replay, which improves sample efficiency by selecting rel-
010
               evant transitions to update parameter estimates, is a crucial component of con-
011            temporary value-based deep reinforcement learning models. Typically, transitions
012            are prioritized based on their temporal difference error. However, this approach
013            is prone to favoring noisy transitions, even when the value estimation closely ap-
014            proximates the target mean. This phenomenon resembles the noisy TV problem
015            postulated in the exploration literature, in which exploration-guided agents get
016            stuck by mistaking noise for novelty. To mitigate the disruptive effects of noise
017            in value estimation, we propose using epistemic uncertainty to guide the prioriti-
018            zation of transitions from the replay buffer. Epistemic uncertainty quantifies the
019
               uncertainty that can be reduced by learning, hence reducing transitions sampled
               from the buffer generated by unpredictable random processes. We first illustrate
020
               the benefits of epistemic uncertainty prioritized replay in two tabular toy models: a
021
               simple multi-arm bandit task, and a noisy gridworld. Subsequently, we evaluate our
022            prioritization scheme on the Atari suite, outperforming quantile regression deep
023            Q-learning benchmarks; thus forging a path for the use of epistemic uncertainty
024            prioritized replay in reinforcement learning agents.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has proven highly effective across a diverse array of problems,
030   consistently yielding state-of-the-art results in control of dynamical systems (Nian et al., 2020;
031   Degrave et al., 2022; Weinberg et al., 2023), abstract strategy games (Mnih et al., 2015; Silver et al.,
032   2016), continual learning (Khetarpal et al., 2022; Team et al., 2021), and multi-agent learning (OpenAI
033
      et al., 2019; Baker et al., 2020). It has also been established as a foundational theory for explaining
      phenomena in cognitive neuroscience (Botvinick et al., 2020; Subramanian et al., 2022). Nonetheless,
034
      a significant drawback of these methods pertains to their inherent sample inefficiency whereby
035
      accurate estimations of value and policy necessitate a substantial demand for interactions with the
036   environment.
037
038   Sample inefficiency has been mitigated through the use of—among other methods—Prioritized
039
      Experience Replay (PER) (Schaul et al., 2016). PER is an extension of Experience Replay (Lin,
      1992), which uses a memory buffer populated with past agent transitions to improve training stability
040
      through the temporal de-correlation of data used in parameter updates. Subsequently, PER extends
041
      this approach by sampling transitions from the buffer with probabilities proportional to their abso-
042   lute Temporal Difference (TD) error, thereby allowing agents to prioritise learning from pertinent
043   data. PER has been widely adopted as a standard technique in DRL; however, despite significantly
044   better performance over uniform sampling in most cases, it is worth noting that PER can encounter
045   limitations under specific task conditions and agent designs. The most prominent example of such a
046   limitation is related to the so-called noisy TV problem (Burda et al., 2018), a thought experiment at
047   the heart of the literature around exploration in RL. Just as novelty-based exploration bonuses can
048   trap agents in noisy states, PER is susceptible to frequently replaying transitions involving high levels
049   of randomness (e.g. in reward or transition dynamics) even if they do not translate to meaningful
050
      learning and thus are not useful for solving the task.
051   To combat this issue, we propose combining epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty measures (Clements
052   et al., 2020; Alverio et al., 2022; Lahlou et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023), originally
053   used to promote exploration, under an information gain criterion for use in replay prioritization.
      Epistemic uncertainty, the uncertainty reducible through learning, is the key quantity of interest.


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      However this need to be appropriately ‘calibrated’, which we show-both empirically, and with
055   justification from Bayesian inference-can be done effectively by dividing the epistemic uncertainty
056   estimate by an aleatoric uncertainty estimate (and taking the logarithm, i.e. the information gain).
057   Intuitively the need for this kind of calibration can be seen by considering the following game: the
058   aim is to estimate the mean of two distributions; the ground truth is that both distributions have
059   identical mean but different variance, and your current estimates for both distributions are the same
060   i.e. your epistemic uncertainty on the mean is the same for both distributions. However if I offer
061   you a new sample from either distribution to refine your estimate you would choose to sample the
062   distribution with lower variance since this is more likely to be informative. In addition to arguing
063
      for this novel prioritization variable, we also provide candidate methods involving distributions of
      ensembles (in the vein of Clements et al. (2020)) to estimate these quantities.
064
065   Our primary contributions are as follows: (1) In Section 3, we present a novel approach for estimating
066   epistemic uncertainty, building upon an existing uncertainty formalisation introduced by Clements
067   et al. (2020) & Jiang et al. (2023). This extension incorporates information about the target value that
068   the model aims to estimate thereby accounting for bias in the estimator; (2) We derive a prioritisation
069
      variable using estimated uncertainty quantities, finding a specific functional form derived from a
      concept called information gain, showing that both, epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty should
070
      be considered for prioritisation; (3) In Section 4, we illustrate the advantages of our proposed
071
      epistemic uncertainty prioritisation scheme through two interpretable toy models—a bandit task and
072   a grid world; (4) In Section 5, we demonstrate the effectiveness of this method on the Atari-57
073   benchmark (Bellemare et al., 2013), where it significantly outperforms baseline models based on a
074   combination of PER, QR-DQN and ensemble agents.

      2.2   P RIORITIZED E XPERIENCE R EPLAY
099
100
      Reinforcement learning algorithms are notoriously sample inefficient. A widely adopted practice to
101
      mitigate this issue is the use of an experience replay buffer, which stores transitions in the form of
102   (st , at , rt , st+1 ) for later learning (Mnih et al., 2015). Loosely inspired by hippocampal replay to the
103   cortex in mammalian brains (Foster & Wilson, 2006; McNamara et al., 2014), its primary conceptual
104   motivation is to reduce the variance of gradient-based optimization by temporally de-correlating
105   updates, thereby improving sample efficiency. It can also serve to prevent catastrophic forgetting
106   by maintaining transitions from different time scales. The effectiveness of this buffer can often
107   be improved further by prioritising some transitions at the point of sampling rather than selecting
      uniformly. Formally, when transition i is placed into replay, it is given a priority pi . The probability

113   Schaul et al. (2016) introduced prioritized experience replay, which most often uses the absolute
114   TD-error |δi | of transition i, as pi = |δi | + ϵ where a small ϵ constant ensures transitions with zero

121   predictions should be replayed more often than transitions on which the agent already has low error.
122   While this heuristic is reasonable and has enjoyed empirical success, TD-errors can be insufficiently
123   distinct from the irreducible aleatoric uncertainty; considering instead uncertainty measures more
124   explicitly, this form of prioritisation can be significantly improved.

126   2.3     U NCERTAINTY E STIMATION IN RL
127
128   Uncertainty is a fundamental concept in statistics. Within machine learning, it has predominately been
129   studied in supervised learning, particularly with Bayesian methods (Lahlou et al., 2022; Narimatsu
130   et al., 2023). Various aspects of the task setting such as bootstrapping and non-stationarity make
131
      uncertainty estimation a significantly more challenging problem in RL; nevertheless, it has featured
      more prominently in recent work, including for use in generalization (Jiang et al., 2023), as reward
132
      bonuses in exploration (Nikolov et al., 2019), and to guide safe actions (Lütjens et al., 2019; Kahn
133
      et al., 2017). We discuss here some of the key concepts around uncertainty relevant to this work,
134   particularly those that address the delineation between aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty. A more
135   comprehensive overview of related work around uncertainty in RL can be found in Appendix A.
136
137   2.3.1    B OOTSTRAPPED DQN
138
139   The concept behind bootstrapping is to approximate a posterior distribution by sampling a prediction
140   from an ensemble of estimators, where each estimator is initialized randomly and observes a distinct
141
      subset of the data (Tibshirani, 1994; Bickel & Freedman, 1981). In RL, Osband et al. (2016)
      introduced a protocol known as bootstrapped DQN for deep exploration, whereby bootstrapping
142
      is used to approximate the posterior of the action-value function, from which samples can be drawn.
143
      Each agent within an effective ensemble, parameterized by ψ, is randomly initialized and trained
144   using a different subset of experiences via random masking. A sample estimate of the posterior
145   distribution, denoted as ψ ∼ P (ψ|D) (D being training data), is obtained by randomly selecting
146   one of the agents from the ensemble. In this work, we use extensions of the bootstrapped DQN idea
147   in our epistemic uncertainty measurements—notably the ensemble disagreement.
148
149   2.3.2    D ISTRIBUTIONAL RL
150
151
      Learning quantities beyond the mean return has been a long-standing programme of RL research,
      with particular focus on the return variance (Sobel, 1982). A yet richer representation of the return
152
      is sought by more recent methods known collectively as distributional RL (Bellemare et al., 2017),
153
      which aims to learn not just the mean and variance, but the entire return distribution. We focus here
154   on one particular class of distributional RL methods: those that model the quantiles of the distribution,
155   specifically QR-DQN (Dabney et al., 2017). A broader treatment of the distributional RL literature
156   can be found in Bellemare et al. (2023).
157
      In QR-DQN, the distribution of returns, for example from taking action a in state s and subsequently
158
      following policy π, η π (s, a) is approximated as a quantile representation (Bellemare et al., 2023), that
159
      is, as a uniform mixture of Diracs, and trained through quantile regression (Koenker & Hallock, 2001).

      In this section we will introduce a new method for estimating epistemic uncertainty, which arises from
      a decomposition of the total uncertainty as defined by the average error over both the ensemble and
230
      quantiles. This decomposition is in the vein of Clements et al. (2020); however, it considers distance
231
      from the target in addition to the disagreement within the ensemble, thereby allowing us to handle—
232   among others—model bias. We go on to derive an expression for prioritisation variables based on
233   the concept of information gain, which trades off epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty with a view to
234   maximizing learnability from each sampled transition. We name this method Uncertainty Prioritised
235   Experience Replay (UPER). Importantly, we are not changing the prioritize replay algorithm itself,
236   but just the variable pi used to prioritise in Equation 2, replacing the TD-error by the information
237   gain.

409    In order to move toward the full RL problem, we consider in this section a tabular gridworld. We take
410    inspiration from ideas in planning within dynamic programming methods (Moore & Atkeson, 1993)
411    to probe uncertainty-guided prioritised replay. Typically under this framework, ‘direct’ reinforcement
412
       learning on interactions with the environment (sometimes referred to as control) is supplemented with
      ‘indirect’ learning of a model from stored experiences (sometimes referred to as planning). In our case,
413
      we learn purely model-free but retain these ideas of offline vs. online learning. In some ways these
414
       methods are the pre-cursor to the use of experience replay buffers in DRL. When making updates on
415    stored data offline (for planning or otherwise), the same questions around criteria for prioritisation
416    arise. Notably, prioritised sweeping (preference over high error samples in memory) was an early
417    extension to the Dyna models that exemplify this learning protocol (Sutton, 1991). In Figure 1e
418    Map, we construct a gridworld where the agent can encounter a set of very noisy states with random
419    rewards early on in the episode while a single deterministic state with a much larger reward is at the
420    end of the maze. Figure 1d shows that this simple task can be solved without the additional planning
421    steps, but ER (sampling uniformly) helps improve sample efficiency. This is improved further by
422    PER (prioritising using TD), but even more so by UPER where we prioritize using the information
423
       gain criterion and the inverse of state visitation counts (a good proxy for epistemic uncertainty in this
       tabular setting). As shown by the heatmaps in Figure 1e, PER over-samples noisy states while UPER
424
       prioritises on novel states towards the end of the trajectory. Full details of the experimental setup and
425
       hyper-parameters can be found in Appendix F.

465
466   Exploration. While UPER is not explicitly promoting exploration through a reward bonus to
467   unexplored or uncertainty states, we borrow methods from this field to estimate epistemic and
468   aleatoric uncertainty (Clements et al., 2020) to prioritize transitions from the replay buffer based on
469   the information gain. A fundamental dilemma faced by RL agents is the exploration-exploitation trade-
470   off (Osband et al., 2016; O’Donoghue, 2023), in which agents must balance competing objectives
471
      for action selection, between uncovering new information about the environment (exploration) and
      accumulating as much reward as they currently can (exploitation). Replay sampling and exploration
472
      strategies both affect the data used to enhance the estimation of the value function. The former
473
      controls the experiences used for value estimation updates, while the latter selects experiences that
474   will end up populating the replay buffer. Many exploration strategies have been built around ideas
475   of intrinsic reward (Oudeyer & Kaplan, 2007) and episodic memory (Savinov et al., 2019; Badia
476   et al., 2020). These are susceptible to pathological behaviour induced by the noisy TV, and later
477   variants are designed partly with this problem in mind; as a result they are frequently concerned with
478   reliable and meaningful estimates of counts and novelty (Ostrovski et al., 2017b; Bellemare et al.,
479   2016b; Burda et al., 2018; Lobel et al., 2023), dynamics (Stadie et al., 2015; Pathak et al., 2017),
480   uncertainty (Mavor-Parker et al., 2022), and related quantities—many of which are relevant to our
481   problem of constructing suitable measures for replay prioritization.
482   PER. Various efforts have been made to understand and improve upon aspects of prioritized expe-
483   rience replay since its introduction by Schaul et al. (2016). Integration of information related to
484   uncertainty has often been in conjunction with strategies for managing the exploration-exploitation
485   trade-off. For instance, in Sun et al. (2020), frequently visited states are sampled more frequently to
      reduce uncertainty around known states. Conversely, Alverio et al. (2022) approach is prioritizing

508   7    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSIONS
509
510
      In this study, we propose using epistemic uncertainty measures to guide the prioritization of
511   transitions from the replay buffer. We demonstrate both via mathematical analysis and careful
512   experiments that the typically applied TD-error criterion can include aleatoric uncertainty, and lead
513   to over-sampling of noisy transitions. Prioritizing by a principled function of epistemic and aleatoric
514   uncertainty in the form of the information gain mitigates these effects. To construct this function, we
515   expand the concept of epistemic uncertainty from Clements et al. (2020) to incorporate the distance
516   to the target, achieving performance advantages in toy settings and complex problems such as the
517   Atari 57 benchmark. In estimating these auxiliary quantities, one concern may be the increased
518   computational cost in the deep learning setting. However, sharing of the lower level representation
519
      over multiple heads alongside efficient implementations can significantly mitigate this burden. To
      demonstrate this, we conducted an experiment on a lower-capacity GPU comparing the training
520
      times of DQN, QR-DQN, and QR-DQN + ensemble networks in the Pong environment. The time per
521
      iteration is presented in Table 1. The comparable training times can be attributed to effective batch
522   processing facilitated by GPU parallelization. In our implementation, each agent in the ensemble is
523   represented by a distinct output head in the network architecture. By extending the batch dimension
524   to (batch, action, quantiles, ensemble), we leverage the parallelization capacity of the GPU, which
525   still operates within capacity for the QR-DQN ensemble network. Further details of this experiment
526   and the computer architecture used are presented in Subsection G.2. Note that this analysis does not
527   aim to evaluate or compare the computational cost of sampling with a priority variable vs. uniform
528   sampling. This is already addressed in the original PER paper and has negligible impact.
529   While we focus our implementation on distributional RL—a widely used set of methods, exploring
530   other forms of uncertainty estimation in RL such as pseudo-counts (Lobel et al., 2023), in
531   combination with different functional forms outside information gain, is a promising research
532   path both for different prioritisation schemes and related parts of the RL problem like exploration
533   (see Appendix D and Appendix E).
534   The framework of combining epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties in an information gain introduced
535   in this work is not restricted to reinforcement learning. In principle, these concepts can be
536   extrapolated to other learning systems. A substantial body of literature exists on the efficient selection
537   of datapoints to enhance learning in other paradigms such as supervised (Hüllermeier & Waegeman,
538   2021; Zhou et al., 2022), continual (Henning et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021), or active learning (Nguyen
539   et al., 2022). In addition, our work has the potential to offer alternative insights into replay events
      in biological agents (Daw et al., 2005; Mattar & Daw, 2018; Liu et al., 2019; Antonov et al., 2022).

947   A.2     BAYESIAN METHODS
948
949
      A more comphrehensive Bayesian approach to the reinforcement learning problem can be formulated
      via so-called Bayes-adaptive Markov decision processes (BAMDPs) (Martin, 1967), where an agent
950
      continuously updates a belief distribution over underlying Markov decision processes. Solutions to
951
      BAMDPs are Bayes’ optimal in the sense that they optimally trade off exploration and exploitation to
952   maximise expected return. However, in all but the smallest environments and settings, learning over
953   this entire belief distribution is intractable (Brunskill, 2012; Asmuth & Littman, 2012).
954
955
      Posterior sampling, which can be viewed as the analogue of Thompson sampling for MDPs, has
      been a popular method to approximate the full Bayesian posterior e.g. via ensembles (Osband et al.,
956
      2016) or dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016); extensions include provision of pseudo priors (Osband
957
      et al., 2018; 2021). While these approaches have been successful in some settings, they have few
958   guarantees. A different line of work includes using methods such as meta-learning to reason on and
959   train the approximate posterior (Zintgraf et al., 2019; Humplik et al., 2019).
960
961
      With regards to the discussions on epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty, the above methods can give
      the model access to a distribution over parameters that can be sampled and operated on (e.g. to
962
      calculate variance). They do not however—Bayes optimal or not—lead per se to a decomposition
963
      into epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty.
964
965
      A.3     C OUNTS
966
967   Another category of methods that are frequently used in reinforcement learning and related paradigms
968   like bandits is based around notions of counts e.g. of state visitation. Such counts can be used
969   to construct intervals/bounds on confidence of learned quantities. This is the foundation of well
970   established exploration methods in tabular settings called upper confidence bounds (Auer, 2002b;a).
971   In function approximation settings, much of the focus has been on constructing accurate pseudo
      counts that incorporate state similarities (Bellemare et al., 2016a; Ostrovski et al., 2017a; Tang et al.,


                                                          18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       2017). Despite the well demarcated distinction between count-based methods and those that address
973    the Bayesian posterior above, with access to any mean-zero unit-variance distribution, an ensemble
974    of mean-predictors of that distribution can be used to estimate pseudo-counts (Lobel et al., 2023). As
975    a result, it is generally possible to convert a Bayesian posterior into pseudo-counts.
976
977    A.4   M ODEL -BASED
978
979    A set of methods that is further removed from those used in our work, but are often motivated by
980    similar questions consists of learning a model of the environment. Downstream quantities like the
981    prediction error of the environment model can be used as proxies for uncertainty or novelty e.g. for
982
       exploration bonuses. Much of this work falls under the domain of intrinsic motivation (Barto, 2013).
       Some of the methods in this area e.g. curiosity (Pathak et al., 2017) attempt implicitly to make the
983
       distinction between epistemic uncertainty and aleatoric uncertainty to avoid the noisy TV problem.

1154
       Having arrived at various methods for estimating epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty using distribu-
       tional reinforcement learning, we now consider how to construct prioritisation variables from these
1155
       estimates. Naively, one might consider prioritising directly using the epistemic uncertainty estimate;
1156
       but neglecting the inherent noise or aleatoric uncertainty entirely ignores the ‘learnability’ of the data.
1157   Many methods in related learning domains can be interpreted as incorporating both uncertainties,
1158   including Kalman learning Welch et al. (1995); Gershman (2017), active learning Cohn et al. (1996),
1159   weighted least-squares regression Greene (2000), and corresponding extensions in deep learning
1160   and reinforcement learning Mai et al. (2022). To gain an intuition on how the choice of functional
1161   form might impact our particular use-case of prioritisation for various magnitudes of epistemic and
1162   aleatoric uncertainty.
1163   E/A has desirable properties. For instance under Bayesian learning of Gaussian distributions,
1164   log(1 + E/A) maximises information gain (see Subsection 3.2), but discontinuities around very low
1165   noise must be dealt with—for instance by adding small constants to the denominator. Normalising
1166   instead with the total uncertainty is another way of handling the discontinuities. E 2 /U in particular
1167   corresponds to maximising reduction in variance under Bayesian learning in the same Gaussian
1168   setting. Both of these forms have the advantage over e.g. E/A of preferring low epistemic uncertainty
1169   for equal ratios of epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties, i.e. they are not constant along the diagonal
1170   of the phase diagram. More generally, it is difficult to say a priori which functional form is optimal.
1171
       Many factors, including the data distributions, model and learning rule will play a role. Further
       discussion on these considerations can be found in Subsection D.4. These trade-offs are also borne
1172
       out empirically in the experimental Section 4 & Section 5 below.

       D.4    B IAS AS T EMPERATURE
1175
1176   Lahlou et al. (2022) and others make an equivalence between excess risk and epistemic uncertainty.
1177   Concretely, if f ∗ (x) is the Bayes optimal predictor, the excess risk is defined as:
1178
                                          ER(f, x) = R(f, x) − R(f ∗ , x),                                   (41)
1179
1180   where R is the risk and R(f ∗ , x) can be thought of as the aleatoric uncertainty.
1181
       One possible issue arises in overstating the connection between excess risk and epistemic uncertainty.
1182   Consider the case where there is model mis-specification, and f ∗ is not in the model class; then
1183   assuming the model class is fixed (as is standard), then the lower bound of ER(f, x) is non-zero.
1184   Stated differently, it is not fully reducible, which is often viewed as a central property of epistemic
1185   uncertainty. For some applications this distinction may not be important; there is some non-zero lower
1186   bound to the epistemic uncertainty but the ordering and correlations are intact under this equivalence.
1187   But it could also play a significant role. For us in particular, adopting this equivalence has two related
       consequences:

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
             1. The model mis-specification acts as a temperature for our prioritisation distribution;
1189
1190
             2. The ratio, or more generally the functional form of our prioritisation variable, can offset this
                temperature.

1193   difference between the Bayes predictor, and the best predictor in the model class i.e. the model bias
1194   or mis-specification term. Let us denote this term by C, and assume it constant over the domain.
1195   And let us denote the fully reducible uncertainty by η. In the case where we use the excess risk, the
1196   prioritisation of sample i is given by

1202   It is of course hard to measure this model mis-specification term. In large networks we can assume
1203   the capacity is unlikely to be restrictive, but perhaps other parts of the training regime could play a
1204   part. Importantly, the above holds true not just for model mis-specification, but also if there is any
1205   systematic error in the epistemic uncertainty estimate (i.e. think of C as an error on the epistemic

1308   Leaving aside the ratio forms, the consequences of the temperature effect may differ depending on the
1309   choice of epistemic uncertainty estimate we use. The methods we discuss in Section 2 & Section 3
1310   all effectively use the equivalence of excess risk and epistemic uncertainty, and so do not explicitly
1311   consider the possibility of model bias. The possible exception is the method resulting from the
1312   expansion of the average error over the quantiles and ensemble in Subsection 3.1. The main difference
1313   between this decomposition and that of Clements et al. (2020) is a term that encodes the distance
1314
       from the target:
                                              2                      2
1315                                         δΘ = (Θ − Eψ,i [θi (ψ)]) .                                 (46)
1316   This term could guard against two possible shortcomings of the decomposition in Clements et al.
1317   (2020):
1318
1319
             1. Consider the pathological case in which each ensemble is initialised identically, then each
                quantile will have zero variance and the epistemic uncertainty measure from Clements
1320
                et al. (2020) will be zero. Even if there is independence at initialisation, there may be
1321
                characteristic learning trajectories or other systematic biases that push the ensemble together
1322            and lead to an underestimate in epistemic uncertainty. Here, the term above—if treated as
1323            part of the epistemic uncertainty—can continue to drive learning in ways we want.
1324
             2. However, it could be that the ensemble behaves nicely and the metric over the ensemble
1325
                from Clements et al. (2020) is principally a good one, *but* that there is significant model
1326            bias. This could also be captured by the term above but would need to be subtracted from
1327            the total error in order to get a fully reducible measure for epistemic uncertainty (as per the
1328            argument discussed above).
1329
1330   Which of the two problems is more pronounced is difficult to know a priori, and could be an avenue
1331   for future work. Empirically, the performance of the UPER agent in Section 5 suggests that the
1332   former is the greater effect—at least on the atari benchmark with the model architecture and learning
1333
       setting used.
