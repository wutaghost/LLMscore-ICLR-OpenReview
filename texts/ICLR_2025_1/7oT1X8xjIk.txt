009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Noise is pervasive in real-world data, posing significant challenges to reliably un-
012            covering latent generative processes. While evolution may have enabled the brain
013            to solve such problems over millions of years, machine learning faces this task in
014            just a few years. Most prior identifiability theories, even under restrictive assump-
015            tions like linear generating functions, are limited to handling only additive noise
016            and fail to address nonparametric noise. In contrast, we study the problem of prov-
017            ably learning nonlinear representations in the presence of nonparametric noise.
018
               Specifically, we show that, under certain structural conditions between latent and
               observed variables, latent factors can be identified up to element-wise transforma-
019
               tions, even when both the generative processes and noise are nonlinear and lack
020
               specific parametric forms. We further present extensions of the general frame-
021            work, demonstrating trade-offs between different assumptions and the identifia-
022            bility of latent variables in the presence of both noise and distortions. Moreover,
023            we prove that the underlying directed acyclic graph can be recovered even with
024            nonlinear measurement errors, offering independent insights into structure learn-
025            ing. Our theoretical results are validated on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Uncovering the underlying generative processes from observational data is a cornerstone of scientific
031   discovery. While modern machine learning excels at capturing complex patterns in real-world data,
032   it often lacks identifiability guarantees that the learned representations correspond to the true latent
033
      factors generating the data (Locatello et al., 2019). For many applications, the ability to reliably
      identify these latent factors is critical for unbiased analysis of complex data, such as in economics
034
      (Hu, 2008), psychology (Bollen, 2002), and biomedical research (Imbens & Rubin, 2015).
035
036   Classical methods for recovering the underlying data-generating process, with theoretical guar-
037   antees, have traditionally focused on linear relationships between latent and observed variables
038   (Comon, 1994). Recent advances in nonlinear Independent Component Analysis (ICA) have ex-
039
      tended this theory to nonlinear contexts (Hyvärinen & Pajunen, 1999; Hyvärinen et al., 2024), incor-
      porating additional assumptions such as auxiliary variables (Hyvärinen & Morioka, 2016; Hyvärinen
040
      et al., 2019), time-series data (Hyvärinen & Morioka, 2017; Hälvä et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2021),
041
      structural conditions (Moran et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2022), or specific functional forms (Taleb &
042   Jutten, 1999; Buchholz et al., 2022). However, many of these approaches operate in deterministic
043   settings, without accounting for noise, which limits their applicability in real-world scenarios where
044   data is often affected by various forms of randomness.
045
      While some studies have integrated noise into latent variable models, existing frameworks remain re-
046
      strictive. Classical factor model literature, for instance, has typically employed additive noise under
047
      specific parametric assumptions related to the data-generating function, such as normality, linearity,
048   or its reducibility to linear models (Reiersøl, 1950; Lawley & Maxwell, 1962; Kenny & Judd, 1984;
049   Bekker & ten Berge, 1997; Ikeda & Toyama, 2000; Beckmann & Smith, 2004; Bonhomme & Robin,
050   2009). Recent works have expanded these frameworks to more general nonlinear settings with
051   non-deterministic transformations, especially with advancements in nonlinear ICA (Khemakhem
052   et al., 2020a; Sorrenson et al., 2020; Lachapelle et al., 2022; Hälvä et al., 2024). However, these
053   methods continue to exhibit limitations, as they are primarily restricted to handling additive noise,
      even when further constraints, such as temporal structures or weak supervision, are incorporated.

054
      Despite considerable advancements in establishing theoretical guarantees for latent variable models,
055   the challenge of provably learning nonlinear representations in the presence of complex noise
056   persists. This issue is particularly relevant in real-world applications, where data is frequently
057   contaminated by various forms of nonparametric noise. In medical imaging, for example, accurately
058   capturing detailed anatomical structures requires models that can disentangle meaningful signals
059   from pervasive noise (Suetens, 2017). Similarly, in autonomous driving, sensor data from lidar and
060   cameras must be interpreted with precision, despite environmental distortions and unpredictable
061   noise, to ensure reliable perception and decision-making (Yurtsever et al., 2020). Large-scale
062   foundation models, trained on extensive text corpora, also face the challenge of handling ambiguous
063
      or noisy data from diverse sources that often lack clear functional forms (Bommasani et al., 2021).
      Relying exclusively on additive noise models can introduce bias into representations, limiting the
064
      model’s ability to veridically discover the process underlying the data. This brings us to a crucial,
065
      yet unresolved, question:
066
067                 Can machines reliably reveal the hidden world amid the chaos of noise?
068
069   Towards addressing this open question, we establish a set of theoretical results for provably learn-
070   ing nonlinear representations in the presence of general noise. We demonstrate that, even when the
071   underlying generative process is nonlinear and the noise lacks a specific parametric form, it is still
072   possible to recover the underlying process with theoretical guarantees. Specifically, we prove that,
073
      under conditions on the hidden connective structure between latent and observed variables, the latent
      variables can be identified up to element-wise indeterminacies (Thm. 1). These guarantees hold in
074
      general settings without imposing restrictions on the distribution of the latent variables, the specific
075
      form of the generating function, or the parametric structure of the noise. To the best of our knowl-
076   edge, this is one of the first results to achieve identifiability for nonlinear representation learning in
077   an unsupervised setting with general noise.
078
079
      Moreover, to illustrate the implications of our theoretical framework, we demonstrate that several
      challenging problems can be addressed under the umbrella of nonparametric identifiability with
080
      noise. First, we show that even weaker assumptions can be sufficient when leveraging the paramet-
081
      ric form of the noise (Thm. 2), exploring the trade-off between different conditions. Next, we prove
082   that the latent variables can be identified despite nonlinear distortions (i.e., element-wise unknown
083   nonlinear transformations) combined with general noise (Cor. 1). Furthermore, we show that the
084   hidden (causal) directed acyclic graph (DAG) among variables can also be uncovered in the general
085   setting, even in the presence of nonlinear measurement error (Thm. 3, Prop. 2). Consequently, our
086   identifiability theory offers broad applicability to a variety of existing problems, and the theorems
087   may hold independent significance in fields such as generative modeling and causal discovery. We
088   validate the theoretical results through experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, but ad-
089   dressing practical challenges like finite sample errors remains a key open problem for future work
090
      to enable broader deployment of identifiability theory.

       Remark. Since we are working with a nonparametric form of noise, rather than additive noise,
       the noise can alter the latent distribution in a rather arbitrary manner. As a result, traditional
164
       distributional assumptions offer limited insight for this general setting. Therefore, in Thm. 1,
165
       we leverage a structural view, focusing on the connective relations between latent and observed
166    variables, which naturally generalize beyond specific functional forms or distributions.

      Proof Sketch. We leverage distributional variability across two domains of the latent variables z
169   to disentangle z and ϵ into independent subspaces. To separate general noise from latent variables,
170   we use the independence between z and ϵ alongside the variability within z. The structural sparsity
171   condition is then employed to identify individual components of z in the nonlinear setting. Specif-
172   ically, for each latent variable, the intersection of parental sets from a subset of observed variables
173   uniquely specifies it. Since we only achieve relations among supports due to the nonparametric na-
174   ture of the problem, an unresolved element-wise transformation remains. Consequently, we achieve
175   element-wise identifiability for the latent variables z (Defn. 1).
176   Insights and Implications. Theorem 1 shows that, under appropriate conditions, the latent vari-
177   ables of a nonlinear data-generating process with nonparametric noise can be identified up to an
178   element-wise invertible transformation and a permutation. This ensures that, no matter how com-
179   plex the noise or mixing process, the underlying generative factors can still be provably recovered
180   and disentangled. Such a result is particularly important for real-world applications, where noise
181   often plays a disruptive role in biasing observations.
182   Furthermore, the assumption that noise is merely additive or follows a specific parametric form,
183   as is common in many traditional frameworks, can also lead to misrepresentations of real-world
184   complexity. For instance, if we assume x = z + ϵ, where ϵ ∼ N (0, 0.1), we might overlook
185   scenarios where noise interacts with latent variables in more complex ways, such as multiplicative
186   noise x = z·(1+ϵ). Our theory ensures that even with nonparametric noise and nonlinear generating
187   process, the latent variables of interest can be provably recovered, without being confounded by
188   noise-induced misrepresentation.
189   On the Assumptions. Recovering latent variables from observational data in Eq. 1 is well-known
190   to be impossible without additional assumptions, even when deterministic transformations are in-
191   volved (Hyvärinen & Pajunen, 1999). The challenge becomes even more pronounced in the pres-
192   ence of nonparametric noise. Revealing the hidden generating process from the vast space of possi-
193   ble functions is inherently ill-posed. Therefore, to make the problem tractable and ensure sufficient
194   information for recovery, we introduce specific conditions that eliminate these ill-posed scenarios.
195   Assumption i (Nondegenaracy) is crucial for linking the dependency structure of the latent
196   variables to the Jacobian of the nonlinear mapping function, following the spirit of methodologies
197   in (Lachapelle et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022). This assumption rules out unlikely cases where data
198   samples originate from a highly restrictive subpopulation that spans only a degenerate subspace.
199   The first part of the assumption ensures that there are enough data points such that the Jacobian
200   matrix of the function spans its corresponding support—a condition typically satisfied as the
201
      sample size is not extremely small compared to the number of latent variables. The second part is
202
      generally mild because the derivative Dẑ fˆ = [Dz f T]i,: naturally resides within its support space
203   Rn(F̂ ) . Even in rare instances where the matrix does not align with the support due to specific
          ẑ i,:

204   combinations of values, the assumption remains valid asymptotically. This is because it only
205   requires the existence of one matrix from the entire set T of matrices that share the support of T.
206   As a result, given the asymptotic nature of the theory, the assumption is almost always satisfied.
207   Assumption ii (Domain Variability) (Kong et al., 2022) requires a specific type of variability in the
208   joint distribution of the latent variables z and the noise variables ϵ across different domains u1 and

211   latent variables. As verified in (Kong et al., 2022), this condition is typically satisfied in practice, as

      Furthermore, it might be worth noting that the variability required here is significantly less
217   restrictive compared to existing results in the literature. Specifically, many identifiability theorems,
218   even without accounting for general noise, typically require 2n + 1 domains to identify n latent
219   variables (Hyvärinen et al., 2024). Differently, our theory does not put a hard constraint on requiring
220   O(n) domains, as long as the specific assumption of Domain Variability holds. However, since
221   the conditions are different, the assumption of Domain Variability is not strictly weaker than the
222   previous assumptions.
223   Assumption iii (Structural Sparsity) originates from prior work on the identifiability of ICA (with-
224   out nonparametric noise) (Lachapelle et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022). In general, it necessitates the
225   existence of a set of observed variables such that the intersection of their parents singles out itself.
226   Since we do not have any assumptions on the functional type (e.g., post-nonlinear models) or the
227   distributions of the latent variables (e.g., exponential distributions), we can only rely on the hidden
228   structure between latent and observed variables. If certain latent variables are consistently entangled
229   across the generation of all observed variables, identifying them individually becomes impossible
230   without further constraints. Therefore, this assumption provides the necessary structural diversity
231
      for nonparametric identifiability. A specific example of when the assumption holds is as follows:
232   Example 1. Suppose for a latent variable z1 , there exists a set of observed variables, say {x1 , x2 },
233   s.t., x1 depends on {z1 , z2 } and x2 depends on {z1 , z3 }. Alternatively, there exists a set {x1 , x2 }
234   s.t. the intersection of their parents singles out z1 . Then the structural sparsity assumption satisfies
235   for z1 . Note that there could be an arbitrary number of observed variables. As long as there exists
236   a subset of them satisfying the condition, the assumption holds for the target latent variable.
237
238   Importantly, the structural sparsity condition only requires a subset of the observed vari-
239   ables—potentially as few as one or two—to satisfy the necessary conditions. This is particularly
240   helpful because our theory allows for a larger number of observed variables compared to latent
241   ones. This enables us to fulfill the required assumptions by incorporating additional observed vari-
242
      ables (e.g., adding more microphones in an audio system). In practice, since the true underlying
      generative process is usually unknown, many assumptions in the literature cannot be directly tested.
243
      However, by augmenting the number of observed variables, we may often meet the structural spar-
244
      sity condition without knowing the ground truth, which significantly increases the applicability.
245
246   It might be worth noting that the structural sparsity implies the independence among latent variables
247   z. Specifically, if two latent variables are dependent, it becomes impossible to disentangle one of
248
      them by the intersection of a set of observed variables that are influenced by these latent variables.
      Moreover, for real-world scenarios, it is extremely challenging to make sure that all conditions on the
249
      latent data generating process are perfectly satisfied and the distributions are perfectly matched after
250
      estimation. Bridging the gap requires a thorough study of the finite sample error and the robustness
251   of the identification, which remains an open challenge in the literature.

255   In this section, we present several theoretical developments grounded in the framework of nonlinear
256   representation learning with general noise. First, we investigate the connections between various
257   assumptions, demonstrating that variability can be bypassed by exploiting the parametric form of
258   the noise (Sec. 4.1). Next, we show that even with nonlinear distortion in addition to general noise,
259
      the latent variables can still be identified up to element-wise indeterminacies (Sec. 4.2). Finally, we
      delve into the hidden structure of the data, proving that the causal DAG of a general nonlinear model
260
      remains identifiable, even when nonlinear measurement error is present (Sec. 4.3).

268   is generated under stable conditions with no variation between domains. For example, in short-
269
      term industrial monitoring systems or continuous physiological monitoring, external conditions may

429   Insights and Implications. Causal discovery aims to find the causal structure underlying the data
430   (Spirtes et al., 2000) based on pure observation. Traditional results on the identifiability of causal
431   discovery usually make parametric assumptions such as post-nonlinear or additive noise models to
      identify the underlying causal DAG. Additionally, most previous methods assume that the observed


                                                                8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      values directly correspond to the variables of interest. While some works have considered structure
433   identification in the presence of measurement error (Zhang et al., 2018; Dai et al., 2022), these
434   approaches impose parametric assumptions on both the SCM (linear non-Gaussian models) and
435   the measurement error (linear distortions). These conditions are important given the challenges of
436   learning causal structure without any interventional data, but still somehow limit the applicability in
437   complicated real-world scenarios. As a result, the question of under what conditions we can identify
438   the causal graph for general nonlinear models with nonparametric measurement error remains open.
439   Fortunately, the connection between the mixing matrix and the causal DAG brings us the oppor-
440   tunity to study the identifiability of this challenging problem. This is because all relations among
441   causal variables (Eq. (7)) can be considered as how exogenous affect variables of interests if we
442   take a view of the whole system (Eq. (5)). This connection has been firstly used in the seminal
443   work of Shimizu et al. (2006) to identify linear non-Gaussian models based on linear ICA. The key
444   insight is that, given the acyclicity constraints, the recovered mixing matrix—despite permutation
445   indeterminacy—can be uniquely transferred to an adjacency matrix of a causal DAG. More recently,
446   Reizinger et al. (2022) extend it to the nonlinear case by bridging the Jacobian of the mixing func-
447
      tion f1 in Eq. (7) and the causal structure (A). Thus, our results on the general factor model with
      nonlinear distortion and additive noise (Thm. 3) inherently lead to the identifiability of the underly-
448
      ing causal structure even in the presence of nonlinear measurement error (Prop. 2). This is exciting
449
      since causal discovery with general functional relations has long been an open problem, and the
450   inclusion of nonlinear distortions adds further complexity. Naturally, this generalization requires
451   an additional nondegeneracy condition on the latent factor model, much like how Reizinger et al.
452   (2022) leverage identifiability conditions for nonlinear ICA in the context of nonlinear causal dis-
453   covery. We believe that the revealed insight may suggest a different direction toward more general
454   solutions for understanding the (causal) structure underlying the data.

522   6     C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we establish theoretical guarantees for nonlinear representation learning in the presence
525   of general noise. Specifically, we prove that latent generating factors can be identified up to trivial in-
526
      determinacies, without imposing parametric constraints on either the generating process or the noise.
      Within this general framework, we explore the relationships between various conditions, highlight-
527
      ing the inherent trade-offs. Moreover, since real-world observations may involve not only noise but
528
      also nonlinear distortions, we extend the proposed nonparametric identifiability to account for both.
529   Finally, we demonstrate that the underlying causal structure is also identifiable even with nonlinear
530   measurement errors. Theoretical results are validated in both synthetic and real-world settings.
531
532
      While we demonstrate nonparametric identifiability for learning with noise, several related questions
      remain open. One intriguing direction involves scenarios where the generating process includes
533
      more latent variables than observed ones, making the function non-injective. In such cases, some
534
      information is inevitably lost, raising the critical question of which part of the hidden world can still
535   be recovered. Additionally, our theory focuses on asymptotic guarantees, leaving the finite-sample
536   regime unexplored. Investigating sample complexity in this context, though distinct from our current
537   focus, could be interesting as well. Many questions remain, but for now, we can confidently answer
538   the question posed in the introduction:
539
                       Yes, machines can reliably reveal the hidden world amid the chaos of noise.

1608   C    A DDITIONAL D ISCUSSIONS
1609
1610   In this section, we discuss the challenge of modeling general noise, emphasizing the distinctions
1611   between noise and content variables as explored in previous works.
1612
       Content variables are typically semantically meaningful and are often explicitly contrasted with style
1613
       variables. This enables existing techniques to disentangle content from style through structured
1614   variability. For example, contrastive learning frameworks (Von Kügelgen et al., 2021) use paired
1615   observations differing only in style (e.g., images with the same object but different backgrounds) to
1616   disentangle content. In multi-domain settings (Kong et al., 2022), content remains invariant across
1617   O(n) distinct domains characterized by different styles. Similarly, in intervention-based settings
1618   (Lachapelle et al., 2024), agents or environments serve as auxiliary variables that induce changes in
1619   the conditional distributions of latent variables. These structured variations provide the foundation
       for effective disentanglement.


                                                             30
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1620
       In contrast, noise variables often lack semantic meaning and cannot be explicitly manipulated across
1621   multiple domains or paired in observations. This makes it infeasible to assume the existence of O(n)
1622   conditional distributions or define contrastive objectives. As a result, existing frameworks designed
1623   for content-style disentanglement cannot be directly applied to general noise modeling.
1624
       To address this, we propose to only leverage the existence of variability in the latent distribution,
1625
       requiring only two distinct distributions as a minimal degree of change. This relaxation reduces
1626
       the need for O(n) distinct distributions, which are common in existing frameworks, and broadens
1627   applicability to scenarios where the distribution is not completely invariant. This shift, from explic-
1628   itly controlling different types of variables to achieve a required degree of change or transition, to
1629   accommodating general variability in scenarios where the distribution is not completely invariant,
1630   represents a significant technical contribution that is essential to address the unique contribution of
1631   modeling general noise.
