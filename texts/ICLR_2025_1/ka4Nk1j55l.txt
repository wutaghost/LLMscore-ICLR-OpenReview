009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing retrieval-based frameworks to enhance large language models (LLMs)
012            requires accessibility to a rich non-parametric knowledge source which contains
013            factual information that directly solves the query. Reasoning based approaches
014            heavily rely on the parametric knowledge of the model to provide domain-specific
015            explicit reasoning chain. However, inclusive knowledge sources are expensive or
016
               infeasible to build for scientific or corner domains, thus not applicable in either
               training or inference time. To tackle the challenges, we introduce Graph Inspired
017
               Veracity Extrapolation (GIVE), a novel reasoning framework that integrates the
018
               parametric and non-parametric memories to enhance both knowledge retrieval and
019            faithful reasoning processes using very limited external clues. By leveraging the
020            structured knowledge to inspire LLM to model the interconnections among relevant
021            concepts, our method facilitates a more logical and step-wise reasoning approach
022            akin to experts’ problem-solving, rather than gold answer retrieval. Specifically,
023            the framework prompts LLMs to decompose the query into crucial concepts and
024            attributes, construct entity groups with relevant entities, and build an augmented
025            reasoning chain by probing potential relationships among node pairs across these
026            entity groups. Our method incorporates both factual and extrapolated linkages to en-
027
               able comprehensive understanding and response generation. Extensive experiments
               on domain-specific and open-domain benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of
028
               our proposed method, thereby underscoring the efficacy of integrating structured
029
               information and internal reasoning ability of LLMs for tackling difficult tasks with
030            limited external resources.

033   1    INTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al., 2022; et al., 2024a;b; 2022; 2020; Raffel et al., 2020)
036   have been shown to be able to generate fluent language, answer questions and induce knowledge
037   from the given text in recent benchmarks. Though it shows a great performance for general question
038   answering, we do not see a similar level of success on similar tasks under the scientific domains or
039
      settings that require specialized knowledge tailored to a certain context (Cai et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
      2024; Dorfner et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024). Two technical
040
      disadvantages of LLMs might explain the unsatisfactory performance. On the one hand, LLMs are
041
      not aware of specialized domain knowledge (Ge et al., 2023; Bang et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024), such
042   as protein-gene relations, drug-disease associations, and actor profiles of newly introduced movies.
043   The specialized knowledge is not obtained through training and needs to be constantly updated. On
044   the other hand, LLMs are not equipped to lay out a multi-step logic chain with domain expertise to
045   identify and solve sub-questions following a correct thinking process (Wei et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
046   2023; Jiang et al., 2024). For example, to identify whether a drug is capable of treating a disease, the
047   model needs to figure out the root cause and the affecting organ of the disease, chemicals that interact
048   with the virus, and the drug formulas that contain the correct chemicals. These thinking processes are
049   unique to questions and are hardly presented in instruction tuning or human preference alignment
050
      training data.
051   Existing works use LLM to guide the factual knowledge search on well-populated external knowledge
052   structures like knowledge graphs (KG) (Sun et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). However, expert-curated
053   domain-specific knowledge graphs such as UMLS are expensive to obtain and update. Enhancing
      LLM reasoning with a sparse external KG is a more realistic and practical setting. Though the

087   In this work, we aim to address the limitations of reasoning exclusively on internal knowledge
088   aor external knowledge frameworks by proposing GIVE, a graph-inspired veracity extrapolation
089   framework. GIVE simulates the thinking processes of the KG constructors and utilizes the structure
090   of KG as inspiration. It populates the sparse KG with silver edges by receiving hints from factual
091   connections, concretizing the internal knowledge of LLMs, constructing counterfactual reasoning to
092   combat hallucinations, and additionally retrieving existing related evidence on KG if needed. GIVE
093
      first obtains a focused set of entities that are mostly related to the question by prompting LLMs.
      Using the potential relations between the relevant KG concepts, we construct a reasoning framework
094
      including all possible concepts and their potential connections that could facilitate question answering.
095
      We introduce additional intermediate node groups by picking the multi-step reasoning plans that
096   are most helpful for the ultimate questions. GIVE includes factual connections backed by KG,
097   internal knowledge obtained through pre-training, and novel relations that bridge similar concepts
098   from the veracity extrapolation process. To complete the reasoning framework, we also incorporate
099   counterfactual connections among nodes to prevent hallucination. Ultimately, we develop a method
100   that (1) retrieves external knowledge for more informed question answering; (2) induces a structured
101   reasoning processes by extrapolating KG triplets to related queried concepts, which we refer to as
102   "veracity extrapolation".
103   We experiment with our proposed method on biomedical, commonsense, and truthfulness question
104   answering. GIVE uniformly achieves the best performance on QA tasks of different domains and
105   types, utilizing KGs of different sizes and sparcities, among all internal knowledge/external knowledge
106   reasoning baselines, indicating the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed framework. GIVE
107   pioneers in boosting LLM’s reasoning ability using very limited external clues to excite its own
      problem solving ability.

131   2   P RELIMINARIES
132
133   Reasoning on Structured Knowledge Base. Structured knowledge bases like knowledge graphs
134   (KG) provides better knowledge traceability and correct-ability due to the structured nature of
135   the knowledge source, thus provides the RAG-framework with better flexibility during knowledge
136   retrieval. Previous studies encode the KGs (Saxena et al., 2020; Zan et al., 2022) and queries, answer
137   is generated using similarities between node embedding and query embedding. To contrast, we
138   propose a one-shot solution for information retrieval from sparse KG and structured reasoning chain
139   development without any training cost. Recently, ToG (Sun et al., 2024) proposes to iteratively
140   query LLM to search and prune the optimal knowledge paths to include; GoG (Xu et al., 2024)
141
      decomposes the query into a set of sub-questions and prompts LLM to iteratively solve each of
      them. GNN-RAG (Mavromatis & Karypis, 2024) formulates the answer-extraction process as a node
142
      classification problem over the knowledge graph. These methods, although proved to be effective in
143
      answering queries in specific KG-QA pairs, are built on the assumption that the high-quality data
144   base that contains the gold knowledge are always accessible and easy to retrieve. In the context of
145   scientific domains, however, building specific knowledge bases is challenging, because it requires
146   advances in both domain-specific natural language processing(NLP) and filed-wide vocabulary
147   standardization (Badal et al., 2019; Verhagen et al., 2012). General knowledge bases like Wikipedia
148   or Freebase contains tens of millions of irrelevant entities and triplets, thus are time and resource
149   consuming for LLMs to search from.
150   Problem Definition. In this paper, we study reasoning-rich domain-specific question answering
151   using very sparse knowledge graphs. A knowledge graph (KG) is defined as G = {EG , RG , EG },
152   EG = {(u, r, v), u, v ∈ EG , r ∈ RG }, where EG is the set of entities and RG is the set of relations.
153   An input query x is a statement about the entities Ex and relations Rx . For example, the query “Is
154   melatonian effective for insomnia?” contains entity set Ex = {melatonian, insomnia} and relation set
155   Rx = {effective for}.
156   To solve an instance of (G, x), the key step is to retrieve highly-relevant knowledge set from the KG.
157   Suppose the gold knowledge set TG (x) for x in G is the collection of knowledge triplets in G that
158   explicitly contains the ground-truth output for x. Previous works focus on the case when the given
159   knowledge graph G contains some gold knowledge about x, i.e. G ∈ Gx∗ . We provide a solution to the
160   general form of KGQA instances (G,x) where G ∉ Gx∗ , by first combining the parametric memory of
161   LLM and the sparse KG G, query x to expand the entity and relation set to consider, before using the
      structure of G to inspire LLM to extrapolate the veracity of the generalised edges among all related

186   3     GIVE: G RAPH -I NSPIRED V ERACITY E XTRAPOLATION
187
188   Our proposed method prompts faithfully inductive reasoning by 1) Using LLM to decompose the
189   query into important concepts and attributes; 2) constructing entity groups by combining the key entity
190   in the query and its relevant concepts in the knowledge graph; 3) inducing inner-group connections
191   between the queried entity and related concepts using parametric knowledge of LLM; 4) build
192
      inter-group connections by probing and pruning all pairwise possible connections, and considering
      intermediate concept groups to facilitate multi-hop reasoning for complicated questions. We present
193
      the overall algorithm for GIVE in Appendix 1 and the prompt and example output in Appendix G.
194

267   Considering only nodes and connections directly related to the ones mentioned in the question would

      a natural reasoning chain is to build the (drug, compound, disease) connections, to form the claim
271   “because certain compound entity is contained in the drug, and the diseases that can be treated by the
272   entity, it could be inferred that the drug can treat entity”.

      To provide sufficient knowledge and thinking hints for such complicated tasks that require linking
      target entities through multi-step reasoning, GIVE explores new node groups as intermediate stopovers
275
      of the thinking process. Firstly, all length-2 paths between two node groups are discovered. Secondly,
276
      LLM is prompted to automatically select the most helpful multi-hop thinking process that benefits the
277   ultimate question-answering task, where each multi-hop thinking process comes from verbalization
278   of the length-2 path we discovered. Using the intermediate node of the optimal length-2 path, GIVE
279   constructs an intermediate entity group by leveraging the same process as illustrated in Section
280   3.2. Note that the intermediate node group is created to build the multi-hop connections between
281   two queried entity groups. The intermediate node groups contain a set of similar "intermediate’
282   entities in the knowledge graph, whereas the queried entity group contains an entity in the query and
283   semantically similar concepts to the queried entity that are contained in the knowledge graph.

      3.4.3    KG- STRUCTURE GUIDED REASONING
285
286   In the previous sections, we pre-process the external structured knowledge source by constructing (1)
287   groups of important concepts in Section 3.2, and any possible intermediate groups in Section 3.4.2.
288   (2) possible connections between any two groups in Section 3.4.1. GIVE utilizes these non-parametric
289   evidence Nx and Rx to inspire LLM conduct reasoning using its parametric knowledge, and formally
290   build the inter-group knowledge set.
291   Assigning relations with external evidence. If there exists an edge on the external knowledge
292   graph between a pair of nodes, we directly inherit the ground-truth relation from the original KG
293   G. We consider all knowledge described on the external KG as ground-truth known facts. When we
294   verbalize such edge in the prompt, we use affirmative tense to indicate such a fact is true with very
295   high confidence.
296
      Veracity Extrapolation with internal knowledge. The potential relations between node groups
297   induced in Section 3.4.1 are crucial to inform us about the possible connections between nodes. The
298   pre-training stage of LLM equips the model with rich factual knowledge from the unstructured corpus.
299   It is important to concertize the relevant internal knowledge to affirm the model’s decision or to reject
300   wrong answers with explicit context. Thus, we prompt the LLM to assign a label to each potential
301   relation among two node groups: “yes”, “no”, or “maybe”. If the LLM yields “yes” for a certain
302   relation, it indicates the model is confident that such a claim is factual.
303   It is important for the QA model to know not only what claims are highly likely to be factual but also
304   the claims that are not going to hold or may not hold. This kind of counterfactual relation information
305   prevents the model from hallucination. If the node pair does not contain a relation in the potential
306   relation set indicated by a “no” answer returned by the LLM, we assign a reversed relation. If the
307   model is not sure about a certain relation using its internal knowledge by answering "maybe", these
308   connections are discarded as the LLM is not sure about its validity thus bears a higher chance of
309   causing hallucination.
310   Discovering open relations for novel connections. To prevent the potential scope limitation of the
311   relations presented in the knowledge graph, we additionally prompt the LLM to freely create a short
312   phrase to describe the relation of a given node pair. Even two nodes can be connected through a novel
313   relation that not presented in either question or the knowledge graph, the open relation discovery
314   design keeps the flexibility of our proposed framework.

334   4     E XPERIMENTS
335
336   The experiments in this section are designed to answer the following research questions: (1) Is GIVE
337   able to provide structured high-quality knowledge using very sparse external resources thus result in
338   higher QA accuracy? We answer this in Section 4.2 by conducting experiments on various biomedical
339   QA benchmarks using a small UMLS knowledge graph (Li et al., 2023). (2) Is GIVE robust to
340   different sparcities of KG to retrieve useful information? To this end, we conduct experiments by
341   randomly sampling different portions of triplets in ConceptNet (Speer et al., 2018), and use the
342   resulted subgraphs to test on CommonsenseQA (Talmor et al., 2019) in Section 4.3 and truthfulQA
343
      (Lin et al., 2022) in Appendix D. (3) Is the performance of GIVE sensitive to the number of
      additional concepts in each group (which is the only hyper-parameter for our method)? We perform
344
      ablation studies in Section 4.4 to answer this. (4) On what kind of questions GIVE achieve the best
345
      performance? We give a detailed analysis in Appendix F.1 and conclude that GIVE improves the
346   performance of LLM by achieving very high accuracy on the questions where the ratio of expert KG
347   knowledge in the overall retrieved knowledge set is relatively high. (5)What are other factors that
348   contribute to the performance of GIVE? We conducted additional ablation studies on a subset of each
349   dataset in Appendix E.1, E.2, E.3 and E.4 on removing inner/inter group connections, the number of
350   seeded examples, prompting strategies and encoder model size for entity group construction. (6) What
351   is the detailed cost comparison between GIVE and the competing methods? We include a detailed
352   discussion of GIVE’s efficiency in terms of both running time and context length in Appendix F.2, in
353   which we first prove that all factors that may influence GIVE’s efficiency can be upper-bounded by a
354
      small number, and that GIVE is generally applicable to KGs of different sizes and sparcities in terms
      of accuracy and efficiency.

358   4.1.1    R EASONING - RICH QA DATASETS
359
360   Since we aim to enable LLM to conduct faithful reasoning utilizing very limited external knowledge,
361   the general KB-QA pairs with ground truth knowledge path does not align with the purpose of our
362   experiments because they are designed to test effectiveness of information retrieval. Our experiments
363   follow the principle that the involved KG should be related to the domain of the question, but not
364
      provide direct solution to the query, which creates an environment akin to human/expert deductive
      reasoning process, by following related high-level hints to solve the reasoning-intensive query. We
365
      quantify all KG and datasets included in our experiments in Appendix Section B.
366
367   We focus on questions that are hard to answer without additional reasoning by ignoring any
368   "gold" knowledge or context. For PubmedQA (Jin et al., 2019), we challenge the competing
369   methods by providing LLM with only the question statement and the retrieved facts, not any ground-
370
      truth gold context in which the answer is self-presented. Similarly, for BioASQ (Krithara et al., 2023),
      we extract all questions in Task 2B, 3B and 4B with both "ideal answer" (long answer) and "exact
371
      answer" (short answer). We ignore the long answer to test the accuracy on the short answer returned
372
      by each method. In the case of Processbank (Berant et al., 2014), we do not provide the ground-truth
373   annotations by only giving the question statement and choices.

      Get inspired, do not recite. The performance jump when increasing the number of KG entities
506   from 0 to 1 proves the effectiveness of the "Graph Inspiration" process we proposed in Section 3.4.3,
507   by introducing additional related concepts from external source and "inspire" the LLM to conduct
508   divergent reasoning using these external clues. This points out that the ability of divergent thinking
509   of LLMs may have long been ignored, as we have been focusing on retrieving the gold knowledge
510   for the model to "recite". Instead, further studies should be conducted on how to utilize the external
511   knowledge as a high-level clue to "inspire" LLMs conduct reasoning, rather than a "long-answer"
512   style gold context. This is especially the case when we deploy LLMs in scientific domains where
513   both training on and retrieving from inclusive knowledge are infeasible.
514   Limitation statement: It remains a heuristic on how to eliminate hallucination caused by in-accurate
515   knowledge GIVE introduced, as there is no performance guarantee on the LLM’s ability to prune
516   out the correct potential knowledge from the wrong. In fact, it is related to the size of the LLM and
517   how extensively it has been trained on the specific domain knowledge. Regarding the complexity of
518   GIVE, suppose we have m entity groups and each group has n concepts, between two entity groups
519   there r candidate relations. The inner-group connections (Section 3.3) takes O(mn) LLM calls. For
520
      inter-group connections (Section 3.4.3), the number of LLM calls needed equal to the number of
      generalized potential connections, which is O(rm2 n2 ). However, as shown in Section 4.4, GIVE
521
      achieves best performance when n = 1 or 2. In Appendix F.2 we further prove that (1) average value
522
      for m is around 3 or 4 for all datasets; (2) average value for r is upper-bounded by 4 for all datasets;
523   (3) both running time and context length of GIVE remain reasonable when we increase the size or
524   sparsity of the KG, and are far from the limits of the widely-used LLMs. (4) the large amount of
525   knowledge retrieved by GIVE is of high-quality and is important to improve the model’s performance.
526   (5) complexity of GIVE can be further reduced by: divide-and-conquer prune the knowledge in
527   batches; include summarization agents to reduce the length of the knowledge.

      5    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We propose Graph Inspired Veracity Extrapolation (GIVE), a knowledge extrapolation framework
532
      for structured reasoning of LLM on sparse knowledge graphs. GIVE neither focuses on explicit
533
      information retrieval, nor relies on improving the internal reasoning ability of LLMs by appending
534   triggering statements to the query. It utilizes the high-level thinking processes mined in sparse
535   knowledge graphs to combine both approaches. It retrieves the most relevant information in the
536   knowledge base and, at the same time, inspires LLM to exploit its internal knowledge by conducting
537   structured reasoning and knowledge extrapolation. GIVE provides substantial amount of performance
538   increase in different QA tasks using KGs of various sizes and sparcities, and mitigates the hallucination
539   issue of retrieval-based methods on non-inclusive knowledge source. It sheds light on the potential of
      LLM to conduct divergent reasoning using very limited external clues.

           B    D IFFERENCE BETWEEN GIVE AND R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTED G ENERATION
736             (RAG)
737
738        Retrieval-Augmented Generation. An LLM pθ (y∣x) generates the probability distribution of output
739        y given an input x. RAG-based systems (Lewis et al., 2021) enhance the capability of language
740        models in knowledge-intensive tasks by leveraging a retriever-generator framework. The retriever
741
           model is denoted as pη (z∣x, D), where x is an input query, D is a comprehensive knowledge base,
           and it generates knowledge distribution over the given input and knowledge base. The generator
742
           pθ (y∣z, x) then autoregressively generates the output sequence based on the retrieved knowledge z
743
           and the input context x. The likelihood of generating an output sequence y = y1∶N can be estimated
744        as:
745                                         p(y∣x, D) ∶= pη (z∣x, D)pθ (y∣x, z)                          (11)
746
747        Recent studies (Edge et al., 2024) proposes to improve RAG by empirically proving that it is
748        beneficial to model the internal connections between information units, rather than feed the model with
749        independent knowledge. These methods, and the algorithms to tackle KBQA, focus on information
750        retrieval and they aim to provide the most accurate external knowledge contained in the documents.
751        GIVE provides another axis for solving problems using external knowledge, is to combine the limited
752        resource and the parametric knowledge to generate high quality response. We are convinced that this
753
           is important as we move forward the test time scaling era of deploying artificial intelligence.
