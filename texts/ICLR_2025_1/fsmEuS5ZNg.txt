009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing Source-free Video Domain Adaptation (SFVDA) aims to learn a target
012            video model for an unlabeled target domain by transferring knowledge from a
013            labeled source domain using a single pre-trained source video model. In this
014            paper, we explore a new SFVDA setting where multiple source domains ex-
015            ist, each offering a library of source models with different architectures. This
016            setting offers both opportunities and challenges: while the presence of multi-
017            ple source models enriches the pool of transferable knowledge, it also increases
018            the risk of negative transfer due to inappropriate source knowledge. To tackle
019
               these challenges, we introduce the Multiple-Source-Video-Model Aggregation
               Framework (MSVMA), comprising two key modules. The first module, termed
020
               Multi-level Instance Transferability Calibration (MITC), enhances existing
021
               uncertainty-based transferability estimation metrics by incorporating scale infor-
022            mation from both group and dataset levels. This integration facilitates accurate
023            transferability estimation at the instance level across diverse models. The sec-
024            ond module, termed Instance-level Multi Video Model Aggregation (IMVMA),
025            leverages the calculated instance-level transferability to guide a path generation
026            network. This network produces instance-specific weights for unsupervised ag-
027            gregation of source models. Empirical results from three video domain adapta-
028            tion datasets demonstrate the state-of-the-art performance of our MSVMA frame-
029            work.

037   availability of large-scale labeled datasets(4; 5). However, the construction of large-scale labeled

057   Unsupervised Video Domain Adaptation (UVDA) has been proposed recently (6), which aims to
058
      transfer knowledge from a labeled video dataset (source domain) to an unlabeled target domain.
      Existing approaches address this task by minimizing the domain discrepancy between the source
059
      and target domain based on adversarial training (7; 8; 9; 10) and self-supervised learning methods
060
      (11; 12; 13).
061
062   While traditional UVDA methods effectively mitigate domain shift between different video sources,
063   they necessitate access to source data during the adaptation step, which poses significant risks of pri-
064
      vacy breaches and incurs substantial data transmission costs. To address these challenges, Source-
      Free Video Domain Adaptation (SFVDA) has emerged as a promising alternative (14). SFVDA
065
      relies solely on a single pre-trained model from a labeled source domain to learn an action recogni-
066
      tion model for the unlabeled target domain, without accessing the original source videos. Recently,
067   a state-of-the-art SFVDA method based on temporal and spatial consistency has been proposed (15).
068   This method adapts the source model to learn the capabilities of motion dynamics and action coher-
069   ence in videos by applying temporal and spatial augmentations to simulate domain transitions.
070
      A primary limitation of current Source-Free Video Domain Adaptation (SFVDA) methods is their
071
      reliance on a single source domain model. However, numerous source models from different sources
072
      with diverse architectures are generally available in real-world scenarios, which offer a wide vari-
073   ety of knowledge. This has motivated us to explore more comprehensive adaptation strategies by
074   integrating information from multiple source domain models, a method we termed Multi-model
075   Source-Free Video Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (MSFVDA). By providing a zoo of well-
076   trained source models with various architectures from the source domain, target users can access
077   and leverage multiple models for domain adaptation, thereby enhancing the knowledge base of the
078   source domain. However, a critical challenge in developing MSFVDA lies in the effective aggrega-
079   tion of the multiple source domain models.
080   To best of our knowledge, this problem has not yet been explored in the video domain. Beyond the
081   video task, a closely related work is SUTE (16), which address multi-model adaptation in image
082   task. The key of this method is to estimate the transferability of each model and select models
083   for aggregation based on the estimated transferability. This method operates at the dataset-level,
084   i.e., estimating the transferability of a model on the entire dataset. However, due to the inherent
085   spatial and temporal complexities in videos, significant variability exists among video instances.
086   Consequently, estimating model transferability solely at the dataset-level fails to account for the
087
      variability of models toward individual instances. Recently, there is a study that focuses on the
      multi-model aggregation at instance-level (17). However, this method relies on labeled data for path
088
      weight learning, which is unsuitable in MSFVDA.
089
090   In this paper, we introduce a Multiple-Source-Video-Model Aggregation (MSVMA) framework to
091   address the MSFVDA. To resolve the challenge of instance-level transferability estimation, we pro-
092   pose a novel Multi-level Instance Transferability Calibration (MITC) algorithm. MITC seeks to
093
      measure the instance-level transferability based on the uncertainty methods (18). Therefore, we
      introduce a novel calibrate function, which further calibrates the uncertainty-based instance-level
094
      transferability by incorporating scale information from both group-level and dataset-level. For more
095
      effective instance-level aggregation, we introduce an Instance-level Video Multi-Model Aggregation
096   (IMVMA), which accounts for the significant differences among video instances. IMVMA learns
097   to assign instance-level weights for each video instance in the path generation network based on the
098   instance-level transferability estimated by MITC, and then selectively activate source domain mod-
099   els to achieve instance-level model aggregation. We demonstrated the effectiveness of our method
100   on three public datasets and achieved state-of-the-art results.

116
117   Source Free Unsupervised Video Domain Adaptation. Image-based Source-Free Unsupervised
118   Domain Adaptation (SFDA) has garnered significant attention recently (19; 20; 21). The primary
119   goal of SFDA is to adapt models trained on a source domain directly to an unlabeled target domain
120   without requiring access to source domain data. In contrast, Source-Free Video Domain Adaptation
121
      (SFVDA) has only recently begun to be explored. The unique temporal properties inherent to video
      data present a significant challenge for SFVDA. Xu et al. (22) proposed a SFVDA method that
122
      leverages the temporal properties of video data based on temporal consistency. Similarly, Li et al.
123
      (15) also took advantage of temporal consistency, by exploring the model’s self-adaptive capabilities
124   in both temporal and spatial information. However, previous studies have primarily focused on
125   the adaptation issues of single models, heavily restricting the overall performance. Recently, Pei
126   et al. (16) introduce a transferability measure to assist in model selection, assigning dataset-level
127   weights during aggregation, and achieving excellent performance in various image recognition tasks.
128   Building on this, our work proposes a new instance-level video multi-model aggregation framework
129   that can simultaneously learn accurate path weights in an unsupervised manner and assist in model
130   selection. This approach further enhances the effectiveness of multi-model aggregation.
131   Transferability Measurements. In scenarios where multiple pre-trained source domain models are
132   available, it is particularly crucial to evaluate the transferability of each source domain model to the
133   target domain. A traditional way predicts the performance of the source model after fine-tuning in
134   a supervised manner on the target domain (23; 24; 25; 26). However, the requirement for target
135   labels in these methods above hinders their application in broader contexts. Recently, some meth-
136   ods are proposed for estimating the transferability for source-free unsupervised tasks (27; 28). These
137   method can be roughly divided into two classes: distribution-induced methods and uncertainty-based
138
      methods. Distribution-induced methods (16; 29) measure transferability by developing and evaluat-
      ing some distribution-related assumption. For example, SUTE (16) proposed hypotheses on dataset
139
      distribution, including Individual Certainty, Semantic Consistency, and Global Dispersion. MDE
140
      (29) proposes to levegate the energy hypothesis and converts the information of all samples into a
141   statistical probability distribution. However, estimating transferability solely from the perspective
142   of dataset distribution overlooks the model’s variability in individual instances. Uncertainty-based
143   methods leverage uncertainty methods for transferability estimation, including entropy, temporal
144   and spatial disturbances, based on the assumption that high transferable model exhibits low uncer-
145   tainty (30; 18). However, uncertainty-based methods fail to accurately estimate transferability across
146   different models (19). To address this, we propose the MITC method to perform instance-level and
147   cross-model transferability estimation.

188   3.3   A NALYSIS OF E XISTING T RANSFERABILITY M EASUREMENTS
189
190   While MSFVDA provides a more extensive knowledge base from the source domains, incorporating
191   multiple distinct source models may increase the risk of including those that underperform in the
192   target domain, thereby causing a substantial decline in overall performance. The experimental re-
193   sults demonstrate that careful model selection, rather than indiscriminately using all available source
194
      models, significantly improves adaptation performance in the target domain. These findings high-
      light the critical need for effective transferability estimation metrics within the MSFVDA framework
195
      to accurately assess and select the most suitable source domain models.
196
197   Existing transferability estimation methods can be roughly categorized into two groups: distribution-
198   induced methods (16; 29) that develop and evaluate some distribution-related assumption and
199   uncertainty-based methods which are based on the assumption that high transferable model exhibits
200
      low uncertainty (30; 18).
201   While these methods demonstrate effectiveness in image model transferability measurement tasks,
202   we observed the experiment result in table 1 that both methods are not suitable in our MSFVDA,
203   which are discussed in the following.
204   Limitation of distribution-induced methods: These methods utilize dataset-level information for
205   cross-model transferability estimation but overlook the fact that source domain models may exhibit
206   different preferences for individual instances. Unlike images, videos consist of a series of consecu-
207   tive frames that contain both spatial and temporal information. The temporal dimension introduces
208   additional complexity and variability. Moreover, videos typically have greater content complexity
209   compared to images, resulting in more significant differences between video instances than between
210   image instances. Ignoring instance-level transferability estimation can hinder further improvements
211   in adaptation performance.
212   Limitation of Uncertainty-based methods: These methods can estimate instance-level transferability
213   within a single model. However, when the repository includes models with diverse architectures,
214   differences between these models introduce bias at the dataset-level. Without addressing these cross-
215   model differences, directly estimating instance-level transferability across different source domain
      models may not provide accurate results.

218   The above discussion highlights two key findings:
219
220          • Existing distribution-induced methods accurately estimate transferability at the
221            dataset-level (coarse-grained) across different models, but they do not extend to fine-
222            grained, instance-level transferability assessments.
223          • Current uncertainty-based methods provide reliable metrics for instance-level (fine-
224            grained) transferability estimation within the same model. However, they lose effi-
225            ciency when estimating transferability across different models.
226

228   ability estimation across different models. Specifically, the calibration function is formulated by:

      Comparative Studies. Table 1 shows the correlation between instance-level transferability esti-
359
      mation methods and instance prediction accuracy. We extend existing dataset-level transferability
360   metrics (distribution-based methods) to the instance level, but found that their performance are sub-
361   optimal. For instance, methods like MDE failed in certain tasks, with the highest average correlation
362   of other methods being only 0.184. Additionally, current instance-level methods (uncertainty-based

364   tency methods failed, and the entropy method achieved an average correlation of only 0.204.

366   instance-level transferability within models, enabling more accurate cross-model comparisons. This

369   task in the Sports-DA dataset, where the minimal domain shift led to similar transferability across

527   5   C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper introduces a new SFVDA setting called MSFVDA, where enables each source domain to
530   provide a zoo of trained source models , and allows the target user to utilize any model from these
531   model zoos without limitations on quantity. We propose a Multiple-Source Video Model Aggrega-
532   tion (MSVMA) framework for this setting. MSVMA employs Multi-level Instance Transferability
533   Calibration (MITC) to integrate group-level and dataset-level scale information, improving existing
534
      uncertainty-based transferability estimation metrics. This allows for accurate instance-level transfer-
      ability estimation across different models. For target domain adaptation, we introduce Instance-level
535
      Multi-Video Model Aggregation (IMVMA), which uses the calculated instance-level transferability
536
      to guide a path generation network. This network assigns instance-specific weights for unsupervised
537   source model aggregation, achieving state-of-the-art performance on MSFVDA tasks.
