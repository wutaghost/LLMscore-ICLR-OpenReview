009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Automated Optical Inspection (AOI) systems play a pivotal role in ensuring qual-
012            ity control during Printed Circuit Boards (PCB) manufacturing. However, the
013            current AOI systems necessitate manual setting of the region of interest (ROI) for
014            all components. To address this, we propose a patch-based preprocessing tech-
015            nique, dividing high-resolution PCB images into small 1024 × 1024 pixel patches
016            and employing the YOLOv7 segmentation model for real-time component ROI
017            segmentation. Our method consistently delivered high accuracy across various
018            PCB components, irrespective of background color, and demonstrated robust per-
019
               formance even with complex structures containing small components. It achieved
               impressive outcomes, with an average IoU, F1 score, pixel accuracy, and mAP
020
               of 0.8889, 0.9401, 0.9961, and 0.8255, respectively. Specifically, utilizing Fea-
021
               ture Pyramid Network (FPN) and Path Aggregation Network (PAN) in YOLOv7’s
022            multi-resolution processing allowed us to accurately segment PCB components of
023            various sizes and process them in real-time. This study underscores the potential
024            of automating real-time component ROI segmentation in the PCB manufacturing
025            process to enhance production speed and quality control.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Printed circuit boards (PCBs) are integral to nearly all electronic products, necessitating stringent
031   quality and reliability standards. Ensuring the performance and safety of the final product involves
032   identifying and correcting defects that may occur during PCB manufacturing. Automated Optical
033   Inspection (AOI) is a key technique employed for these quality controls, such as inspecting PCBs
034   during or after manufacturing to promptly identify defects. AOI systems utilize high-resolution
035   cameras and advanced image processing algorithms to detect defects, including solder bridges, lifted
036   pads, missing components, and misaligned parts (Hecht & Dishon, 1990).
037   The introduction of AOI has revolutionized the defect detection process, offering much faster and
038   more accurate results compared to traditional manual inspection methods (Liao et al., 2018). This
039   advancement significantly accelerated production speed and minimized the number of defective
040   products shipped. Additionally, AOI operates in a non-contact manner, safeguarding PCBs and
041   components from physical damage, thereby solidifying its status as an essential tool in modern PCB
042   manufacturing (Czimmermann et al., 2020).
043   Despite the advancements made by AOI systems, most still require a manual setting process. This
044   process involves defining a region of interest (ROI) for each PCB component to be inspected by
045   the AOI system. Operators manually specify the position and size of each component to ensure the
046   AOI system inspects them accurately. This procedure is exceedingly time-consuming, particularly
047   for complex PCB designs that contain hundreds of components, each requiring precise ROI settings.
048   This not only slows down the production line but also escalates labor costs and operator fatigue due
049   to the high level of expertise and experience needed for the task. Furthermore, manual operations
050
      are prone to errors, resulting in inaccurate ROI settings, which in turn diminishes the AOI system’s
      inspection accuracy and affects overall product quality.
051
052   To address these inefficiencies, there is a growing need for an automated ROI segmentation process.
053   The automation of ROI setting can save time and costs, ensure consistent inspection quality, and
      reduce dependence on skilled labor. This study proposes a deep learning-based automated method

      for partitioning PCB components, removing the need for manual instruction and enhancing the effi-
055   ciency and reliability of the overall inspection process.
056
057   This study presents a novel preprocessing method to raise the learning efficiency and accuracy of
058
      deep learning models. Rather than processing entire PCB images, the images are cropped into
      1024x1024 pixel-sized patches, effectively managing large data volumes and significantly improving
059
      model learning speed (Long et al., 2015).
060
061   Dividing PCB images into smaller patches offers several advantages. It concentrates the information
062   within each patch, allowing the model to learn data more efficiently. This approach captures PCB
063   details more effectively, enabling more accurate ROI settings and handling various component sizes
064
      and formats. Additionally, using small patches optimizes GPU memory usage, resolves memory
      shortage issues, and allows for larger batch sizes, which speeds up model convergence and reduces
065
      training time (Zhang et al., 2024). Training with small patches also enhances the model’s ability to
066
      generalize, ensuring consistent performance across different PCB images.
067
068   With these goals, this study proposes a deep learning-based segmentation method for PCB AOI
069   systems, incorporating preprocessing techniques to enhance learning efficiency and accuracy. This
070
      approach aims to elevate the automation level in PCB manufacturing processes and boost product
      quality. Ultimately, the study aspires to strengthen the competitiveness of the PCB manufacturing
071
      industry and contribute to the development of smarter manufacturing practices.

075

      Image Patch Division. Research on dividing and processing images by patches has been used exten-
077   sively to reduce memory usage and increase processing speed, especially in high-resolution image
078   processing. These techniques can provide efficient and fast processing performance by dividing an
079   image into smaller-sized patches and inputting each patch into the model rather than processing the
080   entire high-resolution image.
081
      An image patch division method was used to more efficiently detect retinal lesions in situations with
082
      a small amount of data by (Lam et al., 2018). When detecting lesions in retinal images, dividing and
083   learning in small patch units rather than processing the entire image generated more training data,
084   even with a small amount of data.
085
086
      In the study by (Gao et al., 2013), a small target was effectively detected from a single infrared im-
      age. In infrared images, to solve the problem of small targets being easily hidden by the background
087
      or the features of the target being unclear, a patch-based model was proposed that divides an image
088
      into small patches and detects targets within each patch. It works in a way that highlights patches
089   containing small targets and suppresses background noise, which helps to reduce computational
090   complexity while maintaining high accuracy in detecting small-sized targets.
091
092
      MegaDetectNet by (Wang et al., 2023b) detects targets from ultra-high resolution images by ex-
      tracting patches of foreground images where the target exists, which avoids unnecessary image
093
      processing, maximizes computational efficiency, and significantly improves detection speed.
094
095   Automated Solutions for PCB AOI. Although the current industry recognizes the need for auto-
096   mated solutions for PCB AOI, several challenges still need to be solved to implement these solu-
097   tions. (Anoop et al., 2015) compared various algorithms for detecting PCB defects, but the problem
098
      of setting ROIs with automatic segmentation has not been fully addressed.
099   Moreover, setting the ROI for numerous components in complex PCB designs is inefficient and
100   increases the likelihood of errors. For instance, (Malge & Nadaf, 2014) utilized traditional image
101   processing techniques to identify PCB defects, but these methods still depend on manual settings and
102   lack automatic segmentation. Therefore, developing an automated segmentation solution to replace
103   the manual process is essential to increase the efficiency of the PCB manufacturing process.
104   Deep Learning-Based Segmentation in Different Domains. Automatic image segmentation tech-
105   niques based on deep learning have been widely studied and applied in various fields, including
106   medical imaging, autonomous vehicles, and agriculture. In medical imaging, convolutional neural
107   networks (CNNs), especially U-Net, have demonstrated strong performance in cell and tissue seg-
      mentation. (Ronneberger et al., 2015) introduced an image segmentation technique using U-Net that

      performed well even with limited datasets. The model showed the ability to process medical images
109   of various sizes and formats effectively.

111   In autonomous vehicles, Mask R-CNN is positioned as a powerful model that can simultaneously

      2.1   PATCH -W ISE P REPROCESSING OF PCB I MAGES
128
129   Learning a model using an image of an entire PCB comes with several challenges due to its size and
130   complexity. PCB images have high resolution and contain a wide variety of components, requiring
131   substantial computing resources to process effectively. In particular, when using large datasets to
132   train deep learning models, problems may occur due to resource limitations such as GPU memory.
133   Thus, a preprocessing process was applied to partition PCB images into small patches of 1024 ×
134   1024 pixels in order to maximize learning efficiency and model performance.
135   In this study, we proposed a preprocessing method to divide PCB images into 1024 × 1024 pixel
136   size patches for processing. Learning a model using entire PCB images results in excessive memory
137   usage and reduces learning efficiency due to high resolution and complexity. PCB images are usually
138   10,000 x 15,000 pixels and can be up to 25,000 x 30,000 pixels. On the other hand, components
139   are small in size, ranging from 40 x 80 pixels to 1,000 x 1,000 pixels, significantly smaller than the
140   overall board size, making it highly inefficient to use the entire board images for training.
141   To solve this, we divided all images into small patches of 1024 × 1024 pixel size based on the bottom
142   left to the top right of the PCB, as shown in Figure 1. If the width and length of the PCB are not
143   divided by 1024, the remaining parts on the right and top edges are filled with black to make the 1024
144   x 1024 pixel size patches. This preprocessing method is advantageous because, unlike domains such
145   as medical images where targets or lesions are concentrated at specific positions, PCB components
146   are spread throughout the board, so all components are evenly distributed in patches. Each patch
147   was processed in a separate learning unit, and the model was trained to segment components in the
148
      patch accurately. After learning about the patches divided in this way, the results were output in a
      patch state and recombined into one PCB image. All components segmented in each patch were
149
      reconstructed across the entire PCB. This made it possible to segment all materials on the PCB by
150
      type.
151
152   This preprocessing optimizes memory usage during learning and inference while providing uniform
153   visual information that can be segmented globally without missing small objects. Moreover, since
154
      each patch is processed independently, it has a fast training speed and can efficiently process large
      PCB images without memory constraints.

      to process multiple images per second efficiently. YOLOv7 achieves 30 FPS or higher on standard

179   Since this study is to draw accurate ROI for all components, a segmentation head was used instead
180   of a detection head so that output could be in pixel-wise class labels instead of bounding boxes. The
181
      segmentation head involves a series of upsampling layers and convolutional layers to increase the
      resolution of the feature maps and predict the class of each pixel. In addition, YOLOv7’s segmenta-
182
      tion capability is particularly noteworthy due to its Enhanced-Efficient Layer Aggregation Network
183
      (E-ELAN) architecture. It is a cutting-edge architecture in object detection that draws inspiration
184   from the successful foundations laid by Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) and Path Aggregation Net-
185   work (PANet). While building upon the strengths of these predecessors, E-ELAN introduces novel
186   techniques to streamline feature aggregation and enhance performance. By efficiently routing in-
187   formation across feature levels and adaptively fusing features, E-ELAN demonstrates a remarkable
188   ability to handle objects of varying sizes and capture fine-grained details. This architectural innova-
189   tion solidifies E-ELAN’s position as a leading contender in the field of object detection and makes
190   the entire network simply look like Figure 2.
191   Furthermore, YOLOv7 enhances its efficiency through lightweight operations and the Bag-of-
192   Freebies technique. Unlike traditional deep learning models that rely on complex calculations,
193   YOLOv7 minimizes computational overhead while maintaining accuracy by integrating various
194   methods that improve performance without requiring additional training. This efficiency makes
195   YOLOv7 well-suited for real-time PCB AOI applications.
196   In this experiment, we utilized Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) as our optimization algorithm
197   with an initial learning rate of 0.01, a momentum of 0.937, a weight decay of 5e-4, and a maximum
198   learning rate of 0.1 for the OneCycleLR scheduler. Weight decay is a regularization technique
199   that helps prevent overfitting by penalizing large weights. The combination of these optimization
200   parameters, along with the OneCycleLR learning rate scheduler, contributed to the model’s effective
201   convergence and generalization performance.
202   In addition, we utilized the Binary Cross Entropy with Logits Loss (BCEWithLogitsLoss) to esti-
203   mate the likelihood of each pixel being associated with a specific class. The BCEWithLogitsLoss
204   is particularly effective in clearly delineating the boundary between two classes and is commonly
205   employed as a loss function, especially in split operations. To optimize GPU memory usage, the
206   batch size was set to 8 and the epoch to 500. Given the high-resolution nature of PCB images, we
207   chose a relatively small batch size to aid in memory management.

407   5   D ISCUSSION
408
409   In the proposed patch-wise learning, since images are divided into a constant size of 1024 × 1024
410   pixels and processed, it was observed that small components could not be recognized when they fell
411   on the patch boundary, or only a part of the object was detected. This issue occurred frequently,
412
      especially for small components or patch boundaries on the PCB, which can be evaluated as one of
      the inherent limitations of patch-wise learning. Hence, the average IoU was relatively low at 0.8889.
413
      Since tiny objects span the boundary in the patch-wise processing, they are undetected or partially
414
      detected.
415
416   In addition, while the body part of the component was segmented with high accuracy, smaller el-
417   ements, such as leads and pads, proved to be relatively difficult to segment, as shown in the third
418
      column-fourth row of Figure 3 that two small parts of pads are undetected. This is interpreted as
      being due to the fact that elements such as leads and pads are small compared to the body, and their
419
      location on the PCB posed difficulties to model learning. Thus, relatively poor boundaries and small
420
      sizes are other factors that lead to a low IoU value on average.

423   6   C ONCLUSION
424
425
      In this study, we proposed a patch-wise preprocessing method to partition PCB images into patches
426   for efficient component ROI setting in PCB AOI systems. By dividing PCB images into 1024 ×
427   1024 pixel patches and processing them, we achieved significantly accurate segmentation results
428   for all components on PCBs. Furthermore, by utilizing YOLOv7 segmentation model, we solved
429   the memory usage problem that occurs when processing high-resolution images and enabled faster
430   and more efficient inference. The proposed patch-wise learning consistently demonstrated strong
431   performance with PCB images of varying background colors and sizes and enabled accurate seg-
      mentation across different components such as resistors, capacitors, and ICs. However, a limit was


                                                       8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      found for small targets such as leads and pads across the patch boundaries, with a relative decrease
433   in segmentation performance.
434
435   In future research, finer patch sizes or multi-resolution approaches can be considered to overcome
436
      these limitations and improve the segmentation performance of small components. Moreover, fur-
      ther experiments in various PCB design and manufacturing environments are necessary to validate
437
      the widespread applicability of the proposed patch-wise method in industrial sites. This study high-
438
      lights the potential of an effective preprocessing method with YOLOv7 segmentation model for
439   automatic ROI setting in the PCB AOI system, aiming to enhance the speed and accuracy of inspec-
440   tions in the PCB manufacturing process.
