                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Graphs are characterized by their versatility in representing objects from a wide
012            range of domains, such as social networks or protein structures. This flexibility
013            and power poses a significant challenge for transfer learning between graph do-
014            mains. Current methods of transfer learning between graph domains tend to focus
015            exclusively on the structure of the underlying graphs, neglecting the characteristics
016            of the nodes and not addressing the difficulties in comparing nodes that represent
017            very dissimilar entities, such as atoms and people for instance. In this paper, we
018            propose a novel universal representation of graphs based on the relative frequency
019            of the node labels. This novel representation enables explainable transfer learn-
020
               ing between labeled graphs from different domains for the first time, without the
               need for additional adaptations. That is, we show that our novel representation
021
               can be readily combined with a data alignment technique that in turn allows trans-
022
               fer learning between data from different domains. Experimental results show that
023            knowledge can be acquired from graphs belonging to chemical and biological do-
024            mains to improve the accuracy of classification models in social network analysis.
025            A comparison with state-of-the-art techniques indicates that our approach outper-
026            forms existing non-topological methods and, in some cases, even graph neural
027            networks. In summary, our technique represents a major advance in graph node
028            representation for transfer learning between different domains, opening up new
029            perspectives for future research.

      1   I NTRODUCTION AND R ELATED W ORK
033
      In pattern recognition and machine learning, graphs are often used for representing complex data.
034
      The reason for this is twofold. First, graphs can explicitly model the intricate relationships that
035
      might exist between entities, and second, graphs can adapt their size and complexity to the size and
036   complexity of the actual data objects. For instance, graphs can be used to describe the structure of
037   molecules (Hu et al., 2020a), where nodes can be used to represent atoms and edges might encode
038   the chemical bonds between the atoms. Another example can be found in social network analysis
039   (Yanardag & Vishwanathan, 2015), where nodes might represent persons and edges then indicate
040   their relationships. Graphs are also successfully applied in image representations (Li & Gupta,
041   2018), where nodes can represent clusters of pixels, for instance, and edges can then be used to
042   measure the similarity between these clusters.
043   Despite the growing interest in graph-based pattern recognition and machine learning, both their
044   complex structures and the scarcity of suitable datasets (Manchanda et al., 2023) pose significant
045   research challenges. These challenges somehow limit the application of diverse powerful pattern
046   recognition and machine learning techniques to graphs. In recent years, solutions have been pro-
047   posed to address these obstacles, such as the expansion of datasets by data augmentation techniques
048   (Fuchs & Riesen, 2022), or the use of unsupervised approaches for pre-training networks (Hu et al.,
049   2020b). Another promising approach would be to adapt transfer learning to the graph domain.
050
      Transfer learning is a machine learning technique in which a model, pre-trained to perform a task, is
      reused and optimized to face a related (but different) task by exploiting previously acquired knowl-
051
      edge (Pan & Yang, 2010).
052
053   Some approaches for transfer learning on graphs have been proposed. For example, (Zhao et al.,
      2023) and (Roncoli et al., 2023) focus on transfer learning between graphs that share similar seman-

075   In this paper, we propose a novel representation of graphs that facilities the transfer of learning be-
076   tween graphs of very different domains. In particular, our approach is based on a frequency analysis
077   of the underlying node labels including a frequency alignment operation between different graph
078   datasets. By representing graphs and their nodes as frequency vectors, the method becomes appli-

080   representation does not require specific adaptations. Moreover, as suggested by recent proposals
081
      (Gillioz & Riesen, 2023), we focus on nodes and their labeling rather than the global topology. This
      is also corroborated by recent studies suggesting that relatively simple non-topological methods

      for graph classification can compete with more sophisticated techniques. For example, it has been
083
      shown that Global Sum Pooling, followed by a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), can achieve similar,
084   or better, results than more complex models on some datasets (Errica et al., 2020). Last but not least,
085   our novel method not only achieves general applicability and high flexibility but also a high degree
086   of explainability, thus enabling a clear understanding of how and why transfer learning occurs.

089

090   a social network dataset. Finally, in Section 4, we draw conclusions, discuss the limitations of our
091   approach and propose possible future research directions.

      2.1   R EPRESENTING G RAPHS AS ABSOLUTE FREQUENCY VECTORS
101
102   Graphs are mathematical structures useful for modeling complex entities and their relationships.
103   Formally, a graph can be defined as a triple g = (V, E, µ), where V is the set of nodes, E ⊆
104   {(u, v)|u, v ∈ V, u ̸= v} is the set of edges, and µ : V → L is a function that assigns a label
105   µ(v) = l ∈ L to each node v ∈ V (where L is a given label alphabet). Sometimes this definition
106   is complemented with a second function, which also assigns a label to the edges in the graph – in
107   this paper, however, we only use node-labeled graphs (in fact, the methods proposed in this section
      could also be adapted to labeled edges).

123   From now on, we suppose that the label alphabet L consists of n discrete symbols l1 , . . . , ln , and
124   the dataset of graphs G = {g1 , . . . , gN } consists of N graphs of the type g = (V, E, µ). To carry
125
      out the transfer of knowledge, we propose to create a new representation of the graphs consisting
      of a vector of relative frequencies of all labels li ∈ L. In a first step, for each label µ(v) = li ∈ L
126
      that can be observed in the dataset G, we compute its relative frequency f (li ) with respect to all N
127
      graphs from G and round its value to the fourth decimal place (see Fig. 1, Step 1). Next, we define a
128   k-dimensional vector termed rG = (f1 , . . . , fk ), which is common to all graphs in the same dataset
129   G (see Fig. 1, Step 2). This vector rG contains all the relative frequencies fi , that actually occur
130   on the nodes of all graphs from G, in ascending order (without duplicates). Since multiple labels
131   in L may have the same relative frequency, the size k of rG may be smaller than the total number
132   n of unique labels in L. Next, for each graph gj ∈ G we count how often each relative frequency
133   f (li ) ∈ rG appears among the nodes of gj based on the vector rG . That is, we replace gj ∈ G with
134   a k-dimensional vector xGj = (ν1 , . . . , νK ) ∈ Rn where νi is the absolute frequency of the relative
135   frequency fi actually observed on the nodes in set Vj of gj . This produces a representation that
136
      reflects the distribution of relative label frequencies into a vector-based representation of the graph
      (see Fig. 1, Step 3).
137
138   To ensure that only information from the training set is actually used in this transformation (and thus
139   avoid data leakage), the dataset must be split into a training and a test set before calculating the new
140   representation. Of course, this can lead to a situation where we see a particular label, for which we
141   need to calculate the relative frequency, for the first time in the test set. In such cases, we use the
142
      minimum relative frequency for this unknown label (present in rG ) to represent the corresponding
      label. Since the label was not observed in the training set (and therefore tends to have a low relative
143
      frequency), this heuristic seems reasonable.

146   2.2        A LIGNMENT OF R EPRESENTATIONS FOR T RANSFER OF K NOWLEDGE
147
148   In the previous section, we show how we can transform graphs from arbitrary graph datasets into
149   a new k-dimensional vector representation. In the following, we assume that we have N different
150   graph datasets G1 , . . . , GN available. For each of these N datasets, we calculate the relative fre-
151   quency vectors rG1 , . . . , rGN of size k1 , . . . , kN respectively. By means of this information, we
152
      now have a ki -dimensional vector space for each of the N graph datasets, in which the graphs are
      mapped (i = 1, . . . , N ). Note that, each vector space (or the associated graph domain) can have
153
      different dimensions and represent objects from very different domains.
154
155   The transfer of knowledge between these different domains is now achieved by aligning the N
156   separate graph representations in a common representation space. In this paper, our main goal is
157   to preserve explainability during the transfer process. Thus, instead of using methods for learning
158
      the representations (Ganin et al., 2016), which might improve the transfer of knowledge but neglect
      interpretability, we thus propose the following suboptimal alignment approach.
159

161   (e.g., a classification model) and a metric β, that measures the model performance (e.g., the classi-

      3.1   V ISUAL A NALYSIS
238
239
      The differences in the number of nodes with the same relative frequency for each class of graphs
240
      are crucial for both classification (Bishop & Nasrabadi, 2007) and transfer learning. To visualize
241   these differences, we present the plots shown in Fig. 3 for all datasets. Each dot in Fig. 3 represents
242   a unique relative frequency f on the Y-axis and the corresponding relative class memberships on
243   the X-axis. That is, on the Y-axis dots close to 1 represent common labels, dots close to 0 indicate
244   uncommon labels. On the X-axis we show the normalized difference in the relative number of nodes
245   associated with the same relative frequency from the graph classes. Values close to -1 indicate that
246   more nodes of graphs from the negative class have this frequency value f , while values close to +1
247   indicate that more nodes of graphs from the positive class have this frequency value f (an X-value
248   of 0.0 means that half of the nodes having frequency f , originate from one or the other class).
249   When we compare the distributions of the three datasets, we notice the following. First, the rather
250   rare labels of REDDIT-B are similar to the rare labels of DD in terms of their relative class member-
251   ship. Our basic hypothesis is that these similarities can be used to transfer the knowledge between
252   these two domains. If, on the other hand, one compares REDDIT-B with MUTAG, there is no
253   obvious correspondence. Therefore, for the MUTAG dataset, we also show the distribution of fre-
254   quencies when the underlying classes are mirrored – this dataset will be referred to as MUTAG-M
255
      from now on. We now see that the common labels of REDDIT-B are similarly distributed to those
      of MUTAG-M. Hence, the presence of many nodes with a common label should increase the prob-
256
      ability of a graph being labeled negatively, regardless of the actual domain.
257
258   To summarize, we see that the distribution profiles of REDDIT-B, DD and MUTAG-M are visibly
259   similar despite the different domains, which at least makes a transfer of knowledge possible.

309

310
      the first row of Table 1 we see that a k-NN trained on DD and then evaluated on the other two
311

317
      The goal of second experiment is to evaluate whether training the models on a joint dataset can
318   improve the classification performance compared to models trained on single datasets. Hence, after
319   the alignment, we merge the different training sets with the reference dataset, creating a joint dataset
320   for training. Next, we apply standardization on the joint dataset and train the models to distinguish
321   the classes. During the training process, we assign a weight to each dataset, which is 1.0 for data
322   from the reference dataset, and a weight equal to the ratio of the number of elements in the dataset
323   and the total number of elements in the joint dataset for the other datasets. This approach ensures a
      higher weight to the reference dataset on which we aim to improve the classification performance.

      The somehow limited increase in the classification accuracy could be due to several factors, includ-
      ing the suboptimal alignment of the common space and the relatively small sizes of the training

      datasets (approximately 1,000, 150, and 1,800 samples for DD, MUTAG, and REDDIT-BINARY,
348
      respectively). It is known that larger datasets are often needed to learn and transfer common knowl-
349   edge. Moreover, in some situations, such as on MUTAG-M trained with the SVM, we even observe
350   a deterioration of the accuracy with the joint dataset. This may be due to the addition of noise along
351   with useful signals when expanding the dataset, which makes it difficult to maintain good perfor-
352   mance given the sensitivity of SVMs (Singla & Shukla, 2020). A strategy to mitigate this issue
353   might be to assign higher weights to the reference datasets, or to increase the number of alignment
354   cycles during joint training. For instance, assigning a weight of 105 to the MUTAG-M data during
355   SVM training and after K = 4 alignment cycles, the balanced accuracy increases from 77.3% to
356   80.6% (though with negative effects on the classification accuracy of the aligned datasets).

446   4   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
447
448
      Both the complexity of the underlying data structures and the scarcity of large datasets pose con-
449
      siderable challenges for the application of transfer learning in the field of graphs. Furthermore, the
450   main limitation for transfer learning with graphs is given by the fact that the nodes in a graph can
451   represent very different entities (e.g., atoms in one graph and users in another graph). This makes it
452   complex to abstract this information and make transfer learning possible.
453
      In this paper, we propose to first represent graphs based on the distribution of their node labels and
454
      second align these features using a reference dataset. By means of this specific alignment of the
455
      features multi-domain transfer learning can be performed on graphs. In an experimental evaluation,
456   we show that it is possible to transfer at least part of the knowledge between proteins, molecules,
457   and social network datasets. Moreover, we show that joint training on aligned datasets produces a
458   transfer of knowledge that increases the classification performance by an average of 0.8 percentage
459   points. Finally, our evaluation shows that the proposed method is able to outperform methods that
460   rely on non-topological graph representations only.
461
      Although the results are encouraging, the proposed method has some significant limitations. The
462   main limitation is related to the absence of the use of graph topology, which is an important source
463   of information and its non-use might significantly reduce the performances in some datasets. In
464   addition, the high computational complexity of the alignment method hinders its application to more
465   than three datasets. Moreover, the alignment process requires some similarity of distributions and
466   possible mirroring of the underlying classes to fit other datasets. Another important limitation is that
467   the novel representation is not learned directly from a model. This means that it is necessary to use
468   one dataset as a reference to align the others, which can lead to suboptimal results if the reference
469   dataset is not representative enough.
470   Future research is focused on both overcoming current limitations and finding other parameters that
471   allow information to pass between graphs with labeled nodes from different domains. In particular,
472   we plan to apply the concept discussed in this paper to graph neural networks, thus integrating the
473   topology of graphs. For instance, we could use autoencoders to learn new generic representations of
474   nodes and optimize dataset alignment, reducing dependence on reference datasets and minimizing
475   manual inspections of node label distributions.
