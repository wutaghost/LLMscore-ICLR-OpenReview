009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Bimanual dexterous manipulation is a critical yet underexplored area in robotics.
012            Its high-dimensional action space and inherent task complexity present significant
013            challenges for policy learning, and the limited task diversity in existing bench-
014            marks hinders general-purpose skill development. Existing approaches largely
015            depend on reinforcement learning, often constrained by intricately designed re-
016            ward functions tailored to a narrow set of tasks. In this work, we present a novel
017
               approach for efficiently learning diverse bimanual dexterous skills from abundant
               human demonstrations. Specifically, we introduce BiDexHD, a framework that
018
               unifies task construction from existing bimanual datasets and employs teacher-
019
               student policy learning to address all tasks. The teacher learns state-based poli-
020            cies using a general two-stage reward function across tasks with shared behaviors,
021            while the student distills the learned multi-task policies into a vision-based policy.
022            With BiDexHD, scalable learning of numerous bimanual dexterous skills from
023            auto-constructed tasks becomes feasible, offering promising advances toward uni-
024            versal bimanual dexterous manipulation. Our empirical evaluation of the TACO
025            dataset, spanning 141 tasks across six categories, demonstrates a task fulfillment
026            rate of 74.59% on trained tasks and 51.07% on unseen tasks, showcasing the
027            effectiveness and competitive zero-shot generalization capabilities of BiDexHD.

               For videos and more information, visit our project page.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Bimanual manipulation is crucial and beneficial. Humans use both hands to do manipulations like
033   using scissors, tying shoelaces, or operating kitchen utensils. The ability to manipulate objects with
034   two hands is fundamental for everyday tasks, because with both hands, we can not only do some
035   “symmetry” collaborative tasks like carrying a heavy box with two hands, but also do “asymmetry”
036   tasks (Liu et al., 2024a) like twisting a bottle cap, which means one hand acts as an auxiliary hand
037   for stabilizing objects and the other acts as an operator.
038   With the rapid development of embodied artificial intelligence, robotic bimanual dexterous manip-
039   ulation is getting more and more important in manufacturing, healthcare, agriculture, construction,
040   and tertiary industry (Zhang et al., 2024b). This emphasizes the effective use of tools or manipula-
041   tions over objects that are deformable or of irregular shapes, overcoming the limitations of low-DOF
042   end-effectors like grippers. Moreover, it addresses complicated human-like hand-object interaction
043   and collaboration. Despite its significance, achieving proficient bimanual manipulation remains a
044   substantial challenge because it severely struggles with the high-dimensional action space. While a
045   line of previous work (Grannen et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024; Kataoka et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024a)
046
      primarily focuses on bimanual manipulation with grippers, there is still much left to explore for bi-
      manual manipulation with dexterous hands. Previous attempts to solve bimanual dexterous manip-
047
      ulation tasks are mainly based on reinforcement learning (RL) (Lin et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023;
048
      Zhang et al., 2024a). However, they require intricate reward designs tailored to specific manually-
049   designed tasks. Therefore, these approaches lack scalability and generalizability to a broader range
050   of tasks. Recent research (Sindhupathiraja et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024; He et al., 2024) has advanced
051   robotic bimanual dexterous manipulation through teleoperation. Nevertheless, human intervention
052   is inevitable. We would ask a question:
053
       “Can we learn diverse bimanual dexterous manipulation skills in a unified and scalable way?”


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Our solution is to use human demonstrations. Compared to robotic demonstrations, human demon-
055   strations are relatively easier to obtain with haptic gloves or MoCap devices rather than deploying
056   a trained policy, and contain much more physically compliant and human-aligned behavior. In this
057   paper, we propose a novel approach to learn diverse bimanual dexterous manipulation skills from
058   human demonstrations. Upon this setting, we propose BiDexHD, a unified and scalable framework
059   to automatically turn a human bimanual manipulation dataset into a series of tasks in the simulation
060   and conduct effective policy learning. The majority of previous bimanual studies primarily focus
061   on existing benchmarks or a limited range of tasks. For RL-based methods (Lin et al., 2024; Huang
062   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a), they tailor specific reward function to specific tasks. For IL-based
063
      methods (Wang et al., 2024a; Cheng et al., 2024), it is inevitable to collect a bulk of data for learning
      specific tasks (typically around 50 trajectories for each single task). In contrast, BiDexHD does not
064
      depend on manually-designed tasks or pre-defined tasks in existing benchmarks and instead consis-
065
      tently constructs feasible tasks from any bimanual manipulation trajectory. Furthermore, BiDexHD
066   gets rid of task-specific reward engineering and instead utilize a unified reward function for all
067   automatically constructed bimanual tasks. In a word, BiDexHD breaks the bottleneck of limited
068   tasks and label-intensive manual designs, which is significant to the further development of general-
069   purpose bimanual dexterous manipulation. Though promising, several challenges must be addressed
070   to fully realize this. It is essential to figure out how to accurately mimic fine-grained bimanual behav-
071   iors from human demonstrations and avoid collisions and disturbances while encouraging smooth
072   trajectories and synchronous collaboration between both hands. To address this, we carefully design
073   a general two-stage reward function to assign curricula for RL training.
074   To sum up, our key contributions can be summarized as follows:
075
076          • We formalize the problem of learning bimanual dexterous skills from human demonstra-
077            tions as a preliminary attempt towards universal bimanual skills.
078
079          • We propose BiDexHD, a unified and scalable reinforcement learning framework for learn-
080
               ing diverse bimanual dexterous manipulation from human demonstrations, advancing the
               capabilities of robots in performing bimanual cooperative tasks.
081
082          • We evaluate BiDexHD across 141 auto-constructed tasks over 6 categories from the
083            TACO (Liu et al., 2024b) dataset and demonstrate the superior training performance and
084            competitive generalization capabilities of BiDexHD.

      2.1   B IMANUAL D EXTEROUS M ANIPULATION
090
091   In recent years, the robotics community has increasingly focused on dexterous manipulation due
092   to its remarkable flexibility and human-like dexterity. Researchers have developed methods using
093   dexterous hands for tasks such as in-hand manipulation (Arunachalam et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023;
094   Handa et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; 2022), grasping (Xu et al., 2023; Wan et al.,
095   2023; Qin et al., 2023a; Ye et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2022a), and manipulating deformable objects (Bai
096   et al., 2016; Ficuciello et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023; Hou et al., 2019). However, most existing work
097   focuses on a single dexterous hand, revealing the potential of bimanual dexterity. In fact, for hu-
098   mans, bimanual collaboration takes place frequently in daily life such as riding, carrying heavy
099
      objects, and using tools. There are heterogeneous research directions towards bimanual dexterous
      manipulation. Some researchers attempt to settle down to specific tasks via reinforcement learning.
100
      For example, recent work (Lin et al., 2024) investigates twisting lids with two multi-fingered hands,
101
      DynamicHandover (Huang et al., 2023) explores throwing and catching, and ArtiGrasp (Zhang et al.,
102   2024a) focuses on a few grasping and articulation tasks. Gbagbe et al. (2024) leveraged large lan-
103   guage models to design a system for bimanual robotic dexterous manipulation, while Wang et al.
104   (2024b) proposed to solve bimanual grasping via physics-aware iterative learning and prediction of
105   saliency maps. A recent work (Gao et al., 2024) adopts keypoints-based visual imitation learning
106   to learn bimanual coordination strategies. Unlike existing work, in this paper, we offer a general
107   solution to learn from bimanual demonstrations by designing a unified reward function to learn a
      state-based policy via reinforcement learning and distilling it into a vision-based policy.

      2.2    L EARNING D EXTERITY F ROM H UMAN D EMONSTRATIONS
109
110   As a sample-efficient data-driven way, learning from human demonstrations has been proven suc-
111   cessful in robot learning (Jia et al., 2024; Mandlekar et al., 2023; Odesanmi et al., 2023). Com-
112   pared with learning dexterity via reinforcement learning which is notoriously challenging for policy
113   learning due to the high degrees of freedom and the necessity of manually designing task-specific
114   reward functions, learning complex dexterous behaviors from diverse accessible human demonstra-
115   tions (Smith et al., 2019; Schmeckpeper et al., 2020; Shao et al., 2021) is a more stable and scal-
116   able approach. A line of previous studies (Arunachalam et al., 2023; Mandikal & Grauman, 2021;
117   Sivakumar et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2022b; Mandikal & Grauman, 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Shaw et al.,
118
      2023b; Chen et al., 2024) explicitly leverages human demonstrations to facilitate the acquisition
      of dexterous manipulation skills mainly by human-robot-arm-hand retargeting and imitation learn-
119
      ing. However, these studies predominantly focus on single-hand manipulation and are often limited
120
      to tasks such as in-hand manipulation (Arunachalam et al., 2023) or video-conditioned teleopera-
121   tion (Sivakumar et al., 2022). With the recent advent of diverse and comprehensive human bimanual
122   manipulation datasets (Zhan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Fan et al., 2023; Razali & Demiris, 2023)
123   which naturally provide a rich resource for high-quality posture sequences of dual hands and biman-
124   ual interaction with diverse real objects, a lot of bimanual manipulation tasks can be automatically
125   defined. Thus, in this work, we aim to address more challenging and general bimanual dexterous
126   skill learning purely based on automatically constructed tasks from human demonstrations.

133   Dec-POMDP. We formulate each bimanual manipulation task as a decentralized partially observ-
134   able Markov decision process (Dec-POMDP). The Dec-POMDP can be represented by the tuple
135
      Z = (N , M, S, O, A, P, R, ρ, γ). Dual hands with arms are separated as N agents, which is rep-
      resented by set M. The proprioception of robots and the information about objects are initialized at
136
      s0 ∈ S according to the initial state distribution ρ(s0 ). At each time step t, the state is represented
137
      by st , and the i-th agent receives an observation oit ∈ O based on st . Subsequently, the policy of the
138   i-th agent, πi ∈ Π, takes oit as input and outputs an action ait ∈ Ai . The joint action of all agents is
139   denoted by at ∈ A, where A = A1 × A2 × · · · AN . The state transits to the next state according to
140   the transition function st+1 ∼ P (st+1 |st , at ). After this, the i-th agent receives a reward rti based
141   on the reward function R(st , at ). The objective is to find the optimal policy π that maximizes the
                                      PT −1 PN
142   expected sum of rewards Eπ [ t=0 γ t i=1 rti ] over an episode with T time steps, where γ is the
143   discount factor.
144

145
      manipulation task in IsaacGym (Makoviychuk et al., 2021). In general, there are a tool and a target
146   object initialized on a table. N = 2 robotic arms are installed in front of the table, with left LEAP
147   Hand (Shaw et al., 2023a) mounted on the left arm and right hand on the right arm. The right hand
148   reaches for the tool, and the left hand targets the object. Both hands coordinate to simultaneously
149   move, pick up, and manipulate the objects above the table. Note that our method applies to all

      3.2        T EACHER -S TUDENT L EARNING
181
      It is well known (Chen et al., 2022; 2023) that directly learning a multi-task vision-based policy for
182
      dexterous hands is extremely challenging. A more popular and scalable approach is teacher-student
183
      learning (Wan et al., 2023), which not only simplifies the complexity of multi-DoF robot multi-
184   task learning but also enhances the efficiency of point cloud-based policy learning. In the teacher
185   learning phase, a single state-based policy is first trained via reinforcement learning, leveraging
186   privileged information to solve multiple similar tasks. Once trained, multiple teacher policies can
187   effectively tackle all tasks. In the student learning phase, a vision-based student policy is distilled
188   from the teacher policies. A key distinction between teacher and student observations is how object
189   information is represented. While the teacher’s observation space includes precise details about an
190   object’s position, orientation, and linear and angular velocities, the student’s observation relies on
191   point clouds consisting of P sampled points from the object’s surface mesh. In this way, the learned
192
      student policy is promising to be deployed in real world to deal with multiple tasks provided that the
      real point clouds can be constructed from real-time multi-view RGB-D camera system.

      4.1        OVERVIEW
198
199
      As illustrated in Figure 1, we propose a scalable three-phase framework. In the first phase, we paral-
200
      lelize the construction of Dec-POMDP bimanual tasks from a human bimanual manipulation dataset
201   within IsaacGym (Makoviychuk et al., 2021). After task initialization, the subsequent two phases
202   adopt a teacher-student policy learning framework. Following the approach of Chen et al. (2022;
203   2023); Wan et al. (2023), we utilize Independent Proximal Policy Optimization (IPPO) (De Witt
204   et al., 2020) during the second phase to independently train state-based teacher policies for con-
205   structed bimanual dexterous tasks in parallel. Each expert focuses on a subset of tasks that require
206   similar behaviors. In the final phase, the teacher policies are distilled into a vision-based student
207   policy, integrating skills across related tasks.
208

526   6     C ONCLUSION & L IMITATIONS
527
528   In this paper, we introduce a novel approach to learning diverse bimanual dexterous manipulation
529   skills that utilizes human demonstrations. Our BiDexHD automatically constructs bimanual manip-
530   ulation tasks from existing datasets and employs a teacher-student learning approach for a vision-
531   based policy that can tackle similar tasks. Our main technical contributions include designing a uni-
532   fied two-stage reward function for multi-task RL training and an in-context vision-based policy that
533   enhances generalization capabilities. Experimental results demonstrate that BiDexHD facilitates ro-
534   bust RL training and policy distillation, successfully solves six categories of bimanual dexterous
535   manipulation tasks, and effectively transfers to unseen tasks through zero-shot generalization.
536   Our work forwards a step toward universal bimanual manipulation skills, and some limitations need
537   to be addressed in future research. Exploring strategies for achieving more precise spatial and tem-
538   poral tracking is a valuable direction for future work. Additionally, incorporating a wider variety
539   of real-world tasks–such as deformable object manipulation and bimanual handover–could reveal
      further potential in dynamic collaborative manipulation scenarios with bimanual dexterous hands.

      B.7   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
949
950   Our codebase for RL and DAgger is built upon UniDexGrasp++ (Wan et al., 2023). For each state-
951   based policy, we employ five-layer multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) for both the actor and the critic,
952   featuring hidden layers with dimensions [1024, 1024, 512, 512] and using ELU activation functions.
953   For the vision-based policy, we utilize a simplified PointNet (Qi et al., 2017) backbone that incor-
954   porates two 1D convolutional layers, a mixture of maximum and average pooling operations, and
955   two MLP layers to process the object point cloud, resulting in an output dimension of 128. Both the
956   actor and the critic share the output of this backbone. The same network architecture is adapted to
957   BC baselines and all PPO variants both for Arctic tasks and TACO tasks.

       E    C OMPARISONS WITH PREVIOUS WORK

1264
       PGDM Dasari et al. (2023) plans with given pre-grasp pose and trains policies to track the human
       trajectories from object trajectories via reinforcement learning. Although the methodology shows
1265
       some similarity to BiDexHD and diverse single-hand manipulation tasks are also addressed, there
1266
       are significant differences between PGDM and BiDexHD:
1267
1268
1269         • Stage Division. PGDM divides the task into distinct stages: planning a pre-grasp pose
1270           (reaching stage) and learning to grasp and move through reinforcement learning (grasping
1271           and moving stages). Their planning-based reaching stage is limited to performing hand-
1272           reaching behaviors, while our BiDexHD can perform general, contact-rich behaviors in
1273           the RL-based alignment stage. BiDexHD employs unified reinforcement learning, start-
1274           ing with aligning both hands and objects to a ready state (alignment stage), followed by
1275
               trajectory tracking (tracking stage). This design allows BiDexHD to flexibly learn diverse
               skills like twisting and pushing, going beyond simple reaching and grasping. Once both
1276
               hands securely hold the objects, they maintain their relative states and learn to track desired
1277
               poses with ease. Our design properly strikes a balance between policy quality and training
1278           difficulty.
1279
1280
1281
             • Data requirement. PGDM relies on human-annotated pre-grasp poses in the TCDM bench-
1282
               mark for planning. BiDexHD uses RL to learn to align the simulation with the datasets
1283
               without extra annotations, enhancing its scalability.
1284
1285
1286
1287
             • Application Scope. PGDM primarily focuses on single Adroit Hand grasping in Mujoco
               simulations, while BiDexHD extends to more complex bimanual arm-hand systems and
1288
               diverse manipulation tasks in highly parallelized IsaacGym simulations.
1289
1290
1291   Dexcap Wang et al. (2024a) proposes a novel motion capture and vision-based data collection sys-
1292   tem for bimanual task learning via imitation. It is worth noting that data collected by Dexcap alone
1293   is insufficient to derive feasible policies. Further human-in-the-loop finetuning is necessary to in-
1294   corporate more kinematics and dynamics. In contrast, BiDexHD uses online reinforcement learning
1295   with a general reward function to learn diverse bimanual skills from object motion capture data
       through trial and error, without additional fine-tuning.
