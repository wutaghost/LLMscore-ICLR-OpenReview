009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Data-driven simulation of physical systems has recently kindled significant atten-
012            tion, where many neural models have been developed. In particular, mesh-based
013            graph neural networks (GNNs) have demonstrated significant potential in pre-
014            dicting spatiotemporal dynamics across arbitrary geometric domains. However,
015            the existing node-edge message passing mechanism in GNNs limits the model’s
016            representation learning ability. In this paper, we proposed a cell-embedded GNN
017            model (aka, CeGNN) to learn spatiotemporal dynamics with lifted performance.
018            Specifically, we introduce a learnable cell attribution to the node-edge message
019
               passing process, which better captures the spatial dependency of regional features.
               Such a strategy essentially upgrades the local aggregation scheme from first order
020
               (e.g., from edge to node) to a higher order (e.g., from volume and edge to node),
021
               which takes advantage of volumetric information in message passing. Meanwhile,
022            a novel feature-enhanced block is designed to further improve the performance of
023            CeGNN and alleviate the over-smoothness problem. The extensive experiments on
024            various PDE systems and one real-world dataset demonstrate that CeGNN achieves
025            superior performance compared with other baseline models, significantly reducing
026            the prediction errors on several PDE systems.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Solving Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) is often essential for analyzing and modeling complex
032   spatiotemporal dynamic processes across various scientific and engineering fields. For example,
033   weather prediction (Scher, 2018; Schultz et al., 2021; Grover et al., 2015), ocean current motion pre-
034   diction (Zheng et al., 2020), nonlinear engineering structure earthquake response prediction (Zhang
035   et al., 2019), material mechanical properties simulation (Wang & Sun, 2018), etc. Traditionally,
036   classical numerical methods (e.g., Finite Difference Method (FDM) (Godunov & Bohachevsky,
037   1959; Özişik et al., 2017), Finite Volume Method (FVM) (Eymard et al., 2000), and Finite Element
038   Method (FEM) (Hughes, 2012)) are utilized to solve these PDEs, requiring substantial analytical or
039
      computational efforts. Although this problem has been simplified via discretizing the space, the issue
      of trade-off between cost and precision intensifies when dealing with varying domain geometries
040
      (e.g., different initial or boundary conditions or various input parameters), especially in real-world
041
      scenarios. In the last few decades, Deep Learning (DL) (Pinkus, 1999; Tolstikhin et al., 2021; Albawi
042   et al., 2017; Koutnik et al., 2014; Sundermeyer et al., 2012) models have made great progress in
043   approximating high-dimensional PDEs benefiting from existing rich labeled or unlabeled datasets.
044   However, there are certain drawbacks in this simple approach of learning the non-linear mapping
045   between inputs and outputs from data. For example, their performance is severely limited by the
046   training datasets, the neural network lacks interpretability and generalizes poorly.
047
      Embedding domain-specific expertise (e.g., Physics-informed Neural Networks (PINNs) (Raissi
048   et al., 2019)) has shown the potential to tackle these problems (Krishnapriyan et al., 2021; Gao et al.,
049   2021; He et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b). However, the core part of PINNs, Automatic Differentiation
050   (AD) approach, has two major drawbacks: (1) it is necessary to formulate explicit governing equations
051   into the loss function, and (2) the parameters in high-dimensional feature spaces cannot be efficiently
052   optimized when facing highly complex networks like graph networks. As shown in Figure 1e,
053   there are no any predefined equations available to represent the evolution patterns of sea surface
      temperature at varying depths. Neural Operators, such as DeepONet (Lu et al., 2021) and Latent

064   DeepONet (Kontolati et al., 2024), have emerged as another paradigm to learn these complex non-
065   linear behaviors. The most well-known models, Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2021a) and
066   its variants (Tran et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2022; Ashiqur Rahman et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a), utilize

068   Likewise, they inevitably have the same shortcoming as those traditional methods: over-reliance on
069
      data and biased towards the grid domain.
070   The other representative models, Transformer models (Vaswani et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2024)
071   and GNNs (Liu et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2022; McCardle, 2023; Horie & Mitsume, 2024), have
072   demonstrated significant influence in predicting spatiotemporal dynamics across arbitrary geometric
073   domains. In particular, mesh-based graph neural networks (GNNs) (Gilmer et al., 2017; 2020; Pfaff
074   et al., 2021; Brandstetter et al., 2022) learn vastly different dynamics of various physical systems,
075
      ranging from structural mechanics and cloth to fluid simulations. However, the existing node-edge
      message passing mechanism in GNNs overestimates the primary role of “message” passing function
076
      on the neighbor “edges”, limiting the model’s representation learning ability. In general, this strategy
077
      leads to highly homogeneous node features after multiple rounds of message passing, making the
078   features ineffective at representing distinct characteristics, namely, the over-smoothness problem.
079
080   To further address the above issues, we proposed an end-to-end graph-based framework, Cell-
081
      embedded Graph Neural Network (CeGNN), to model spatiotemporal dynamics across various
      domains with improved performance. Specifically, after detecting discontinuities in space, we
082
      introduce a learnable cell attribution to the node-edge message passing process, which better captures
083
      the spatial dependency of regional features. Such a strategy essentially upgrades the local aggregation
084   scheme from the first order (e.g., from edge to node) to a higher order (e.g., from volume and edge to
085   node), which takes advantage of volumetric information in message passing. Meanwhile, a novel
086   feature-enhanced (FE) block is designed to further improve the performance of CeGNN and relieve
087   the over-smoothness problem, via treating the latent features as basis functions and further processing
088   these features on this concept. In detail, it regards the node latent feature hi as basis and builds
089   a higher-order tensor feature via an outer-product operation, e.g., hi ⊗ hi . This process creates
090   abundant second-order nonlinear terms to enrich the feature map. We then use a mask operation to
091   randomly sample these terms, filtering the appropriate information by a learnable weight tensor to
092
      enhance the model’s representation capacity. Figure 2 shows an outline of our proposed model. Our
      extensive experiments on many PDE-centric systems and real-world datasets show that CeGNN can
093
      significantly enhance spatiotemporal dynamic learning in various scenarios, particularly with limited
094
      datasets. The key contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
095
096              • We introduce cell attributions legitimately to learn second-order information from connected
097
                   nodes of any cell, allowing us to rapidly identify non-local relationships that traditional
                   message-passing mechanisms often fail to capture directly.
098
                 • We propose the FE block to enrich the feature representations and filter more effective
099
                   information via learnable parameters.
100              • Our approach stands out for its lower error, better interpretability, and robust generalizability,
101                making a substantial progress in the spatiotemporal dynamic field.

134
135   Without loss of generality, the time-dependent PDEs generally describe the evolution of a con-
136                                  intervals, which can be cast into the following form: ∂u/∂t =
      tinuous field over certain time
137   F θ, t, x, u, ∇u, ∇2 u, . . . , where F(·) denotes an unknown linear or nonlinear function com-
138   prised of the spatiotemporal variable u(x, t) ∈ Rd , its corresponding partial derivatives (e.g.,
139   ∇u, ∇2 u), and some related parameters θ. Here, x ∈ Rm denotes the m-dimensional spatial
140   coordinate, t ∈ R1 the time, ∇ the Nabla operator, ∇2 the Laplacian operator. The PDE is subjected
141   to specific initial and boundary conditions.
142
      2.2   C LASSICAL SOLVERS
143
144
      To solve time-dependent PDEs, a common way is the method of lines (MOL). By discretizing in
145   all but one dimension, it allows solutions to be computed via methods and software developed
146   for the numerical integration of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) and differential-algebraic
147   equations (DAEs). Meanwhile, the multigrid method (Wu et al., 2020) is another algorithm for
148   solving PDEs via a hierarchy of discretizations. Other classical numerical methods (e.g., Finite
149   Difference Method (FDM) (Godunov & Bohachevsky, 1959; Özişik et al., 2017), Finite Volume
150   Method (FVM) (Eymard et al., 2000), and Finite Element Method (FEM) (Cao et al., 1999)) have
151   also been utilized for practical applications (Reich, 2000; Hughes, 2012).
152
153
      2.3   N EURAL SOLVERS
154
      PINN Methods. Two main approaches, Physics-informed Neural networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al.,
155
      2019; Krishnapriyan et al., 2021; He et al., 2023) and Physics-informed Neural Operators (Li et al.,
156   2021b; Hao et al., 2023; Kovachki et al., 2023), were developed to learn fluid and solid mechanisms.
157   With formulating the explicit governing equation as the loss function, PINNs constrain the latent
158   feature spaces to a certain range, effectively learning from small data or even without any labeled data.
159   Such a novel method immediately attracts the attention of many researchers and has been utilized in
160   a wide range of applications governed by differential equations, such as heat transfer problems (Cai
161   et al., 2021), power systems (Misyris et al., 2020), medical science (Sahli Costabal et al., 2020), and
      control of dynamical systems (Antonelo et al., 2024).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Neural Operators. Neural operators (Lu et al., 2021; Kontolati et al., 2024) combine various basis
163   transforms (e.g., Fourier, multipole kernel, wavelet (Gupta et al., 2021)) with neural networks to
164   accelerate PDE solvers in diverse applications. For example, Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) and
165   its variants (Tran et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2022; Ashiqur Rahman et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b)
166   learn parameters in the Fourier domain for turbulence simulation. Especially, geo-FNO (Li et al.,
167   2024a) maps the irregular domain into an uniform mesh with a specific geometric Fourier transform
168   to fit irregular domains. However, they all follow the assumptions of periodicity and time-invariance
169   property, making them fail in complex boundaries.
170   Transformer Methods. As another paradigm, Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) and its “x-former”
171   family (Jiang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024) have also been utilized to solve complex PDEs. Since the
172   attention mechanism results in higher complexity, many researchers are trying to alleviate this issue
173   through various means. For example, linear attention mechanism (Li et al., 2023a; Hao et al., 2023)
174   is a well-known method to address this limitation. Although the above methods alleviate the need for
175   specific domain expertise, they all share the same limitations: instability in long-range prediction and
176   weak generalization ability.
177   Graph Methods. Abundant works about Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Liu et al., 2020; Gao
178   et al., 2021; 2022; Horie & Mitsume, 2024) and geometric learning (Bronstein et al., 2017; Hajij
179   et al., 2020; 2022; Horie & Mitsume, 2024) attempt to utilize customized substructures to generalize
180   message passing to more complex domains. For example, graph kernel methods (Anandkumar
181   et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020) try to learn the implicit or explicit embedding in Reproducing Kernel
182   Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) for identifying differential equations. (Belbute-Peres et al., 2020) considers
183
      solving the problem of predicting fluid flow using GNNs. Message Passing Simplicial Networks
      (MPSNs) (Bodnar et al., 2021) perform message passing on simplicial complexes (SCs). Most
184
      notably, Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) (Gilmer et al., 2017; 2020; Janny et al., 2023;
185
      Perera & Agrawal, 2024) are utilized to tackle these issues, which learn latent representations on
186   graphs via message passing mechanism. Especially, MeshGraphNets (Pfaff et al., 2021) and MP-PDE
187   solver (Brandstetter et al., 2022) are two representative examples. While powerful and expressive,
188   we still find that there are some problems with these methods: their expression ability is still not
189   strong enough, their prediction error is still somewhat high, and they still rely on a large amount of
190   data. Hence, we designed our model to address these three problems.

194   In this section, we illustrate how our method effectively learn the solution of spatiotemporal PDEs

      under various parameters (e.g., the initial or boundary conditions, constant or variable coefficients)

197   3.1     N ETWORK ARCHITECTURE
198
199   To enhance the performance of long-range prediction, we adapt the conventional “Encoder-Processor-
200   Decoder” framework in (Pfaff et al., 2021; Brandstetter et al., 2022) as the backbone of our method,
201   which is primarily designed to effectively learn the complex spatiotemporal dependencies on graphs.
202   As shown in Figure 2, our proposed method mainly consists of the Feature-Enhanced (FE) block
203
      and the Cell-embedded MPNN (CellMPNN) block. These two key components update features in a
      sequential process to achieve the cascaded enhancement effect, described as follows: (1) Updating
204
      node-edge-cell features with the CellMPNN block; (2) Enriching higher-order node features with
205
      the FE block; (3) Iteratively repeating the above two steps until the specified number of processor
206   layers is reached. The synergy of these two sequentially placed blocks in turn improves the model’s
207   representation learning capacity and generalization ability.
208

      GNNs due to excessive aggregation. Please see the ablation results on FE’s efficacy in Table 3. More
213   details are provided in Appendix Section C.3.

259   3.1.2    C ELL - EMBEDDED G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORK
260
      Generally, the traditional message passing (MP) mechanism can be regarded as a refinement on
261
      a discrete space, analogous to an interpolation operation, which implies that edges are essentially
262
      interpolated from nodes. A MP mechanism introducing the cell has potential to further enhance the
263   refinement of the discrete space (namely, secondary refinement), thereby reducing the magnitude of
264   discretization errors spatially and paving the way for its application in complex graph structures.
265
266   Definition 3 (Cell in Graph) Let G = (V, E) be a graph, where V is the set of nodes v and
267   E ⊆ V × V is the set of edges. A cell in G is a subset of nodes C ⊆ V , such that the nodes in C
268   form a complete subgraph (clique) or satisfy predefined structural relationships. In particular, a
269   k-cell Ck in a graph G contains k + 1 nodes, where ∀i, j ∈ Ck ,, (vi , vj ) ∈ E, representing various
      structures, such as node (k = 0), edge (k = 1), triangle (k = 2), tetrahedron (k = 3), and so on.

339   In our experiments, we mainly focus on predicting much longer time steps with lower error and

342   strategy (i.e., one-step forward, one-step backward) for all tasks. All experiments are run on one
343   NVIDIA A100 GPU. The Adaptive Moment Estimation (Adam) optimizer is utilized for the model
344   training. All models are trained by injecting random Gaussian noise of varying standard deviation

      Square Error (RMSE) loss function is utilized to optimize parameters θ in networks. Given the

354   We consider three different types of study cases: (1) the generalization test, (2) the feature-enhanced
355   effect, and (3) an ablation study. All our experiments revolve around the following questions: Can
356   our model generalize well? Can our model achieve lower error with small data?
357   Generalization test. We varied the initial input field, randomly sampled from a Gaussian distribution
358   with various means and standard deviations, in order to test the generalization ability. According
359   to the results in Figures 5 and 6, we found that CeGNN generalizes to different ICs robustly on all
360   datasets. It is evident that the performances of CeGNN and all baselines in the multi-step long-term
361   prediction vary significantly. However, the experiment results of FNO show that it performs relatively
362   poorly on all datasets except for the Burgers equation. FFNO outperforms FNO across all datasets.
363
      Unexpectedly, GeoFNO, which incorporates the IPHI technique, achieves the worst performance.
      Similarly, Transolver also underperforms. From the experimental results of these models, we can
364
      infer that these methods on small datasets, exhibit poor generalization ability, falling short compared
365
      to graph-based methods. All results in Table 1 demonstrate that all graph-based models have great
366   generalization ability. GATv2, the advanced variant of GAT, underperforms GAT on Burgers, FN, 2D
367   GS RD, and BS datasets, but outperforms GAT on 3D GS RD equation, yet both methods fall short
368   of MGN. Surprisingly, the performance of MP-PDE is mediocre. Although MP-PDE is trained by

528   5    C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we proposed an end-to-end graph-based framework (namely, CeGNN) to learn the
531
      complex spatiotemporal dynamics by utilizing the local information, CeGNN predicts future long-
      term unobserved states and addresses the over-smoothness problem in GNNs. Firstly, the learnable
532
      cell attribution in CellMPNN block captures the spatial dependency of regional features, upgrading
533
      the local aggregation scheme from the first order to a higher order. Secondly, the FE block enriches the
534   node features, maintaining strong representational power even after multiple rounds of aggregation.
535   The effectiveness of CeGNN has been proven through results on various datasets. Although CeGNN
536   achieves superior performance on extensive experiments, there are several directions for future work,
537   including that (1) pushing our model to learn on a finer mesh with more complex boundary conditions,
538   and (2) further exploring the potential of cell attribution to learn higher-order information in a more
539   refined way, rather than the rough handling in our article. We attempt to accomplish these goals in
      our future research work.

779   B    BACKGROUND : OOD TEST IN S PATIOTEMPORAL P REDICTION
780
781   In this part, we would like to clarify the generalization tests with different random ICs in spatiotem-
782
      poral dynamics are OOD. For a nonlinear PDE system, even if the ICs are IID, the spatiotemporal
      solution can be OOD, because of the following reasons.
783
784   Nonlinearity and Emergence of Complex Patterns. Nonlinear PDEs are characterized by their
785   ability to produce highly complex behavior over time. This nonlinearity can amplify small differences
786   in ICs, leading to the emergence of patterns or behaviors that are vastly different from what was
787   initially expected. Even if the ICs are IID, the interactions dictated by the nonlinear terms in the PDE
788
      can cause the solution to evolve in a way that is not reflective of the initial distribution. As a result,
      the system may exhibit behaviors that are not represented in the original distribution, leading to an
789
      OOD trajectory dataset. For example, in the Burgers and FN examples, the ICs are generated based
790
      on Gaussian distribution with different random seeds (e.g., IID); however, the corresponding solution
791   trajectories remain OOD judging from the histogram plots.
792
793   Chaotic Dynamics. Nonlinear PDEs may exhibit chaotic behavior. In these systems, small perturba-
794
      tions in ICs can lead to exponentially different solutions. Over time, this chaotic evolution can cause
      the solution to become highly sensitive to ICs (e.g., the 2D FN and 2D/3D GS RD test data examples
795
      shown in Figure 1b-d), resulting in a distribution that is very different from the IID distribution of
796
      ICs, yielding an OOD solution space.
797
798   Long-term Evolution. In many nonlinear PDEs, solutions tend to evolve toward certain stable
799   structures or steady states known as attractors. These attractors can be complex structures in the
800
      solution space. Over time, the solution might converge to or oscillate around these attractors,
      regardless of the IID nature of ICs (e.g., the Burgers, FN and GS RD examples in our paper). The
801
      distribution of solutions near these attractors can be very different from the IC IID distribution.
802
      Essentially, the system’s long-term behavior is determined more by the attractors rather than by ICs.
803
804   Spatialtemporal Correlations. The assumption of IID ICs implies no spatial/temporal correlations
805   initially. However, the evolving dynamics governed by PDEs can introduce correlations over time.
806
      These correlations can lead to a solution that has a distribution quite different from the original IID
      distribution. The emergence of such correlations indicates that the evolved solution is not just a
807
      simple extension of ICs, producing datasets with OOD.
808
809   Breaking of Statistical Assumptions. As the system evolves, the assumptions that justified the
      IID nature of ICs may no longer hold. The dynamics of the PDE can induce structures, patterns,


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      or dependencies that were not present in ICs. As a result, the statistical properties of the evolved
811   solution may diverge from those of ICs, leading to an OOD.
812
813   Hence, even though the ICs are IID, the resulting solution trajectories are OOD. This might be a
814
      little bit different from our understanding of IID/OOD datasets in common practices of NLP, CV,
      etc. In addition, we have indeed considered OOD ICs in our tests, e.g., the ICs of the 2D/3D GS
815
      RD examples are randomly placed square cube concentrations (1 or 2 square/cube blocks) as shown
816
      in Figure 1c-d. The resulting solution datasets are obviously OOD. Therefore, all the results of
817   generalizing to different ICs represent OOD tests. Given the same comparison test sets, our model
818   shows better generalization performance over other baselines.

895   C.3   I NSPIRATION OF F EATURE - ENHANCED (FE) B LOCK
896
      The process of FE block is inspired by interaction models in physics and mathematically hypothesized
897
      to capture nonlinear dependencies, enhancing the model’s representation power.
898
899   Hypothesis. The FE block’s hypothesis could be framed as: By capturing second-order interactions
900   between latent features and applying selective filtering, the model can better represent complex
901
      structures or relationships in the data.
902   Physical Analogy. In physics, the outer product and second-order terms are often used to model
903   interactions, such as stress tensors in mechanics or pairwise correlations in quantum mechanics. Here,
904   the module could draw an analogy to systems where interactions between individual components
905   (features) are crucial to the overall behavior.
906   Process Overview. In detail, it regards the node latent feature hi ∈ RD×1 as basis and builds a
907   higher-order tensor feature Hi ∈ RD×D via an outer-product operation, e.g., hi ⊗ hi . This process
908   in Algorithm 1 creates abundant second-order nonlinear terms to enrich the feature map. We then
909   use a mask operation with M ∈ RD×D to randomly sample these terms, filtering the appropriate
910   information by a learnable weight tensor W ∈ RD×D×D to enhance the model’s representation
911   capacity.

1178   D.3.5     D ISCUSSION WITH OTHER H IGHER - ORDER G RAPHS
1179
1180   In this part, we mainly focus on discussing the excellent works CCNNs in (Hajij et al., 2022) and
1181   MPSNs in (Bodnar et al., 2021). A summary of these models is shown in the following Table S16.
1182   Firstly, MGN achieves message passing through a two-level structure (edge → node). On this basis,
1183   CCNNs in (Hajij et al., 2022) leverages combinatorial complexes to achieve message passing through
1184   a three-level structure (cell → edge → node). Meanwhile, MPSNs in (Bodnar et al., 2021) performs
1185   message passing through a two-level structure (complexes → simplex) on various simplicial com-
1186   plexes (SCs), which primarily include one simplex (node) and four types of complexes with varying
1187   adjacent simplices (e.g., boundary adjacencies, co-boundary adjacencies, lower-adjacencies, and
       upper-adjacencies), to enhance feature distinguishability and thus improve classification performance.
