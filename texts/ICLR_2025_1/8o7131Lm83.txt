011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have emerged as powerful tools
014            for processing modalities beyond text by combining a visual encoder with Large
015
               Language Models (LLMs) to incorporate visual context. This integration, how-
               ever, leads to higher computational costs during LLM inference, specifically in the
016
               Prefill and Decoding stages. Existing MLLM acceleration methods primarily fo-
017
               cus on reducing the cost of long prefills caused by visual context, but this approach
018            has limitations: (1) From a latency perspective, it mainly benefits the prefill stage,
019            offering minimal improvements for decoding. (2) It does not guarantee output
020            distributions that are identical to those of the original MLLM. To ensure identical
021            output distribution while mitigating decoding latency, we focus on speculative de-
022            coding (SD)—an acceleration technique that uses a smaller draft model verified
023            by a larger model. Despite its importance for LLM acceleration, SD’s applica-
024            tion to MLLMs remains largely unexplored, even though decoding constitutes a
025            significant portion of MLLM inference latency. We investigate various drafting
026
               techniques—multimodal, text-only, image-pooling, and caption-based—for mul-
               timodal scenarios and analyze their integration with MLLMs. Building on these
027
               insights, we propose In-batch Ensemble Drafting (IbED), which combines prob-
028
               ability distributions from multiple drafting methods via batch inference during
029            the SD draft phase. This approach requires no additional model parameters, in-
030            curs minimal overhead, and significantly increases the likelihood of draft tokens
031            passing verification, thereby enhancing performance and robustness across diverse
032            input scenarios.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large Language models are rapidly advancing, and in particular, Multimodal Large Language Mod-
038   els (MLLMs) that can process various modalities beyond text are gaining significant attention (Ope-
039
      nAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Gemini Team Google: Anil et al., 2023). MLLMs share the charac-
      teristics of LLMs (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023), which include:
040
      (1) The Prefill Stage, involving parallel processing of the provided input context. (2) The Decod-
041
      ing Stage, where generation is performed through an autoregressive decoding method based on the
042   processed context. Specifically, decoding n tokens requires a total of n serial runs of the model. In
043   addition, MLLMs require an extra process before the decoding stage: (3) The Vision Encoder Stage,
044   where image inputs are converted into visual context tokens by embedding patches through a visual
045   encoder (Radford et al., 2021). Typically, each image yields several hundred visual context tokens.
046
      As a result, the computational cost of inference with MLLMs has significantly increased. To mit-
047
      igate this cost, various methodologies have been proposed to accelerate MLLMs by focusing on
048   reducing the number of visual tokens. These approaches include dynamically retaining only the
049   most important visual tokens based on attention sparsity (Shang et al., 2024), layer-wise pruning
050   of less significant visual tokens to enhance efficiency (Chen et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2024), and
051   reducing redundant key-value caches through consolidation and compression strategies (Liu et al.,
052   2024b; Wan et al., 2024). Despite effectively minimizing performance degradation from the original
053   model, these approaches have fundamental limitations: (1) From a latency perspective, reducing
      prefill length mainly benefits the prefill stage while offering negligible advantages for the decode

072   Recently, Speculative Decoding (SD) (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) has been rapidly
073   emerging in the field of LLMs. It accelerates language models while preserving the output distribu-
074   tion generated by the model, offering a quality-neutral advantage. Specifically, SD methods split the
075
      decoding process into two distinct stages: (1) a Draft Phase, where a small “draft” model sequen-
      tially creates low-cost tokens; and (2) a Verification Phase, where a large “target” model reviews
076
      these draft tokens in parallel. The efficiency comes from the insight that combining autoregres-
077
      sive decoding with a small model for drafting, followed by parallel verification with a large model,
078   reduces costs by avoiding the iterative process, compared to using the large model alone for au-
079   toregressive decoding. In the LLM field, various attempts have been made to enhance acceleration
080   through SD, such as performing knowledge distillation on the draft model (Zhou et al., 2024), gen-
081   erating multiple draft candidates to find a better draft (Sun et al., 2024b), or altering the verification
082   phase based on a tree structure (Miao et al., 2023b).
083
      However, to the best of our knowledge, research on Speculative Decoding for MLLMs has been
084   far less explored, with only one study (Gagrani et al., 2024) available. This paper is significant
085   as the first attempt to apply SD to MLLMs, demonstrating that a draft model with multimodality
086   processing capabilities can surprisingly accelerate the target MLLM even when it does not use the
087   image input. However, the paper did not factorize and analyze the time cost associated with choosing
088   each drafting method, and it also has limitations in that it is impossible to know in advance which
089   drafting method to choose when none shows consistent superiority, leaving these questions for future
090   work.
091   In this study, we present a comprehensive analysis aimed at elucidating the fundamental principles
092   of Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM) Speculative Decoding across diverse input sce-
093   narios. Based on extensive benchmarking, we primarily focus on a comparative analysis between
094   multimodal drafting and text-only drafting approaches.
095
      Secondly, we explore several key questions that arise during the drafting stage when applying SD
096   to MLLMs: Can a very small model effectively handle multimodality, which often results in long
097   context lengths during the Prefill Stage? Is it necessary for such a small draft model to process the
098   lengthy context derived from image inputs? And can effective drafting still occur if this long image
099   context is compressed or replaced by much shorter text?
100
      Lastly, we propose In-batch Ensemble Drafting (IbED). Based on our observation that different
101
      drafting approaches available in the SD for MLLM settings have unique advantages (Figure 5), this
102   method combines the probability distributions from these approaches by batch inference to decode
103   each draft token during the Draft Phase of SD (Figure 2). Unlike conventional ensembles, it requires
104   no additional model parameters, resulting in negligible cost. This approach significantly improves
105   the likelihood of draft tokens passing target model verification and enhances performance across
106   tasks and datasets, making it more robust. Furthermore, it can be effectively integrated with existing
107   MLLM acceleration techniques that focus on the Prefill stage and SD methods optimized for the
      verification phase.

129   MLLMs Frontier proprietary MLLMs (OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Gemini Team
130   Google: Anil et al., 2023) demonstrate state-of-the-art performance across multimodalities beyond
131   just text. Meanwhile, open-source models like the LLaVA series (Liu et al., 2023; 2024a; Li et al.,
132   2024b;a) and LLaMA 3.2 (Dubey et al., 2024) are also rapidly advancing. While various methods
133   exist for embedding image inputs (Yin et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024), one of the most prominent ap-
134   proaches, LLaVA, employs an off-the-shelf vision encoder (Radford et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2023)
135   and a trainable projector to convert its output into the visual tokens of an LLM.
136
137   Inference Acceleration for MLLMs To address the inefficiency of handling visual tokens from
138   images, several approaches have been proposed based on a common finding: only a sparse subset of
139   the hundreds of visual tokens is important, allowing for reduced computational cost with minimal
140   information loss. Shang et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2024b); Lin et al. (2024) dynamically prune
141
      significant visual tokens based on attention sparsity. Further focusing on reducing redundant key-
      value caches, (Liu et al., 2024b; Wan et al., 2024) retain key-value vectors by merging or discarding
142
      less critical caches during output generation. However, from a latency perspective, these approaches
143
      primarily benefit the prefill stage while providing negligible advantages for the decode stage.

147
      Speculative Decoding for LLMs Although forefront LLMs demonstrate revolutionary perfor-
148
      mance (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2024), deploying these large models is com-
149
      putationally intensive, posing significant challenges to serving efficiency. To improve the inference
150   process for large language models, various approaches have been proposed, ranging from algorith-
151   mic innovations to system optimizations (Miao et al., 2023a; Khoshnoodi et al., 2024).
152
153
      Recently, Speculative Decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) has gained significant
      attention for accelerating inference using a small draft model while preserving the model’s output
154
      distribution. To improve the drafting stage in SD, various efforts have been made, including gen-
155
      erating multiple draft candidates to select the best one (Sun et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024), and
156   finetuning the draft model with knowledge distillation (Zhou et al., 2024). On the other hand, some
157   research focuses on modifying the verification phase using a tree structure (Miao et al., 2023b). Ad-
158   ditionally, several studies address cases with exceptionally long prefill lengths (e.g., 100k), which
159   significantly affect decoding efficiency (Sun et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024a). These studies system-
160   atically varied prefill length and batch size, finding that with prefill lengths of 1k to 10k tokens and
161   low batch sizes, decoding speed is generally unaffected, which is typical of real-world multimodal
      input scenarios.

      3.1   T HEORETICAL L ATENCY OF T RANSFORMERS
175
176   Compute bounded vs Memory Bounded The latency bottlenecks in transformer models can
177   be categorized into two primary constraints: compute-boundedness and memory-boundedness.
178   Compute-bound operations are limited by processing speed, typically during matrix calculations
179   and attention mechanisms. Memory-bound scenarios arise when available memory becomes a lim-
180   iting factor, often due to large model sizes or long input sequences. Arithmetic intensity, the ratio
181   of computational operations to memory operations, bridges these concepts and influences overall
182
      efficiency. High arithmetic intensity operations tend to be compute-bound, while low intensity op-
      erations are often memory-bound. In transformers, this balance varies depending on the generation
183
      phase (i.e., prefill or decode), model architecture, hardware specifications, and other factors.
184
185
186
      Prefilling Since prefilling requires parallel computations for a large number of tokens, it is
      compute-bound, leading to significant increases in latency as the prefill length grows. In the case of
187
      MLLMs, the proportion of visual tokens within the prefill length is significantly large. Therefore,
188
      addressing the redundancy of visual tokens is essential for cost-efficient prefilling.
189
190
      Decoding Because only one token is processed at each step during decoding, the process is
191
      memory-bound. The memory access cost is divided between the model weights and the key-value
192   cache. Except for long contexts, model weights dominate this cost. Consequently, decoding latency
193   remains nearly constant regardless of context length. Similarly, parallel decoding with a small num-
194   ber of tokens—as in the verification stage of speculative decoding—or slightly increasing the batch
195   size from 1 has minimal impact on latency.

      Draftings: Multimodal and Text-only The multimodal drafting process is the same as the general
271   MLLM generation process. After concatenating text embeddings and image embeddings, which are
272   obtained by passing images through a vision encoder and projector, the prefill process is performed
273   in the language model. Then, tokens are decoded up to a predefined chunk length γ. In our setting,
274   each image is converted into an embedding of length 576 and γ = 5. In contrast, text-only drafting,
275   whose potential was first recognized in (Gagrani et al., 2024), eliminates the image input and relies
276   solely on textual data as input for the draft model. Its generation process then follows that of a stan-
277   dard LLM. All drafting is performed using greedy decoding with a batch size of 1. The maximum
278   number of newly generated tokens is fixed at 128. See Appendix C for details on the prompt used
279
      for each drafting.

      By adopting the perspective of LLM acceleration, we divide the generation process of MLLMs into
      vision token processing, prefill, and decoding stages to identify bottlenecks.

286   Target Model: Generation for the Whole Sequence We visualize the factorized generation time
287
      in Figure 3. The time taken in the vision encoder and prefill stages is proportional to the number of
      images. Since each image is converted into several hundred context tokens and processed through
288
      the prefill stage, images have a greater impact than text tokens. Decoding time is more variable than
289
      the previous two stages, as the number of decoded tokens depends on the input scenario and the
290   model’s learned distribution. We selected the TextVQA, Spot, and PororoSV datasets to represent
291   datasets containing 1, 2, and 5 images, respectively. Though maximum number of newly generated
292   tokens is fixed, the resulting number of decoded tokens for each dataset in average is 91.89, 116.52,
293   and 88.17, respectively. In conclusion, the latency induced by the decoding phase exceeds the
294   combined latency of the other two stages.
295

297   the vision encoder and prefill stages align with those of the target model. Multimodal drafting,

299   tokens, thereby incurring a higher prefill cost compared to text-only drafting, which operates with a
300   shorter text context. However, the absolute scale of this cost is very small and can be overshadowed
301   by the target model’s prefill time. As shown in Figure 9, the per-step latency for decoding tokens

      remains consistent up to a context length of 3K (equivalent to around five input images), indicating

352   In this section, we address several questions that arise during the drafting stage when applying SD

      7                       L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
502
503
      Integration with Acceleration Methods While our work focuses on a single draft candidate and
      single verification scheme to understand the fundamentals of multimodal speculative decoding, other
504
      approaches use multiple draft candidates (Yang et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2024) and multi-verification
505
      schemes with tree attention (Miao et al., 2023b). Our method is easily compatible with token tree
506   verification and could benefit from such integrations.
507
508
      Extending to additional Modalities Most MLLMs focus on text and image modalities, but recent
509   efforts are expanding to include other types, such as audio Fu et al. (2024). A lightweight Automatic
510   Speech Recognition (ASR) model could convert audio to text for integration into text-only drafting,
511   particularly since audio data often involves long context and high computational costs. This ap-
512   proach could also support ensemble drafting, potentially improving performance and robustness.

      8                       C ONCLUSION
516
517
      This paper provides a comprehensive analysis of MLLM speculative decoding, exploring and inte-
      grating drafting techniques for speculative decoding in multimodal scenarios, with a focus on the
518
      often-overlooked decoding stage of MLLM inference. We introduce In-batch Ensemble Drafting
519
      (IbED), which combines probability distributions from multiple drafting methods by batch inference
520   during speculative decoding, requiring no additional model parameters and adding negligible over-
521   head. This approach significantly improves block efficiency and robustness across diverse inputs.
522   Our work demonstrates that efficient acceleration of MLLMs is achievable without compromising
523   output fidelity, paving the way for practical and widespread applications of MLLMs.

758   The process for creating LLaVA-ft was divided into two stages: pre-training and instruction fine-
759   tuning (IFT). Pre-training focuses on training the projector while the parameters of the LLM and

761   to follow multimodal instructions. The vision encoder remains frozen throughout both stages. We
762   trained the draft model using datasets curated by the original author of Llava (Liu et al., 2023). For

      Single-Image vs Multi-Image In the LLaVA-1.5 model, each image is represented by 576 visual
782   tokens. Therefore, the proportion of visual tokens is significantly higher compared to text tokens,
783   and as the number of images increases, this proportion becomes even larger. Hence, it is important
784   to examine how the evaluation results vary based on the number of images. Consequently, we assess
785   the performance of speculative decoding across a range of images, from single-image datasets to
786   multi-image datasets.
787
788   Open-ended vs Closed-ended Although the prefilling stage is significantly more time-consuming
789   than a single decoding step, speculative decoding has been developed primarily for the decoding
790   stage rather than the prefill stage. Therefore, open-ended questions are better than closed-ended
791
      ones for generating sufficiently long outputs to evaluate the performance of speculative decoding.

854   We use the following system prompts for their respective tasks. The <image> token is used to
855   represent image data within a prompt. [QUESTION] and [CAPTION] are a placeholders denot-
856   ing information unique to each sample of a dataset. For text-only drafting, the <image> token is
857   replaced by the escape character \n. We experimented with several replacement methods: (1) tok-
858   enizing the <image> string into three tokens, and (2) retaining the special token <image> without
859   replacing it with an image embedding. Method (2) resulted in very poor block efficiency, but method
860   (1) showed comparable block efficiency. Our replacement approach is simple because it ensures that
861   the prompt length remains consistent before and after replacement.
