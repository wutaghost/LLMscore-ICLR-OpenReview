009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have become increasingly prevalent, raising con-
012            cerns about potential vulnerabilities and misuse. Effective red teaming methods
013            are crucial for improving AI safety, yet current approaches often require access
014            to model internals or rely on specific jailbreak techniques. We present TIARA
015            (Tokenizer-Independent Adversarial Red-teaming Approach), a novel method for
016
               automated red teaming of LLMs that advances the state-of-the-art in transfer-
               able adversarial attacks. Unlike previous token-level methods, TIARA eliminates
017
               constraints on gradient access and fixed tokenizer, enabling simultaneous attacks
018
               on multiple models with diverse architectures. By leveraging a combination of
019            teacher-forcing and auto-regressive loss functions with a multi-stage candidate
020            selection procedure, it achieves superior performance without relying on gradient
021            information or dedicated attacker models. TIARA attains an 82.9% attack success
022            rate on GPT-3.5 Turbo and 51.2% on Gemini Pro, surpassing previous transfer and
023            direct attacks on the HarmBench benchmark. We provide insights into adversarial
024            string length effects and present a qualitative analysis of discovered adversarial
025            techniques. This work contributes to AI safety by offering a robust, versatile tool
026            for identifying potential vulnerabilities in LLMs, facilitating the development of
027
               safer AI systems.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The rapid development of generative models (Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024) has signifi-
032   cantly accelerated the adoption of Artificial Intelligence (AI) technologies across various domains,
033   including education (Kasneci et al., 2023), customer service (Soni, 2023), finance (Li et al., 2023),
034   medicine (Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023), and software development (Chen et al., 2021). Large Lan-
035   guage Models (LLMs) have become key tools in these areas due to their impressive performance and
036   versatility (Brown et al., 2020). Despite these advancements, however, LLMs remain susceptible to
037   adversarial attacks, commonly called jailbreaks, which can manipulate these models into producing
038
      harmful or unintended content (Jin et al., 2024).
039   The potential misuse of LLMs poses significant risks, including the generation of malware (Bhatt
040   et al., 2023), dissemination of large-scale disinformation (Vykopal et al., 2023; Williams et al.,
041   2024), provision of inappropriate medical advice (Hager et al., 2024), and even assistance in design-
042   ing chemical and biological weapons (Gopal et al., 2023). Mitigating these risks while preserving
043   the utility of LLMs for benign tasks presents a critical challenge in AI safety and security.
044   Red teaming, a process of systematically probing AI systems to uncover vulnerabilities, has emerged
045   as a strong defense mechanism against such threats (Ganguli et al., 2022). While manual and semi-
046   automated red teaming approaches have shown promise, they often rely on prior knowledge of
047   jailbreak strategies or require specific model access, limiting their ability to discover novel vul-
048   nerabilities (Shen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). Moreover, existing automated methods, such as
049   token-level adversarial attacks, typically depend on access to model gradients and fixed tokenization
050
      schemes, constraining their flexibility and applicability to diverse model architectures (Zou et al.,
      2023).
051
052   To address these limitations, we introduce TIARA (Tokenizer-Independent Adversarial Red-teaming
053   Approach), a novel framework for generating transferable adversarial examples across diverse LLM
      architectures. As illustrated in Figure 1, TIARA leverages an ensemble of open-source models to

      2.1   T OKEN - LEVEL A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
096
097   Adversarial attacks in natural language processing have been extensively studied, particularly at
098   the token-level optimization (Alzantot et al., 2018; Ebrahimi et al., 2018). Early methods such as
099   HotFlip (Ebrahimi et al., 2018), UAT (Wallace et al., 2019), AutoPrompt (Shin et al., 2020), BertAt-
100   tack (Li et al., 2020), and GBDA (Guo et al., 2021) focused on generating constrained adversarial
101   examples for specific NLP tasks. More recent gradient-based approaches like PEZ (Wen et al.,
102   2023) and GCG (Zou et al., 2023) have extended these techniques to red teaming scenarios, aiming
103   to induce harmful outputs in LLMs.
104   While effective, these methods typically require direct access to model gradients and often operate
105   with a fixed tokenization scheme. This dependency limits their applicability to ensemble of diverse
106   models and constrains the exploration space for adversarial inputs. In contrast, TIARA eliminates
107   the need for gradient access and fixed tokenization for perturbation sampling, allowing for a more
      flexible and extensive exploration of the adversarial space.

      2.2   AUTOMATED R ED T EAMING
109
110   Recent years have seen the development of various automated red teaming approaches aimed at
111   discovering jailbreaks or adversarial inputs in LLMs (Wei et al., 2023). Methods such as PAIR (Chao
112   et al., 2023), TAP (Mehrotra et al., 2023), and AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024) rely on prompting attacker
113   LLMs or utilizing manually crafted jailbreak examples to expose harmful behavior. While these
114   techniques do not require direct model access, they often depend on prior knowledge of jailbreak
115   strategies, which limits their ability to uncover novel, fine-grained vulnerabilities.
116   TIARA addresses these limitations through automatic token-level optimization, enabling the discov-
117   ery of new vulnerabilities without relying on attacker LLMs or pre-existing jailbreak prompts.
118
119   2.3   T RANSFERABLE ATTACKS
120
121   The concept of transferable adversarial attacks has been extensively studied in computer vi-
122   sion (Dong et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2019; Gu et al., 2024), developing various strategies to improve
123
      attack transferability such as data augmentation, optimization techniques, loss objectives, and model
      component analysis. In natural language processing, transferability poses unique challenges due to
124
      the discrete nature of text and has been studied less extensively.
125
126   Yuan et al. (2021) explored the transferability of adversarial attacks in text classification using a
127   genetic algorithm to derive optimal model ensembles based on pairwise transferability rates. In
128   the context of LLM red teaming, GCG-T (Zou et al., 2023) attempts to increase transferability by
129
      attacking multiple models, but requires shared tokenizers, limiting the diversity of model ensembles.
      TAP-T (Mehrotra et al., 2023), which directly targeted GPT-4 using GPT-4 as a judge and Mixtral
130
      8×7B as an attacker, demonstrated good transferability to other models.
131
132   TIARA addresses the transferability challenge by adopting a loss objective technique that attacks a
133   diverse ensemble of models with different tokenization schemes. This novel approach enables the
134   discovery of highly transferable adversarial sequences, facilitating attacks on closed-source models
135
      using open-source model ensembles. Our results demonstrate that TIARA outperforms existing
      methods in terms of transferability and achieves superior performance compared to most direct
136
      attack methods.

      3.1   BACKGROUND AND N OTATION
147
148   Tokens and Vocabulary Tokens are the fundamental units of input and output for a language
149   model. They are derived from raw text through tokenization, which segments the text into discrete
150   units. These units can be words, subwords, or individual characters, depending on the specific
151   tokenization algorithm. The set of all possible tokens forms the tokenizer’s vocabulary V, which is
152   fixed and finite.
153   Let T : S → V ∗ denote the tokenizer function that maps a string s ∈ S to a sequence of tokens
154   x = (x1 , . . . , xn ) ∈ V ∗ , where V ∗ is the set of all finite sequences of elements from V.
155
156   Causal Language Model Definition We formally define a causal language model, coupled with
157   a tokenizer T , as a function LM that takes a sequence of input tokens and produces a sequence of
158   output vectors:
159                                          LM : V ∗ → (R|V| )∗                                    (1)
160
161   Given an input sequence of tokens x = (x1 , . . . , xn ) ∈ V ∗ , the language model outputs a sequence
      of vectors L = (ℓ1 , . . . , ℓn ), where each ℓi ∈ R|V| represents the un-normalized probabilities

163   corresponds to the logits for the token at position (i + 1).
164
165   In a causal language model, each token’s logits are predicted only by being conditioned on previous
166
      tokens. The probabilities for each possible next token are obtained by applying the softmax function
      to the corresponding logit vector. Therefore the probability of the token at position (i + 1) being
167

172   Token Generation Process For generation purposes, we assume a greedy sampling approach. Let
173   argmax : R|V| → V be the function that returns the token corresponding to the highest logit value.
174   The generation process can be described as follows:
175
            1. Initialize the input sequence x0 = (x1 , . . . , xn ).
176
177         2. For each step i = 0, 1, . . . until a stop condition is met:
178             (a) Compute Li = LM(xi ). Let ℓlast be the last vector in Li
179             (b) Compute xnext = argmax(ℓlast ).
180             (c) If xnext is a stop token or the maximum sequence length is reached, terminate.
181             (d) Otherwise, set xi+1 = xi ⊕ (xnext ), where ⊕ denotes sequence concatenation.

245   3.3    TIARA: T OKENIZER -I NDEPENDENT A DVERSARIAL R ED - TEAMING A PPROACH
246
247   Building upon the problem formulation described in the previous section, we introduce TIARA
248   (Tokenizer-Independent Adversarial Red-teaming Approach), a novel method for generating adver-
249   sarial inputs in the context of chat interfaces. TIARA is designed to find an adversarial suffix xi:j
250   that maximizes the probability of generating a target response y, as formulated in equation 7.
251   The key innovation is that TIARA decouples the process of perturbing an optimized string from the
252   loss computation. This independence enables the use of arbitrary perturbation generation methods,
253   significantly expanding the space of potential adversarial inputs. In particular, it allows using arbi-
254   trary tokenizers for sampling token-level perturbations. This is in stark contrast to gradient-based
255   methods such as GCG (Zou et al., 2023), which require using the same tokenizer as the source model
256   for generating token replacements.
257   The core algorithm of TIARA is summarized in Algorithm 1. It operates in several key stages:
258
259          1. Perturbation Generation: TIARA generates perturbations of the input string using an
260             arbitrary perturbation tokenizer Tp , which may differ from the source LLM’s default to-
261             kenizer. It retokenizes the input string with Tp and replaces single tokens with random
262             tokens sampled from a predefined allowed subset of Tp ’s vocabulary. Finally, it decodes
263             the perturbed token sequences back to strings using Tp−1 .
264          2. Loss Computation: For each perturbed string, TIARA computes a loss function based on
265             the source LLM(s). The specific loss functions are detailed in the next subsection.
266
             3. Iterative Optimization: The algorithm iteratively selects the best candidate based on the
267
                computed loss and continues the perturbation process.
268
269          4. Multi-Stage Candidate Selection (MSCS): After the optimization process, TIARA em-
                ploys a multi-stage selection procedure to identify the most effective adversarial strings.

      4.3   Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS OF A DVERSARIAL S TRINGS
453
454   To gain deeper insights into the nature of transferable adversarial strings, we conducted a qualitative
455   analysis of the strings generated by TIARA-T.
456
457
      Our analysis revealed six primary categories of techniques: (1) Formatting Manipulation, (2) In-
      struction and Context Manipulation, (3) Linguistic Obfuscation, (4) Role-Playing and Style Re-
458
      quests, (5) Technical Jargon Insertion, and (6) Command and Code Injection. Detailed descriptions
459
      and examples are provided in Appendix B.
460
461   Figure 3 illustrates the distribution of these techniques, with formatting and instruction/context ma-
462   nipulation being the most prevalent. Notably, TIARA independently identified known strategies like
463
      role-playing and instruction manipulation, while also uncovering less-studied techniques such as
      linguistic obfuscation and code injection. This analysis provides insights into shared vulnerabilities
464
      across current models.
465
466   Examples of transferable adversarial attacks with filtered model responses are provided in Ap-
467   pendix A. Raw results are included in supplementary materials to facilitate further research.
468   To understand the performance of TIARA-T across various types of harmful behavior, we analyzed
469   attack success rates across different semantic categories (Appendix C, Figure 6). This analysis
470   revealed varying defense priorities among closed-source models, with GPT-4 excelling in general
471   harm and harassment/bullying categories, while Gemini showing stronger defenses in misinforma-
472   tion and illegal activities.

531
532
      We introduced TIARA, a tokenizer-independent approach for generating transferable adversarial
533
      examples in large language models. TIARA outperforms existing methods in both single-model
534   direct attacks and multi-model transfer attacks across open-source and closed-source models. Its
535   effectiveness in uncovering diverse adversarial techniques and transferring attacks across model
536   architectures suggests shared vulnerabilities in LLM safety mechanisms. These findings highlight
537   the need for more robust defense strategies and raise questions about the security limitations of
538   current LLM architectures. As AI systems become more prevalent, methods like TIARA will be
539   crucial for identifying and addressing vulnerabilities, contributing to the development of safer AI
      technologies.

      E                                          L IMITATIONS
951
952
      While TIARA demonstrates significant advancements in generating transferable adversarial exam-
953
      ples for large language models, it is important to acknowledge several limitations of our approach:
954
955                                                  1. Computational Resources: The multi-stage candidate selection process and the need for
956                                                     multiple iterations to generate effective adversarial strings can be computationally inten-
957                                                     sive, especially when dealing with larger language models or when exploring a wide range
958                                                     of perturbations.
959
                                                     2. Model Logits Access: Although TIARA does not require gradient access, it still necessi-
960                                                     tates query access to the logits of the source models for optimization. This might limit its
961                                                     applicability in scenarios where such access is restricted or costly.
962
963
                                                     3. Generalization Across Tasks: Our current evaluation focuses primarily on jailbreaking
                                                        tasks. The effectiveness of TIARA in generating transferable adversarial examples for
964
                                                        other types of tasks or objectives remains to be explored.
965
966                                                  4. Defensive Measures: Our study does not extensively explore the effectiveness of TIARA
967                                                     against models equipped with advanced system-level defensive mechanisms. The perfor-
968
                                                        mance of TIARA against such models remains an open question.
969                                                  5. Interpretability: While we provide a qualitative analysis of the generated adversarial
970                                                     strings, a deeper understanding of why certain perturbations are more effective or transfer-
971                                                     able than others is still limited. Improving the interpretability of these adversarial examples
                                                        remains a challenge.

972
       Addressing these limitations presents opportunities for future research, including the development of
973    more efficient optimization techniques, exploration of TIARA’s effectiveness across a broader range
974    of tasks and model types, investigation of its performance against advanced defensive strategies,
975    and improvement of the interpretability of generated adversarial examples. Furthermore, continued
976    ethical considerations and responsible development practices will be crucial as this field of research
977    advances.
