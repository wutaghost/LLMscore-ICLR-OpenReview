009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The rapid advancement of video generation models has made it increasingly chal-
012            lenging to distinguish AI-generated videos from real ones. This issue underscores
013            the urgent need for effective AI-generated video detectors to prevent the dissem-
014            ination of false information through such videos. However, the development of
015            high-performance generative video detectors is currently impeded by the lack
016
               of large-scale, high-quality datasets specifically designed for generative video
               detection. To this end, we introduce GenVidBench, a challenging AI-generated
017
               video detection dataset with several key advantages: 1) Cross Source and Cross
018
               Generator: The cross-generation source mitigates the interference of video content
019            on the detection. The cross-generator ensures diversity in video attributes between
020            the training and test sets, preventing them from being overly similar. 2) State-of-
021            the-Art Video Generators: The dataset includes videos from 8 state-of-the-art AI
022            video generators, ensuring that it covers the latest advancements in the field of
023            video generation. 3) Rich Semantics: The videos in GenVidBench are analyzed
024            from multiple dimensions and classified into various semantic categories based
025            on their content. This classification ensures that the dataset is not only large but
026            also diverse, aiding in the development of more generalized and effective detec-
027
               tion models. We conduct a comprehensive evaluation of different advanced video
               generators and present a challenging setting. Additionally, we present rich experi-
028
               mental results including advanced video classification models as baselines. With
029
               the GenVidBench, researchers can efficiently develop and evaluate AI-generated
030            video detection models.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In recent years, video generation models like Sora have seen remarkable advancements (29; 5; 1),
036   leading to a significant enhancement in the quality of AI-generated videos. The line between these
037   realistic synthetic videos and real videos has become increasingly blurred, posing a variety of
038   challenges, such as the spread of misinformation, damage to personal and corporate reputations,
039
      and an escalation in cybersecurity threats (3; 27). To address these risks, there is an urgent demand
      for the development of sophisticated AI-generated video detectors that can accurately identify and
040
      differentiate between real and fake videos. However, there is still a lack of challenging datasets to
041
      develop and evaluate AI-generated video detectors. To this end, we present, GenVidBench, a rich and
042   challenging dataset for the development of AI-generated video detectors.
043
044   GenVidBench is an innovative dataset with cross-generation sources and cross-generators. It covers
045
      8 types of videos generated by state-of-the-art video generators, such as Mora, MuseV, SVD, Pika,
      ensuring that the generated videos are of exceptional quality. GenVidBench contains two real video
046
      sources: Vript and HD-VG-130M. To make the dataset challenging, we construct 2 video pairs, each
047
      with the same text prompt/image. Specifically, we generate prompts and images based on the real
048   video and use Image-to-Video (I2V) models and Text-to-Video (T2V) models to generate videos with
049   the same source. Videos with the same generation source have more similar attributes, which makes
050   it more difficult to distinguish real videos from fake videos. In GenVidBench, video pairs from the
051   same source as the real video are used to form the test set, and video pairs from other sources are used
052   to form the training set. Furthermore, the generators in the training set and the test set are different, so
053   as to avoid the case in which videos generated by the same generator have the same attribute. Based
      on the above analysis, the task of cross-sources and cross-generators is extremely challenging.

097   2.1   AI-G ENERATED C ONTENT D ETECTION DATASET
098
099   With the rapid developments of generative models, the requisite expertise and effort to generate
100   content has been reduced. This fact has led to a growing focus on discriminating real items from
101   AI-generated content and the construction of corresponding detection datasets. We categorize these
102   detection datasets into 3 parts: AI-generated images, deepfake videos, AI-generated videos.
103   AI-Generated Image Detection Dataset. As a result of blossoming of diffusion models, AI-
104   generated images also has become more realistic. GenImage (47) is a million-scale benchmark for
105   detecting AI-generated images, which contains generated image pairs based on ImageNet (10) using
106   various diffusion-based models and GAN-based models. Additionally, WildFake(16), ArtiFact (31),
107   and DiffusionDB (40) also demonstrate the potential to provide a more comprehensive benchmark
      for fake image detection.

      Deepfake Video Detection Dataset. There is a significant amount of research has focused on
109   detecting deepfake videos (19; 44; 45; 9; 17) based on Deepfake datasets such as Deepfake Detec-

114

116   generated video detection dataset that collects videos from 10 different generated models for training,
117   and videos from other 10 different generated models for testing. However, neither GVD nor GenVideo
118   has the original prompt or images, video pair, semantic label, and cross source settings, which is

122   same prompts extracted from real videos. As illustrated in Table 1, GVF contains prompts/images,

      video pairs, semantic labels. But it too small and only has 2.8k videos. The GVF does not have

124
      containing semantic labels and prompts/images used to generate videos. Furthermore, GenVidBench
125
      performs cross-source setting of the training set and the test set, which is extremely challenging for
126   fake video detection.

129
130   AI-generated videos have the potential to accelerate the dissemination of misinformation, prompting
131   significant concern. In the past, much of the research has concentrated on detecting videos with
132   synthetic faces (41). However, the content of the fake face video is single, which is greatly different
133   from the real-world scenario. Due to the lack of large-scale high-quality AI-generated video datasets,
134   there are few work on generate video detectors. Recent work on AI-generated video detection includes
135   AIGDet (3), DeCoF (27). AIGDet (3) captures the forensic traces with a two-branch spatio-temporal
136
      convolutional neural network to improve detection accuracy. DeCoF (27) is based on the principle of
      video frame consistency to eliminates the impact of spatial artifacts. Beyond the specialized models
137
      dedicated to the detection of generated videos, several video classification models have demonstrated
138
      remarkable performance on this task, including VideoSwin (26) and UniFormer V2 (22). In this paper,
139   we present the experimental results across various models on GenVidBench, which will provide a
140   good research foundation for developers in related fields.

228   3.2       C ONTENT A NALYSIS
229
230   To ensure the realism of the fake videos, we select the most state-of-the-art generators. MuseV (1),
231
      Mora (43), and VideoCrafter2 (8) are the newest generators, introduced in 2024. SVD (5) and
      Modelscope (37), proposed in November 2023 and August 2023, respectively, are also considered at
232
      the forefront of high-performance methods. Additionally, Pika (2), CogVideo (15), and Text2Video-
233
      Zero (20) are recognized for their excellent performance in video generation. To facilitate the
234   development of more generalized and effective detection models, we categorized the available
235   captions across multiple dimensions and extracted a balanced set of categories based on a primary
236   dimension. As depicted in Fig. 2, we initially divided captions into three key levels: objects, actions,
237   and locations, as these three elements can form a complete sentence or story with rich semantics.
238
      1) Objects. The main subject of the given video, determining the primary spatial content. 2) Actions.
239
      Reflect the temporal attributes of the video. For instance, categorizing the action as Static Postures
240   implies that the main subject of the video will not exhibit rapid movement. If the action is categorized
241   as Presenting and Displaying, the main subject may engage in walking around objects or performing
242   other bodily movements. 3) Locations. This indicates the complexity of the scenario. For example, a
243   Nature Landscape may have a clean background, whereas a Transportation scenario might include
244   intricate machinery in the background.
245
      For each level, taking the object level as an example, we sampled 10% of the entire caption set and
246   utilized Large Language Models (LLMs) to extract the subjects. We then aggregated these subjects
247   into more abstract categories. The final categories were constrained to no more than ten options to
248   construct a classification tree. Based on the classification tree, we can effortlessly classify captions
249   and obtain the definitive class-labels for each caption.
250
      We selected objects as our primary dimension for selecting and generating video pairs, ensuring
251
      that our benchmark is balanced and semantically rich in the spatial dimension. Fig. 3 illustrates the
252   distribution of various caption sets across multiple dimensions. Fig. 4 presents the distribution of the
253   entire benchmark. These figures demonstrate that our benchmark encompasses a vast array of data,
254   covering extensive category ranges in both spatial and temporal aspects.

      3.4   C ROSS -S OURCE AND C ROSS -G ENERATOR TASK
325
326   We first evaluate the performance of the detector when trained and tested on videos generated by
327   the same generator. Our evaluation utilizes the widely recognized VideoSwin-tiny model (26). The
328   proposed GenVidBench dataset can be divided into eight distinct subsets, each corresponding to
329   a specific generator. Within each subset, we further divide the data into training and testing sets.
330   As shown in Table 3, training and testing within each subset consistently yield accuracy exceeding
331   97.6%. Impressively, the VideoCrafter2 and Modelscope subsets achieve an exceptional accuracy
332
      of 99.9%. Drawing from this observation, detecting a video synthesized by a specific generator is
      relatively straightforward.
333
334   However, this approach is inherently generator-dependent and may fail to produce satisfactory
335   results when dealing with unknown generators. It can be observated that a substantial performance
336   degradation when training and testing are conducted using different generators. For instance, the
337   accuracy of VideoSwin-tiny drops to 54.66% when it is trained on Pika and tested on SVD. In
338
      practical scenarios, the identity of the generator is often undisclosed. In this study, our objective is to
      Effectively evaluate the generalization performance of detectors, specifically its ability to discern
339
      between real and fake videos without reliance on the specific generator’s identity. To achieve this,
340
      the cross-generator video classification task is introduced, aimed at test the detector’s robustness
341   and adaptability across a variety of video sources, thereby enhancing its applicability in diverse and
342   unpredictable real-world scenarios.

      In this paper, we introduce GenVidBench, an innovative dataset designed for the detection of AI-
534   generated videos, characterized by its cross-source and cross-generator settings. GenVidBench is the
535   first 100,000-level dataset that provides rich semantic content labels. Comprehensive experimental
536   results are presented to establish a solid foundation for researchers working on the development and
537   evaluation of AI-generated video detectors. Moreover, we conduct a detailed analysis of particularly
538   challenging cases, employing semantic content labels to identify and filter the most challenging
539   categories. The results from these difficult categories are also detailed, offering an expanded set of
      benchmarks to further assist researchers in various scenarios.
