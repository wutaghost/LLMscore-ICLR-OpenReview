011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Current methods for reconstructing training data from trained classifiers are re-
014
               stricted to very small models, limited training set sizes, and low-resolution images.
               Such restrictions hinder their applicability to real-world scenarios. In this paper,
015
               we present a novel approach enabling data reconstruction in realistic settings for
016
               models trained on high-resolution images. Our method adapts the reconstruction
017            scheme of Haim et al. (2022) to real-world scenarios – specifically, targeting mod-
018            els trained via transfer learning over image embeddings of large pre-trained models
019            like DINO-ViT and CLIP. Our work employs data reconstruction in the embed-
020            ding space rather than in the image space, showcasing its applicability beyond
021            visual data. Moreover, we introduce a novel clustering-based method to identify
022            good reconstructions from thousands of candidates. This significantly improves
023            on previous works that relied on knowledge of the training set to identify good
024            reconstructed images. Our findings shed light on a potential privacy risk for data
025
               leakage from models trained using transfer learning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028   Understanding when training data can be reconstructed from trained neural networks is an intriguing
029   question that attracted significant interest in recent years. Successful reconstruction of training sam-
030   ples has been demonstrated for both generative models (Carlini et al., 2021; 2023) and classification
031   settings (Haim et al., 2022). Exploring this question may help understand the extent to which neural
032
      networks memorize training data and their vulnerability to privacy attacks and data leakage.
033   Existing results on training data reconstruction from neural network classifiers focus on restricted and
034   unrealistic settings. These methods require very small training datasets, which strongly limit their
035   ability to generalize. Additionally, they are constrained to low-resolution images, such as CIFAR or
036   MNIST images, and simple models like multilayered perceptrons (MLPs) or small CNNs.
037   We aim to overcome these limitations in a transfer-learning setting. Transfer Learning leverages
038   knowledge gained from solving one problem to address a related problem, often by transferring
039   learned representations from large pre-trained models (known as Foundation Models) to tasks with
040   limited training data. In the context of deep learning, transfer learning is commonly implemented by
041   fine-tuning the final layers of pre-trained models or training small MLPs on their output embeddings,
042   known as deep features (Oquab et al., 2014). This approach often achieves high generalization even
043   for learning tasks with small training sets, while also requiring less computing power. Thus, transfer
044
      learning is very common in practice.
045   In this work, we demonstrate reconstruction of training samples in more realistic scenarios. Specifi-
046   cally, we reconstruct high-resolution images from models that achieve good test performance, within
047   a transfer learning framework. Our approach involves training an MLP on the embeddings of
048   common pre-trained transformer-based foundation models, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or
049   DINO-ViT (Caron et al., 2021) (see Fig. 1). Our findings have implications for privacy, particularly
050
      when transfer learning is being used on sensitive training data, such as medical data. Consequently,
      preventing data leakage in transfer learning necessitates the development of appropriate defenses.
051
052   Additionally, our work addresses a key limitation of prior reconstruction works: their reliance on
053   training images for identifying good reconstructions from thousands of candidates. While this
      approach demonstrated that training images are embedded within the model’s parameters, it’s

084   Data Reconstruction Attacks. Reconstruction attacks attempt to recover the data samples on
085   which a model is trained, posing a serious threat to privacy. Earlier examples of such attacks include
086   activation maximization (model-inversion) (Fredrikson et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2019), although they
087   are limited to only a few samples per class or assume knowledge of all-but-one sample (Balle et al.,
088   2022). Reconstruction in a federated learning setup (Zhu et al., 2019; He et al., 2019; Hitaj et al., 2017;
089   Geiping et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2022) where the attacker assumes knowledge
090   of samples’ gradients. Other works studied reconstruction attacks on generative models like LLMs
091   (Carlini et al., 2019; 2021; Nasr et al., 2023) and diffusion-based image generators (Somepalli et al.,
092
      2022; Carlini et al., 2023). Our work is based on the reconstruction method from Haim et al. (2022),
      which relies only on knowledge of the parameters of the trained model, and is based on theoretical
093
      results of the implicit bias in neural networks (Lyu & Li, 2019; Ji & Telgarsky, 2020). This work was
094
      generalized to multi-class setting (Buzaglo et al., 2023) and to the NTK regime (Loo et al., 2023).
095
096
097   Transfer Learning. Deep transfer learning, a common technique across various tasks (see surveys:
098   (Tan et al., 2018; Zhuang et al., 2020; Iman et al., 2023)), leverages pre-trained models from large
099
      datasets to address challenges faced by smaller, domain-specific datasets (e.g., in the medical do-
      main (Kim et al., 2022)). While convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have been the go-to approach
100
      for transfer learning (Oquab et al., 2014; Yosinski et al., 2014), recent research suggests that vision
101
      transformers (ViTs) may offer stronger learned representations for downstream tasks (Caron et al.,
102   2021; He et al., 2022). For example, ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), pre-trained on ImageNet (Deng
103   et al., 2009), provides robust general visual features. Beyond supervised pre-training, self-supervised
104   learning methods like DINO (Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023) learn informative image rep-
105   resentations without requiring labeled data, allowing the model to capture strong image features
106   suitable for further downstream tasks. Additionally, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) has emerged as a
107   powerful technique, leveraging a massive dataset of paired text-image examples and contrastive loss
      to learn semantically meaningful image representations.

128   3        M ETHOD
129
130   Our goal is to reconstruct training samples (images) from a classifier that was trained on the corre-
131   sponding embedding vectors of a large pre-trained model in a transfer learning manner.
132
      The classifier training is illustrated in Fig. 2a. Formally, given an image classification task Ds =
133   {(si , yi )}ni=1 ⊆ Rds × {1, . . . , C}, where ds is the dimension of the input image1 and C is the
134   number of classes, we employ a large pre-trained model F : Rds → Rd (e.g., DINO) to transfer each
135   image si to its corresponding deep feature embedding xi = F(si ) ∈ Rd , where d is the dimension of
136   the feature embedding vector (the output of F). We then train a model ϕ(·, θ) : Rd → RC to classify
137   the embedding dataset Dx = (xi , yi )ni=1 ⊆ Rd × {1, . . . , C}, where θ ∈ Rp is a vectorization of the
138   trained parameters. Typically, ϕ is a single hidden-layer multilayer perceptron (MLP). Also note that
139   F is kept fixed during the training of ϕ. The overall trained image classifier is ϕ(F(s)).
140   Our reconstruction approach is illustrated in Fig. 2b and presented in detail below. Given the trained
141   classifier ϕ and the pre-trained model F, our goal is to reconstruct training samples si from the
142   training set Ds . The reconstruction scheme comprises two parts:
143
144       1. Reconstructing embedding vectors from the training set of the classifier ϕ.
145       2. Mapping the reconstructed embedding vectors back into the image domain. Namely, computing
146          F −1 (e.g., by “inverting” the pre-trained model F).
147
148   3.1        R ECONSTRUCTING E MBEDDING V ECTORS FROM ϕ
149
150   Given a classifier ϕ : Rd → Rc trained on an embedding training-set Dx = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 , we apply
151   the reconstruction scheme of (Haim et al., 2022; Buzaglo et al., 2023) to obtain {x̂i }m i=1 , which
152   are m “candidates” for reconstructed samples from the original training set Dx . In this section we
153
      provide a brief overview of the reconstruction scheme of (Haim et al., 2022; Buzaglo et al., 2023)
      (for elaboration see Sec. 3 in Haim et al. (2022)):

184   3.2   M APPING E MBEDDING V ECTORS x̂i TO THE I MAGE D OMAIN ŝi
185
186   Unlike previous works on data reconstruction that directly reconstruct training images, our method
187
      reconstructs embedding vectors. To evaluate the effectiveness of our reconstructed candidates, we
      must first map them back to the image domain. In this section we describe how we achieve training
188
      images from image-embeddings. Namely, given reconstructed image-embeddings x̂i , our goal is to
189
      compute ŝi = F −1 (x̂i ). To this end we apply model-inversion methods and in particular, the method
190   proposed in Tumanyan et al. (2022).
191
192   Given a vector x̂i (an output candidate from the reconstruction optimization in Section 3.1), we search
193
      for an input image ŝi to F that maximizes the cosine-similarity between F(ŝi ) and x̂i . Formally:
194
195                                                                      F(ν) · x̂i
                                       ŝi = F −1 (x̂i ) = argmax                    .                            (3)
196                                                                 ν   ∥F(ν)∥∥x̂i ∥
197
198   We further apply a Deep-Image Prior (DIP) (Ulyanov et al., 2018) to the input of F. I.e., ν = g(z)
199   where g is a CNN U-Net model applied to a random input z sampled from Gaussian distribution.
200
      The only optimization variables of the inversion method are the parameters of g. See Appendix B.3
      further explanation and full implementation details.
201
202   By applying model-inversion to DINO embeddings, Tumanyan et al. (2022) demonstrated that the
203   [CLS] token contains a significant amount of information about the visual appearance of the original
204   image from which it was computed. Even though their work was done in the context of image to
205   image style transfer, their results inspired our work and motivated us to apply their approach in the
206
      context of reconstructing training image samples.
207   A significant modification to Tumanyan et al. (2022) in our work is by employing a cosine-
208   similarity loss instead of their proposed MSE loss. We find that using MSE loss (i.e., F −1 (x̂) =
209   argminν ∥F(ν) − x̂i ∥2 ) is highly sensitive to even small changes in the scale of x̂. The scales of x̂
210   can be very different from the unknown x = F(s). Using cosine similarity alleviates this issue while
211   simultaneously achieving similar quality for the inverted image result (see also Appendix A.1).
212   The above-mentioned technique is used for mapping embeddings to images for most transformers
213   that we consider in our work. However, this technique did not produce good results when applied to
214   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). Therefore, to map CLIP image embeddings to the image domain, we
215   employ a diffusion-based generator conditioned on CLIP embeddings by Lee et al. (2022) (similar in
      spirit to the more popular DALL-E2 (Ramesh et al., 2022); see also Appendix A.7 and Appendix B.4).

      3.3     S ELECTING R ECONSTRUCTED E MBEDDINGS TO BE I NVERTED
217
218   Applying the model-inversion described in Section 3.2 to a large pretrained model is computationally
219   intensive. Inverting a single embedding vector takes about 30 minutes on an NVIDIA-V100-32GB
220   GPU. Therefore, it is not feasible to invert all 25k-50k output candidates of Section 3.1.
221
      To determine which reconstructed candidates to invert, we pair each training embedding xi with its
222   nearest reconstructed candidate x̂j (measured by cosine similarity) and select the top 40 vectors with
223   the highest similarity for inversion. This approach proves effective in practice, yielding images with
224   high visual similarity to the original training images, as demonstrated in the results (e.g., Fig. 1).
225
      In practice, the original training embeddings are not available (and inverting all candidates is compu-
226
      tationally prohibitive). In Section 5 we introduce a novel method to identify good reconstructions
227
      without relying on either ground-truth embeddings or exhaustive inversion.

336   solution of the maximum-margin implies that reconstruction is only possible for samples lying on

      In this section, we introduce a clustering-based approach to identify “good” reconstructed candidates
404   without relying on the original training data. This is an important step towards an effective privacy
405   attack. Previous works (Haim et al., 2022; Buzaglo et al., 2023; Loo et al., 2023), including Section 3.3
406   in this work, rely on the original training images for demonstrating that training images are embedded
407   in the model parameters. However, it is not applicable to real-world privacy attacks, as attackers don’t
408   have access to the original training data.
409
      When directly reconstructing training images, this issue can be mitigated by manual inspection of
410   the thousands of output image candidates – a time-consuming but feasible approach. However, this
411   approach is irrelevant when reconstructing image embeddings. The reconstructed embeddings must
412   first be inverted into images, which is computationally expensive (inverting a single vector takes

            481
                    In this work, we made design choices when training the models to align with realistic transfer learning
            482
                    practices. However, some choices led to better reconstruction results than others, revealing limitations
            483
                    of our method. Here we discuss these limitations, their impact on our results, and identify potential
            484     future research directions:

       • The quality of reconstructed images relies heavily on the backbone model (F) and the inversion
487      method (Section 3.2). Fig. 9 shows the inverted original embeddings F −1 (F(s)) (blue), which
488      are the “best” we can achieve (independent of our reconstruction method). It also shows how some
489      backbones are easier or harder to invert, as evident in the difference between F −1 (F(s)) (blue)
490      and the original image s (red), for different F’s. It can also be seen that the inverted reconstructed
491      embeddings F −1 (x̂) are sometimes more similar to F −1 (F(s)) than to s, which may hint that the
492      challenge lies in the inversion more than in the reconstruction part. Certainly, improving model
493      inversion techniques is likely to enhance the quality of reconstructed samples.
494    • CNN-based backbones F (e.g., VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014)) proved more challenging
495      for inversion than Transformer-based backbones F. Since Transformers are also being more
496      frequently employed due to their better generalization, we decided to focus our work on them and
497      leave CNN-based backbones for future research.
498    • Linear-Probing (i.e. train a single linear layer ϕ) is common practice in transfer learning. However,
499      current reconstruction methods, including ours, struggle to perform well on linear models. This
500      may stem from the small number of parameters in linear models (see Appendix A.5).
501
       • We use weight-decay regularization since it is a fairly common regularization technique. However,
502      the reconstruction method is known to perform much worse on models that are trained without
503      it (Buzaglo et al., 2023).
504
       • We experimented with an embedding vector that is a concatenation of [CLS] and the average of
505
         all other output tokens (of F). This had minor effect on the results, see Appendix A.6 for details.
506
507    • Fine-tuning the entire model F (together with ϕ) is resource-intensive and less common compared
508      to training only on fixed embedding vectors. While we followed the latter approach, full fine-tuning
509
         can be an interesting future direction.
510
511   7    C ONCLUSION
512
513   In this work, we extend previous data reconstruction methods to more realistic transfer learning
514   scenarios. We demonstrate that certain models trained with transfer learning are susceptible to training
515   set reconstruction attack. Given the widespread adoption of transfer learning, our results highlight
516   potential privacy risks. By examining the limitations of our approach, we identify simple mitigation
517   strategies, such as employing smaller or even linear models, increasing training set size or training
518   without weight-decay regularization. However, some of these mitigation (removing regularization or
519
      using smaller models) may also come at a cost to the generalization of the model. Furthermore, these
      techniques may not be effective against future advanced reconstruction attacks. We aim for our work
520
      to inspire the development of new defense methods and emphasize the importance of research on
521
      data reconstruction attacks and defenses.

         A.3            C OSINE -S IMILARITY AS A PROXY FOR G OOD R ECONSTRUCTIONS
865
866      Determining whether a candidate is a reconstruction of an original sample is a difficult challenge.
867      It is highly unlikely that the two will be exactly the same, which is why selecting an appropriate
868      similarity measure is important. Unfortunately, there is no ”best” metric for comparing two images,
869      which is a known open problem in computer vision (see e.g., Zhang et al. (2018)).
870      The task becomes even more complex when dealing with reconstructed embedding vectors, as in
871      our work. Given our computational constraints, we must choose wisely which embeddings to invert,
872      adding another layer of complexity to the comparison process. Throughout our work, we frequently
873      employ cosine similarity as a metric for evaluating embedding similarities. However, whether this
874      metric accurately reflects visual quality is unclear. We set out to explore this question empirically.
875
         Previous work on data reconstruction (Haim et al., 2022; Buzaglo et al., 2023) directly reconstruct
876      training images, allowing us to a directly compare between cosine similarity and image similarity
877      measures. Both works established SSIM (Wang et al., 2004) as a good visual metric for CIFAR10
878      images (see Appendix A.2 in Buzaglo et al. (2023)), and defined SSIM> 0.4 as a good threshold
879      for declaring two images as sufficiently similar. In fact these works also use cosine similarity to
880      find nearest neighbors between candidates and training images when normalized to [−1, 1], and only
881      after shifting the images to [0,1] they use SSIM. Which means that they also implicitly assume that
882      cosine-similarity is a good proxy for visual similarity.
883      In Fig. 13, we quantitatively evaluate this assumption using reconstructed images from a CIFAR10-
884      trained model (as in Haim et al. (2022)). The left panel is for simply reproducing the results of Haim
885      et al. (2022). By looking at both middle and right panel, we see that CosSim=0.75 is a good cut-off
886      for determining ”good” reconstruction, since from this point there is a good correlation between the
887      two metrics. This is also the reason that we use this threshold for determining good reconstruction in
888      other experiments in the paper.
889      By further observing the middle panel: if SSIM> 0.4 (horizontal black line) is considered a criterion
890      for good image reconstruction, then cosine similarity (CosSim> 0.75, vertical black line) may
891      overlook some potentially high-quality reconstructions, indicating room for further improvement in
892      our approach.

      We set to explore how ”reconstructability” (i.e., how many good samples we can reconstruct from) de-
919   pends on the number of training iterations. We note from empirical observations that reconstructability
920   certainly improves with longer training, which should not be surprising because according to theory,
921   the model converges more to the KKT solution.
922

923
      or not? To define ”overtrained”, we observe how the generalization accuracy increases. Obviously,
924
      the longer we train, the better the model will be reconstructable. But is it reconstructable before the
925   generalization accuracy saturates? (Or do we have to keep training long after that?)

928

929

930
      2000 iterations, where the test accuracy (even though quite high in the beginning), keeps increasing
931   by more than 1.5% until 10k iterations.
932
933   The implication is that reconstructability is achieved in a reasonable time (measured by the time taken
934

      method as a viable privacy threat to models trained in a similar fashion.

1158

1160

1163   training embeddings, and invert them to images by computing F −1 (x) (this is the same pipeline as
1164   we use for the reconstructed candidates of our approach). The results of this approach can be seen
1165   in Fig. 21.

1173   L, with a given y ∈ {−1, 1} (instead of comparing the output of F to a given embedding vector).
1174   Note that as described in Appendix B.3, the only optimization variables are the parameters of the
1175   Deep-Image Prior g (denoted as ν in the equation above). The results of this approach are shown

1459
1460   We follow similar methodology to Tumanyan et al. (2022), using their code9 and changing the recon-
1461   struction loss from MSE to Cosine-Similarity as mentioned in Section 3.2, and specifically Eq. (3)
1462   (see justifications in Appendix A.1).
1463
       The Deep-Image Prior model g is a fully convolutional U-Net model (Ronneberger et al., 2015)
1464   (initialized at random with the default pytorch implementation). The optimization is run for 20,000
1465   iterations, where at each iteration the input to g is z+r, where z is initialized from z ∼ N (0ds , Ids ×ds )
1466   and kept fixed throughout the optimization, and r is sampled at each iteration as follows:
1467
        iteration i < 10,000:                      r ∼ N (0ds , 10 · Ids ×ds )
1468
        iteration 10,000 < i ≤ 15,000:             r ∼ N (0ds , 2 · Ids ×ds )
1469
        iteration 15,000 < i ≤ 20,000:             r ∼ N (0ds , 0.5 · Ids ×ds )
1470
1471   Note that the input to g is of the same size of the input to F, which is simply and image of dimensions
1472   ds = c × h × w. At each iteration, the output of g is fed to F, and the output of F (which is an
1473
       embedding vector of dimension d = 768), is compared using cosine-similarity to the embedding
       vector that we want to invert. At the end of the step, the parameters of g are changed to increase the
1474
       cosine similarity between the embeddings.

       B.4     I NVERSION WITH U N CLIP
1477
1478   While the method in Appendix B.3 is used for ViT, DINO and DINOv2, for CLIP we use a different
1479   method to invert, which is by using the UnCLIP implementation of Lee et al. (2022). Unlike the
1480   inversion in Appendix B.3 that uses cosine-similarity, with UnCLIP, the embeddings (that go into
1481   to UnCLIP decoder) should have the right scale. For each CLIP embedding of a training image (x),
1482   we search for its nearest neighbour candidate (x̂) with cosine similarity, but before feeding x̂ into
1483   the UnCLIP decoder, we re-scale it to have the same scale as x, so that the input to the decoder is
1484   in fact (∥x∥/∥x̂∥)x̂. Unfortunately we could not resolve this reliance on the training set (as is also
1485
       done in previous reconstruction works, and discussed in the main paper), but we believe this may be
       mitigated by computing and using general statistics of the training set (instead of specific training
1486
       samples). We leave this direction for future research.
