009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, there has been rapid development in 3D generation models, opening
012            up new possibilities for applications such as simulating the dynamic movements
013            of 3D objects and customizing their behaviors. However, current 3D generative
014            models tend to focus only on surface features such as color and shape, neglecting
015            the inherent physical properties that govern the behavior of objects in the real
016
               world. To accurately simulate physics-aligned dynamics, it is essential to pre-
               dict the physical properties of materials and incorporate them into the behavior
017
               prediction process. Nonetheless, predicting the diverse materials of real-world
018
               objects is still challenging due to the complex nature of their physical attributes. In
019            this paper, we propose Physics3D, a novel method for learning various physical
020            properties of 3D objects through a video diffusion model. Our approach involves
021            designing a highly generalizable physical simulation system based on a viscoelastic
022            material model, which enables us to simulate a wide range of materials with high-
023            fidelity capabilities. Moreover, we distill the physical priors from a video diffusion
024            model that contains more understanding of realistic object materials. Extensive
025            experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our method with both elastic and
026            plastic materials. Physics3D shows great potential for bridging the gap between
027
               the physical world and virtual neural space, providing a better integration and
               application of realistic physical principles in virtual environments. Project page:
028
               https://physics3d-3dgs.github.io

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In recent years, 3D computer vision has witnessed significant advancements, with researchers
034   focusing on reconstructing or generating 3D assets Mildenhall et al. (2021); Kerbl et al. (2023);
035   Tang et al. (2023); Poole et al. (2022); Hong et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024), and even delving into
036   the realm of 4D dynamics Ren et al. (2023); Ling et al. (2023). However, a common feature in
037   these works is the emphasis on color space, which can be difficult in modeling realistic interactive
038   dynamics without any physical priors, especially for applications in areas such as virtual/augmented
039
      reality and animation. Physics simulation is one of the most crucial methods to achieve a deeper
      understanding of the real world and enhance the effectiveness of interactive dynamics. Although
040
      conventional methods Stewart (2000); Felippa (2004); Kilian & Ochsendorf (2005) describe behavior
041
      using continuous physical dynamic equations based on body-fixed mesh, they are usually difficult
042   and time-consuming to generate complex 3D objects and suffer from highly nonlinear issues, large
043   deformations or fracture-prone physical phenomenaJiang et al. (2016).

      hall et al. (2021) and 3D Gaussian Splatting Kerbl et al. (2023)), some researchers Xie et al. (2023);

105   in incorporating temporal dynamics into the 3D modeling of dynamic objects and scenes. Various
106   explicit or hybrid representation techniques have demonstrated impressive outcomes, including planar

109   (2022). Recently, 3D Gaussian Splatting Kerbl et al. (2023) has revolutionized the representation via
110   its outstanding rendering efficiency and high-quality results. Efforts have been made to extend static
111   3D Gaussians into dynamic versions, yielding promising results. Dynamic 3D Gaussians Luiten et al.
112   (2023) refine per-frame Gaussian Splatting through dynamic regularizations and shared properties
113   such as size, color, and opacity. Similarly, the concept of 4D Gaussian Splatting Wu et al. (2023);
114   Yang et al. (2023) employs a deformation network to anticipate time-dependent positional, scaling,
115   and rotational deformations. In addition, DreamGaussian4D Ren et al. (2023) learns motion from
116   image-conditioned generated videos Blattmann et al. (2023a), enabling more controllable and diverse
117
      3D motion representations.
118   Viscoelastic materials. In the realm of computer graphics and animation, there has been significant
119   interest in accurately simulating the behavior of nonrigid objects and their interactions with physical
120   environments. Conventional elastic models Terzopoulos et al. (1987); Zong et al. (2023) predicated
121   on Hooke’s law Rychlewski (1984) are the cornerstone for simulating the deformation of objects.
122   These models are effective in representing materials that exhibit perfectly elastic behavior, returning
123
      to their original shape after the applied force is removed. However, real-world materials often exhibit
      more complex behaviors that cannot be captured by simple elastic models. The introduction of
124
      viscoelastic materials in computer graphics Terzopoulos & Fleischer (1988) has expanded the range
125
      of simulated material behaviors. Viscoelastic materials combine the characteristics of both viscous
126   fluids and elastic solids, leading to time-dependent deformations under constant stress, a phenomenon
127   known as creep Christensen (2003). Compared with elastic models, viscoelastic models offer a more
128   versatile framework for animating nonrigid objects. They can simulate the slow restoration of a
129   material’s shape after the cessation of force, as well as the permanent deformation that occurs due to
130   prolonged stress.
131
      Video generation models. With the emergence of models like Sora Brooks et al. (2024), the field
132   of video generation Villegas et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2022); Bar-Tal et al. (2024); Blattmann et al.
133   (2023c) has drawn significant attention. These powerful video models Kondratyuk et al. (2023);
134   Singer et al. (2022); Ho et al. (2022); Hong et al. (2022) are typically trained on extensive datasets of
135   high-quality video content. Sora Brooks et al. (2024) is capable of producing minute-long videos with
136   realistic motions and consistent viewpoints. Furthermore, some large-scale video models, such as
137   Sora Brooks et al. (2024), can even support physically plausible effects. Inspired by these capabilities,
138   we aim to distill the physical principles observed in videos and apply them to our static 3D objects,
139   thereby achieving more realistic and physically accurate results. In our framework, we choose Stable

      Video Diffusion Blattmann et al. (2023b) to optimize our physical properties.

144   Given a static representation through 3D Gaussians, our goal is to estimate the physical attributes of
145   each Gaussian particle and generate physics-plausible motions by organizing the interaction of force
146   and velocity among these particles. For pure-elastic models, these physical properties include mass
147   (m), Young’s modulus (E), and Poisson’s ratio (ν). Young’s modulus and Poisson’s ratio control the
148   dynamics of elastic objects. For example, with a fixed amount of external force applied, the system’s
149   higher Young’s modulus will have smaller deformation.
150   However, only modeling the property of elastic objects is inadequate for recovering diverse physics
151   with heterogeneous materials in real-world applications, which significantly limits the recent work
152   like PhysDreamer Zhang et al. (2024). For example, they usually suffer from complex mixed
153   materials, especially in scenarios of rapid deformation where viscosity emerges as a significant factor
154   in dynamics. Therefore, our key insight is to build a more comprehensive physics model that includes
155   additional parameters, notably viscosity, to enrich the descriptive capacity for real-world objects,
156   especially in inelastic scenarios.
157   To model viscoelastic stresses with physical fidelity, we explore continuum mechanics, where Lamé
158   constants (also referred to as Lamé coefficients or parameters), denoted by λ and µ, emerge as
159   pertinent material-related quantities within the strain-stress relationship, while viscosity coefficient
160   νv and νd governs the viscous dynamics. Consequently, our framework pivots towards the estimation
161   of the viscosity coefficient (νv and νd ) and the two Lamé parameters (λ, and µ). As for other physical
      attributes, we align with the conventional methods Zhang et al. (2024); Xie et al. (2023), where the

258   Our goal is to generate 3D dynamics for diverse materials, especially inelastic materials and composite
259   materials with more complex motion modeling. An intuitive solution is to capture these viscous
260   effects by directly adding a viscosity part following the current elastic pipeline as PhysDreamer Zhang
261   et al. (2024). However, such a simple series connection of components is prone to coupling of the two
262   dissipative behaviors (elastoplastic and viscoelastic), which increases the complexity of the model
263   simulation and disrupts the original elastic properties of the model.
264   In our paper, we instead design a novel parallel framework to avoid the coupling issue. We decompose
265   the material space into elastoplastic and an additional viscoelastic part, aiming to unify the simulation
266   of a wide range of materials. It showcases the flexibility of this framework, and by adding more
267   blocks into both the static and dynamical modeling, it can be expected to be widely useful for
268   more complicated scenes. To simulate the physical process with Gaussian particles, we employ a
269   particle-based method MLS-MPM Hu et al. (2018a) as our simulator, and we formalize the simulation
      process for a single sub-step as follows:

      4.3   P HYSICS - DRIVEN D ISTILLATION
325
326   Through iterations of the Material Point Method (MPM), we obtain a set of Gaussians evolving over
327   time t. The general practice is that in order to optimize physical properties for each 3D Gaussian, we
328   need high-quality 3D Gaussian models and corresponding motion ground truth. This ground truth
329   could come from various sources, such as single-view or multi-view real-captured videos, 4D videos,
330   or ideally, detailed 3D models with comprehensive physical property labels.
331   However, acquiring such datasets is currently challenging. Inspired by former works like Zhang
332   et al. (2024); Ren et al. (2023) which learn 3D dynamics from 2D videos, we intend to create 3D
333   dynamics that appear as realistic videos when given different initial external forces and rendered
334   from random angles. Such 3D dynamics can be specified as a differentiable video parameterization,
335   where a differentiable generator g (here indicates MPM processor and following Gaussian rasterizer)
336   transforms both physical parameters and Gaussian parameters θ to create a video x = g(θ). Therefore,
337   we extend the score distillation sampling (SDS) to iteratively optimize both 3D Gaussian parameters
338
      and physical parameters from the video diffusion model. To ensure consistency, the camera remains
      stationary as we capture and render each Gaussian to produce a reference image Itr . To supervise and
339
      optimize this process, we employ two optional models: image-to-video and text-to-video diffusion
340
      models. Then, we use SDS loss to guide our optimization process:

490
491   We conduct ablation study in Figure 6 to evaluate the efficacy of our physics modeling. Specifically,
492   we investigate the importance of elastoplastic and viscoelastic components from the model architec-
493   ture. We observe that removing either of the modules leads to a notable degradation in the realism of
494   the physical simulations. Particularly, the absence of the elastoplastic component results in a lack
495   of elasticity, making objects more susceptible to shape deformation and fluid-like behavior. On the
496
      other hand, the absence of the viscoelastic component leads to a deficiency in sustained damping and
      rebound effects, especially in scenarios with minimal external disturbances where energy dissipation

      occurs rapidly. These show the significance of both components of our model in capturing the

      dynamics of physical objects. Please refer to our Appendix B.2 for more ablations.

513   6     C ONCLUSION
514
515   In this paper, we present Physics3D, a novel framework to learn various physical properties of
516   3D objects from video diffusion model. Our method tackles the challenge of estimating diverse
517   material properties by incorporating two key components: elastoplastic and viscoelastic modules.
518   The elastoplastic component facilitates simulations of pure elasticity, while the viscoelastic module
519   introduces damping effects, crucial for capturing the behavior of materials exhibiting both elasticity
520   and viscosity. Furthermore, we leverage a video generation model to distill inherent physical priors,
521
      improving our understanding of realistic material characteristics. Extensive experiments show
      Physics3D is effective in creating high-fidelity and realistic 3D dynamics.
522
523   Limitations and Future Work. In complex environments with a lot of entangled objects, our method
524   requires manual intervention to assign the scope of movable objects and define the filling ranges for
525   objects, which is not efficient for more real applications. In the future, we aim to utilize the prior of
526   large segmentation models to solve the problem with more comprehensive physics system modeling.
527
      We believe that Physics3D takes a significant step to open up a wide range of applications from
      realistic simulations to interactive virtual experiences and will inspire more works in the future.

1059   Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) is a technique used in machine learning, particularly in the
1060   context of generative models, to improve sample quality and diversity. It addresses the challenge of
1061   generating high-quality samples from complex probability distributions, such as those learned by

1252   C.5    L EARNABLE INTERNAL FILLING
1253
1254   In Xie et al. (2023), it employs a static filling strategy where the filled particles inherit the parameters
1255   σp and Cp from their nearest Gaussian kernels. These parameters correspond to the Gaussian
1256   properties of the filled particles. However, this static filling strategy encounters challenges in dynamic
1257   situations, such as tearing or compression shown in Figure 10, where large internal regions are
1258   exposed to the outside. The issue arises because the internal particles are not well-initialized and
1259
       remain fixed during optimization.
1260   To address these challenges, we propose an iterative optimization strategy that refines both physical
1261   parameters and internal Gaussian parameters. This approach ensures that the distribution of these two
1262   components does not interfere with each other, thereby preventing mode collapse. In other words, one
1263   set of parameters can be optimized independently, avoiding "self-sacrifice" for the optimization of
1264   the other set. Please refer to the visual comparison of the original and learnable strategy in Figure 11.
1265
       Specifically, for each epoch in the iterative optimization, we first fix all physical parameters and focus
       on optimizing the internal filling Gaussian parameters (e.g., Gaussian center x ∈ R3 , an opacity value
1266
       α ∈ R, a color feature c ∈ R3 and so on) by Eq. 15. Subsequently, we fix the Gaussian parameters
1267
       and optimize the physical properties (e.g., Young’s modulus E, Poisson’s ratio ν , and so on) of each
1268   Gaussian by Eq. 14. This iterative process is designed to ensure thorough optimization and stability.
1269   Moreover, to enhance the generalizability, we choose a random view for each epoch. This ensures
1270   that our model is robust and performs well across different perspectives.
