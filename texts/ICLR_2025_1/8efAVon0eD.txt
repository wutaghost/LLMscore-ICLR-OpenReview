009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization is challenging because distribution
012            shifts come in many forms. A multitude of learning algorithms exist and each
013            can improve performance in specific OOD situations. We posit that much of the
014            challenge of OOD generalization lies in choosing the right algorithm for the right
015            dataset. However, such algorithm selection is often elusive under complex real-
016            world shifts. In this work, we formalize the task of algorithm selection for OOD
017            generalization and investigate whether it could be approached by learning.
018            We propose a solution, dubbed OOD-C HAMELEON that formulates the task as
019            a supervised classification over candidate algorithms. We construct a dataset of
020
               datasets to learn from, which represents diverse types, magnitudes and combi-
               nations of shifts (covariate shift, label shift, spurious correlations). We train the
021
               model to predict the relative performance of algorithms given a dataset’s charac-
022
               teristics. This enables a priori selection of the best learning strategy, i.e. without
023            training various models as needed with traditional model selection.
024
               Our experiments show that the adaptive selection outperforms any individual al-
025            gorithm and simple selection heuristics, on unseen datasets of controllable and
026            realistic image data. Inspecting the model shows that it learns non-trivial data/al-
027            gorithms interactions, and reveals the conditions for any one algorithm to surpass
028            another. This opens new avenues for (1) enhancing OOD generalization with ex-
029            isting algorithms instead of designing new ones, and (2) gaining insights into the
030            applicability of existing algorithms with respect to datasets’ properties.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The many faces of OOD generalization. Out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization refers to a
036   model’s ability to remain accurate when the distributions of the training and test data differ. “OOD”
037
      is a catch-all term since it encompasses many types of distribution shifts (Wiles et al., 2021; Ye
      et al., 2022; Nagarajan et al., 2021). In medical imaging for example (Oakden-Rayner et al., 2020),
038
      a model may have to process X-rays from various demographics (covariate shift), pathologies (label
039
      shift), and co-occurrences of patient attributes (spurious correlations). These types of shifts have
040   often been studied independently, and are often best addressed with different algorithms. However,
041   real data is often complicated by combinations of shifts of different types and magnitudes (Wiles
042   et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023) that interact in complex ways with the learning algorithms (Jiang
043   et al., 2023; Cabannes et al., 2023; Benoit et al., 2024).
044
045   The trade-offs of learning algorithms. OOD generalization is challenging because it is funda-
046   mentally underspecified (D’Amour et al., 2022; Teney et al., 2022). The training set alone is not
047   enough to inform about the nature of the shifts nor to constrain the behavior of the model on OOD
048   test data. This is why a multitude of learning algorithms exist, which rely on different assumptions
049   or side information (e.g. domain labels), from standard ERM (Vapnik, 2000) to simple interventions
050
      such as Resampling, GroupDRO (Sagawa et al., 2020a), and more-complex ones (Liu et al., 2023).
051   A well-known study by Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz (2020) showed that none of the methods, at the time
052   of their study, surpasses an ERM baseline across a collection of datasets. This is not surprising
053   though: each dataset exhibits different characteristics and types or magnitudes of shifts that call for
      different methods (Benoit et al., 2024). Numerous newer analyses indeed confirm that (at the time of

      We posit that OOD generalization can be improved if we knew which algorithm to apply in
076
      each situation. However, under complex real-world shifts, finding the right learning algorithm
077   without trial and error is elusive for human experts. We therefore investigate the possibility of
078   learning to predict a priori, for a new unseen dataset, the algorithm that will produce the most robust
079   model. This a priori prediction contrasts with traditional model selection that first requires training
080   multiple models and often relies on restrictive heuristics (Garg et al., 2022; Baek et al., 2023; Miller
081   et al., 2021; Teney et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). In a concurrent work, Bell et al. (2024) proposed
082   to select an algorithm to deal with spurious correlations based on past performance on benchmarks
083   most similar to the target data. In comparison, we target many types of shifts (spurious correlation,
084   covariate shift, label shift) and propose a learning-based approach instead of similarity heuristics.
085
086   OOD-C HAMELEON. We frame the algorithm selection as a classification over a set of candidate
087
      algorithms, and study it in a data-driven manner. We train an algorithm selector named OOD-
      C HAMELEON in a setup akin to meta-learning (Vanschoren, 2018; Öztürk et al., 2022) in two ways.
088
      (1) It is trained on a dataset of datasets that exemplify diverse types, magnitudes, and combinations
089
      of shifts. Concretely, we construct such a dataset of datasets by sampling from synthetic distribu-
090   tions or real datasets e.g. CelebA (Liu et al., 2015), in controlled ways to simulate various distri-
091   bution shifts. (2) We seek to learn to learn, or learn to apply a learning algorithm, by discovering
092   interactions between a dataset’s characteristics and the performance of candidate algorithms. We
093   evaluate the performance of various algorithms on the dataset of datasets, and we train the algorithm
094   selector with supervision to predict the relative performance of the algorithms given a statistical de-
095   scriptor of a dataset (see Figure 1). We investigate training objectives such as regression, multi-label
096   classification (Fürnkranz et al., 2008), and pairwise comparison (Bradley & Terry, 1952).
097   Our experiments start with a controllable setup to evaluate various design choices. We then push
098   the concept further with realistic image data. Results show that the system consistently selects
099   algorithms with significantly lower test error than any single candidate algorithm, on unseen datasets
100   with complex types of distribution shifts. We further verify that it achieves this by learning non-
101   trivial, non-linear data/algorithm interactions. More importantly, we show transfer across datasets
102   by training the selector on CelebA-derived datasets, then applying it to COCO (Lin et al., 2014). As
103   a byproduct, we demonstrate that the algorithm selector can reveal which dataset characteristics are
104
      important for any candidate algorithm to outperform another.
105   In summary, our findings open new avenues for improving OOD generalization by learning to
106   better apply existing algorithms, instead of designing new ones. Additionally, this line of research
107   can help understand the applicability of existing algorithms, especially under complex conditions
      such as combined distribution shifts that are difficult to study theoretically.

      Our contributions are summarized as follows.
109
      • We propose the new task of algorithm selection for OOD generalization in a standard setting
110
        where attribute labels of the training data are available (Section 2). We present a first proof of
111     concept that predicts the most suitable strategy to obtain a robust model on a given dataset.
112
113   • We describe a reusable workflow to construct a dataset of datasets that exemplifies a variety of
114
        distribution shifts in different types, magnitudes, and combinations. (Section 3.1).
115   • We propose a demonstrator of the viability of the task (Section 3): an algorithm selector trained
116     with supervised learning to predict the most suitable algorithm from a set of candidates.
117
      • We empirically verify that the proposed model predicts algorithms leading to high OOD accuracy,
118     by learning non-trivial data/algorithm interactions (Section 4). We also show that the model can
119     reveal properties of datasets that make algorithms more effective than others (Section 3.4).

124   2.1       I S THE S ELECTION OF THE B EST L EARNING A LGORITHM E VEN P OSSIBLE ?
125
126
      A universal solution to the selection of a learning algorithm cannot exist according to the no-free-
      lunch theorem (Wolpert & Macready, 1997)). The key here is to restrict ourselves to a distribution
127
      of distribution shifts that are likely in real-world data (Wiles et al., 2021; Goldblum et al., 2023). We
128
      consider the three major types of shifts (Yang et al., 2023): covariate shifts (CS), label shifts (LS),
129   and spurious correlations (SC). Formally, given a joint distribution P (X, Y ) over inputs X and
130   labels Y , the three types correspond to shifts of P (X), P (Y ) and P (Y |X). Each sample x ∈ X is
131   typically characterized by both a robust feature xc ∈ Xc that is reliably predictive, and an attribute
132   a ∈ A that is not. A covariate shift implies a variation of the attribute a such as a shift in the
133   background typically associated with a specific object in an image. A shift of spurious correlations
134   implies a variation of an attribute/label co-occurrences, which means a shift on P (Y |A) but not
135   P (Y |Xc ). Because of the co-occurrence, a model could learn to rely on parts of the input related
136   to the attribute. This model would become unreliable on data where the co-occurrence has shifted.
137
      Finally, to measure OOD performance, We use the standard worst-group accuracy (WGA) on test
      data. A group G ∈ Y × A refers to a unique attribute–label combination (Sagawa et al., 2020a). We
138
      focus on the most common setting in the study of OOD generalization (Yong et al., 2022) where the
139
      training data includes labels of a potentially-spurious attribute a. We will also evaluate the use of
140   pseudo-attribute labels obtained from a heuristic method in Appendix E.

      We now describe our solution to tackle OOD algorithm selection including three consecutive steps:
182
183
      1. Obtaining a collection of example datasets with a variety of distribution shifts to learn from.
184   2. Assembling the meta-dataset D. That is, training candidate algorithms on each dataset to get
185      the corresponding performance, and summarizing each dataset with some dataset descriptor f (·).
186   3. Training an algorithm selector on the meta-dataset to learn robust data/algorithm relations.
187   Each step poses special challenges which we address as follows.

279   3.3    DATASET D ESCRIPTORS : A C ASE S TUDY WITH C ONTROLLABLE E XPERIMENTS
280
281   Now that we focus on algorithm selector’s input – the vectorized datasets (i.e., dataset descriptors).
282
      The dataset descriptors should summarize properties relevant to the performance of the various
      algorithms. Recent work (Nagarajan et al., 2021; Hermann et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
283
      2022; Ye et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024) discovered various properties related to OOD performance
284
      that could serve as dataset descriptors. However, many are difficult or impossible to measure before
285   training, or work only under restricted conditions. Other works on learning model selection (Arango
286   et al., 2024; Öztürk et al., 2022) use simple descriptors (e.g., number of training samples, image
287   channels, number of classes, etc.) that clearly cannot predict OOD performance.
288
      We propose two categories of properties to include in dataset descriptors: (1) distribution shift
289
      characteristics and (2) data complexity characteristics. We hypothesize that the former includes
290
      the degrees of the distribution shifts (dsc , dls , dcs in Equation 2), and the availability2 r of the
291   spurious correlation (i.e., how easily the model relies on the spurious feature to make predictions).
292   And the latter includes the size of the training set n and the input dimensionality d (e.g. image
293   resolution in vision data).
294

296   thetic test case. We consider the binary classification from Figure 2. The input X’s distribution
297   follows the modified synthetic example from Sagawa et al. (2020b). The distribution of each group
298   is defined by their input x = [xc , xa ] ∈ R2d , with xc and xa of dimension d generated from Gaussian
299   distribution:

      We now push the concept further with realistic data to answer the following research questions.
396   (RQ1) Is the learned algorithm selection also effective with datasets of real images? (RQ2) Does
397   our algorithm selector, trained on the semi-synthetic datasets built from CelebA for example, trans-
398   fer to unseen datasets from another domain (e.g. COCO (Lin et al., 2014))? (RQ3) How is the
399   system affected by different dataset descriptors and different training variations of OOD tasks (lin-
400   ear probing vs. fine-tuning)? (RQ4) How complex are the learned data-algorithm interactions?
401
402   Experimental setup. The setup resembles that of Section 3.3 (see Appendix D for more details).
403   • Dataset of datasets. We generate 1,056 example datasets from CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) with
404     various sizes, types of shifts, etc. We simulate various “availabilities” of the spurious feature
405
        by using various annotations from CelebA, e.g. mouth slightly open as class label and
        wearing lipstick as spurious attribute (see Table 8).
406
407   • Dataset descriptors. We now estimate the availability
                                                           P       of
                                                                   Pspurious feature (rather than using
408     ground truth values as in Section 3.3) with r = y dy / a da , where dy and da are average
409     distances of the samples’ embeddings to the cluster center of labels or attributes, respectively.
410     Using estimated availabilities allows for generalizing to unseen availabilities, see Appendix D.1
411
        for the rationale and details.
412   • Obtaining OOD performance on the collection of datasets. We use a pre-trained ResNet18 (He
413     et al., 2015) or CLIP model (ViT-B/32) (Radford et al., 2021) to solve each OOD task and obtain
414     the “ground truth” performance of each algorithm. We do a linear probing or a fine-tuning (on
415     ResNet18 for the experiment of Table 5). In addition, we train for long enough (1000 epochs)
416
        to ensure convergence with the same hyperparameters in each run. The rationale is that no OOD
        validation data should be relied on for hyperparameter search, otherwise this OOD data could
417
        simply be used as additional training data to achieve OOD generalization.
418
419   • Evaluation. We create 264, 47 and 150 unseen datasets with CelebA, COCO, and Colored-
420     MNIST respectively. We use the {cat, dog} and {indoor, outdoor} images for COCO.
421
        These are classical datasets that we selected because others such as MetaShift (Liang & Zou,
        2022) are not large enough to create diverse resampled versions.

481   5     R ELATED W ORK
482
483   OOD generalization has been widely studied but several benchmark studies concluded that there
484   is no one-fits-all solution to distribution shifts (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2020; Wiles et al., 2021;
485   Nguyen et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2022; Liang & Zou, 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Bell et al., 2024). These
      meta-studies show that OOD algorithms perform differently in different situations. An algorithm


                                                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      only shines when its underlying assumptions are met. This is the motivation for our goal of adap-
487   tively using existing algorithms, and investigating whether this can be learned and automated.
488
489   Algorithm selection.        Model selection (Forster, 2000; Raschka, 2020) and algorithm selec-
490
      tion (Rice, 1976; Kerschke et al., 2019) are integral parts of any machine learning workflow. Re-
      garding OOD generalization, a series of works (Liu et al., 2024; Baek et al., 2023; Garg et al., 2022;
491
      Miller et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2023) use heuristics to estimate a models’ OOD performance on a
492
      target dataset when no labeled OOD data is available. These heuristics have one or several of these
493   downsides: (1) they can only be estimated after training, (2) they require (unlabeled) test data, and
494   (3) they work only under restricted conditions. In contrast, our work aims for a priori selection of
495   the best algorithm, i.e. before training on the target dataset.
496
      Concurrently to our work, Bell et al. (2024) identified a very similar motivation. They proposed to
497
      select among algorithms that deal with spurious correlations based on their performance on bench-
498
      marks most similar to the target dataset. The differences with our work are: (1) we use a learning
499   approach rather than a similarity heuristic, (2) our learning process relies on semi-synthetic data
500   rather than existing benchmarks, which helps cover a broader set of distribution shifts, and (3) we
501   also consider label and covariate shifts and their combinations, not only spurious correlations.
502
      Meta-learning. Our “learning-to-learn” process resembles meta-learning (Vilalta & Drissi, 2002;
503
      Hospedales et al., 2022) in that it carries over prior experience from historical tasks to future similar
504
      ones. Achille et al. (2019); Öztürk et al. (2022); Arango et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2023) learn to se-
505   lect among pretrained models for downstream tasks or for outlier detection Zhao et al. (2021). They
506   either first require training a model on the target dataset or they are not suited to OOD generalization.
507
508
      AutoML. Our work relates to AutoML which aims to identify ML workflows, often by trial-and-
      error (Hutter et al., 2019; He et al., 2021). Our work differs in its aim for a priori algorithm selection
509
      and its potential for unveiling datasets’ properties predictive of the applicability of algorithms.
510

      6    D ISCUSSION
513
514
515   This work explored a new avenue for improving OOD generalization by better using existing algo-
516   rithms instead of creating new ones. We formalized the task of OOD algorithm selection and took
517
      the first step to learn it. We treated it as a classification over candidate algorithms, learned with
      supervision from a dataset of datasets representing a variety of distribution shifts. The resulting
518
      system not only predicts low-test-error algorithms on unseen datasets, but also reveals key proper-
519
      ties of datasets that allow algorithms to outperform one another. More could be learned in the future
520   about existing algorithms e.g. by training the selector as an interpretable decision tree.
521
522
      Limitations and future work. The proposed solution served to verify the viability of the new
523
      task. Future work will expand this approach in several ways.
524
525
      • Candidate algorithms. Our proof of concept uses simple algorithms representative of several
        broad categories. They were selected because they are effective in a range of settings and some-
526
        times even superior to sophisticated alternatives Idrissi et al. (2022). The fact that our approach
527
        is effective with such simple algorithms is testament to its potential for better exploiting the
528     plethora of other existing algorithms. A direct future extension will include more algorithms and
529     their variations, e.g. by including some of their hyperparameters in the search space.
530
531   • Can we transfer the approach to real-world settings? As discussed in Section 2, we consider
532     a restricted but representative distribution of distribution shifts and learn the algorithm selector.
533
        To verify that most real-world scenarios fall into this distribution, the approach will be applied to
        real-world data (e.g. from WILDS, Koh et al. (2021)) and larger-scale models. This will evaluate
534
        the limits of transferability of the learned selector as started in Section 4.
535
536   • Are the proposed dataset descriptors optimal? The proposed dataset descriptors (Section 3.3)
537     are interpretable but (1) they may not capture all relevant data properties, (2) and they require the
538     knowledge of the spurious attribute (as many OOD generalization works do (Yong et al., 2022)).
539     A promising direction is to replace our dataset descriptors with learned representations of datasets
        e.g. with Set Transformers Lee et al. (2018) or Dataset2Vec Jomaa et al. (2021).

1068   E     A LGORITHM S ELECTION WITH E STIMATED DATASET D ESCRIPTORS
1069
1070   Here we study the scenarios where the information to compute dataset descriptors, such as the
1071   attribute for each sample (Liu et al., 2021), is not easily obtained at test time. Recent works (Liu
1072   et al., 2021; Kirichenko et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023; Pagliardini et al., 2022)
1073   for OOD generalization aim to eliminate the need for attribute annotation by either: (1) infer the
1074   attribute annotation and then use them to run algorithms that require attribute annotation, (2) run
1075   ERM on the training set assuming that the ERM learns the spurious feature, and then build invariant
1076
       classifier on top of the ERM classifier (e.g. fit a model that disagrees with the ERM).
1077   We leverage the above first line of research, i.e. inferring the attribute annotation and use them to
1078   compute the dataset descriptors on the target training set. Then, we can use OOD-C HAMELEON
1079   to select the suitable algorithms. We infer the attributes by clustering the embeddings from frozen
       backbones, following Sohoni et al. (2020); You et al. (2024). The intuition is that different attributes,


                                                           20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       such as cows in grass or desert, can be considered as ’subclasses’ or ’hidden stratifications’, and they
1081   are observed to be separable in the feature space of the deep models. Hence, for example we can
1082   infer which cow belongs to which environment, by clustering on the embeddings. In particular,
1083   the training samples are passed through the backbone of the OOD task (i.e. ResNet18 or CLIP in
1084   our case), and get the embeddings. For each semantic class, we cluster the corresponding samples
1085   with K-means and assign different attribute labels to different clusters. This gives each sample its
1086   inferred attribute annotation. We can then use these inferred attribute annotations to compute dataset
1087   descriptors, where they are used to compute the degree of spurious correlation dsc and covariate
1088   shift dcs , as well as the availability of spurious features r, see Appendix D.1 on how we compute the
1089
       availability.
1090   With the inferred dataset descriptors, we use the algorithm selector to predict suitable algorithms. In
1091   Tab 10, we show that the suitable algorithms are still predictable with estimated dataset descriptors.
1092   Interestingly, while having performance drops in most cases, using estimated dataset descriptors
1093   boosts the performance on COCO with ResNet18.
