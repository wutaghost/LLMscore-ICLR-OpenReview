009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in diffusion models have demonstrated exceptional capabilities in
012            image and video generation, further improving the effectiveness of 4D synthesis.
013            Existing 4D generation methods can generate high-quality 4D objects or scenes
014            based on user-friendly conditions, benefiting the gaming and video industries.
015            However, these methods struggle to synthesize significant object deformation of
016
               complex 4D transitions and interactions within scenes. To address this challenge,
               we propose T RANS 4D, a novel text-to-4D synthesis framework that enables re-
017
               alistic complex scene transitions. Specifically, we first use multi-modal large
018
               language models (MLLMs) to produce a physic-aware scene description for 4D
019            scene initialization and effective transition timing planning. Then we propose a
020            geometry-aware 4D transition network to realize a complex scene-level 4D transi-
021            tion based on the plan, which involves expressive geometrical object deformation.
022            Extensive experiments demonstrate that T RANS 4D consistently outperforms exist-
023            ing state-of-the-art methods in generating 4D scenes with accurate and high-quality
024            transitions, validating its effectiveness.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Recent diffusion model (DM) advances have revolutionized video and 3D synthesis. By harnessing
029
      the generative capability of DM, video generation methods (Liu et al., 2024b; Bao et al., 2024) have
030   achieved high-quality video production that meets commercial standards. DreamFusion (Poole et al.,
031   2023) introduced Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) to guide NeRF model optimization, marking a
032   significant breakthrough in high-fidelity 3D generation.
033
      Building on these remarkable breakthroughs, 4D generation methods have demonstrated impressive
034
      performance. These methods can be broadly categorized into three types: text-to-4D (Singer et al.,
035
      2023; Bahmani et al., 2024b; Zheng et al., 2024; Ling et al., 2024), single-image-to-4D (Zhao et al.,
036   2023; Zheng et al., 2024), and monocular-video-to-4D (Ren et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Yin et al.,
037   2023; Zeng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024a). Text-to-4D and Image-to-4D
038   methods (Yu et al., 2024; Bahmani et al., 2024b; Zheng et al., 2024) combine video and multi-view
039   generation models with SDS to synthesize 4D objects, though the motion remains limited due to
040   current constraints in video generation models. Monocular-video-to-4D methods (Jiang et al., 2024;
041   Wang et al., 2024a) utilize prior dynamics from video conditions to achieve high-quality 4D object
042   synthesis with large-scale and natural motion, constrained by the requirement for videos with clear
043   foreground subjects that are difficult to obtain. However, these methods primarily address local
044
      deformations of individual objects and fall short of generating complex 4D scenes that involve global
      interactions between multiple objects.
045
046   Rather than merely focusing on 4D object generation, text-to-4D methods like Comp4D (Xu et al.,
047   2024) and monocular-video-to-4D methods such as Dreamscene4D (Chu et al., 2024) have achieved
048   4D scene generation. These methods still use deformation networks to adjust local coordinates and
049   simulate movements of objects within 4D scenes, similar to 4D object generation methods. However,
050
      deformation networks are limited in handling significant object deformation in the 4D scene, which
      complicates the generation of 4D transitions with complex interactions, such as a missile transforming
051
      into an exploded cloud or a magician conjuring a dancer.
052
053   To address these challenges, we propose a text-to-4D method T RANS 4D, which leverages multimodal
      large language models (MLLMs) for geometry-aware 4D scene planning, and introduces a Transition

      2     BACKGROUND & P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
109
110   2.1   4D C ONTENT G ENERATION
111
112   Research on 4D content generation begins with reconstructing dynamic 3D representations based
113   on multi-view videos. Existing 4D reconstruction models (Pumarola et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024a;
114   Huang et al., 2024) achieve realistic 4D generation by extending 3D models such as NeRF and 3DGS.
115   However, obtaining multi-view videos for 4D synthesis is challenging. Recently, more researchers
116   have focused on 4D generation using simpler conditions, and these methods can be broadly divided
117
      into three categories: text-to-4D, image-to-4D, and monocular-video-to-4D. The text-to-4D (Singer
      et al., 2023; Bahmani et al., 2024b; Ling et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024) and image-to-4D (Zhao et al.,
118
      2023; Zheng et al., 2024) methods are the first to be explored by researchers, typically extending
119
      3D objects into 4D objects using SDS loss based on pretrained video DM. However, due to the
120   limitations of SDS loss based on video DM, the dynamics of these 4D objects often seem unrealistic.
121   Subsequently, some methods (Yin et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
122   2024b; Wang et al., 2024a) leverage monocular video as a condition to generate high-quality and
123   naturally dynamic 4D objects. Nevertheless, generating 4D scenes remains challenging for these
124   methods, as they often require monocular videos with clear foreground subjects, which are difficult
125   to obtain. The text-to-4D method (Xu et al., 2024; Bahmani et al., 2024a), and the monocular-video-
126   to-4D method (Chu et al., 2024), can generate 4D scenes, but they struggle with situations involving
127   geometrical 4D scene transitions. To address this, we propose T RANS 4D, which enables the stable
128
      and convenient generation of 4D scenes with physical 4D transitions.
129
130   2.2   G ENERATION WITH L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
131
      Inspired by the advancements in LLMs and MLLMs (Touvron et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; Lin
132   et al., 2023a; Hong et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2024), many works have leveraged these models to achieve
133   higher-quality generation. In image generation (Dong et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a; Hu et al.,
134   2024; Han et al., 2024; Berman & Peysakhovich, 2024) and image editing (Fu et al., 2024; Li et al.,
135   2024; Jin et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024b), LLMs are first utilized to enhance the
136   quality of output images. Thanks to the powerful planning abilities of LLMs, these image generation
137   and editing methods can handle more complex scenarios. Subsequently, with the research surge
138   sparked by Sora (Liu et al., 2024b), more and more video generation methods (Bao et al., 2024;
139   Wu et al., 2024c; Tian et al., 2024c; Maaz et al., 2024) and storytelling approaches (Soldan et al.,
140
      2021; Tian et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024c) have harnessed the impressive capabilities of LLMs to
      achieve coherent and realistic video synthesis, significantly contributing to the multimedia industry’s
141
      development. Furthermore, with advancements in text-to-3D techniques (Poole et al., 2023; Lin
142
      et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024b; Zeng et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024), some 3D (Sun et al., 2023;
143   Feng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024) and even 4D (Xu et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
144   2024a; Chu et al., 2024) generation methods now involve LLMs to produce high-fidelity 3D or 4D
145   outputs with complex geometrical structures based on simple conditions. However, simultaneously
146   planning temporal progression and spatial layout remains challenging for existing LLM and MLLM
147   methods, making generating highly complex 4D scenes difficult. In this work, we equip MLLMs with
148   enhanced capabilities for 4D planning, enabling more effective generation of complex 4D scenes.
149
150   2.3   T RANSITION G ENERATION
151
152   According to the current research landscape, video transition synthesis is less explored than the more
153
      popular text-to-video and image-to-video generation methods. However, this direction is crucial in
      generating complex scenes and long stories. Scene transitions link two consecutive periods smoothly
154
      through location, setting, or camera viewpoint changes. This seamless transition ensures the coherent
155
      progression of the scene or story. Before video scene transitions, related research primarily focused
156   on non-deep learning algorithms with fixed patterns, as well as Morphing (Wolberg, 1998; Shechtman
157   et al., 2010) that identify pixel-level similarities and generative models (Van Den Oord et al., 2017;
158   Gal et al., 2022) that leverage latent features of linear networks to achieve smooth and reliable
159   transitions. Recent works (Chen et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2024; Xing et al., 2024; Feng et al.,
160   2024; Zhang et al., 2024a) have advanced the field by enabling smooth and creative video transitions,
161   paving the way for the creation of story-level, long-form videos. In addition to the video transition,
      our work first involves the geometry-aware transition into the text-to-4D synthesis.

      3.2   P HYSICS - AWARE 4D T RANSITION P LANNING
217
218   To optimize the 4D scene effectively, it is crucial to plan the placement and trajectories of objects
219   within the generated scene based on the textual prompts y. This process includes determining
220   which objects to generate, as well as specifying their initial positions η, movement speeds v, initial
221   orientation angles ϕ, rotational speeds ϖ, and scene transition times Ttrans . Unlike Comp4D (Xu
222   et al., 2024), which only uses LLMs to predict simple trajectory functions for 4D synthesis, our
223   T RANS 4D method leverages MLLM vision-language priors and introduces a physics-aware prompt
224
      expansion and transition planning approach. This advancement facilitates more reliable and complex
      initialization of 4D scenes.
225
226
      Physics-aware Prompt Expansion and Transition Planning. The target of 4D planning is to
227
      derive spatial and temporal information from a given textual prompt. However, spatiotemporal data
228
      in a 4D scene are abstract and complex, making it difficult for LLMs or MLLMs to directly interpret
229   and generate accurate physics-aware 4D scene data from a simple textual prompt. To overcome
230   this challenge, we propose a physics-aware 4D prompt expansion and transition planning method.
231   First, the method applies physical principles to analyze the original prompt, deriving spatiotemporal
232   information and decomposing it into scene prompts {yi , i = 1, 2, ..., M }. These prompts guide the
233   creation of 3D objects within the scene. By utilizing both these prompts and the language-vision
234   priors of MLLM, we extend the original textual input into a comprehensive, physics-aware scene
235   description for the target 4D scene. This description provides specific details, including the placement
236   of objects, their movements, and rotations along the x, y, and z axes over time, as well as key events
237
      (e.g., changes in motion speed or the appearance and disappearance of objects). By converting this
      description into a specific data format, the desired 4D scene data is obtained. As illustrated in Fig 2(b),
238
      this method enables MLLM to generate detailed and physically plausible 4D scene data, including η,
239
      v, ϕ, ϖ, and Ttrans . The detailed reasoning prompts are provided in the Appendix.
240
241
      Initialization of 4D Scene. Based on the {yi , i = 1, 2, ..., M } obtained through the planning
242   method, we utilize SDS with text-to-image generation model (Ye et al., 2023) to guide basic 3DGS
243   models {Gi , i = 1, 2, ..., M } synthesis. Using the planning 4D scene data, We calculate the transfor-
244   mation function for any position within these 3DGS models at each time t as:
245
246
                                          x = R(ϕ + ϖ · t)xξ + η + v · t                                     (3)
247   where R denotes the rotation matrix, x represents the arbitrary position in the 3DGS model, and xξ is
248   the coordinate of x when the 3DGS model is at (0, 0, 0). By integrating {Gi , i = 1, 2, ..., M } with
249   the transformation function, we obtain an initial 4D scene.
250
      After obtaining the physics-aware planning, we use geometry-aware 4D transitions to effectively
251
      visualize the physical dynamics derived from this planning. In the next section, we detail how our
252   proposed transition network realizes these geometry-aware 4D transitions.

      3.3   G EOMETRY- AWARE 4D T RANSITION
255
256   By utilizing the initial 4D scene and the deformation network, we can achieve 4D scene synthesis
257   in certain scenarios through global object positioning and local dynamics. However, depending
258   exclusively on movement is limited, as it cannot support geometry-aware 4D transitions that involve
259   significant object deformation, such as the appearance or disappearance of objects in a 4D scene.
260   To overcome this limitation, we propose a geometry-aware Transition Network (TransNet), which is
261   a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with a Sigmoid activation function at the output layer. As shown
262   in Fig. 2(c), TransNet takes the position of the point cloud and the time t as inputs, and processes
263   them through several linear layers to produce an intermediate output. This intermediate output is then
264   scaled by a coefficient wtrans before inputting into the final Sigmoid function. The final output of
265   TransNet denotes as ptrans , which lies between 0 and 1 and serves as a reference for 4D transition.

269   Sigmoid activation function, and wtrans is a scaling coefficient, typically set to 10 or higher, to

286   produce 4D results with reliable dynamics, which incurs high computational costs. To efficiently

288

289
      3DGS models, allowing for increased point cloud counts with lower computational overhead.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
315
      Implementation Details. In this work, all experiments are conducted on four A100-SXM4-80GB
316
      GPUs. In Stage 1, we optimize for 5000 steps using the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) to obtain
317
      the 3DGS models. In Stage 2, we perform 4500 optimization steps to train the deformation network
318   and the Transition Network. During the refinement phase, we further optimize the 3DGS models for
319   objects that cannot be represented in high quality with only 20000 points (e.g., complex structures
320   like “volcano”). This refinement is performed over 4000 steps using the SDS loss. We ensure a fair
321   comparison by using the same models across all methods for both generation and supervision. For
322   any use of a text-to-image generation model, we use Stable Diffusion 2.1 (Rombach et al., 2022); for
323   any use of a multiview generation model, we use MVDream (Shi et al., 2024); and for any use of a
      text-to-video generation model, we use VideoCraft (Chen et al., 2024a).

      Efficiency and Quality of Refinement. When a 4D scene contains over 200,000 point clouds,
516
      directly supervising it with video SDS loss consumes 80GB or more GPU memory limit, while
517
      leading to suboptimal quality. In contrast, by separating the training process, we reduce memory
518   usage to around 50GB, almost halving the requirement, while significantly improving the quality
519   of the generated 4D scene. Specifically, we initially represent the 4D scene using minimal point
520   clouds while training the deformation and transition networks. Then, we apply a refinement process
521   to improve the quality of each 3DGS model by increasing the number of point clouds as needed.
522   This stepwise training manages memory efficiently while producing high-quality 4D scenes. As
523   demonstrated in Fig. 7, for massive 3D objects like “volcano erupting”, sparse point clouds cannot
524   represent them effectively. Hence, refining such 3D objects is essential. In conclusion, our training
525   strategy balances efficiency and quality, enabling the generation of high-quality 4D scenes with
526
      relatively limited computational resources.

528   5         C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
529
530   In this work, we propose T RANS 4D, a novel text-to-4D scene generation method that produces high-
531   quality 4D scenes involving complex object interactions and significant deformations. Specifically,
532   we introduce a Physics-aware 4D Transition Planning method, which enables MLLM to initialize
533   realistic 4D scenes with multiple interacting objects. To facilitate geometry-aware transitions in
534   the generated 4D scene, we design a Transition Network that dynamically determines whether each
535   point cloud in the 4D scene should appear or disappear, allowing our method to handle substantial
536
      object deformations naturally. Our experiments demonstrate that T RANS 4D consistently generates
      high-quality 4D scenes with complex interactions and smooth, geometry-aware transitions.
537
538   For future work, We will continue to improve the quality of multi-object interactions in 4D scenes,
539   which will help achieve more realistic 4D scene generation, and support the development of the video
      multimedia and gaming industries.
