009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Video processing is generally divided into two main categories: processing of the
012            entire video, which typically yields optimal classification outcomes, and real-time
013            processing, where the objective is to make a decision as promptly as possible. The
014            latter is often driven by the need to identify rapidly potential critical or dangerous
015            situations. These could include machine failure, traffic accidents, heart problems,
016            or dangerous behavior. Although the models dedicated to the processing of entire
017            videos are typically well-defined and clearly presented in the literature, this is
018
               not the case for online processing, where a plethora of hand-devised methods
               exist. To address this, we present PrAViC, a novel, unified, and theoretically-based
019
               adaptation framework for dealing with the online classification problem for video
020
               data. The initial phase of our study is to establish a robust mathematical foundation
021            for the theory of classification of sequential data, with the potential to make a
022            decision at an early stage. This allows us to construct a natural function that
023            encourages the model to return an outcome much faster. The subsequent phase is
024            to present a straightforward and readily implementable method for adapting offline
025            models to the online setting with recurrent operations. Finally, PrAViC is evaluated
026            through comparison with existing state-of-the-art offline and online models and
027            datasets, enabling the network to significantly reduce the time required to reach
028            classification decisions while maintaining, or even enhancing, accuracy.

      2    R ELATED WORKS
088
089
      While video-based 3D networks have been widely studied in offline settings (Bhola & Vishwakarma,
090   2024; Karim et al., 2024; Kaseris et al., 2024; Ming et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2019), developing online
091   models remains still a challenge. Such online models are able to real-time classification, enabling
092   applications such as emergency situations detection or medical diagnostics.
093
      In consideration of the video as a series of consecutive frames, each frame may be classified on an
094
      individual basis through the application of 2D CNN models (Kumar, 2019). This approach represents
095
      an online technique and demonstrates effectiveness in real-time classification, due to the limited
096   number of parameters involved. However, the absence of temporal information from the video
097   as a whole may present a limitation, potentially leading to misclassifications such as predicting
098   “sitting down” when the action involves transitioning from sitting to standing. Consequently, in order
099   to develop a model capable of processing online data in real-time, researchers frequently elect to
100   utilize 2D networks, complemented by supplementary mechanisms for the management of temporal
101   data (Chang & Huang, 2024; Shen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023).
102   For instance, the authors of (Xiao et al., 2023) employed a temporal shift module (TSM). Their
103   approach involves shifting a portion of the channels along the temporal dimension, thereby facilitating
104
      the capture of temporal relationships.
105   On the other hand, it has been demonstrated that 3D CNNs yield superior accuracy in video classifi-
106   cation tasks compared to 2D CNNs (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017). Consequently, novel approaches
107   have been devised that utilize distinct versions of 3D convolutional kernels. A number of studies
      have proposed the development of dedicated architectures for online 3D networks, including those


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      presented by Kim et al. (2024), Krenzer et al. (2023), Sapitri et al. (2023), and Yang & Dai (2023),
109   which are designed to operate in real-time.
110
111   Another commonly utilized approach is the combination of 3D CNN architectures with various long
112
      short-term memory (LSTM) models. For example, Lu et al. (2024) introduce a DNN that merges 3D
      DenseNet variants and BiLSTM. In turn, Chen et al. (2023) propose a combination of R2plus1D and
113
      ConvLSTM in a parallel module. The proposed network utilizes the attention mechanism to extract
114
      the features that require attention in the channel and the spatial axes.
115
116   An alternative approach to the problem of online video classification, as proposed by Üstek et al.
117   (2023), involves combining a vision transformer for human pose estimation with a CNN-BiLSTM
118
      network for spatio-temporal modelling within keypoint sequences. Similarly, attention mechanisms,
      in conjunction with transformer layers, have also been employed in (Huang et al., 2023).
119
120   The concept of converting various well-known resource-efficient 2D CNNs into 3D CNNs, as
121   proposed by Köpüklü et al. (2020), is the most closely related to our approach. In this context,
122   Köpüklü et al. (2022) investigate the potential of adapting 3D CNNs (particularly the 3D ResNet
123   family of models) for online video stream processing. Their approach involves the elimination of
124
      temporal downsampling and the utilization of a cache to store intermediate volumes of the architecture,
      which can then be accessed during inference. A comparable solution has also been proposed by
125
      Hedegaard & Iosifidis (2022), who advanced the concept of weight-compatible reformulation of 3D
126
      CNNs, designated as Continual 3D Convolutional Neural Networks (Co3D CNNs). Co3D CNNs
127   facilitate the processing of videos in a frame-by-frame manner, utilizing existing 3D CNN weights,
128   thereby obviating the necessity for further finetuning.

131   3    P ROBABILISTIC A DAPTATION F RAMEWORK FOR R EAL - TIME V IDEO
132        C LASSIFICATION (P R AV I C)
133
134   This section presents the details of the proposed PrAViC model (Probabilistic Adaptation Framework
135   for Real-time Video Classification). For the reader’s convenience, the problem of video classification
136   is divided into offline and online. In the offline case, where the entire video is available, classification
137
      can be performed by processing the entire video. In the online case, where consecutive images are
      obtained, the objective is to make a decision using only a partial subset of the potentially available
138
      frames. For the sake of simplicity, we limit our discussion to the case of a binary classification.
139
140
      Standard offline case We are given a video V = [V0 , . . . , Vn ], which is represented as a sequence
141
      of images Vi (frames). Then, the network ϕ : V → [0, 1] returns the (soft) probability that V belongs
142   to the positive class. The final decision is based on the threshold τ , where typically τ = 1/2. Thus if
143   ϕ(V ) ≥ τ , we conclude that the class of V is positive.
144
145
      An important observation is that given the knowledge what was the decision of the model for every
      threshold τ , we can compute the probability ϕ(V ).
146
147
      Proposition 3.1 We have
148
149                                  ϕ(V ) = Prob(ϕ(V ) ≤ τ : τ ∼ unif [0,1] ).                               (1)
150
151   The above formula can be interpreted as selecting a random threshold value τ from the interval [0, 1]
152   and calculating the probability of being below the threshold. This is useful as in the case of online
153   models, we have the natural definition of the threshold, and consequently it will allow to deduce the
154   probabilistic model behind.
155   The subsequent paragraph addresses the question of how the offline model can be applied to the
156   online procedure.
157
158   Online (early exit) procedure We describe the standard general setting for the online early exit
159   model. We assume that the frames arrive consecutively (on occasion, we permit them to arrive in
160   groups of, e.g., two, four, or eight frames). Thereafter, given a trained classification network ϕ (as
161   described in the previous paragraph), we fix a threshold τ ∈ [0, 1] and proceed with the following
      steps:

                   1. start with k = 0;
163
164                2. load Vk and compute pk = ϕ([V0 , . . . , Vk ]);
165                3. if pk ≥ τ , then return class 1 (positive) for V = [V0 , . . . , Vn ]; else put k = k + 1;
166
                   4. if k = n + 1, then stop the algorithm and return class 0 (negative); else return to step 2.
167
168   The objective of the aforementioned procedure is to allow making the decision of V being in a
169   positive class before loading all frames from V . It should be noted that if we operate in the offline
170   mode, where we have access to all data, the above algorithm can be constrained to computing
171   p = max{p1 , . . . , pk }, and then determining that the class of V is negative if p < τ , or positive
172   otherwise.
173
      The underlying concept of our proposed solution is that of a continuous probabilistic model for the
174   decision threshold of activity detection. This is discussed in detail in the following paragraph.
175
176

177
      only aware of the decision that has been made, but we lack the information necessary to calculate the
178   probability of the given outcome. Without this information, it is not possible to use BCE loss and
179   fine-tune the model. To address this issue, we apply the probabilistic concept from Proposition 3.1,
180   which allows us to calculate the soft probability of the decision, given the knowledge that the decision
181   was made for an arbitrary threshold. Then we get that the probability that V has the positive class in

      The function NE is defined as a normalization of Exit(V ) with respect to the number of frames. It

224   Loss function for PrAViC Now we will describe how to incorporate the NE function into the loss
225   function in a way that encourages the model to exit earlier. However, we do not want to stimulate
226   the model too much so as not to provoke wrong decisions. To do this, we set a parameter λ ∈ [0, 1]

228   make the decision early. Consequently, we define the objective for image V with class y ∈ {0, 1} as

236   model was trained by using it as the subscript in PrAViCλ .

241

244   4   A RCHITECTURE OF THE MODEL
245
246   This section presents the modifications and extensions to the CNN-3D architecture that form the basis
247
      of our proposed approach. They include specific changes to key layers, including the convolution
      and batch normalization layers, as well as a unique method for processing the network’s head. These
248
      modifications are designed to leverage the strengths of traditional CNNs while addressing specific
249
      challenges related to depth processing and feature extraction. The following paragraphs provide a
250   detailed breakdown of each component and its role in our approach.
251
252   Architecture for fine-tuning We outline the modifications made to the classic 3D CNN architecture
253   to adapt it to our approach. While most components of a standard CNN architecture remain unchanged,
254   we specifically alter the 3D convolution processing, batch normalization, and layer pooling. For
255   3D convolutions, we modify only those layers where the kernel size responsible for the depth (i.e.,
256   processing movie frames) is greater than 1. Our modification ensures that the kernels do not extend
257   to the last deep channel. To achieve this, we replicate the input boundary on the front side before
258   performing the multiplication operation, as illustrated in Fig. 4. For pooling layers, our modification
259   involves replicating only the first depth channel.
260   The next essential mechanism in CNN networks is batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015). It
261   involves calculating the mean and standard deviation for individual dimensions within mini-batches
262   and training gamma and beta parameters during network training. This process ensures consistent and
263   stable training across the network by maintaining the integrity of feature scaling and normalization,
264   which is crucial for effective spatiotemporal pattern learning. In our approach, we modify this
265   mechanism by statically determining the number of depth channels from which the statistics (mean
266
      and standard deviation) will be calculated. These statistics are then used to transform the remaining
      depth channels.
267
268   It is important to note that through these operations, each layer of our model only retains information
269   from the preceding depth channels. In addition, our approach has a unique property: when provided
      with inputs of the same video, one containing k frames and the other n frames (k < n, where the

347   5           E XPERIMENTS
348
349   In this section, the experimental setup is detailed and the results that validate the effectiveness of
350   the proposed approach are presented. Five datasets were employed for testing purposes, comprising
351   four publicly available datasets (UCF101 (Soomro et al., 2012), EgoGesture (Zhang et al., 2018),
352   Jester (Materzynska et al., 2019), and Kinetics-400 (Kay et al., 2017)), as well as one closed-access
353   dataset (Ultrasound). The experiments employed a variety of architectural approaches, which are
354   described in detail in the corresponding subsections. The numerical experiments were conducted
355   using two different types of GPUs: NVIDIA RTX 4090 and NVIDIA A100 40GB.
356
357   PrAViC vs. offline baselines In this paragraph, we undertake a comparative analysis of PrAViC
358   with two non-online baseline approaches, namely ResNet-3D-18 (R3D-18) (Tran et al., 2018) and
359   Separable-3D-CNN (S3D) (Xie et al., 2018). Experiments were conducted on the widely used video
360   benchmark dataset UCF101 (Soomro et al., 2012).
361   From each video clip, 16 consecutive frames were extracted, starting with a random one. The R3D-18
362   model was resized to a resolution of 128×171, with each frame randomly cropped to 112×112. In
363   contrast, the S3D model was resized to 128×256 resizing, with each frame randomly cropped to a
364   size of 224×224.
365
      Initially, the R3D-18 and S3D models underwent pre-training on UCF101. Subsequently, they were
366   modified in accordance with the specifications outlined in Sec. 4, with the objective of transforming
367   the offline models into online ones. In both cases, we employed SGD as the optimizer and cross-
368   entropy as the loss function. The learning rates were set to 0.0002 for both of the offline models and
369   to 0.002 and 0.0001 for the modified R3D-18 and S3D models, respectively. Lastly, a custom loss
370   function, as defined in Eq. (5), was applied with the intention of forcing the model to make an earlier
371   decision. The effectiveness of varying values for the λ parameter, starting with 0.1 and increasing to
372   1, was evaluated.
373   Fig. 6 presents the accuracy and exit time (normalized to the interval [0,1]) obtained by the PrAViCλ
374   modification (for the λ parameter varying from 0.1 up to 1) applied to the considered baselines,
375   namely R3D-18 and S3D. It is noteworthy that as λ increases, both the accuracy and the exit time
376   demonstrate a tendency to rise. Furthermore, our model ultimately attains a performance level that is
377   nearly equivalent to that of the underlying offline models, while exhibiting a superior normalized exit
      time.

      PrAViC vs. online models This paragraph presents a comparison between PrAViC and a collection
379   of state-of-the-art online models, including 3D-SqueezeNet, 3D-ShuffleNetV1, 3D-ShuffleNetV2,
380   and 3D-MobileNetV2 (Köpüklü et al., 2020). The models were trained on the EgoGesture (Zhang
381   et al., 2018) and Jester (Materzynska et al., 2019) datasets, which have been specifically designed for
382   the purpose of recognizing hand gestures from an egocentric perspective.
383
      In setting up these experiments, the learned model weights (see (Köpüklü et al., 2020)) were utilized
384
      on the data. Initially, the model heads were trained anew, followed by light retraining of the entire
385   model, with or without incorporation of an additional cost function to promote earlier detection. The
386   architectures employed in our approach were identical to those utilized in the baseline models, with
387   the requisite modifications detailed in Sec. 4. The models were trained using the SGD optimizer with
388   standard categorical cross-entropy loss. The momentum, damping, and weight decay were set to 0.9,
389   0.9, and 0.001, respectively. The network learning rate was initialized at 0.1, 0.05, and 0.01, then
390   decreased by a factor of 3 with a factor of 0.1 when the validation loss reached convergence.
391   During the training phase, input clips were selected from random time points within the video clip. If
392   the video was composed of a smaller number of frames than the specified input size, a loop padding

477   6    C ONCLUSIONS
478
479
      In this work, we propose PrAViC, a general framework for automatically modifying networks that
480   have been adapted for video processing to their online counterparts. The objective of PrAViC is to
481   identify potentially dangerous situations at the earliest possible stage. To achieve this, we introduce a
482   probabilistic theoretical model that underlies online data processing. We compute the mean expected
483   exit time and use it as a component of the loss function to encourage the model to make early
484   decisions. Furthermore, we propose a simple framework that translates offline models into online
485   counterparts. It is demonstrated that we can encourage the model to make decisions earlier without a
      significant decrease in accuracy.

486
      Limitations In PrAViC, standard convolutions with a time component are employed. While this
487   enables the utilization of pre-trained networks, it does not permit the comprehensive utilization of
488   the full frames of the video in the residual network. This may potentially result in a minor loss of
489   accuracy in comparison to the offline model.
490
491   Broader impact As the utilization of online video analysis becomes increasingly prevalent in both
492   medical and social contexts, our methodology can be employed as a straightforward instrument for
493   researchers engaged in applied video processing.
