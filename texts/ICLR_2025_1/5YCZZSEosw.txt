009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The current iterative development process for large language models (LLMs) is
012            heavily data-centric, relying on human researchers and engineers to manually an-
013            alyze model performance and determine what data to acquire for further training.
014            However, this human-supervised approach is costly and may fail to identify opti-
015            mal training signals. Its scalability is further limited as models become increas-
016
               ingly capable and may eventually exceed human intelligence. To address these
               issues, we propose an automated framework that enables models to autonomously
017
               discover and strategically acquire the most valuable training data to enhance their
018
               performance. It establishes a self-improving framework where models can invoke
019            APIs to crawl and/or generate tailored datasets from various resources and en-
020            vironments, and retrain themselves. The data selection decisions are shaped by
021            reinforcement feedback signals that reward performance gains while penalizing
022            computational overhead. This formulation incentivizes models to develop self-
023            knowledge about their strengths and areas for improvement in order to efficiently
024            select training data. Empirical results demonstrate that LLMs operating within
025            our framework are able to autonomously and strategically acquire valuable train-
026            ing data to enhance their performance across a variety of skills in 1,000 diverse
027
               in-house test tasks and three public benchmarks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) (Zhao et al., 2023) have seen remarkable progress in various do-
032   mains and tasks with extensive training on massive data. Currently, one of the most important
033
      aspects of developing LLMs is data engineering, which typically involves teams of researchers
      and engineers meticulously examining model outputs, identifying shortcomings, and then collecting
034
      and curating next-iteration training data from various sources to refine model performance (Achiam
035
      et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024). While this method has yielded impressive results,
036   it is not without inherent limitations. The human-driven nature of this process introduces poten-
037   tial biases and inefficiencies, as it relies heavily on the intuition and expertise of the development
038   team (Wang et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024). Furthermore, as these AI models continue to evolve in
039   complexity and capability, there is a growing concern that they may soon exceed human intelligence
040   in certain domains, which raises questions about the long-term viability and scalability of current
041   development practices (Burns et al., 2024).
042   In this work, we conceptualize and prototype an ACTIVE DATA S EARCH (ADS) framework that fa-
043   cilitates LLMs to automatically acquire training data from external environments for training them-
044   selves, without human supervision. In ADS, existing data collection and curation methods are
045   encapsulated into APIs and the LLMs themselves determine when and how they use these APIs for
046   data acquisition. The APIs may involve a wide range of data crawling, filtering, cleaning, refine-
047   ment, synthesis, and manual annotation techniques to obtain tailored training data. The data sources
048   are also varied, from raw texts extracted via web searches to documents sourced across various
049   platforms, as well as supervised demonstrations generated by AI assistants.
050   We achieve the above automatic process through development of an optimizer that generates these
051   APIs sequentially based on the target task. Specifically, the optimizer first analyzes the required
052   capabilities for successful task completion, then strategically invokes appropriate API calls to collect
053   training data for performance improvement. To facilitate the optimizer’s decision-making process in
      obtaining optimal training data, we propose a reinforcement learning strategy for optimizer training,

054
      leveraging feedback reward signals from the policy to maximize task performance while minimizing
055   computational costs by iterative rejection sampling (RS) (Bai et al., 2022a) and direct preference
056   optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024). This refinement process fosters the development of
057   self-knowledge regarding the model’s strengths and weaknesses, enabling more efficient training
058   data discovery and utilization. Compared to recent studies (Lozhkov et al., 2024; Wettig et al.,
059   2024; Zhou et al., 2024a) that focus on improving the quality of training data agnostically, a unique
060   characteristic of our framework is its model- and task-specific nature for data acquisition, enabling
061   more tailored and targeted performance enhancement.
062   In our experiments, we build on top of two popular open-source LLMs: Qwen-2-7B-Instruct (Yang
063   et al., 2024a) and Gemma-2-9B-Instruct (Riviere et al., 2024), and establish three distinct types
064   of APIs for knowledge acquisitions, utilization, and enhancement. Experimental results across
065   1,000 in-house test tasks with both reward model (RM) and GPT-4 evaluation demonstrate that
066   the LLM equipped with ADS presents consistent performance improvements, achieving a win rate
067   of more than 80% in RM judgment compared to the initial baseline. Moreover, in three public
068   benchmarks: AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024), Arena-Hard (Li et al., 2024), and MT-Bench
069
      (Zheng et al., 2024), ADS exhibits generalized performance enhancement, even enabling the rel-
      ative smaller 7B/9B LLMs to compete with the larger 72B/27B counterparts. It is worth noting
070
      that across tasks of varying categories and complexities, ADS typically improves performance in
071
      knowledge, reasoning, and challenging tasks. To sum up, we highlight our contributions as follows:
072
073          • We pioneer the idea of automating the data search process for training LLMs, a task cur-
074            rently handled by expert human efforts, making a further step towards fully automated
075            self-improving AI systems capable of continuous learning and adaptation.
076          • We propose the ACTIVE DATA S EARCH (ADS) framework, which encapsulates existing
077            data collection and synthesis methods into APIs, enabling LLMs to generate optimal API
078            calls for efficient data discovery through interactions with diverse environments.
079          • We showcase the effectiveness of ADS by conducting comprehensive experiments on 1,000
080            diverse in-house test tasks and three public benchmarks. Our results demonstrate that LLMs
081            equipped with ADS exhibit substantial performance improvements. Furthermore, we pro-
082            vide an in-depth analysis of the underlying factors contributing to the efficacy of ADS.

086   LLM as an Optimizer With the progressive advancement of LLMs, LLM-as-an-optimizer (i.e.,
087   maximizing a downstream metric of an AI system using LLMs without human intervention) has
088   emerged as a new paradigm, where the input optimization task and the output solution are described
089   in natural language and processed by an LLM. This approach has been pioneered in automatic
090   prompt engineering (Pryzant et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024;
091   Yang et al., 2024b; Xiao et al., 2024), pipeline optimization of LLM-based agents (Zhang et al.,
092
      2024; Khattab et al., 2024; Yuksekgonul et al., 2024; Zhuge et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024b), and new
      algorithm discovery (Liu et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2024). Our research distinguishes itself from these
093
      previous studies as our optimization goal is to enhance the fundamental capabilities of LLMs through
094
      automated data collection and curation, which is very important given that data is the (perhaps most)
095   major power engine for today’s LLMs. Moreover, existing work simply prompts off-the-shelf LLMs
096   for optimization, which can lead to sub-optimal performance as these LLMs have not been explicitly
097   trained to do so. Conversely, we develop methods to fine-tune the optimizer models.
098

101

107   BAAI, 2024; Cheng et al., 2024). Nevertheless, these approaches require extensive evaluation to
      spot the weaknesses, which sometimes necessitates expert-level knowledge for test set design and

132   Self-Improving of LLMs Developing an autonomous self-improving system has been a major
133   focus of current LLM research. Conventional reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)
134   methods (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
135   2024b; Rosset et al., 2024) train a reward model from human preference data and subsequently use
136   this model to fine-tune LLMs via reinforcement learning. The reliance on human preference data of
137
      course can be removed for scenarios where the correctness of output can be validated automatically
      and unambiguously (Zelikman et al., 2022; Gulcehre et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2023). For general
138
      instruction following, recent studies have built upon the success of LLM-as-a-judge (Zheng et al.,
139
      2024) and leverage AI feedback for self-improvement, an approach also known as RLAIF (Bai et al.,
140   2022b; Lee et al., 2023). Yuan et al. (2024) and Wu et al. (2024) have further advanced this concept
141   by employing LLMs to provide self-rewarding feedback on their own generated outputs. Unlike
142   previous work that constrains the system from leveraging external signals, our framework allows
143   for information inflow from external environments through the interactions of API calls, potentially
144   elevating the upper bounds of self-improving.

146   3     S ELF -I MPROVING VIA ACTIVE DATA S EARCH
147
148
      The primary objective of our ACTIVE DATA S EARCH (ADS) framework is to endow AI systems
      with the capability to acquire valuable training data from external environments for retraining them-
149
      selves, thereby facilitating self-improvement with minimal human supervision. As illustrated in
150
      Figure 1, we develop an optimizer model for a policy model. The optimizer model is designed to
151   discover tailored training datasets to improve the policy by generating textual API calls to inter-
152   act with various resources and environments. In this section, we start with a brief introduction to
153   the problem formulation, followed by an overall description of policy optimization and optimizer
154   refinement processes. We defer the details of our prototype implementations in Section 4.
155
156   3.1     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
157   Given a target task T and a policy model Mp , the goal of ADS is to maximize the performance of
158   the policy model on T through active search for optimal training data. The data search process is
159   conducted by an optimizer model Mo and a set of pre-defined APIs. Each API requires some input
160   parameters and returns output data gathered from various environments. These APIs can be imple-
161   mented through a variety of established methods, which may encompass but are not limited to data
      crawling, filtering, cleaning, refinement, synthesis, and manual annotation. The resultant data can


                                                                   3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      also be sourced from a broad spectrum, such as internet search engines, LLM-based AI assistants,
163   or crowdsourcing annotation services. To mitigate excessive and unnecessary API invocations, we
164   assign varying costs to different APIs according to their practical complexities. Following com-
165   mon practice, each target task is represented by a set of instructions Q, which serve as input for the
166   optimizer model. The output of this model is an action trajectory A, comprising multiple API calls.
167
168   3.2   P OLICY O PTIMIZATION
169   The system optimizes its policy model Mp for a given target task T through the following steps.
170   First, the optimizer model Mo is instructed to conduct a comprehensive analysis of the instructions
171   Q, identifying the essential required capabilities. Following this, Mo performs an introspective
172   assessment of its own proficiencies and deficiencies on these identified capabilities, and generates
173   sequential API calls A = [a1 , a2 , ..., aK ] for data acquisition, where K is the number of API calls
174   and is determined on-the-fly. Notably, the above process is completed by the optimizer as free-form
175   text generation, enabling seamless incorporation of any pre-defined APIs.
176   Next, we execute each API call ak in the environment E, and collect the returned data to obtain a
177   training dataset D, which may include a variety of data types such as raw documents, supervised
178   demonstrations, and preference data, following the best practice of current LLM training. Finally,
179   we train the original Mp on the tailored training dataset to update its knowledge and capacities,
180   resulting in an updated policy model M̂p .
181
182   3.3   O PTIMIZER R EFINEMENT
183   To make efficient data-collecting decisions, the optimizer model Mo should be aware of the
184   strengths and weaknesses of the policy model Mp . Therefore, we propose an iterative training pro-
185   cess for optimizer refinement. First of all, we assume a set of training tasks T = {T }, where reliable
186   performance measurements are available. It is important to note that we do not make this assumption
187   at test-time since the evaluation of arbitrary real-world tasks can be complex and resource-intensive.
188   Instead, we posit that the self-knowledge developed for the optimizer during the training phase can
189   be effectively generalized to novel test tasks.
190   We then employ reinforcement learning and consider both performance gains and API costs in re-
191   ward design. The training is conducted iteratively. At each iteration, we sample multiple API
192   trajectories A = {A} for each training task T . We then execute the data acquisition and policy
193   training processes accordingly and collect the corresponding performance gain S(A) and API cost
194   C(A). To avoid potential data leakage, we partition the instruction set Q of T into an observed set
195   Qo and another held-out set Qh . The optimizer can only see the examples in the observed set and
196   the performance gains are measured based on the held-out set. This results in a set of quadruple
197
      B = {(T, A, S, C)}. We then optimize Mot to Mot+1 on B with reinforcement learning algorithms
      such as rejection sampling (Bai et al., 2022a) and direct preference optimization (Rafailov et al.,
198
      2024). The complete process of optimizer refinement is described in Algorithm 1.

      We design three distinct data-collecting APIs to facilitate the acquisition, utilization, and enhance-
      ment of knowledge. The first API is Information Retrieval, which employs both sparse and
227
      dense retrieval to retrieve relevant documents from external knowledge databases such as Wikipedia,
228
      thereby supporting knowledge acquisition, analogous to the pre-training stage of LLMs. The second
229   API is Demonstration Generation, which utilizes the policy model to generate appropriate
230   exemplar instruction-response pairs, tailored to various knowledge application scenarios, reminis-
231   cent of the alignment stages of LLMs. The last API is Question Answering, which resorts
232   to the wisdom of human experts, mimicking how humans learn from each other. 1 . It provides to-
233   the-point answers to questions proposed by the optimizer model. Notably, this framework is not
234   restrictive and can accommodate additional APIs as needed. In each API trajectory, these APIs
235   are invoked sequentially, e.g., api name 1(api param 1)...api name n(api param n).
236   Subsequently, the corresponding API calls are executed to formalize the training dataset. We then
237
      combine the training datasets from different API calls to update the policy model. For tasks where
      the model possesses the required capabilities, a “none” option is incorporated. We present the API
238
      implementation details in Table 1.
239
240   To avoid excessive API calls, e.g., asking questions that the policy model already knows the an-
241   swer to, we take into account the API cost in our implementation. The cost associated with each
242   API varies according to their practical complexities and computational overhead. Concretely, we
243
      assign relative costs as follows: Information Retrieval is at one since retrieval is fast and
      cheap, Demonstration Generation at two given generation is slow and resource-intensive,
244
      and Question Answering at three as it requires a more powerful model or manual efforts.
245
      Further implementation details of APIs are provided in Appendix A.

247   4.2       DATASET
248
      To train and evaluate the optimizer model, it is essential to collect a diverse set of “target tasks”
249
      for which we aim to optimize the performance of the policy model. For this purpose, we adopt the
250
      general instruction-following dataset Llama-3-Magpie-Air-3M-v0.1 (Xu et al., 2024). Each instruc-
251   tion in this dataset is associated with several labels such as task category, intent, and difficulty. The
252   instructions and labels are generated by Llama-3-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024). Subsequently, we
253   group the instructions with identical labels into different clusters, with each cluster representing a
254   distinct “target task”. We discard tasks with fewer than five instructions. The resulting dataset com-
255   prises 10,239 distinct tasks, which we partition into 8,739 tasks for training, 500 tasks for validating,
256   and 1,000 tasks for testing. In the train and valid splits, we allocate three observed instructions per
257   task for trajectory generation and reserve two held-out instructions for performance measurement.
258   This approach ensures that the optimizer learns to improve performance at task level rather than in-
259
      stance level, as over-fitting on the observed instructions may not necessarily translate performance
      gains on the held-out instructions. For the test split, to enhance the robustness of evaluation, we aug-
260
      ment the initial five instructions per task to 100 using Self-Instruct (Wang et al., 2023), with three
261
      instructions as observed and 97 as held-out. More details of our dataset are shown in Appendix B.

281

282
      we make the following adaptations. First, we cluster the original instructions based on task cate-
283
      gory and difficulty, utilizing the approach in Xu et al. (2024). This process yields 38, 34, and 21

288   a standard evaluation using GPT-4 judgment. We adhere to the default setups and report the win
289   rate of the updated policy models against the GPT-4 baseline for each benchmark. Additionally,
290   we conduct a supplementary evaluation using RM as judgment and report the average adjusted win
291   rate, calculated as win rate + 0.5 × tie rate. For both RM and GPT-4 judgments, we also report the

298   During each iteration of ADS, the optimizer model generates five API trajectories per target task,

301   ries are executed in the data-collecting environments to construct a comprehensive training dataset.
302

303

304

305   et al., 2023; Agarwal et al.). We then determine the reward signal of each API call trajectory by

344   select the one with the highest cost within [Rmin , (1 − τ )Rmin + τ Rmax ]. To maintain an optimal
345   balance between reward and cost, we empirically determine the cost tier parameter at 0.1. We further
346   conduct experiments to illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed cost-control approach in Section
347   6.2. More information on training details can be found in Appendix C.

465   6.1                 A BLATION S TUDIES
466   In this section, we delve into the key components in our practical implementation of ADS, focusing
467   on the training algorithm and the construction of API trajectories. We first investigate and compare
468   the performance of the optimizer model when trained iteratively using rejection sampling and direct
469   preference optimization. Subsequently, we analyze the influence of adding default API trajectories
470   into the optimizer model’s training process. The evaluation results across in-house test tasks with
471
      RM judgment and public benchmarks with GPT-4 judgment are shown in Figure 4.
472   DPO enhances discrimination between chosen and rejected trajectories. We can observe that
473   compared to rejection sampling, the implementation of direct preference optimization substantially
474   improves the optimizer model’s capacity to differentiate between chosen and rejected API trajec-
475   tories, which is illustrated by a significant increase in the average win rate from 49.1% to 84.3%
476   across in-house test tasks. Similarly, in public benchmarks, the win rate improved from 28.6% to
477   32.8%. This enhanced discriminative capability facilitates more effective weakness identification
478   and decision-making processes of our optimizer model, ultimately leading to high-reward API tra-
479   jectory exploration and optimal training data acquiring for deficiency enhancement.
480   Incorporating default API trajectories enhances trajectory diversity. In addition to the five
481   trajectories sampled by the optimizer during its training process, we incorporate three default tra-
482   jectories, each corresponding to a distinct data-collecting API in our implementation. Table 4 in
483   Appendix C illustrates an example of both default and generated trajectories. The inclusion of these
484   default API trajectories alongside the self-generated candidates results in a more diverse set of tra-
485   jectories for optimizer model training, facilitating improved average win rates across all iterations,
      both in in-house test tasks and public benchmarks.

      7     C ONCLUSION & L IMITATIONS
525
      In this study, we explored enabling LLMs to autonomously acquire optimal training data for self-
526
      improvement with minimal human intervention. We proposed a novel framework, ACTIVE DATA
527
      S EARCH (ADS), which utilizes LLMs themselves as an optimizer to strategically invoke appro-
528   priate APIs, facilitating the discovery of tailored training datasets from external environments for
529   self-training. To ensure efficient data-collecting decisions, we introduced an iterative refinement
530   algorithm for the optimizer, guided by reinforcement feedback signals aiming to maximize task per-
531   formance while minimizing computation costs. Through a series of experiments on 1,000 in-house
532   test tasks and three public benchmarks, we demonstrated the effectiveness and generalizability of
533   ADS. Notably, ADS exhibited the capacity to enhance the performance of smaller and weaker lan-
534   guage models to be comparable with larger and stronger LLMs on AlpacaEval 2.0. This automated
535   process of data discovery and self-training presents opportunities to reduce the reliance on human
536
      expertise in LLM development, providing a new direction for future research in this domain.
537   The current implantation is a proof-of-concept with several limitations. First, the optimizer and
538   policy models are separate. Unifying them is an interesting avenue for future work. Second, the
539   APIs included currently are far from covering all existing data techniques. Third, we did not consider
      multi-turn optimization of the policy models in the framework.
