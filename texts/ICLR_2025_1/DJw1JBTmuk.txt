009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Humans can accurately anticipate their movements to behave as expected in var-
012            ious manipulation tasks. We are inspired to propose that integrating prior knowl-
013            edge of robot dynamics into world models can effectively improve the sample
014            efficiency of model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) in visual robot con-
015            trol tasks. In this paper, we introduce the Robo-Centric World Model (RCWM),
016            which explicitly decouples the robot dynamics from the environment and enables
017            pre-training to learn generalized and robust robot dynamics as prior knowledge
018
               to accelerate learning new tasks. Specifically, we construct respective dynam-
               ics models for the robot and the environment and learn their interactions through
019
               cross-attention mechanism. With the mask-guided reconfiguration mechanism,
020
               we only need a few prior robot segmentation masks to guide the RCWM to dis-
021            entangle the robot and environment features and learn their respective dynamics.
022            Our approach enables independent inference of robot dynamics from the environ-
023            ment, allowing accurate prediction of robot movement across various unseen tasks
024            without being distracted by environmental variations. Our results in Meta-world
025            demonstrate that RCWM is able to efficiently learn robot dynamics, improving
026            sample efficiency for downstream tasks and enhancing policy robustness against
027            environmental disturbances compared to the vanilla world model in DreamerV3.
028            Code and visualizations are available on the project website: https://robo-centric-
029
               wm.github.io.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) holds significant promise for achieving sample-
034
      efficient learning in visual robot control tasks (Ebert et al., 2018; Hafner et al., 2019; 2020a; Sekar
035
      et al., 2020; Mendonca et al., 2021; Rybkin et al., 2021; Seo et al., 2023). By constructing world
036   models (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) that approximate the dynamics of the real environment, agents
037   can leverage generated imaginary trajectories for planning (Hafner et al., 2019; Ye et al., 2021;
038   Hansen et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2023; Hansen et al., 2024) or behavioral learning (Hafner et al.,
039   2020b; 2023; Seo et al., 2023), thereby reducing the interaction with the environment. However,
040   learning accurate world models from scratch, especially in the absence of prior knowledge, poses
041   a formidable challenge. The presence of model errors hinders the policy learning, restricting the
042   further improvement of sample efficiency in MBRL.
043   One feasible way to address this challenge is to pre-train world models to obtain useful information
044   for downstream tasks to accelerate learning. Inspired by the success of pre-training paradigms in
045   domains such as computer vision (CV) (Deng et al., 2009; Radford et al., 2021) and natural language
046   processing (NLP) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2022), recent works have aimed to investigate effec-
047   tive pre-training methods for world models (Seo et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Mazzaglia et al., 2023;
048   Rajeswar et al., 2023). For visual manipulation tasks that require precise estimation of the current
049   robot state, it is necessary for the world model to accurately capture the robot dynamics to generate
050
      realistic imaginary trajectories in response to the action for policy learning. However, existing works
      rarely recognize this and typically focuses on pre-training effective representations.
051
052   When humans interact with physical world, they typically have the ability to anticipate their own
053   movements, allowing them to act aligned with their expectations (Miall & Wolpert, 1996). With this
      in mind, we naturally ask the following question:

082       Can we integrate prior knowledge of robot dynamics into world models to mitigate the disparity
083
                     between imagined and actual behaviors for improving sample efficiency?
084
      However, existing methods often rely on a single model to learn world dynamics, which results
085
      in the entanglement of robot and environment dynamics, making it difficult to provide accurate
086   robot state estimation when confronted with new tasks. In this paper, we introduce the Robot-
087   Centric World Model (RCWM), which is designed to extract robot dynamics through pre-training
088   and apply this prior knowledge on new tasks to provide accurate dynamics prediction, as shown
089   in Figure 1. Specifically, we construct two branches to learn the dynamics of the environment and
090   robot, respectively, and use an interaction model with cross-attention mechanism to learn robot-
091   object interaction relations. In order for both branches to capture their respective relevant features,
092   we design the mask-guided reconstruction mechanism and use only a few prior robot segmentation
093   masks generated by Segment-Anything-Model-2 (SAM 2) (Ravi et al., 2024) to achieve implicit
094
      feature disentanglement at the beginning of the pre-training period.
095   RCWM offers several advantages: (1) Accurate prediction of robot dynamics. We find that explic-
096   itly modeling robot dynamics individually results in more accurate predictions compared to learning
097   global dynamics with a single model; (2) Robust against environmental disturbance. Due to implicit
098   feature disentanglement, the robot branch is hardly affected by environmental disturbances, provid-
099   ing robust robot representation for the policy; (3) Utilization of prior masks. The architecture of
100
      RCWM naturally introduces the use of robot segmentation masks, which can enhance the prediction
      accuracy of robot dynamics. We evaluate and analyze RCWM on Meta-world and show that RCWM
101
      is more suitable for robots to learn a variety of manipulation tasks than the vanilla world model in
102
      DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023) that uses a single dynamic model to learn global dynamics.

204   4.1   ROBOT DYNAMICS D ECOUPLING
205
206   We consider visual robot control scenarios in which the same robot is employed for various manip-
207
      ulation tasks. Although MBRL approaches can efficiently learn visual control policies, training the
      world model from scratch for each task still requires a significant amount of interaction with the
208
      environment, which limits further improvements in sample efficiency. Intuitively, the intrinsic dy-
209
      namics of the robot remain consistent across different tasks. However, existing approaches typically
210   use a single model to learn the world dynamics which couples the robot dynamics with the environ-
211   ment dynamics. As a result, when confronted with new tasks, the world model is unable to avoid
212   the visual interference on robot dynamics prediction caused by environmental changes, thus making
213   it difficult to directly provide generalized prior knowledge about the robot dynamics for learning
214   new tasks. If we can individually extract this prior knowledge from the collected trajectories and
215   integrate it into the world model, we can leverage it to provide accurate predictions of the robot
      dynamics when learning new tasks, without requiring significant training data.


                                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      However, the intricate interactions between the robot and objects in the environment make it chal-
217   lenging to learn intrinsic robot dynamics in isolation. For example, when the robot operates the
218   lever switch, it encounters resistance from the lever, and when it pushes all the way down, it can no
219   longer move forward. Additionally, the lever itself moves as a result of the robot’s movement. This
220   interplay complicates learning generalized robot dynamics that are applicable across various tasks.
221
      Therefore, we propose a simplified interaction framework as shown in Figure 2. We decouple world
222
      dynamics into robot dynamics and environment dynamics. The robot dynamics need to take into
223   account the intrinsic dynamics of robot itself as well as potential effects of the environment on the
224   robot, such as resistance to encountering an object, with no need to know the detailed environmental
225   state transitions. The environment dynamics then infer the movement of objects in the environment
226   based on the execution results of the robot rather than directly through action signals. This frame-
227   work simplifies the robot-object interaction by explicitly modeling the robot’s effects on the objects
228   while implicitly modeling the objects’ influences on the robot.
229   Based on this framework, we allow inference on robot dynamics to be relatively independent of
230   the environment dynamics, thus learning intrinsic robot dynamics that can be generalized across a
231   variety of tasks. For example, as shown in Figure 2(a), the robot first executes the action according
232   to the control signal, and it does not need to know how the lever moves, but only needs to know that
233   the reaction force of the lever will be applied when it pushes forward. We denote the potential effects
234   on the robot by the lever switch at the edge of the dashed line. Then, based on the robot’s execution
235   results, we can infer the specific movement track of the lever switch. Eventually, we combine the
236   two inference results to obtain the complete state estimation.

342   4.3   I MPLEMENTATION P IPELINE
343
      In this subsection, we introduce the pre-training and fine-tuning pipeline for our proposed Robo-
344
      Centric World Model, as shown in Figure 1. Note that unlike previous approaches, we concentrate
345   on pre-training on upstream tasks that are entirely different from downstream tasks but involve the
346   same robot.
347
348
      First, we need to obtain a few prior robot masks to guide the warm-up of RCWM. We randomly se-
      lect a small number of trajectories from the pre-training dataset formed by the trajectories collected
349
      from upstream tasks. In our experiments, we sample 16 full trajectories totaling 4000 steps. Then
350
      we segment the robot in the image to obtain the mask with the help of SAM2. SAM2 can automati-
351   cally track segmented objects, so we only need to provide detection points for the first frame of the
352   trajectory. Since the initial position of the robot is fixed, we only need to set identical positive and
353   negative points for the first frame of all trajectories. We provide details in Appendix A.4. Then we
354   utilize these trajectories with masks to warm up the RCWM, thus ensuring that both the robot branch
355   and the environment branch capture the corresponding features. Since RCWM is naturally capable
356   of separating structured information, it does not heavily rely on prior masks, thereby avoiding ex-
357   cessive costs. In cases where prior masks are readily available, e.g., directly from the environment
358   simulator, we can use them throughout the pre-training phase to guide training of RCWM, thus
359
      enhancing robot dynamics learning.
360   After warmup, RCWM already has the ability to capture robot features. With mask reconstruc-
361   tion mechanism, RCWM is still able to accurately predict robot masks without using prior masks.
362   Therefore, we can automatically learn robot dynamics by mask free pre-training of RCWM on the
363   full pre-training dataset. After pre-training, the robot branch has been able to accurately predict the
364   robot’s movements based on actions. We keep the robot dynamics model and reset the environment
365
      dynamics and interaction models to prepare for learning new tasks. The robot branch is not affected
      by the reset of these two models, as its inference is completely independent of the environment
366
      branch.
367
368   During the online fine-tuning phase, although the environmental observations change in new tasks,
369   the robot branch remains capable of robustly predicting the robot mask, allowing for the fine-tuning
370   of RCWM without using prior robot masks. Despite being well-trained, the robot branch still needs
371
      to learn about the potential effects of the environment on the robot when faced with new tasks. With
      online fine-tuning of the RCWM overall, it is able to quickly adapt to the dynamics of new tasks and
372
      learn about the robot-object interaction.

401   5.1                       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
402
403   Environments Our approach is focused on visual robot control scenarios that utilize the same
404   robot for various manipulation tasks. Therefore, we select the Meta-world benchmark (Yu et al.,
405   2020) to evaluate our approach, which includes 50 manipulation tasks performed with the Sawyer
406
      robot and is friendly to reinforcement learning. We first select 4 simple tasks for pre-training data
      collection. Then we select 8 challenging tasks for sample efficiency evaluation. All of these tasks
407
      involve complex robot-object interactions and require precise manipulation, which places significant
408
      demands on the predictive accuracy of world models. we set the action repeat to 2 and the episode
409   length to 500 for all tasks. See Appendix A.3 for detailed task description and parameter setting.
410
411   Pre-training dataset We utilize the replay buffer from 4 tasks trained by DreamerV3 as our
412   dataset, with each replay buffer containing 500K steps of interaction data. We randomly selected 16
413   full trajectories from the pre-training dataset and then used SAM 2 to generate a total of 4k masks
414   for mask-guided warmup.
415
416   Baseline We choose Dreamerv3 with vanilla world models (WM) as our baseline since our ap-
417   proach is built upon it. As RCWM utilizes prior robot masks, we also add a mask predictor to the
418   vanilla world model for fairness. The pre-training process for vanilla WM is identical to that of
419   RCWM, except that it does not require resetting the dynamic model after pre-training.
420
421
      Implementation details Since Meta-world features hard-exploration tasks, this leads to signifi-
      cant variance in the learning curves, making the evaluation of sample efficiency challenging. In
422
      addition, exploring randomness can seriously impact the assessment of pre-trained world models.
423
      This is because trying to explore a successful trajectory often requires numerous interactions with
424   the environment, causing the advantages of pre-training to diminish as online interactions and train-
425   ing progress. To mitigate these problems, we randomly select 20 successful trajectories to pre-fill
426   the replay buffer at the beginning of training. This practice is effective in improving the stability of
427   training on Meta-world tasks (Wu et al., 2024).

518   if the environmental representation is disturbed to some extent, the policy can still make decisions

      6   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
523
524   In this paper, we introduce the Robo-Centric World Model (RCWM), which can decouple the dy-
525
      namics of the robot and the environment, and learn their interaction via an interaction model based
      on the cross-attention mechanism. With RCWM, we can extract robot dynamics through pre-training
526
      on upstream tasks with the assistance of a few prior robot masks, and use this prior knowledge about
527
      robot dynamics to improve sample efficiency on downstream tasks. Compared to vanilla world mod-
528   els, the RCWM has significant advantages in that it can be efficiently pre-trained to learn generalized
529   robot dynamics applicable to various new tasks and can remain robust against noise disturbances.
530   In addition, we provide an efficient way to introduce external prior robot masks into the training
531   of the world model to enhance robot dynamics learning. Our experiments on Meta-world support
532   our argument that integrating prior knowledge of robot dynamics into world models can effectively
533   improve sample efficiency for downstream visual robot control tasks.
534   Despite these results, some limitations still remain. Our approach focuses on visual robot control
535   tasks and is difficult to effectively extend to more general tasks such as video games or autonomous
536   driving. In addition, our approach requires using the same robot’s interaction data for pre-training
537   and cannot be directly expanded to general in-the-wild robot data. However, we still believe that we
538   provides a novel idea for constructing world models applicable to robot manipulation tasks, which
539   can further improve the sample efficiency for MBRL. In future work we would like to explore
      utilizing the long horizon predictive accuracy of the RCWM for robot manipulation planning.

783   A.4    ROBOT M ASK G ENERATION
784
785   We utilize Segment-Anything-Model 2 (SAM 2) to generate robot masks. Since SAM 2 has the abil-
786   ity to track and segment objects in the video, we select complete trajectories rather than fragments,
787   thus only needing to give positive and negative points to the first frame of each trajectories. We find
788   that there is no randomization of the initial position of the robot in Meta-world, so the initial position
789   of the robot is fixed for each trajectory. In our selected tasks, there are a total of two initial positions
790
      involved. We manually create two sets of prompt points, as shown in Figure 10. The positive points
      for the robot cover the robot’s body as well as the gripper, while the negative points are used to avoid
791
      segmentation failures due to the object’s initial position being too close to the gripper. In order for
792
      SAM 2 to generate masks accurately, we additionally save high-quality image observations obtained
793   from the simulator with a resolution of 256*256. After generated the mask, we resize it to 64*64 for
794   model training. This can effectively improve the accuracy of mask generation. In our tests, SAM 2
795   performs well in all tasks.

      With the help of prior robot masks, RCWM can disentangle robot-related information and

      environment-related information from high-dimensional image inputs. Figure 11 shows the recon-

851   trained with the prior mask. We can see that each of the two branches captures different structured
852   information. However, without the prior information, we find that we cannot control with certainty

1040   A.7    C OMPUTATIONAL RESOURCES
1041
1042   We use a single Nvidia RTX3090 GPU and 10 CPU cores for each training run. In terms of parameter
1043   counts, RCWM consists of 24.7M parameters, while the vanilla world model in DreamerV3 consists
1044   of 19.1M parameters. In terms of training time, it takes ∼50 hours for pre-training of RCWM over
1045   500K updates and ∼90 hours for fine-tuning of RCWM with MBRL for each run of Meta-world
1046   experiments over 1M environment steps. This takes longer than training of vanilla DreamerV3,
1047
       which requires ∼60 hours for pre-training and ∼80 hours for fine-tuning. We consider that this is
       mainly because RCWM creates two RNNs that compute in sequential order. In terms of memory
1048
       usage, RCWM requires ∼5GB GPU memory for each run of Meta-world experiments, while vanilla
1049
       DreamerV3 requires ∼4GB GPU memory.

       A.8    H YPERPARAMETERS
1052
1053   We report the hyperparameters used in our experiments in Table 2. Unless otherwise specified, we
1054   use the same hyperparameters as DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023). In the pre-training phase, we set
1055   the number of pre-training update steps to 500k, at which point the model has converged. To avoid
1056   performance gains due to the increase in the number of model parameters, we reduce the hidden
1057   dimension of the dynamic model in RCWM to 256, while the vanilla world model is 512. In order
1058   to improve training stability, we increase the scale of the reward loss to 100 with reference to Ma
1059   et al. (2024). We set the mask-guided warmup steps to 1000, which is much larger than DreamerV3’s
1060
       original pre-training step of 100 after random prefilling. This is because we want to make RCWM
       learn to capture robot-related features with only a few prior robot mask data.
