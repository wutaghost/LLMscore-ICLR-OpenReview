009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Models of dense prediction based on traditional Artificial Neural Networks
012            (ANNs) require a lot of energy, especially for image restoration tasks. Currently,
013            neural networks based on the SNN (Spiking Neural Network) framework are be-
014            ginning to make their mark in the field of image restoration, especially as they
015            typically use less than 10% of the energy of ANNs with the same architecture.
016            However, training an SNN is much more expensive than training an ANN, due
017            to the use of the heuristic gradient descent strategy. In other words, the process
018
               of SNN’s potential membrane signal changing from sparse to dense is very slow,
               which affects the convergence of the whole model. To tackle this problem, we
019
               propose a novel distillation technique, called asymmetric framework (ANN-SNN)
020
               distillation, in which the teacher is an ANN and the student is an SNN. Specifi-
021            cally, we leverage the intermediate features (feature maps) learned by the ANN as
022            hints to guide the training process of the SNN. This approach not only accelerates
023            the convergence of the SNN but also improves its final performance, effectively
024            bridging the gap between the efficiency of the SNN and the superior learning
025            capabilities of ANN. Extensive experimental results show that our designed SNN-
026            based image restoration model, which has only 1/300 the number of parameters
027            of the teacher network and 1/50 the energy consumption of the teacher network,
028            is as good as the teacher network in some denoising tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Image restoration, a classical research area in computer vision, focuses on recovering high-quality
      images from degraded observations. Most existing frameworks for image restoration use artificial
034
      neural networks (ANNs), which have high performance but also often rely on large-capacity models
035
      to achieve optimal performance. For instance, Restormer [47] and PromptIR [30] networks have
036   26.10M and 35.59M parameters, respectively, making them unsuitable for deployment on edge
037   devices. The growing importance of devices with low power or battery constraints in various real-
038   world applications, such as spiking neural networks (SNNs) offers a promising alternative [2, 6, 16,
039   36, 40, 41].
040
      SNNs utilize binary signals (spikes) instead of continuous signals for neuron communication, re-
041
      ducing data transfer and storage overhead. Moreover, Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) feature
042   asynchronous processing and event-driven communication, which can eliminate redundant compu-
043   tations and synchronization burdens. When implemented in neuromorphic hardware, as mentioned
044   in [26, 29], SNNs demonstrate exceptional energy efficiency. Unfortunately, in the challenging do-
045   main of image restoration, there is a notable absence of an SNN-based benchmark that can achieve
046   performance levels comparable to those of its ANN counterpart. This is largely due to the slow
047   training process of SNNs, which, relying on spike-based signaling, require extensive data exposure
048   to generate predictions that match the accuracy of ANNs. This reliance on prolonged data exposure
049   is particularly problematic when it comes to the extraction of subtle information from images that
050
      are visually redundant, as the training process becomes even more time-consuming.
051   In recent years, knowledge distillation as a promising approach for training heterogeneous models
052   for knowledge transfer [11, 24, 28]. These efforts have piqued our curiosity, prompting us to explore
053   the question: Is it feasible to transfer knowledge from ANNs to SNNs effectively? In this paper, our
      objective is to address the prolonged training times in SNNs by leveraging the exceptional perfor-

      3.1       E NERGY C ONSUMPTION
137
138
      The number of operations is commonly used to estimate the computational energy consumption of
139   neuromorphic hardware. In ANNs, each operation consists of multiplication and addition (MAC) in-
140   volving floating-point numbers, and the computational burden is typically measured using floating-
141   point operations (FLOPs)1 . In contrast, SNNs offer an energy-efficient alternative for neuromor-
142   phic hardware, as neurons only engage in accumulation calculations (AC) when they spike. This
143   efficiency allows SNNs to perform computations using a similar number of synaptic operations
144   (SyOPs), significantly reducing energy consumption compared to   Ptraditional ANN architectures.
145   We quantify the energy consumption of vanilla SNNs as ESNN = n Eb , for each block n:
146
                                             Eb = T × (f r × EAC × OPAC + EMAC × OPMAC ),                                                      (1)
147
148   where T and f r represent the total time steps and the block firing rate. The blocks are normally
149   convolutional or fully connected, and the energy consumption is determined by the number of AC
150   and MAC operations (OPAC , OPMAC ). In this work, we adopt the same structure of SNN and ANN to
151   compare the energy consumption and assume that the data for various operations are 32-bit floating-
152   point implementation in 45nm technology [12], where EMAC = 4.6pJ and EAC = 0.9pJ.
153

159   the single degraded image as the input for each time step X = {Xt }Tt=1 (in this paper, we set T to

      4     M ETHOD OVERVIEW
163
164   As illustrated in Figure 2, our method comprises two primary components: an encoder-decoder
165   framework (student network) designed to learn features for capturing information, and the distilla-
166   tion of knowledge from the decoders of ANNs. These parts are collaboratively worked to improve
167   the image restoration performance of SpikerIR.
168
      Specifically, SpikerIR incorporates a lightweight encoder designed to extract degradation features
169
      from the degraded image sequence. To balance training efficiency and performance, we introduce
170   ‘prompts’ derived from the ANN’s network, where a prompt refers to the output of each ANN
171   decoder layer, which guides SpikerIR’s learning process. We employ Mean Squared Error (MSE)
172   loss to align the outputs of each SNN decoder layer with those of the ANN, thereby facilitating
173   learning from the ANN’s output. Furthermore, to avoid the decreasing flexibility of our SpikerIR,
174   we leverage the L1 loss and an FFT-based frequency loss function, which is defined as:
175                                L = ∥IR − IGT ∥1 + λ∥F(IR ) − F(IGT )∥1 ,                            (2)
176
177
      where IR is the output of our model, IGT is the high-quality ground-truth image, ∥ · ∥1 denotes the
      L1 loss, F represents the Fast Fourier transform, and λ is a weight parameter that set to be 0.1
178
      empirically. Algorithm 1 (H-KD training strategy) procedures can be written:
179
180   Algorithm 1 the H-KD training strategy
181
182
      Input:
         The output of each ANN’s decoder layer Fprompt ;
183
         The output of each SpikerIR’s decoder layer Foptimize ;
184
         The randomly initialized parameters W of a SpikerIR.
185   Output:
186      The optimized parameters W∗ of SpikerIR.
187
188         Fprompt ← {Fprompt 1 , . . . , Fprompt i };
189         Foptimize ← {Foptimize 1 , . . . , Foptimize i };

                      Foptimize                             W

194   Fprompt and Foptimize may not match in the size of the feature maps, which can be aligned by interpo-
195   lation and pooling.

197   5     E XPERIMENTS
198
199   We experimentally evaluate our method on five degradation types of tasks: motion blurry, hazy,
200   noise, rainy and defocus blurry. In addition, we use three existing image restoration models as
201   teacher networks to evaluate the effectiveness of our algorithm.
202
203   5.1    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
204
205   We train five sets of model parameters for these five image restoration tasks within the same network
206   framework. Our SpikerIR uses a 4-layer encoder-decoder structure, where each layer of the network
207
      is a convolutional layer and a ReLU layer. From level-1 to level-4, the number of each level Spik-
      erIR Blocks is 2, and the number of channels is {48,96,192,384}. For teather networks, we adopt
208
      Restormer [46], PromptIR [30] and AdaIR [8] as the teacher models. We train models with AdamW
209
      optimizer (β1 =0.9, β2 =0.999, weight decay 0.05) for 51 epochs with the initial learning rate 0.0005
210   gradually reduced to 0.00001 with the cosine annealing for image denoising tasks. Distinct tasks,
211   however, were trained for different numbers of epochs to optimize performance, with the details as
212   follows: Motion deblurring for 77 epochs, dehazing for 5 epochs, deraining for 8 epochs, and defo-
213   cus deblurring for 208 epochs. We start training with patch size 64 × 64 and batch size 8. For data
214   augmentation, we use horizontal and vertical flips. Two well-known metrics, Peak Signal-to-Noise
215   Ratio (PSNR) and Structural Similarity (SSIM), are employed for quantitative comparisons. Higher
      values of these metrics indicate superior performance of the methods.

      i) The inference time in the SNN model is slower compared to its ANN counterpart (on platforms

494   optimization in SNNs, where updating spiking neurons’ membrane potentials becomes more com-
495   putationally demanding as the model learns. Addressing this issue will require the development of
496   more efficient training algorithms or hardware-accelerated solutions specifically designed for SNNs.

533   8    C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we develop an efficient, low-energy network named SpikerIR for a variety of image
536   restoration tasks. On both GPU platforms and embedded platforms, our model demonstrates excel-
537   lent performance, with clearer images than those recovered by the teacher network on some datasets.
538   In addition, we attempted to explain the role of distillation, which acts to enable the conversion of a
539   sparse voltage to a denser one. We also discuss some of the limitations of the model, in particular
      the fact that SNNs rely heavily on efficient I/O operations.
