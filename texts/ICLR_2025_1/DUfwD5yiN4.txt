009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning the structure of a Bayesian network is currently practical for only a limited
012            number of variables. Existing distributed learning approaches approximate the true
013            structure. We present an exact distributed structure-learning algorithm to find a
014            P-map for a set of random variables. First, by using conditional independence,
015            the variables are divided into sets X1 , . . . , XI such that for each Xi , the presence
016            and absence of edges that are adjacent with any interior node (a node that is not
017            in any other Xj , j ̸= i) can be correctly identified by learning the structure of Xi
018            separately without using the information of the variables other than Xi . Second,
019
               constraint or score-based structure learners are employed to learn the P-map of Xi ,
               in a decentralized way. Finally, the separately learned structures are appended by
020
               checking a conditional independence test on the boundary nodes (those that are in
021
               at least two Xi ’s). The result is proven to be a P-map. This approach allows for a
022            significant reduction in computation time, and opens the door for structure learning
023            for a “giant” number of variables.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Bayesian networks constitute a primary subfield within the realm of probabilistic graphical models,
029   which serve as powerful tools for data modeling. These networks leverage directed acyclic graphs
030   (DAGs) to represent probabilistic relationships in datasets. The process of structure learning in
031   Bayesian networks involves the derivation of a DAG from empirical data (van den Boom et al., 2022).
032   Two primary methodologies for learning the DAG from data are the constraint-based and score-based
033   approaches (Kitson et al., 2021).
034   Constraint-based algorithms, such as PC algorithm (Spirtes et al., 2000), rely on the principles
035   of sufficiency, Markov condition, and faithfulness assumption. These algorithms are designed to
036   identify dependencies between variables without mediator variables. This is achieved by employing
037   conditional independence (CI) tests (Guo et al., 2020). Score-based algorithms adopt an optimization-
038   based strategy, wherein they define a likelihood function, often employing criteria like Bayesian
039   Information Criterion (BIC). Both approaches yield a class of graphs known as independence-
040   equivalent (I-equivalent) graphs, represented as partially Directed Acyclic Graphs (PDAGs) (Koller
041   & Friedman, 2009).
042   Performing CI tests across all variables or optimizing the likelihood function over all potential
043   graphs leads to computational challenges, often resulting in a computational explosion (Spirtes et al.,
044   2000). This problem represents a significant challenge and limitation, particularly when dealing
045   with a substantial number of variables (Peters et al., 2017; Ramsey et al., 2017). Several techniques
046   have been developed to reduce the runtime, by for example, first running some fast conditional
047   independence tests to quickly eliminate many edges in constraint-based algorithms (Giudice et al.,
048
      2022), limiting the conditioning set in the CI tests, (Sondhi & Shojaie, 2019), finding an order on
      the variables (Chen et al., 2019b;a) and (Gao et al., 2020), and parallelizing the CI tests (Zarebavani
049
      et al., 2019; Shahbazinia et al., 2023; Le et al., 2016).
050
051   Nevertheless, regardless of how much the speed of the structure learning algorithms are improved,
052   their application will be limited to a small number of variables in practice. Score-based algorithms
                                                                                         2
053   require an exhaustive search over the space of all DAGs, which is of size O(2n ). Loading these
      many edges or DAGs on a single computing machine becomes readily infeasible for large values of n,


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      despite the many optimizations on reducing the order. As a result, existing computational resources
055   are incapable to perform exact structure-learning on a “large” number of variables (Franzin et al.,
056   2017), unless approximation techniques are used. Constraint-based algorithms, such as PC, start or
057   interact with a fully connected network, which has O(n2 ) edges for n variables. This is more feasible
058   to load on a single machine, however, the complexity of the algorithm itself is O(np+2 ), where p is
059   the maximum number of parents of a variable in the “true” DAG (Koller & Friedman, 2009).
060
      Reducing the structure-learning problem to several sub-problems that can be learned separately can
061   be the key to solve this issue. An approximation distributed structure-learning approach was proposed
062   in (Gu & Zhou, 2020), where the variables are partitioned into clusters that are learned in a distributed
063   way and then appended to obtain the final DAG. Nevertheless, the result is an estimation of the true
064   DAG and under the assumption of Gaussian-distributed variables.
065
      The partitioning of variables is the main part of this approach. In many represented approaches, the
066
      resulting network by distributed learning is an approximation of a network that is obtained from
067   centralized learning (Talvitie et al., 2019; Scanagatta et al., 2015). Additionally, in some other
068   approaches (Xie et al., 2006) and (Liu et al., 2017), partitioning is performed using expert knowledge
069   and requires conditional independence tests with high-order conditioning variables that cannot be
070   used in many practical problems. (Zhang et al., 2020) proposed an optimization-based approach
071   for partitioning using lower conditioning variables; however, the number of conditioning variables
072   cannot be controlled.
073   We develop an exact distributed structure-learning algorithm that obtains the true P-map for a given
074   set of random variables in three steps. First, the algorithm performs a reduction on the set of variables,
075   by dividing them into sets X1 , . . . , XI . Each set Xi has a boundary bd(Xi ) that is the subset of nodes
076   shared with other Xj , i.e., ∪j̸=i Xi ∩ Xj , and an interior Xio which is the remainder, i.e., Xi \ bd(Xi ).
077   The reduction is such that the P-map confined to each set Xi is a conditional P-map for the marginal
078   distribution of the variables in Xi ; namely, the presence and absence of all edges that are adjacent
079   with the interior nodes of Xi are correctly learned by performing a structure-learner to find the P-map
080   of Xi . Roughly speaking, the “interior edges” of each set Xi can be learned separately, without the
081
      information about the nodes in the other Xj ’s. This naturally leads to the second step, where separate
      structure-learners, either constraint or score-based, are deployed to learn the local P-map structure
082
      of every Xi . Finally, the local P-maps are concatenated to obtain the global P-map by performing a
083
      distributed PC-like algorithm on all boundary nodes. We prove that the resulting DAG is a P-map.

086   2    BACKGROUND
087
088
      Consider a set of random variables X = {X1 , . . . , Xn } with joint probability distribution P . Let
      I(P ) denote the set of all conditional independencies implied by the distribution P , i.e., I(P ) =
089
      {(X1 ⊥ X2 | X3 ) : X1 , X2 , X3 ⊆ X }. Let G be a DAG with node set X . The DAG induces
090
      conditional independencies between the nodes using the notion of d-separation defined below. A
091   collider in G is a triple of nodes X1 → X2 ← X3 , where two of them are linked to the third. The
092   collider is an immorality if the ending nodes X1 and X3 are not adjacent (connected). Three nodes
093   are a non-collider if they do not form a collider.
094   Definition 2.1 (d-separation). (Koller & Friedman, 2009) Consider the DAG G with node set X . A
095   trail (path) T between two nodes X1 and X2 in X is active relative to a set of nodes Z if (i) every
096   non-collider on T is not a member of Z, and (ii) every collider on T is an ancestor of some member
097   of Z. Otherwise, the trail is said to be blocked by Z. The node subsets X1 and X2 are d-separated
098   given the subset Z, denoted d-sepG (X1 , X2 | Z), if there is no active trail between any node X1 ∈ X1
099   and any node X2 ∈ X2 given Z.
100
      The set of all d-separations in G is denoted by I(G). We assume that for the distribution P , there
101
      exists a DAG G that satisfies both of the following well-known conditions: (i) Markovness, that is,
102   I(G) ⊆ I(P ), and (ii faithfulness, that is I(P ) ⊆ I(G). This results in I(P ) = I(G); namely, all
103   conditional independencies in P are captured by the d-separations in G and vice versa. DAG G is
104   called a P-map (perfect map) for P . The problem is to find P-map G for distribution P . This problem
105   is known as structure learning. There can be more than one P-map for a distribution P , e.g., two
106   DAGs G1 and G2 where I(G1 ) = I(G2 ) = I(P ). P-maps of the same distribution have the same
107   skeleton and immoralities (Koller & Friedman, 2009). Consequently, the set of all P-maps for a
      distribution P is represented by a partially DAG (PDAG) that is a graph over nodes X where two


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      nodes are adjacent, if they are adjacent in all of the P-maps and the connected edge is directed if all
109   of the P-maps have the same direction, otherwise the edge is undirected. This PDAG is called the
110   P-map class PDAG for P . The structure learning problem is often reduced to finding the P-map class
111   PDAG for P .
112   Problem 1 (Structure learning). Consider the set of random variables X with distribution P that
113   admits a P-map. Find the P-map class PDAG for P .
114
115   Several constraint-based algorithms, such as Peter-Clark (PC) (Spirtes et al., 2000), and score-based
116
      algorithms with a consistent score, such as BIC, that perform an exhaustive search over the DAG
      space, are shown to solve Problem 1. We call an algorithm that solves Problem 1, a P-map learner
117
      (Koller & Friedman, 2009). Problem 1 is NP-hard and cannot be practically solved for a large number
118
      of variables n (Koller & Friedman, 2009).

123   3.1    T HE IDEA
124
      Our goal is to solve Problem 1 in a distributed manner as explained intuitively below.
125
126   Example 1. The DAG in figure 1 (a), denoted by G, is a P-map for the joint distribution of random
127   variables X1 , . . . , X5 . Instead of learning the whole DAG at once, one can learn separately the P-map
128
      class PDAG of each of the sub-DAGs for X1 = {X1 , X2 , X3 }, X2 = {X4 , X3 }, and X3 = {X5 , X3 }
      (figure 1 (b)), and then concatenate (and orient) them to obtain P-map G (figure 1 (c)). The reason is
129
      that each of the three subsets are d-separated, and hence, independent, from one another given their
130
      shared variable X3 , i.e.,
131
132                         X1 , X2 ⊥ X4 | X3 , X1 , X2 ⊥ X5 | X3 , X4 ⊥ X5 | X3 .
133
      Thus, when learning the structure of say the subset {X1 , X2 , X3 }, there is no active path between
134   any of X1 and X2 to the other nodes (excluding X3 ). This ensures two points. First, X1 and X2 are
135   not connected by a path outside of {X1 , X2 , X3 }; that is, all of their dependencies are captured by
136   this set. Hence, a structure learner can correctly learn the structure between these nodes without
137   using the information from the other nodes X4 and X5 . Second, when concatenating the graphs, no
138   additional link between the subsets are needed. This idea does not apply to the partitioning subsets
139   {X1 , X4 } and {X2 , X3 , X5 }, because X1 and X4 do depend on {X2 , X3 , X5 }:
140
                                            {X1 , X4 } ̸⊥ {X2 , X3 , X5 }.
141
142   Nodes X1 and X4 are related by a path outside of {X1 , X4 }, e.g., X1 → X3 → X4 . Hence, when
143   learning the structure of {X1 , X4 }, the structure learner will incorrectly make X1 and X4 adjacent,
144   because they are dependent. Similarly, subsets {X1 , X3 , X4 } and {X2 , X3 , X5 } would not work
145
      either. Because although the P-map of each subset can be learned correctly, the concatenation would
      require an additional link between X1 and X2 to recover G.

      P [Y] denote the marginal probability distribution of variables Y, and G[Y] denote that sub-graph of

      G limited to nodes Y and their connecting edges. The goal is to solve Problem 1 by an algorithm that
163   is distributed over the nodes, that is, to divide nodes X into possibly overlapping subsets X1 , . . . , XI ,
164   so that every sub-graph G[Xi ], i = 1, . . . , I, of the P-map class PDAG G for P can be learned
165   separately without using the information of the other nodes Xj , j ̸= i, and at the end to concatenate
166   the sub-graphs so that the resulting is a P-map class PDAG for P .
167
      The key step in this approach is the division of the nodes. Define a cover of X as a family of distinct
168
      nonempty subsets X1 , . . . , XI ⊆ X for some I ≥ 1 such that ∪Ii=1 Xi = X . Define the boundary of
169   Xi , i = 1, . . . , I, by bd(Xi ) = Xi ∩ (∪j̸=i Xj ), and the interior of Xi by Xio = Xi \ bd(Xi ). The
170   union of all boundaries is called the separator, denoted W = ∪i bd(Xi ). Correspondingly, the cover
171   {X1 , . . . , XI } for X is referred to as the cover separated by W. In Example 1, W = {X3 }. The
172   union of arbitrary graphs G1 , . . . , GI , denoted ∪Ii=1 Gi is a graph with the node and edge set equal to
173   the union of the nodes and edges of the graphs Gi , and an edge X − Y is directed from X to Y if it is
174   so in every Gi that includes this edge; otherwise, it is undirected.
175   Definition 3.1 (P-map reduction). Consider the set of random variables X with distribution P that
176   admits a P-map G. Let d ≥ 1 be an integer. A cover {X1 , . . . , XI }, I > 1, of X is a (capped-d)
177   P-map reduction if for all i = 1, . . . , I, (i) |Xi | ≤ d, (ii) G[Xi ] is a P-map for P [Xi ], and (iii) for all
178   j ̸= i, there is no edge between Xio and Xjo in G.
179
180   Condition (ii) ensures that separate P-map learners can be used to learn the P-map class PDAG of
181   each of the subsets Xi . Namely, they can be learned in parallel and without communication, i.e.,
182   decentrally. Condition (i) restricts each subset Xi to include at most d variables. The value of d can
183
      be chosen based on the computational capacity of the P-map learners. Once all G[Xi ]’s are learned,
      Condition (iii) ensures that their union will be a P-map for the complete distribution P , and hence,
184
      solves Problem 1.
185
186   The cover in Example 1 is a P-map reduction (Remark A.2). Does a P-map reduction exist for every
187   DAG? The answer is negative. For example in Figure 2-a, every pair of nodes are connected by two
188   paths. Thus, to satisfy Condition (iii), every element of a P-map reduction must be of size at least
189
      two. However, then at least one element of the cover violates Condition (ii). For example, G[X1 ],
      which is the path X12 → X13 ← X1 → . . . → X9 , is not a P-map for P [X1 ] as that would require

      X9 and X12 to be adjacent.

214   consider subset Z ⊆ X . DAG G defined over X is a conditional P-map for P given Z if (i) P is

      faithful to G, and (ii) G is a conditional I-map for P ; that is, if d-sepG (X1 , X2 | X3 ), X1 , X2 , X3 ⊆ X ,
      then there exists Z0 ⊆ Z such that X1 ⊥ X2 | X3 ∪ Z0 .

      Definition 3.3 (Conditional P-map reduction). Consider the set of random variables X with distri-
217   bution P that admits a P-map G. Let d ≥ 1 be an integer. A cover {X1 , . . . , XI }, I > 1, of X is a
218   (capped-d) conditional P-map reduction if for all i = 1, . . . , I, (i) |Xi | ≤ d, (ii) G[Xi ] is a conditional

      According to Condition (ii) in Definition 3.3, every conditional independence in I(P [Xi ]) is included
223   in I(G[Xi ]). This ensures that constraint-based algorithms, such as PC, will not incorrectly eliminate
224   an edge when learning the structure of Xi . On the other hand, every d-separation in I(G[Xi ]) exists
225   in I(P [Xi ]) either itself or when some of the boundary nodes bd(Xi ) are additionally observed
226   (see Remark A.3 in the Appendix for why the second case does not always hold). This ensures
227   that PC can correctly identify the edges that do not exist between two interior nodes or an interior
228   node and a boundary node in G[Xio ]. Therefore, the P-map structure of the interiors Xio and their
229   connections to the boundary nodes bd(Xi ) can be learned in a decentralized way. Although the
230   intra and inter connections of the boundaries bd(Xi ) cannot be learned decentrally and generally
231
      requires information from the all of the elements. Moreover, Condition (iii) ensures that no appending
      between the interiors is required to obtain the P-map for X . In the following subsection, we explain
232
      how to learn the conditional P-maps and the structure of the boundaries of conditional P-map cover,
233
      yet we will first focus on finding the cover.

235   Problem 2. Given integer d ≥ 0 and set of random variables X with distribution P that admits a
236   P-map, find a capped-d conditional P-map reduction for X .
237
238
      The idea in Example 1 and Figure 2 to solve Problem 2 was to divide the nodes into subsets that are
239   d-separated given their common nodes. More specifically, we need a separator W and a partition of
240   the set X \ W into some subsets C1 , . . . , CI that are pairwise independent conditioned on W.
241
242
243   Definition 3.4 (Separated-by cover). Consider random variables X with distribution P and a subset
244   W ⊂ X . The cover for X separated by W is a collection of sets {W ∪ Ci }Ii=1 , I ≥ 1, such that (i)
245   {C1 , . . . , CI , W} is a partition for X , (ii) Ci ⊥ Cj | W for all distinct i, j = 1, . . . , I, (iii) and I is
246
      maximal.
247
248
249   To solve Problem 2, one can iteratively apply separators to the elements of a cover until they no longer
250
      decompose. How to find the cover separated by W? Consider an order for variables X , represented by
      vector X = [X1 , . . . , Xn ]⊤ . Vector XW is defined as X where the elements of W are removed and
251
      let [XW ]i be the ith entry of XW . For subset W ⊂ X , define the symmetric (n − |W|) × (n − |W|)
252
      dependency matrix DW by DW (i, j) = 1 if [XW ]i ̸⊥ [XW ]j | W and otherwise DW (i, j) = 0 for
253   all i, j ∈ {1, . . . , n − |W|}, with DW (i, i) = 1 for all i. By using a permutation matrix P , we have
254   D̄W = P DW P −1 where D̄W is the block diagonal form of DW . Then each group of entries of the
255   transformed vector X̄W = P XW that correspond to a block of D̄W constitutes one of the desired
256   partitions, which combined with W form an element of the P-map reduction (see Example 1-revisited
257   in the appendix). Nevertheless, finding the permutation matrix can be computationally costly. An
258   alternative is to treat the dependency matrix as an adjacency matrix, defining an undirected graph and
259   find the connected components of this graph. This can be done in O(n2 ) (Cormen et al., 2001).

503   5    C ONCLUSION
504
505
506   We developed a distributed approach for structure learning applicable to both constraint-based and
507   score-based algorithms. The main concept is to identify a cover set for the set of variables using
508   conditional independence (CI) tests. Two key parameters, the upper bound of the cardinality of
509   cover elements d and the number of conditioning variables W play crucial roles in determining an
510   appropriate cover set. Reducing the value of d while increasing W can decrease the cardinality of
511   the greatest cover element and increase the number of cover elements. However, this adjustment
512   may lead to an increase in the number of CI tests, which in turn could raise the runtime of the cover-
513
      finding algorithms. Algorithms 2 and 3 are executed in parallel across multiple CPUs. Consequently,
      increasing the number of CPUs can help reduce runtime. Thus, it is essential to select the values of d,
514
      W , and the number of CPUs carefully to ensure that the cardinality of the greatest cover element
515
      remains small, enabling the runtime to be less than that of the standard version of the structure
516   learning algorithm.
517
518   One might argue that increasing the number of CPUs, only to reduce runtime by a factor of two,
519
      might seem like an inefficient use of resources. However, it is important to recognize that in the
      realm of parallel computation, particularly across nodes, alternatives for comparison are limited.
520
      Existing methods either parallelize the CI tests for each edge, which still requires substantial memory
521
      to load the entire graph, or they depend on expert knowledge to inform the process. Our approach
522   complements these by focusing on breaking the graph into manageable pieces, allowing any of these
523   methods to be applied efficiently to the cover elements. Furthermore, this process can now occur
524   in parallel across CPUs, which are generally more cost-effective and accessible than GPUs. This
525   flexibility not only broadens the applicability of our approach but also makes it feasible in a wider
526   range of computational environments.
527
      The proposed approach results in exact distributed structure learning algorithms. Specifically, it has
528   been demonstrated that the output of Algorithm 1 yields the exact structure without any approximation
529   in cover finding, local structure learning, and the concatenation of local structures. In addition, unlike
530   other exact distributed algorithms (Xie et al., 2006) and (Liu et al., 2017), which rely on expert
531   knowledge and conditional independence tests with high-order conditioning variables, the proposed
532   approach utilizes only a low-order conditioning set bounded by W .
533
      In summary, our distributed structure learning approach efficiently handles large Bayesian networks
534   by breaking the problem into smaller, manageable components, allowing for parallel execution on
535   multiple CPUs. This method achieves exact results without requiring expert knowledge or high-order
536   conditioning, offering a practical solution to the scalability issues in traditional algorithms. By
537   reducing memory demands and enabling flexible integration with existing techniques, our approach
538   enhances computational efficiency while preserving accuracy, making it a valuable tool for large-scale
539   structure learning across diverse domains. As computational demands continue to grow, this work
      lays a strong foundation for the scalable and accurate learning of complex probabilistic models.
