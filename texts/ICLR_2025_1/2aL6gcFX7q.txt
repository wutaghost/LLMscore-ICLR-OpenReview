009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved success across various domains
012            but also exhibit problematic issues, such as hallucinations. Retrieval-Augmented
013
               Generation (RAG) effectively alleviates these problems by incorporating external
               information to improve the factual accuracy of LLM-generated content. However,
014
               recent studies reveal that RAG systems are vulnerable to adversarial poisoning
015
               attacks, where attackers manipulate retrieval systems by poisoning the data corpus
016            used for retrieval. These attacks raise serious safety concerns, as they can easily
017            bypass existing defenses. In this work, we address these safety issues by first
018            providing insights into the factors contributing to successful attacks. In particular,
019            we show that more effective poisoning attacks tend to occur along directions where
020            the clean data distribution exhibits small variances. Based on these insights, we
021            propose two strategies. First, we introduce a new defense, named DRS (Directional
022            Relative Shifts), which examines shifts along those directions where effective
023            attacks are likely to occur. Second, we develop a new attack algorithm to generate
024
               more stealthy poisoning data (i.e., less detectable) by regularizing the poisoning
               data’s DRS. We conducted extensive experiments across multiple application sce-
025
               narios, including RAG Agent and dense passage retrieval for Q&A, to demonstrate
026
               the effectiveness of our proposed methods.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive performance across various bench-
031   mark tasks in many domains (Achiam et al., 2023; Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023). However, LLMs
032   can also exhibit several problematic behaviors, such as hallucinations (Ji et al., 2023) and bias (Ben-
033
      der et al., 2021), which could possibly lead to dire consequences when applied in safety-critical
      areas like healthcare (Tian et al., 2024). To address these issues, Retrieval-Augmented Generation
034
      (RAG) (Khandelwal et al., 2019; Lewis et al., 2020; Borgeaud et al., 2022; Ram et al., 2023) has been
035
      introduced as a promising approach that integrates external knowledge into LLM outputs, offering a
036   potential solution.
037
038   Typical RAG operates through two procedures: retrieval and generation. When a query is received, it
039
      first retrieves relevant information from an external data source, such as Wikipedia, and then combines
      this retrieved nonparametric knowledge (i.e., external knowledge) with the LLM’s parametric knowl-
040
      edge (i.e., internal knowledge) to generate the final output. Extensive empirical evidence suggests
041
      that LLMs leveraging RAG can effectively reduce hallucination (Bender et al., 2021; Kirchenbauer
042   & Barns, 2024; Li et al., 2024a) and improve prediction accuracy in knowledge-intensive domains
043   such as finance and medicine (Borgeaud et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2024). For example, research
044   by (Kirchenbauer & Barns, 2024) incorporated a RAG system into the Mistral-family LLMs and
045   observed significant improvements in factual accuracy.
046
      Despite the aforementioned benefits of RAG, a line of very recent work has demonstrated that RAG
047
      systems are vulnerable to adversarial poisoning attacks across multiple application scenarios (Zou
048   et al., 2024; RoyChowdhury et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b;a; Tan et al., 2024; Shafran et al., 2024;
049   Xue et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024). In these attacks, malicious attackers exploit the openly accessible
050   nature of the database corpus used for retrieval in RAG—such as Wikipedia (Zou et al., 2024; Deng
051   et al., 2024). By injecting attacker-specified data into the corpus, attackers can manipulate the
052   retriever to return the poisoned data as the most relevant documents in response to attacker-specified
053   queries, thereby increasing the chance that LLMs will generate adversarial outputs when relying on
      the poisoned data.

055   summarized in Table 1, they all involve poisoning the data corpus used for retrieval, even though the
056   attacks may differ in their level of access to the retrievers and/or LLMs. The ultimate goal of all these
057   attacks remains the same: to have the injected poisoned data retrieved in response to attacker-specified
058   queries, thereby influencing the subsequent LLM generation. On the one hand, categorizing these
059   RAG attacks as data poisoning is promising, given the extensive body of research on defending
060   against such attacks. On the other hand, data poisoning attacks, like those in the computer vision
061   literature, remain difficult to defend against (Liang et al., 2022). This naturally raises a question: are
062   data poisoning attacks against retrieval systems just as difficult to defend against as data poisoning
063
      attacks in computer vision?
064   Several recent studies have demonstrated that existing state-of-the-art defenses can be ineffective
065   against these attacks. For instance, perplexity-based filters, which examine the perplexity of doc-
066   uments and flag those with abnormally high or low perplexity values, have proven ineffective for
067   detecting poisoned documents (Chen et al., 2024a; Zou et al., 2024). Given the increasing use of
068   RAG systems in safety-critical domains like healthcare, it is crucial to identify why current defenses
069   are failing and to design new approaches to ensure their secure implementation.
070
      In this work, we investigate the safety vulnerabilities in RAG systems, focusing on targeted data
071   poisoning attacks aimed at retrieval systems. We begin by analyzing why these attacks are effective
072   (in terms of attack success rates) and difficult to defend against. Building on this analysis, we
073   introduce two designs: (1) a novel algorithm to mitigate these attacks, and (2) a method for generating
074   adversarial poisoning data that is harder to detect. Our main contributions are summarized as follows.
075
             • Insights into understanding the effectiveness of targeted data poisoning attacks against
076
               retrieval systems. There are two types of data poisoning attacks for RAG: (i) targeted
077
               attacks and (ii) untargeted attacks. Targeted attacks refer to attacks aimed specifically at a set
078            of attacker-specified data (e.g., pre-selected questions (Zou et al., 2024)), while untargeted
079            attacks aim to affect all data. We focus on targeted attacks, which make up most data
080            poisoning attacks (see Table 1), as previous research has shown that untargeted attacks can
081            be effectively mitigated using existing methods (Zhong et al., 2023). First, we demonstrate
082            that these targeted attacks can be characterized using a common objective function(s). This
083            formulation allows us to provide both quantitative and qualitative insights into the question:
084            What are the most effective methods for conducting these attacks? In particular, we show
085            that more effective attacks should occur along the directions where the clean data distribution
086
               (to be specified) diminishes most rapidly.
087          • Derivations from developed insights (I): a new defense method against retrieval poi-
088            soning attacks. Based on developed insights, we propose a new metric, dubbed DRS
089            (Directional Relative Shifts), along with a corresponding filter-based defense utilizing the
090            proposed DRS. Specifically, the DRS (to be defined) measures the relative shifts of future
091            test documents that occur along the directions of clean documents with low eigenvalues. If
092            the DRS score of a future test document is sufficiently abnormal compared to those of clean

      Information retrieval In recent decades, several key trends have emerged in the Information Retrieval
      (IR) literature. Classical sparse retrievers, such as BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009), rely on lexical
135
      matching and perform well when there is simple lexical overlap. However, in many domains, their
136
      performance lags behind that of dense retrievers (Zhao et al., 2024). Dense retrievers (Karpukhin
137   et al., 2020; Izacard et al., 2021; Cohan et al., 2020), which leverage deep neural networks to match
138   queries and documents based on semantic meaning, have demonstrated superior performance across
139   a wide range of tasks (Zhao et al., 2024). One limitation of dense retrievers is that their components
140   are often trained in isolation, which can negatively impact performance (Li et al., 2024b). To address
141   this, generative retrievers have emerged, leveraging LLMs to generate relevant content in response to
142   queries rather than retrieving documents (Bevilacqua et al., 2022).
143
      Adversarial attacks against dense retrieval systems Dense retrieval (DR) systems have recently
144
      been shown to be vulnerable to a series of adversarial attacks (Liu et al., 2023b; Zhong et al., 2023;
145
      Long et al., 2024), which share similarities with black-hat search engine optimization techniques
146   that have targeted traditional search engines (Gyongyi & Garcia-Molina, 2005). These adversarial
147   attacks against DR systems share the same goal: manipulating the retriever to return attacker-crafted
148   content, though their specific approaches differ. For example, (Liu et al., 2023b) assumes black-box
149   access to the underlying retriever, using a surrogate model built to mimic the original system and
150   craft poisoned data. In contrast, (Long et al., 2024) explores backdoor attacks under the assumption
151   of white-box access, where attackers inject pre-specified query-response pairs into the training data,
152   causing the retriever to return manipulated responses when presented with corresponding poisoned
153   queries during inference.
154
      Adversarial attacks against RAG The majority of existing RAG attacks focus on compromising
155
      retrieval systems with the goal of tricking them into retrieving adversarial documents (Zou et al.,
156   2024; RoyChowdhury et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b;a; Tan et al., 2024; Shafran et al., 2024; Xue
157   et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024). These attacks require varying levels of access to the retrievers and/or
158   the LLMs, such as white-box (Tan et al., 2024) or black-box (Zou et al., 2024). However, all of these
159   attacks need access to inject poisoned data into the underlying data corpus used by the RAG system,
160   as summarized in Table 1. Additionally, almost all of them are targeted attacks, aimed at a particular
161   subset of data, rather than indiscriminately affecting the entire dataset. In this sense, RAG attacks can
      essentially be regarded as targeted data poisoning attacks against the retrievers.

252   Theorem 1 (On the effectiveness of attacks). Under the above assumptions, attackers’ goals as

255   The above result can be intuitively interpreted as follows: The attacker aims to ensure that, when using

257   the attacker can create a set of adversarial queries that are significantly different from the clean ones,

260   food-related topics. The attacker could achieve their goal by using queries related to mathematics,
261
      which are irrelevant to the clean documents.

263   from the clean ones, they might be easily detected by simple human inspection. Therefore, to ensure
264   that attacks remain effective under potential defenses, the attacker is more interested in the following
265   question: Given the maximum deviation (e.g., ℓ2 distance) between the normal distribution Qnormal
266   and adversary distribution Qadv , what is the most effective direction(s) for moving Qnormal to Qadv ?

      Remark 3 (Intuitions behind Corollary 1). We provide a detailed interpretation of the above results.
271   First, directions in Dclean whose density decays rapidly can typically corresponds to those directions
272   with low variance across a broad range of distributions with well-behaved tails. Low variance in
273   a given direction means that the majority of the probability mass is concentrated around the mean.
274   Consequently, even a small deviation from the mean significantly reduces the probability mass in that
275   area.
276
      This behavior aligns well with the attacker’s objectives. If a direction has low variance, perturbing a
277
      clean query along that direction will greatly decrease the likelihood of clean documents being near the
278
      perturbed query. As a result, adversarial queries will more likely retrieve poisoned documents, as the
279   nearest neighbors around the query will predominantly be adversarial (according to Assumption 1).
280   This indicates that the attacker’s goals are effectively achieved.

309   is to protect a set of pre-selected queries of interest
310   denoted as Q, such as specific medical questions,
311
      from data poisoning attacks. Here, our defense aims preventing poisoned documents targeting these

      targeted queries rather than all possible queries. As the number of queries increases significantly, the

      4.2    N EW ATTACK ALGORITHMS FOR GENERATING MORE STEALTHY POISONING DATA
367
368   Fig. 2 demonstrates that the proposed DRS can effectively distinguish between clean and adversarial
369   documents. This raises a question: if the attacker is aware that the defender will employ the DRS based
370
      detection, how will an attacker respond? In this section, we address this question by proposing new
      attack algorithms designed to generate more stealthy poisoning data that may bypass the previously
371
      established DRS scores.
372
373   The high-level idea behind this new series of attack algorithms is to apply regularization techniques
374   when creating poisoning data to penalize large DRS scores for that data. Because each attack,
375   as outlined in Table 1, has its own way of achieving the attacker’s dual goals (as specified in
376   Section 3), their corresponding new attack algorithms (by adding DRS regularization) may slightly
377   differ depending on the context. In the following, we will use the attack proposed in Chen et al.
      (2024a) to demonstrate how our newly proposed attack algorithm works specifically.

401

402
      minimizing the distances within the poisoned documents to enhance retrieval performance. However,
403   their attacks can be effectively filtered out using our proposed DRS, as demonstrated in Fig. 2(d)
404   under the attack name Ap. Now we propose to add a regularization term to the above original
405   objective function, resulting in our proposed algorithm:
406
                                   min −O1 + λ1 O2 + λ2 DRS scores on poisoned data,
407
408   where the DRS scores on poisoned data are calculated according to Algorithm 1 and λ2 > 0 is
409   a regularizing parameter. Since the calculation of DRS does not involve any non-differentiable
410   operations, existing gradient-based methods developed for optimizing the original objective functions
411   (namely, the objectives without the DRS scores) will still remain effective. The resulting poisoned
412
      documents from this new objective will have smaller DRS scores compared to those generated under
      the original objective, making them more likely to bypass the proposed defense.

532
      In this work, we study the safety issues associated with using RAG. In particular, we first show
533
      that most existing RAG attacks are essentially targeted data poisoning attacks. We then provide a
534   unified framework to examine these attacks and offer insights into their effectiveness. Specifically,
535   we demonstrate that more effective poisoning attacks tend to occur in directions where the clean
536   data distribution exhibits low variance. Based on these insights, we propose a new defense for
537   detecting poisoned data and introduce a series of new attacking algorithms that can potentially lead
538   to more stealthy (in terms of detection) data. We test both our proposed attacks and defenses on
539   various applications and observe consistent improvements. Proofs, detailed experimental setups, and
      additional ablation studies are included in the appendix.
