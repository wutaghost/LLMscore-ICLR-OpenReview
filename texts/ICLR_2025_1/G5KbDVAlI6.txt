011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The study of gene regulatory network inference (GRNI), with a focus on uncovering
014            causal relations among genes, holds significant potential to explain fundamental
015
               biological processes, such as how cellular identity is established or disrupted in
               disease. Unfortunately, current methods fail to adequately interpret the widespread
016
               phenomena of differential gene expression. The limitation can largely be attributed
017
               to the overlook of the selection process (e.g., survival bias), which is ubiquitous
018            and fundamental in biology. Furthermore, recent studies have shown that gene
019            expression is regulated by latent confounders (e.g., non-coding RNAs). Both of
020            which can lead to spurious dependencies, thereby distorting GRNI results. To
021            mitigate these challenges, we propose a novel algorithm, called Gene Regulatory
022            Network Inference in the presence of Selection bias and Latent confounders (GISL).
023            It is designed to uncover the causal structure by leveraging data across multiple
024            distributions obtained via gene perturbation. Surprisingly, we find that the qualita-
025            tive structure information, selection process, and latent confounders are partially
026
               identifiable without any parametric assumption under mild graphical conditions.
               Experimental results on both synthetic and real-world single-cell gene expression
027
               datasets demonstrate the superiority of GISL over existing strong baseline methods.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Gene Regulatory Networks (GRNs), where nodes represent genes and directed edges signify cross-
033   gene causal relations (Levine & Davidson, 2005), playing a pivotal role in understanding the biological
034   processes at the molecular level and disease mechanisms like cancer (Hanahan & Weinberg, 2000).
035   The differential gene expression (Robinson et al., 2010), particularly the variation in the distribution
036   of the same gene across different cell types, is usually interpreted by latent factors, including but
037   not limited to latent confounders, e.g., Non-coding RNAs, environmental stimuli, and cell type
038   composition (Gasch et al., 2000; Statello et al., 2021; Razin & Gavrilov, 2021). However, the
039
      distribution changes of unregulated genes in gene perturbation data raise our curiosity to explore the
      underlying true data generation process. We argue that this is due to the overlook of the selection
040
      process, which is ubiquitous and fundamental in cells. Then, both selection processes and latent
041
      confounders lead to spurious edges, which severely bias the GRNI, as they do not have causal
042   relations in between. This motivates us to identify selection processes and latent confounders, and to
043   recover regulatory relations from observed dependencies.

107   and perturbation data, some interesting findings offer insight into tackling this problem. Specifically,
      the dependencies arising from causation, selection process, and latent confounders exhibit differences

131
      A Gene regulatory network (GRN) (Levine & Davidson, 2005), focusing on the causal relations
132
      and governing gene activities in cell populations, can be represented by a causal model (Ram et al.,
133
      2006). The data X = {X1 , X2 , ..., XN } consists of observed variables where each Xi represents an
134   individual gene. Let G = (V , E) be a directed acyclic graph (DAG) model with the vertex set V
135   and edge set E, where V = {X, S, L, I} encapsulates all observed variables X, latent selection
136   variables S, latent confounders L, and perturbation indicator I. Data Do represents observational
137   data, and Dpi is perturbation data with perturbing gene Xi .
138
      To introduce the different structures of a causal model, the definition of basic terms should be clear.
139
      A causal relation is represented by a directed edge, e.g., Xi → Xj , where Xi , Xj ∈ X. This is
140   also described as Xi is the parent of Xj . In biology, gene Xi regulates gene Xj by intermediate
141   medium, i.e. protein. We also refer to the mechanism underlying a causal relationship as a causal
142   process. If there is a direct path like Xi → · · · → Xj between them, Xi is called the ancestor of Xj .
143   We denote latent confounder as Lk ∈ L, which is a hidden common cause working on confounded
144   pair in 2.2 contributing to dependence that does not have cause relation. Different from observed
145   variables and latent confounders, the selection process represented by structures (Xi → Sk ← Xj )
146   with selection variable Sk ∈ S. We can only observe the data points for which the selection criterion
147   is met, i.e., Sk = 1. As Sk is always given, the data distribution actually is P (X|S), resulting in
148
      spurious dependence between Xi and Xj . Some other basic concepts can be found in A.
149
150
151   Definition 2.1 (Selection bias) The distribution P of the variable in the set V is biased by the
152
      selection processes.
153
154   Definition 2.2 (Confounded pair) A pair (Xi , Xj ) is a confounded pair, denoted as (Xi , Xj )l . If
155
      there exists a latent variable Lk ∈ L that is the ancestor of a pair (Xi , Xj ), and the vertices (apart
      from Xi , Xj ) on the path between Lk and Xi , Xj are latent (Xi ← · · · ← Lk → · · · → Xj ).
156
157   Definition 2.3 (Selection pair) A pair (Xi , Xj ) is a selection pair, denoted as (Xi , Xj )s , if it fol-
158   lows the structure (Xi → Sk ← Xj ).
159
160   Definition 2.4 (DAG-inducing path) In a DAG G, if a path p between two observed vertices
161   (Xi , Xj ) relative L, S is called a DAG-inducing path, if it satisfies the following criteria: 1. There
      is at least one collider on the path p apart from (Xi , Xj ). 2. Every vertex on p is either in L or a

      4    PARTIAL I DENTIFIABILITY W ITH L ATENT C ONFOUNDERS

      We previously discussed methods for identifying direct causal relationships and selection mechanisms
239
      between genes, assuming no latent confounders. However, in practical scenarios using scRNA-seq
240   data, latent confounders, such as non-gene regulators, transcription factors, and technical covari-
241   ates, can indeed exist. This raises the question: What can be definitively identified about causal
242   relationships when latent confounders are present?
243
      A most generalized model might include latent variables, perturbation indicators, and observed
244
      variables all involved in the selection process (e.g., under some unrecorded experimental conditions,
245
      only cells with certain gene expression patterns can successfully receive some gene knockout).
246   Nonetheless, such generalized assumptions often render causal relationships too indeterminate, and
247   thus the results less informative. For example, a direct causal edge X → Y can generally always
248   be replaced with X → S ← L → Y , where X, Y are observed, L is latent, and S is a selection
249   indicator, rendering them indistinguishable in terms of all conditional independence constraints, even
250   with interventional data for IX and IY on both sides.
251
      To address this, we have to adopt a structural assumption: selection processes involve only observed
252   variables, disallowing any causal edges from latent variables (L) and perturbation indicators (I) to
253   the selection indicators S. This assumption is partly justified by the typically lower prevalence of
254   confounders compared to observed variables in scRNA-seq data. Under this framework, what can we
255   identify? We first notice that even without selection and with interventional data, latent confounders
256   can still make the direct causal relations unidentifiable. Consider the case X → Z → Y with a latent
257   confounder L pointing to both Z and Y shown in Figure 9 (b). Adding a direct edge X → Y renders
258   the scenarios equivalent, even if perturbation data IX , IY are available, as the dependence between
259   X and Y cannot solely be explained by Z.
260   This leads us to question whether ancestral causal relationships (X has a direct path to Y ), instead
261   of direct causal relations, are identifiable with latent confounders. Unfortunately, the answer is still
262   negative. For instance, in the model S1 ← X ← L → Y → S2 (Figure 9 (a)), with latent confounder
263   L and selection indicators S1 , S2 , whether adding a direct edge X → Y or not, the two scenarios are
264   unidentifiable, even with interventional data: perturbing X alters P (Y ), and this change cannot be
265   solely attributed to X; the same happens at the Y side.
266   Thus, given all the above unidentifiable cases, we conclude that we can only identify the ancestral
267   causal relations and the absence of selection. If all of the following hold: 1) IX ̸⊥⊥ Y , i.e., the pertur-
268   bation on X results in a change in P (Y ), 2) IX ⊥⊥ Y |X, i.e., this change is completely explainable
269   by X, and 3) IY ⊥  ⊥ X, i.e., the perturbation on Y does not affect P (X), then it can be concluded
      that X is an ancestor of Y , and there is no selection for each of X, Y . This is a sufficient condition,

      6    C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
514
515
      Rethinking differential gene expression and the observed distributional changes in unregulated genes
516
      from gene perturbation data, we argue that the overlooked selection process and the presence of
517   latent confounders significantly bias the performance of gene regulatory network inference (GRNI)
518   in single-cell gene expression data. Many confusing dependent patterns observed from data can be
519   explained by the selection inclusion and latent confounders. Although with a single distribution, it is
520   generally difficult to identify the causal process, selection process, and latent confounders, thanks to
521   gene perturbation data, which provides observations of the differences in symmetry and perturbation
522   effect among them, resulting in distinguishable conditional independent patterns. This motivates us
523   to establish a set of theoretical results demonstrating the partial identifiability of qualitative structure
524   information, latent confounders, and selection processes without any parametric and graphical
525
      assumptions. At the same time, we propose a novel GISL algorithm to recover the selection process
      and latent confounders from causal relations in confusing dependencies among genes. The validity
526
      of the presence of the selection process, theoretical claims, and the algorithm’s efficacy have been
527
      rigorously evaluated on synthetic and real-world data.
528
529   Discussion and Limitations. In cells, we argue the different intracellular environments, acting as
530   selection mechanisms, constrain the expression of genes. When the environment remains, a selection
531
      mechanism is always present. Genes stay in cells with the remaining environment, showing the
      reasonability of our setting. However, at the algorithmic level, if selection does not occur consistently,
532
      whether the intervention happens before or after the selection process will lead to different phenomena.
533
      A toy example is designed to introduce this as shown in Figure 14 in Appendix. This interesting
534   discussion is a kind reminder to readers when they apply this algorithm to some specific data, like
535   patients in hospitals. When they recovered, they were still the sample in the dataset. At this time
536   the selection mechanism disappears. Some limitations are listed that are willing to be improved
537   in the future. In our setting, we assume the gene regulatory network is DAG dealing with acyclic
538   relations. The selection process may also work on latent confounders. We focus on the selection
539   process determined by measured genes. Moreover, we focus on the soundness and efficacy of our
      algorithm and do not pay much attention to the efficiency of the CI test.

      A    C ONCEPTS
709
710
711   Definition A.1 (Marginal independence test) Check whether two variables X and Y are indepen-
712   dent of each other without considering any other variables. Mathematically: X ⊥⊥ Y , meaning X
713   and Y are independent in the overall data distribution.
714
715   Definition A.2 (Conditional independence test) Evaluate whether two variables X and Y are
716
      independent given a third variable or set of variables Z. Mathematically: X ⊥⊥ Y |Z, meaning X
      and Y are independent conditioned on Z.
717
718   Definition A.3 (d-separation) If every path from a node in X to a node in Y is d-separated by Z,
719   then X and Y are always conditionally independent given Z.

       the perturbed one and the normal one, which means under the selection process. In some cell lines, it

1072   I             C OMPARED WITH COMPUTATIONAL METHODS
1073
1074   We rethink the gene regulatory network inference from a causal view and focus on identifying the
