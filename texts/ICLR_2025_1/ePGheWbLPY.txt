009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Many important problems require modelling large-scale spatio-temporal datasets,
012            with one prevalent example being weather forecasting. Recently, transformer-
013            based approaches have shown great promise in a range of weather forecasting
014            problems. However, these have mostly focused on gridded data sources, neglect-
015            ing the wealth of unstructured, off-the-grid data from observational measurements
016            such as those at weather stations. A promising family of models suitable for such
017            tasks are neural processes (NPs), notably the family of transformer neural pro-
018
               cesses (TNPs). Although TNPs have shown promise on small spatio-temporal
               datasets, they are unable to scale to the quantities of data used by state-of-the-art
019
               weather and climate models. This limitation stems from their lack of efficient at-
020
               tention mechanisms. We address this shortcoming through the introduction of
021            gridded pseudo-token TNPs which employ specialised encoders and decoders
022            to handle unstructured observations and utilise a processor containing gridded
023            pseudo-tokens that leverage efficient attention mechanisms. Our method consis-
024            tently outperforms a range of strong baselines on various synthetic and real-world
025            regression tasks involving large-scale data, while maintaining competitive com-
026            putational efficiency. The real-life experiments are performed on weather data,
027            demonstrating the potential of our approach to bring performance and computa-
028            tional benefits when applied at scale in a weather modelling pipeline.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Many spatio-temporal modelling problems are being transformed by the proliferation of data from
      in situ sensors, remote observations, and scientific computing models. The opportunities presented
034
      have led to a surge of interest from the machine learning community to develop new tools and models
035
      to support these efforts. One prominent example of this transformation is in medium-range weather
036   and environmental forecasting where a new generation of machine learning models have improved
037   performance and reduced computational costs, including: Aurora (Bodnar et al., 2024); GraphCast
038   (Lam et al., 2022); GenCast (Price et al., 2023); PanguWeather (Bi et al., 2022); ClimaX (Nguyen
039   et al., 2023), FuXi (Chen et al., 2023b); and FengWu (Chen et al., 2023a). All of these models
040   operate on sets of environmental variables which are regularly structured in space and time, allowing
041   them to leverage architectures developed in the vision and language community such as the Vision
042   Transformer (ViT; Dosovitskiy et al. 2020), Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021), and Perceiver
043   (Jaegle et al., 2021). These models are trained and deployed on data arising from computationally
044
      intensive scientific simulation and analysis techniques, which integrate observational data (e.g. from
      weather stations, ships, buoys, radiosondes, etc.) with simulation data to provide the best estimate
045
      of the atmosphere’s state. They are not currently trained on observational measurements directly.
046
047   We are now on the cusp of a second generation of these models which will also ingest unstructured
048   observational, alongside analysis data, or which will replace the need for analysis data entirely. This
049   second generation of forecasting systems will further improve accuracy and reduce computational
050
      costs. Thus far, there have only been a handful of early attempts to tackle this problem (Vaughan
      et al., 2024; McNally et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2023) and it is unclear what architec-
051
      tures would best support this setting. We therefore consider it fertile ground for impactful research.
052
053   Many problems considered can be formulated as repeatedly performing predictive inference condi-
      tioned on an ever-changing, large set of observations. Posed with the question of what framework

055   tured data, we turn towards neural processes (NPs; Garnelo et al. 2018a;b): a family of meta-learning
056   models that are able to map from datasets of arbitrary size and structure to predictions over outputs
057   at arbitrary input locations. NPs support a probabilistic treatment of observations, making them
058   capable of outputting uncertainty estimates that are crucial in, for example, forecasting systems.
059   Moreover, NPs are a flexible framework—unlike other models that only perform forecasting, they
060   are able to solve more general state estimation problems, including forecasting, data fusion, data in-
061   terpolation and data assimilation. Although early versions of NPs were severely limited, a string of
062   recent developments (Kim et al., 2019; Gordon et al., 2019; Nguyen & Grover, 2022; Ashman et al.,
063
      2024a; Feng et al., 2023; Bruinsma et al., 2021; Ashman et al., 2024b) have significantly improved
      their effectiveness, particularly for small-scale spatio-temporal regression problems. Their broad
064
      applicability has been demonstrated throughout literature, from a wide variety of spatio-temporal
065
      tasks such as climate downscaling, data assimilation and sensor placement (Vaughan et al., 2022;
066   Andersson et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Niu et al., 2024), to tasks as diverse as molecular property
067   prediction (Garcı́a-Ortegón et al., 2024). Notably, the family of transformer NPs (TNPs; Nguyen
068   & Grover 2022; Feng et al. 2023; Ashman et al. 2024a), which use transformer-based architectures
069   as the computational backbone, have demonstrated impressive performance on a range of tasks.
070   However, unlike the aforementioned large-scale environmental prediction models, TNPs are yet to
071   fully take advantage of efficient attention mechanisms. As a result of the quadratic computational
072   complexity associated with full attention, they have been unable to scale to complex spatio-temporal
073   datasets. This is because such techniques require structured—more specifically, gridded—datasets,
074
      and are thus not immediately applicable.
075   We pursue a straightforward solution: to encode the dataset onto a structured grid before passing
076   it through a transformer-based architecture. We refer to this family of models as gridded TNPs,
077   serving as a general-purpose tool for spatio-temporal state estimation. Our core contributions are:
078   1. We develop a novel attention-based grid encoder, based on the ideas of ‘pseudo-tokens’ (Jaegle
079      et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2019), which we show improves upon the performance
080      of traditional kernel-based interpolation methods.
081
      2. Equipped with the pseudo-token grid encoder, we are able to use efficient attention mechanisms
082      within the transformer backbone of the TNP, utilising advancements such as the ViT (Dosovitskiy
083      et al., 2020) and Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021).
084
      3. We develop an efficient k-nearest-neighbour attention-based grid decoder, facilitating the evalu-
085      ation of predictive distributions at arbitrary spatio-temporal locations. Remarkably, we find that
086      this improves performance over full attention.
087
      4. We empirically evaluate our model on a range of synthetic and real-world spatio-temporal re-
088
         gression tasks, demonstrating both the ability to 1) maintain strong performance on large spatio-
089      temporal datasets and 2) handle multiple sources of unstructured data effectively, all while main-
090      taining a low computational complexity.

147   2.2.2    P SEUDO -T OKEN -BASED T RANSFORMERS
148
      Pseudo-token-based transformers, first introduced by Jaegle et al. (2021) with the Perceiver, remedy
149
      the quadratic computational complexity induced by the standard transformer by condensing the
150
      set of N tokens, Z ∈ RN ×Dz , into a smaller set of M ≪ N ‘pseudo-tokens’, U ∈ RM ×Dz ,
151   using MHCA operations.1 These pseudo-tokens are then processed instead of operating on the
152   original set directly, reducing the computational complexity from O(N 2 )—the cost of performing
153   MHSA operations on the original set—to O(N M +M 2 )—the cost of performing MHCA operations
154   between the original set and pseudo-tokens, followed by MHSA operations on the pseudo-tokens.
155

179   3       R ELATED W ORK
180
181   Transformers for Point Cloud Data A closely related research area is point cloud data modelling
182   (Tychola et al., 2024), with transformer-based architectures employed for a variety of tasks (Lu et al.,
183   2022). Given the large amount of data used in some works, the use of efficient attention mechanisms
184   has been considered. A number of notable approaches use voxelisation, whereby unstructured point
185   clouds are encoded onto a structured grid (Mao et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022), prior to employing
186   efficient architectures that operate on grids. Both aforementioned works voxelise the unstructured
187   inputs through rasterisation, but implement efficient self-attention mechanisms such as Swin Trans-
188   former. Our pseudo-token grid encoder is heavily inspired by the voxel-based set attention (VSA)
189
      introduced by He et al. (2022). Similar to our approach, VSA cross-attends local neighbourhoods
      of unstructured tokens onto a structured grid of pseudo-tokens. However, VSA uses the same initial
190
      pseudo-token values for all grid locations, as the ‘cloud’ in which points exist are not equipped with
191
      unobserved information. Their use of a fixed set of initial values manifests a specific implementation
192   in which all tokens attend to the same set of pseudo-tokens. In contrast, for many spatio-temporal
193   problems there may exist potentially unobserved, fixed topographical information such as elevation,
194   land use and soil type, hence we employ different initial pseudo-token values for each grid location
195   which are capable of capturing this.
196
197   Models for Structured Weather Data The motivation behind our method is to develop mod-
198   els that are able to scale to massive spatio-temporal datasets. In recent years, doing so has gar-
199   nered a significant amount of interest from the research community, and several models have been
200   developed—some of the most successful of which target weather data. However, the majority of
201   these methods operate on structured, gridded data, and focus solely on forecasting.2 Models such as
202   Aurora (Bodnar et al., 2024), GraphCast (Lam et al., 2022), GenCast (Price et al., 2023), Pangu (Bi
203
      et al., 2022), FuXi (Chen et al., 2023b) and FengWu (Chen et al., 2023a) share a similar encoder-
      processor-decoder to our construction of CNPs presented in Section 2.1, in which the input grid
204
      is projected onto some latent space which is then transformed using an efficient form of informa-
205
      tion propagation (e.g. sparse attention with Swin Transformer (Bodnar et al., 2024; Bi et al., 2022),
206   message passing with GNNs (Price et al., 2023)) before being projected back onto the grid at the
207   output.3

      Although yielding impressive performance, these methods are inherently constrained in their
      application—forecasting with structured data—with predictions sharing the same locations as the
210
      inputs at each time point. The distinct advantage of NPs is their ability to model stochastic pro-

      cesses, which can be evaluated at any target location, and their ability to flexibly condition on un-

233
      unable to scale to the size characteristic of large spatio-temporal datasets. To address this short-
234

      4.2    G RID P ROCESSOR : E FFICIENT ATTENTION M ECHANISM -BASED T RANSFORMERS
301
302   While we are free to choose any architecture for processing the pseudo-token grid, including CNNs,
303   we focus on transformer-based architectures employing efficient attention mechanisms. Specifically,
304   we consider the use of ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021).
305   Typically, ViT employs patch encoding to coarsen a grid of tokens to a coarser grid of tokens,
306   upon which regular MHSA operations are applied. We consider both patch encoding and encoding
307   directly to a smaller grid using the grid encoder, which we collectively refer to when used in gridded
308
      TNPs as ViTNPs. The use of Swin Transformer has the advantage of being able to operate with a
      finer grid of pseudo-tokens, as only local neighbourhoods of tokens attend to each other at any one
309
      time. We refer to the use of Swin Transformer in gridded TNPs as Swin-TNP.

343   point-wise through some function. We refer to this approach as the multi pseudo-token grid encoder.

      Note that both methods can be applied analogously to the kernel-interpolation grid encoder.

      5.4   D ISCUSSION OF R ESULTS
511
512   We summarise our conclusions across all three experiments as follows: 1. gridded TNPs signifi-
513   cantly outperform all baselines while maintaining relatively low computational complexity; 2. the
514   PT-GE outperforms the KI-GE in almost all gridded TNPs, particularly when the grid is coarse;
515   3. when modelling multiple data sources, the use of source-specific grid encoders outperforms a sin-
516   gle, unified grid encoder; 4. Swin-TNP achieves either comparable or better performance than the
517   ViTNP at a smaller computational cost; and 5. in the grid decoder, nearest-neighbour cross-attention
518
      both reduces the computational cost and improves predictive performance relative to full cross-at-
      tention. In Appendix E, we provide additional experimental results showing that these conclusions
519
      are robust to changes in model size and dataset composition.

521
522   6     C ONCLUSION
523
524   This paper introduces gridded TNPs, an extension to the family of TNPs which facilitates the use
525
      of efficient attention-based transformer architectures such as the ViT and Swin Transformer. Grid-
      ded TNPs decompose the computational backbone of TNPs into three distinct components: 1. the
526
      grid encoder, which moves point-wise data representations onto a structured grid of pseudo-tokens;
527
      2. the grid processor, which processes this grid using computationally efficient operations; and 3. the
528   grid decoder, which evaluates the processed grid at arbitrary input locations. In achieving this, we
529   develop the pseudo-token grid encoder—a novel approach to moving unstructured spatio-temporal
530   data onto a grid of pseudo-tokens—and the pseudo-token grid decoder—a computationally efficient
531   approach of evaluating a grid of pseudo-tokens at arbitrary input locations. We compare the perfor-
532   mance of a number of gridded TNPs against several strong baselines, demonstrating significantly
533   better performance on large-scale synthetic and real-world regression tasks involving context sets
534   containing over 100, 000 datapoints. This work marks an initial step towards building architec-
535   tures for modelling large amounts of unstructured spatio-temporal observations. We believe that
536
      the methods developed in this paper can be used to both improve and broaden the capabilities of
      existing machine learning models for spatio-temporal data, including those targeting weather and
537
      environmental forecasting. We look forward to pursuing this in future work.

925   In this section we provide more details, alongside schematics, of our nearest-neighbour cross-
926   attention scheme. As mentioned in the main text, in the grid decoder we only allow a subset of
927   the gridded pseudo-tokens to attend to the target token (those in the vicinity of it). Finding the near-
928   est neighbours is not done in the standard k-nearest neighbours fashion, because we use the same
929   number of nearest neighbours along each dimension of the original data (i.e. latitude and longitude
930
      for spatial interpolation; latitude, longitude and time for spatio-temporal interpolation). This is to
      ensure that we do not introduce specific preferences for any dimension. In the case the data lies on
931
      a grid with the same spacing in all its dimensions, the procedure becomes equivalent to k-nearest
932
      neighbours.
933
934   In practice, we specify the total number of nearest-neighbours we want to use k. We then com-
                                                                                          1
935   pute the number of nearest-neighbours in each dimension by kdim = ceil(k dim(x) ), where dim(x)
936   represents the dimensionality of the input. For efficient batching purposes, we tend to choose
937   k = (2n − 1)dim(x) , where n ∈ N (i.e. 9 for experiments with latitude-longitude grids, 27 for
938   experiments with latitude-longitude-time grids). We then find the indices in each dimension of these
939   nearest-neighbours by performing an efficient search that leverages the gridded nature of the data,
940
      leading to a computational complexity of O(kNt ), with Nt the number of target points. When the
      neighbours go off the grid (i.e. for targets very close to the edges of the grid), we only consider the
941
      number of viable (i.e. within the bounds of the grid) neighbours.
