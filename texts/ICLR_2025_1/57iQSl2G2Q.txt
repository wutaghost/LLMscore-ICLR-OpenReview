009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Controller tuning and optimization have been among the most fundamental prob-
012            lems in robotics and mechatronic systems. The traditional methodology is usually
013            model-based, but its performance heavily relies on an accurate mathematical sys-
014            tem model. In control applications with complex dynamics, obtaining a precise
015            model is often challenging, leading us towards a data-driven approach. While var-
016            ious researchers have explored the optimization of a single controller, it remains a
017            challenge to obtain the optimal controller parameters safely and efficiently when
018
               multiple controllers are involved. In this paper, we propose S AFE C TRL BO to op-
               timize multiple controllers simultaneously and safely. We simplify the exploration
019
               process in safe Bayesian optimization, reducing computational effort without sac-
020
               rificing expansion capability. Additionally, we use additive kernels to enhance the
021            efficiency of Gaussian process updates for unknown functions. Hardware exper-
022            imental results on a permanent magnet synchronous motor (PMSM) demonstrate
023            that compared to existing safe Bayesian optimization algorithms, S AFE C TRL BO
024            can obtain optimal parameters more efficiently while ensuring safety.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Optimizing the parameters of complex systems with multiple controllers is a challenging task, par-
030   ticularly in configurations like the cascade feedback control architecture commonly used in motor
031   control, as well as advanced controllers involving feedforward control, disturbance observers (DOB)
032   (Jung & Oh, 2022), and active disturbance rejection control (ADRC) (Cao et al., 2024). For example,
033
      in permanent magnet synchronous motor (PMSM) control, field-oriented control (FOC) is widely
      employed (Gabriel et al., 1980; Lara et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2016). The closed-loop FOC config-
034
      uration involves three independent proportional-integral (PI) controllers, each requiring the tuning
035
      of two control gains. These six gains have different parameter ranges and must be optimized simul-
036   taneously to achieve the best control performance. Each adjustment of the parameter combination
037   requires an evaluation process lasting several minutes and also demands significant expertise from a
038   control engineer. Therefore, there is a strong need for an efficient, automatic optimization approach.
039
      Traditional automatic tuning and optimization methods rely on simplified reduced-order models
040
      with assumptions such as linearity. These assumptions, along with modeling errors, often lead to
041
      suboptimal performance of controllers in real-world systems (Berkenkamp et al., 2016). Mean-
042   while, motion data from real-world systems operating under suboptimal conditions often contain
043   valuable information that traditional model-based methods fail to fully exploit. Data-driven con-
044   trol optimization addresses this limitation by directly leveraging the information in the motion data
045   to optimize controller parameters. It typically models the system’s performance as a function of
046   controller parameters and then explores the optimal parameter iteratively. In this line of research,
047   various algorithms have been designed, with gradient-based algorithms being among the most pop-
048   ular approaches; however, they require accurate gradient estimates (Li et al., 2024), which can be
049   challenging to obtain with noisy experimental measurements and often lead to convergence at local
050
      optima. Genetic algorithms, on the other hand, require extensive testing, making them impractical
      for real-world applications (Davidor, Jan. 1991).
051
052   Bayesian optimization (BO) (Mockus, 2012) offers a solution to these challenges by modeling the
053   system’s performance function using a Gaussian process (GP) (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006). In
      this framework, each controller parameter combination is associated with a performance value repre-

054
      sented by a Gaussian distribution, which includes noise measurements. However, the BO procedure
055   iteratively tests parameters with the highest uncertainty, often evaluating potentially unsafe con-
056   troller parameters, which may lead to system instability. Therefore, controller optimization requires
057   the use of a safety-aware BO algorithm, and some relevant work is introduced as follows.
058
059   Related work. The S AFE O PT (Sui et al., 2015) and S TAGE O PT (Sui et al., 2018) algorithms were
060   among the first to address the safety concerns in Bayesian optimization (BO). They introduce the
061   concept of a safe set to avoid evaluating parameters that do not meet a predefined safety thresh-
062   old, thus ensuring safety. Subsequent work includes (Turchetta et al., 2019b; Bottero et al., 2022;
063   Fiedler, 2023; Fiedler et al., 2024; Whitehouse et al., 2023), focusing on algorithm performance
064   improvement or theoretical analysis. Berkenkamp et al. (2016) applied S AFE O PT to quadrotor con-
065   troller tuning, validating its practical effectiveness. They employed the Matérn kernel with ν = 3/2
066
      as the covariance function of the Gaussian process, which is effective mainly for low-dimensional
      problems (Bengio et al., 2005). In Berkenkamp et al. (2016), the x, y, and z-axis PD controllers
067
      of the quadrotor were optimized separately, with each controller having only two parameters. Sim-
068
      ilarly, Fiducioso et al. (2019) automated the tuning of only two parameters for a room temperature
069   controller in a simulator. Moreover, S AFE O PT employs a maximum uncertainty sampling acquisi-
070   tion function to balance exploration and exploitation, but this can cause fluctuations in the evaluated
071   objective function values, making it difficult to converge. While the stage-wise algorithm in Sui
072   et al. (2018) guarantees convergence in the optimization phase, it still does not improve efficiency
073   in higher dimensions.
074
      Djolonga et al. (2013) suggested that high-dimensional problems could be decomposed into several
075   lower-dimensional subspaces for optimization. Building on this, Kirschner et al. (2019) proposed
076   the L INE BO algorithm, which decomposes a high-dimensional space into multiple one-dimensional
077   subspaces for safe BO within each subspace. However, this method often requires hundreds or
078   even over a thousand iterations to find an optimal solution. While feasible for general optimization
079   problems where performance evaluation can be easily simulated, this approach is less practical for
080   real-world experiments, such as those in complex control optimization.
081   There are two main differences between the complex control optimization tasks we focused on and
082   the general high-dimensional optimization problems tackled by L INE BO (Kirschner et al., 2019),
083   making it less effective for our control problems. First, complex control optimization typically
084   involves problems with dimensions ranging from low to moderate (6 to 20 parameters). For instance,
085   the electric motor field-oriented control (FOC) system has three PI controllers with six parameters
086   (Gabriel et al., 1980; Lara et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2016); the quadrotor system has six control
087   parameters for three axes, or twelve if angle control is included (Berkenkamp et al., 2016; Yuan
088   et al., 2022); and the gantry system consists of three axes with outer-loop P controllers and inner-
089
      loop PI controllers, resulting in six to nine parameters (Rothfuss et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022;
      2023). In contrast, the problems studied by Kirschner et al. (2019) typically involve 10 to 100
090
      parameters, making the problem scale significantly different. Second, in control optimization, each
091
      iteration involves applying the controller parameters to the hardware to obtain performance and
092   safety evaluations, which can take several minutes or longer. Additionally, repeated iterations cause
093   wear on the hardware, making an excessive number of iterations unacceptable. In contrast, the
094   problems in Kirschner et al. (2019) allow for hundreds or even thousands of iterations. Therefore, a
095   more efficient safe optimization algorithm is required for complex control problems.
096
      As noted by Bengio et al. (2005), the locality of Gaussian kernels limits Gaussian process models
097
      in capturing non-local structures. To address this, Duvenaud et al. (2011) introduced additive Gaus-
098   sian processes, creating a high-dimensional additive structure for Gaussian kernels, significantly
099   improving the Gaussian process’s capability to model high-dimensional unknown functions. Stud-
100   ies by Rolland et al. (2018); Kandasamy et al. (2015); Mutny & Krause (2018); Bardou et al. (2024)
101   show that additive Gaussian processes increase efficiency in high-dimensional BO. However, exper-
102   imental validation of these methods remains limited, and their integration with safety constraints has
103   not been fully explored.
104
105   Our contributions. Given the traits of multi-parameter complex control systems, our main con-
106   tributions are as follows: 1) We introduced additive kernels to safety-aware BO for the first time
107   and theoretically evaluated its convergence under safety constraints. These kernels accommodate
      different signal variances and lengthscales across dimensions, making them well-suited for complex

      6.1   S IMULATIONS ON S YNTHETIC B ENCHMARK F UNCTIONS
327
328   In this section, we evaluate the safe optimization performance of S AFE C TRL BO against base-
329   line methods using commonly used benchmark functions, including Camelback (2D), Hartmann
330   (6D), and Gaussian (10D). We compare S AFE C TRL BO with established safe BO methods such as
331   S WARM S AFE O PT, S WARM S TAGE O PT (high-dimensional implementations of S AFE O PT and S TA -
332   GE O PT), and the latest high-dimensional safe BO method, L INE BO (Kirschner et al., 2019). Addi-
333   tionally, we incorporate the state-of-the-art high-dimensional additive BO method, D U MBO (Bar-
334   dou et al., 2024), as an unconstrained reference.
335   Since all three benchmark functions are designed for minimization, we invert their outputs and
336   introduce safety constraints. For the Camelback function, the minimum value is approximately -
337   1.0316, and after inversion, the maximum value becomes 1.0316. We set the safety constraint at 0,
338   meaning that if an optimization method evaluates a point with a function value below 0, it constitutes
339   a safety constraint violation. Similarly, for the Hartmann function, the inverted maximum value is
340   approximately 3.32237, and we impose a safety constraint of 0.3. For the Gaussian 10D function,
341   f (x) = −exp(−4∥x∥22 ), the inverted maximum value is 1, and we set the safety constraint at 0.1.
342   To ensure a fair comparison, we conducted 100 runs for each method on each benchmark func-
343   tion, plotting the mean and standard error of the simple regret (Figure 2). Each run begins with a
344   randomly generated safe initial point (where the function value exceeds the safety threshold). All
345   optimization methods were run for 150 iterations on the Camelback function and 200 iterations on
346   the Hartmann and Gaussian functions. For L INE BO and D U MBO, we used publicly available im-
347   plementations with default hyperparameters for each benchmark function. For S WARM S AFE O PT,
348
      S WARM S TAGE O PT, and S AFE C TRL BO, we manually selected suitable hyperparameters. For de-

499   7   C ONCLUSION
500
501   In this study, we implemented additive Gaussian kernels to enhance the efficiency of safe Bayesian
502   optimization in complex control problems, while also proposing a simplified safe expansion process
503
      to mitigate the additional computational cost introduced by the additive kernels. Empirical results
      from both benchmark function simulations and hardware experiments demonstrate that our method
504
      is highly competitive among baseline approaches and can be seamlessly integrated into real-world
505
      complex control applications. Although tested primarily for PMSM control, the proposed algorithm
506   has the potential to be applied to other control architectures and to a wide range of robotic and
507   mechatronic systems.
508
509
      However, a limitation of this work is that the computation of high-dimensional additive kernels
      becomes increasingly complex as the dimensions of control problems grow. For instance, a 100-
510
      dimensional problem would involve 100 orders of additive kernels. Designing and combining these
511
      kernel components effectively requires extensive domain knowledge and substantial experimental
512   effort. Furthermore, the computational cost associated with these combinations becomes a signifi-
513   cant barrier to scaling this approach to very high-dimensional problems. A possible solution is to
514   use kernel selection methods (Cristianini et al., 2001; Kandola et al., 2002; Ding et al., 2020) to
515   obtain one or more additive kernels with the highest efficiency in exploring the parameter space, and
516   use the selected kernels for subsequent optimization.

713
      In the hardware experiment of our method, there are in total 39 constraint violations in 5 runs. Most
      constraint violations pertain to the first constraint function, where the performance value is lower
714
      than the preset threshold. The first constraint is a soft constraint, and a violation indicates that
715
      the performance is below expectations, such as exhibiting a large overshoot or a slower transient
716   response. Importantly, this violation does not imply that the system is unsafe. The constraint could
717   be set to −∞ to ensure no violations; however, this may reduce optimization efficiency.
718
719
      A smaller number of violations involve the second constraint function, representing the steady-state
      error being lower than the preset threshold. Such a violation implies that the steady-state value of
720
      the motor speed deviates significantly from the desired setpoint, e.g., requiring the motor to stabilize
721
      at 100 rad/s, but it instead stabilizes at 90 rad/s. Similar to the first constraint, this violation does
722   not indicate system unsafety. Instead, it is introduced to enhance the optimization efficiency of the
723   algorithm by discouraging the selection of parameters that lead to high steady-state errors.
724
725
      The third constraint ensures that the value of the function representing signal safety remains above
      the preset safe threshold throughout the optimization process, thereby guaranteeing the safety of the
726
      system during optimization.
727
728
729   C.2   D ISCUSSION ON T RADE - OFF B ETWEEN S AFETY AND E FFICIENCY
730
731   The violations primarily occurred during the exploration phase. This can be reduced by adjusting
732   the hyperparameters of the base kernels or modifying the performance function to make it smoother.
733   However, such adjustments may require more iterations, thus increasing computational costs in
734   practical applications, leading to a trade-off between safety and optimization efficiency.
735
      Moreover, as noted by (Sui et al., 2018), safe BO aims to ensure safety with high probability rather
736   than guaranteeing 100% safety. Therefore, safe BO methods based on confidence intervals inher-
737   ently have a small probability of constraint violation, regardless of how sophisticated the hyper-
738   parameter design may be. For example, constraint violations are also reported in (Khosravi et al.,
739   2023).
740
741
742   C.3   D ISCUSSION ON H OW TO H ANDLE THE T RADE - OFF
743
744   How to manage this trade-off depends on the specific task requirements. It requires a comprehensive
745   optimization of multiple aspects of the task. For instance:
746   Iteration tolerance: How many iterations can the task afford? In systems where each iteration
747   runs quickly and a large number of iterations do not cause significant wear, a relatively conservative
748   strategy can be adopted. This may reduce optimization efficiency but enhance safety guarantee.
749
      Safety requirements: How stringent are the safety requirements? For example, the control opti-
750   mization process of a drone system may have high safety requirements, as unsafe control parame-
751   ters may result in crashes or collisions. In such cases, safety must be guaranteed even at the cost
752   of reduced optimization efficiency. Conversely, in automotive motor control optimization, safety
753   requirements are relatively lower. Some poorly performing control parameters may require human
754   intervention to early terminate their operation, but in most cases, they will not cause direct damage.
755   Therefore, as set in the hardware experiment, we can appropriately relax the constraints on tracking
      performance while ensuring signal safety to improve optimization efficiency.
