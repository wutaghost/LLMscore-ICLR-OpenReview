011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Time Series Forecasting (TSF) is key functionality in numerous fields, includ-
014            ing in finance, weather services, and energy management. While TSF methods
015
               are emerging these days, many of them require domain-specific data collection
               and model training and struggle with poor generalization performance on new do-
016
               mains. Foundation models aim to overcome this limitation. Pre-trained on large-
017
               scale language or time series data, they exhibit promising inferencing capabilities
018            in new or unseen data. This has spurred a surge in new TSF foundation models.
019            We propose a new benchmark, FoundTS, to enable thorough and fair evaluation
020            and comparison of such models. FoundTS covers a variety of TSF foundation
021            models, including those based on large language models and those pretrained on
022            time series. Next, FoundTS supports different forecasting strategies, including
023            zero-shot, few-shot, and full-shot, thereby facilitating more thorough evaluations.
024            Finally, FoundTS offers a pipeline that standardizes evaluation processes such
025            as dataset splitting, loading, normalization, and few-shot sampling, thereby facil-
026
               itating fair evaluations. Building on this, we report on an extensive evaluation
               of TSF foundation models on a broad range of datasets from diverse domains
027
               and with different statistical characteristics. Specifically, we identify pros and
028
               cons and inherent limitations of existing foundation models, and we identify di-
029            rections for future model design. We make our code and datasets available at
030            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/FoundTS-C2B0.

033   1   INTRODUCTION
034
035   Time Series Forecasting (TSF) is core functionality in a multitude of applications, including in fi-
036   nance, weather services, and energy management (Wu et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2022;
037   Qin et al., 2023). Given historical observations, predicting future states is valuable for decision mak-
038   ing and taking appropriate actions. Consequently, TSF is a very active research field, as evidenced by
039
      the continued emergence of prediction models. However, most existing TSF models require training
      on specific datasets in preparation for performing inference on corresponding datasets. Such models
040
      (called specific models in this paper to be distinguished from foundation models) do not generalize
041
      well and experience suboptimal performance when applied to new or unseen data (Shi et al., 2024;
042   Huang et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024). Efforts to address challenges such as these
043   have led to a recent surge in the development of foundation models for TSF (Liang et al., 2024; Woo
044   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024).
045
      While foundation models encompassing diverse architectures and training paradigms continue to
046
      appear, our understanding of their strengths and limitations remains limited. Existing research on
047
      understanding such models has focused primarily on the qualitative analysis and categorization of
048   foundation models for TSF (Liang et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2023b; 2024). For instance, (Jin et al.,
049   2024) propose a potential framework for LLM-based time series analysis, highlighting key opportu-
050   nities and challenges for future research and advocating for increased interdisciplinary collaboration
051   and exploration in this promising field. Similarly, (Liang et al., 2024) adopt a methodology-centered
052   classification approach, outlining critical elements of time series foundation models, such as model
053   architectures, pre-training techniques, and adaptation strategies. However, these studies often lack
      a quantitative evaluation of foundation models, which is crucial for assessing and comparing per-

055   also enables researchers to make informed decisions about model selection and improvements.

      Robust and thorough benchmarks enable researchers to evaluate new models more rigorously, which

068   forecasting benchmarks target performance evaluation of specific models, while evaluations of foun-

074   Motivated by these observations, we present FoundTS, a benchmark designed to facilitate fair
075   and comprehensive empirical evaluation and comparison of time series foundation models. First,
076   to assess the performance of models thoroughly, FoundTS includes datasets spanning different
077   domains and with diverse characteristics. Second, FoundTS covers a variety of time series foun-
078   dation models, including LLM-based models and pre-trained models. This is in addition to state-
079   of-the-art specific models that are included to enable comparison with the foundation models. (3)

081

      thoroughly. Fourth, to ensure fair comparisons, FoundTS provides an experimental setup that stan-
082
      dardizes processes such as dataset splitting, loading, normalization, and few-shot sampling. These
083
      properties combine to enable a fair and complete pipeline, ensuring thorough evaluations with find-
084   ings that are comparable. FoundTS thus enables comprehensive evaluation of time series foun-
085   dation models, providing reliable insight into their characteristics and pros and cons. In addition,
086   we identify inherent limitations in current foundation models and offer directions for future model
087   design. In summary, we make the following main contributions:
088
089   • Diversified models and datasets: FoundTS covers state-of-the-art time series foundation mod-
090     els, including LLM-based and pre-trained time series models. Additionally, it features compre-
091
        hensive datasets that encompass a wide range of domains and characteristics.
092   • Comprehensive and fair evaluation strategies and pipelines: FoundTS integrates zero-shot,
093     few-shot, and full-shot approaches, facilitating improved assessment of model performance. Fur-
094     ther, it provides a unified experimental setup that standardizes dataset splitting, loading, and
095     few-shot sampling, thereby facilitating fair comparisons of models.
096   • In-depth quantitative analysis and insights: Employing FoundTS, we report on extensive
097     experiments that cover different time series foundation models. This way, we identify pros and
098     cons of the time series models covered and offer insights for us in future model design and
099     optimization.

      2.2    T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING B ENCHMARKS
135
136   Several benchmarks have recently been proposed for TSF—see Table 2. However, their inherent
137   limitations make comprehensive and fair comparison of foundation models and specific models for
138   time series forecasting out of reach. First, most benchmarks target only specific models and ignore
139   time series foundation models. As shown in Table 2, the only exception is ProbTS (Zhang et al.,
140   2023), which, however, only cover time series pre-trained models, not LLM-based models. With
141   foundation models now offering impressive features like zero-shot prediction, there is a need for fair
142
      and thorugh comparisons with specific models, considering the challenges they pose, such as high
      computational costs.
143
144   Second, current benchmarks do not support diverse evaluation strategies. Most time series fore-
145   casting benchmarks disregard emerging features like zero-shot and few-shot prediction, focusing
146   instead on full-shot scenarios. While ProbTS (Zhang et al., 2023) supports zero-shot prediction, it
147   does not support few-shot prediction, which enables models to leverage small amounts of relevant
148
      data to fine-tune their performance. This capability not only enhances accuracy but also increases
      a model’s flexibility at adapting to new tasks, making it more effective in dynamic environments
149
      and across diverse environments. Furthermore, the absence of standardized sampling methods for
150
      few-shot prediction compromizes fair model comparison. There is a need for a more inclusive and
151   fair evaluation pipeline.
152
153   FoundTS is designed to be a more reliable, comprehensive, and user-friendly benchmark, featuring
154
      a wider range of TSF models and evaluation strategies. In addition, it offers a unified experimental
      setup, ensuring consistent model evaluations within a robust pipeline.

184   3.1         DATA
185
      High-quality and diverse time series data enable comprehensive evaluation of model performance,
186
      facilitating the selection of models that are most suitable for specific forecasting scenarios. The
187   data offers broad coverage of domains as well as statistical characteristics, to more comprehensively
188   compare the prediction and generalization performance of models.
189
190
      (1) Domains: We include datasets from ten domains, including stock (NASDAQ (Feng et al., 2019)),
      health (ILI (Wu et al., 2021)), energy (Solar (Lai et al., 2018)), electricity (ETT (Zhou et al., 2021)
191
      and Electricity (Trindade, 2015)), environment (Weather (Wu et al., 2021)), traffic (Traffic (Wu
192
      et al., 2021)), nature (ZafNoo (Poyatos et al., 2020)), banking (NN5 (Taieb et al., 2012)), web
193   (Wike2000 (Gasthaus et al., 2019)), and economics (Exchange (Lai et al., 2018)), for evaluation.
194
195   (2) Characteristics: We consider a range of important time series characteristics, including sea-
196
      sonality, trend, stationarity, transition, shifting, correlation, and non-Gaussianity (Qiu et al., 2024;
      Zhang et al., 2023). Seasonality refers to repeating patterns or cycles at regular intervals. Trend
197
      indicates overall movements in a time series. Stationarity reflects the statistical properties of a time
198
      series, such as mean and variance, which do not change over time. Transition represents sudden
199   or gradual shifts in a time series. Shifting refers to changes in the level or timing of the data and
200   includes vertical and horizontal shifts. Correlation represents the relationship or dependence among
201   different channels. Non-Gaussianity (N-Gau) represents deviations from normal distribution, often
202   exhibiting skewness or kurtosis. The formula used to calculate these characteristics can be found in
203   Appendix B. The “Data” part of Figure 1 shows data domains with varying characteristic distribu-
204   tions. This facilitates comprehensive evaluation of prediction accuracy and generalization cap abili-
205   ties under varying data characteristics. More details of the datasets are included in Appendix A.1.

209
      3.2.1         T IME S ERIES F OUNDATION M ODELS
210   LLM-based models: LLMs-based methods leverage the strong representational capacity and se-
211   quential modeling capability of LLMs to capture complex patterns for time series modeling. To more
212   comprehensively evaluate the foundation models, we incorporate existing LLMs-based methods
213   into FoundTS, focusing primarily on parameter-efficient fine-tuning and prompting. 1) Parameter-
214   efficient fine-tuning methods: GPT4TS (Zhou et al., 2024), S2 IPLLM (Pan et al., 2024) selectively
215   adjust specific parameters such as positional encoding and layer normalization of LLMs, enabling
      the model to quickly adapt to time series while retaining most of pre-trained knowledge. 2) Prompt-


                                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      ing methods, such as UniTime (Liu et al., 2024a) and Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2023a), focus on design-
217   ing prompts, such as learnable prompts, prompt pools, and domain-specific instructions to activate
218   time series knowledge in LLMs.
219
220
      Time-series pre-trained models: Pre-training on multi-domain time series data has gained sig-
      nificant attention in recent years. We incorporate time series pre-trained models into FoundTS,
221
      categorizing them into four types based on the pre-training approach: reconstruction, autoregres-
222
      sive, direct prediction, and hybrid training. 1) Reconstruction methods: MOIRAI Woo et al. (2024),
223   UniTS Gao et al. (2024), Moment Goswami et al. (2024) restore the features of time series data,
224   enabling them to extract valuable information in an unsupervised manner. This type of method
225   mainly adopts the encoder architecture. 2) Autoregressive methods: TimesFM Das et al. (2023),
226   Timer Liu et al. (2024b), employ next token prediction to learn time series representation. This
227   type of method mainly adopts the decoder architecture. 3) Direct prediction methods: TTM Ekam-
228   baram et al. (2024), unify the training process between pre-training and downstream tasks, allowing
229   models to exhibit strong adaptability when transitioning to downstream forecasting tasks. 4) Hybrid
230   pre-training methods: ROSE Wang et al. (2024c), combines the strengths of both reconstruction and
231
      direct prediction to learn generalized time series representations.
232
233   3.2.2    T IME S ERIES SPECIFIC M ODELS
234
      Time series specific models typically require training on specific datasets and perform inference
235
      on the corresponding datasets. To better showcase the capabilities of time series foundation mod-
236   els, we select several SOTA specific models for comparison. We include: 1) CNN-based models:
237   TimesNet (Wu et al., 2022), which treat time series as sequences of vectors and leverage CNNs
238   to capture temporal dependencies. 2) Transformer-based models: FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022),
239   iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023), and PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022), which are capable of capturing
240   more complex temporal dynamics, leading to significantly improved forecasting performance. 3)
241   MLP-based models: FITS (Xu et al., 2024), TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024b), and DLinear (Zeng
242   et al., 2023), with their simple architecture and relatively few parameters, have demonstrated strong
243   forecasting accuracy as well.

245   3.3     EVALUATION
246
247   To ensure a fair and comprehensive evaluation of the performance of various time series forecasting
248   models, we standardize the evaluation in three key areas: strategies, settings, and metrics.
249
250   3.3.1    STRATEGIES
251
      Considering that current benchmarks typically adopt a single evaluation approach, focusing only on
252
      zero-shot or full-shot scenarios, this limits the ability to comprehensively assess prediction perfor-
253
      mance. We propose a more comprehensive quantitative evaluation that offers researchers a broader
254   understanding under different conditions, including zero-shot, few-shot, and full-shot. As shown in
255   Figure 1, we divide the downstream evaluation data into train, validation, and test data.
256
257
      (1) The zero-shot evaluation only uses the test data to evaluate the generalization ability of founda-
      tion models to new datasets, assessing whether the model has truly learned general knowledge from
258
      vast amounts of pre-training data.
259
260   (2) The few-shot evaluation only utilizes a subset of training data and full validation data for fine-
261   tuning, reflecting the prediction performance in low-data learning scenarios. This approach assesses
262   whether models can effectively generalize and reason with minimal data support.
263   (3) The full-shot evaluation utilizes full train data and validation data for fine-tuning. It evaluates
264   the performance when utilizing all available data, revealing its upper-bound performance.
265
266   3.3.2    S ETTINGS AND METRICS
267
268   Different evaluation settings can cause significant discrepancies in model performance, leading to
269   unfair comparisons of their actual capabilities. To address this, we standardize the settings, including
      lookback and prediction length, data splits, and sampling strategy.

494   4.3   TAKEAWAYS FROM B ENCHMARKING AND A NALYSIS
495
496   Based on our benchmarking and analysis with FoundTS, we summarize some takeaways consider-
497
      ing the following critical questions related to foundation models for time series forecasting.
498   Do foundation models outperform specific models? Current foundation models, especially those
499   time series pre-trained models, exhibit superior zero-shot and few-shot learning abilities compared
500   with specific models, which indicates their advantages in data-insufficient scenarios. However, when
501   sufficient training data are available, foundation models do not consistently outperform specific
502   models with full-shot learning, indicating their limitations in fully utilizing sufficient data.
503   Which foundation models are better? The advantages of different foundation models for time series
504   forecasting depend on diverse aspects of evaluation, and no single model dominates across these
505   aspects. 1) Considering the two types of foundation models, current time series pre-trained models
506   exhibit better overall performance than LLM-based models. 2) Different foundation models show
507   their advantages in dealing with datasets from diverse domains or with diverse characteristics. 3)
508   Large-scale time series pre-trained models such as TimesFM show the best zero-shot performance,
509
      but the situation changes given few-shot data for fine-tuning, where models like Timer perform bet-
      ter. 4) The scaling law does not hold strictly in current foundation models for time series forecasting,
510
      and some small-sized models such as ROSE and TTM achieve a better balance between precision
511
      and efficiency. It calls for benchmarks such as FoundTS to provide comprehensive evaluations of
512   foundation models for time series forecasting to answer this question.
513
514   What improvements are needed for foundation models? 1) More universal capabilities for diverse
515
      datasets and scenarios: Considering that no foundation model wins all situations, a meaningful
      goal is to explore a more universal model for time series forecasting to handle diverse forecasting
516
      situations simultaneously. From the comparison between foundation models and specific models, the
517
      development of more powerful foundation models should not only consider enhancing data-scarce
518   performance but also increasing the upper bound forecasting performance given more sufficient
519   training data. 2) Better designs for utilizing large-scale pre-training knowledge: Proper training
520   data, architecture, and pre-training strategies need to be investigated to make time series models
521   truly take advantage of the scaling law. Since multivariate appears as a common characteristic in
522   time series, how to embed generalizable correlation modeling in foundation models from large-scale
523   data remains an open problem. For LLM-based models, more in-depth analysis should be made to
524   fully extract and adapt LLM knowledge for time series forecasting. 3) More efficient training and
525   inference: Considering that specific models are easy to train, an efficient foundation model that
526
      balances performance and costs is also valuable to make foundation models more practical in real-
      world applications.

529   5     C ONCLUSION
530
531   Foundation models for time series forecasting have recently gained significant attention due to their
532   impressive generalization capabilities in zero-shot and few-shot situations, leading to a surge of in-
533   novative models. This paper introduces FoundTS, the first comprehensive benchmark designed
534   for quantitatively evaluating foundation TSF models. FoundTS encompasses a diverse array of
535
      state-of-the-art models and includes three experimental scenarios: zero-shot, few-shot, and full-
      shot. Additionally, it provides a unified pipeline to ensure consistent evaluations. Using FoundTS,
536
      we thoroughly assess 11 foundation TSF models, revealing their strengths and weaknesses. Fur-
537
      thermore, we highlight the inherent limitations of current models and propose critical directions for
538   future model design. Overall, FoundTS and our evaluation offer researchers enhanced tools for
539   developing new foundation TSF models.

1047   not consistently enhance with longer look-back lengths and may occasionally decline significantly.

       This suggests that when designing models, we should ensure that they can flexibly handle varying

1050

1051   arbitrary prediction lengths, exhibit larger fluctuations in MSE differences than other models that can
1052   output predictions at arbitrary lengths. This highlights the need to design models that can flexibly
1053   predict across different prediction lengths.
