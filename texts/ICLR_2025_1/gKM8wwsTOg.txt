011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has catalyzed the emer-
014            gence of LLM-based agents. Recent research has shifted from single-agent sys-
015
               tems to multi-agent frameworks, demonstrating that collaboration can outperform
               the capabilities of individual LLMs. However, effectively pre-configuring a Multi-
016
               Agent System (MAS) for a specific task remains a challenging problem, with
017
               performance outcomes only observable after execution. Inspired by the well-
018            established scaling laws in LLM development that model downstream task perfor-
019            mance or validation loss as functions of various factors during training, we seek
020            to investigate the predictability of MAS performance. Specifically, we explore
021            whether it is possible to predict the downstream task performance of a configured
022            MAS. In addition, MAS face a growing challenge in ensuring reliable and trust-
023            worthy responses. The introduction of malicious agents can lead to the generation
024            and spread of harmful content, which poses significant security risks. To address
025            the above issues, we introduce AgentMonitor, a framework that integrates with
026
               existing MAS at the agent level. AgentMonitor captures inputs and outputs at
               each step; this enables (1) transforming them into relevant statistics supporting the
027
               training of a regression model to predict task performance and (2) the application
028
               of on-the-fly corrections to mitigate negative impacts on final outcomes. Exten-
029            sive experiments demonstrate that training a simple XGBoost model achieves a
030            high Spearman rank correlation of 0.89 in an in-domain setting. In more challeng-
031            ing scenarios, where the statistics of a specific task or architecture is absent from
032            the training set, our method maintains a moderate average correlation of 0.58.
033            Furthermore, by employing AgentMonitor in a maliciously configured MAS, the
034            system ultimately generates 6.2% less harmful content and 1.8% more helpful
035            content on average, reducing safety risks and improving reliability.

      Recently, the rapid development of LLMs has been widely reported (Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey
040
      et al., 2024; Gemini et al., 2023). These models exhibit strong capabilities, achieving success in
041
      various tasks of Natural Language Processing (NLP) (Radford et al., 2019). Leveraging training
042   processes such as instruction tuning (Longpre et al., 2023), LLMs have demonstrated the ability to
043   articulate reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024), self-correct errors (Madaan et al., 2024),
044   utilize external tools (Schick et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2023), and retain long-term memory (Huang
045   et al., 2023b) during inference. By combining these capabilities with various techniques, researchers
046   have successfully built on off-the-shelf LLMs to create single-agent systems capable of solving more
047   complex tasks. Notable examples include AutoGPT (Significant Gravitas, 2024), XAgent (XAgent,
048   2023), and OpenInterpreter (OpenInterpreter, 2023).
049   Beyond the aforementioned single-agent applications, research has emerged that focuses on en-
050   abling multiple LLMs to collaborate on specific tasks. Inspired by evidence of collective intelli-
051   gence (Woolley et al., 2010) arising in groups of humans, various multi-agent frameworks have been
052   proposed to mimic human collaborative scenarios. Typically, in these frameworks, each agent is con-
053   trolled by an LLM with an assigned role, and a predefined executable pipeline is configured. Fol-
      lowing the pipeline, agents collaborate towards a common goal. This approach has shown promising

148   2.1        D ESIGN OF AGENT M ONITOR
149
150   In this section, we begin by introducing the parameter-efficient fine-tuning scenario and the prac-
151   tical use of popular non-invasive frameworks for building models with PEFT. We then discuss the
152
      motivation for our proposed AgentMonitor in this context.
153   Fine-tuning a large language model with all parameters is costly and time-consuming. As a solution,
154   researchers have proposed various parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques, such as LoRA (Hu
155   et al., 2021), Adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019), and Prompts (Lester et al., 2021), which achieve
156   near-lossless downstream task performance by only training a small proportion of the model param-
157   eters. Despite the success of these techniques, individually modifying the original structure of the
158
      pretrained model for each method can be inefficient.
159   In response, incremental frameworks have emerged that adapt various techniques to the original
160   structure without invasive modifications. These frameworks wrap functions around the original
161   model, preserving the original workflow while maintaining high scalability for broader applications.
      As shown in the upper part of the Code Listing in Appendix A, after initializing a pretrained model,

184   2.2   D ETAILS OF I NDICATORS U SED
185
186   In this section, we detail how we transform the stored information into indicators that are used to
187
      train the regression model. Specifically, we categorize the indicators into two groups. The first
      group consists of scores generated by using an LLM to assess performance, including the agents
188
      personal score and the collective score. Intuitively, the personal score measures how well an agent
189
      completes its own task following the given instruction, while the collective score evaluates how the
190   agents’ behaviour contributes to the overall system. For example, an agent given the instruction to
191   generate helpless or nonsense responses might excel at its specific task and receive a high personal
192   score. However, it would earn a low collective score, as it does not contribute significantly to the
193   final result.
194
      The second group of indicators is generally determined by a configured architecture and is not
195
      significantly related to the backbone LLM. For each MAS, a workflow is designed, forming an
196   execution flow. From this, we extract graph attributes such as the number of nodes, number of
197   edges, transitivity, etc. The rationale behind these indicators is that different workflow mechanisms
198   result in distinct graph attributes. For instance, a linear configuration (A → B → C) has 3 nodes,
199   2 edges, and a transitivity of 0. This implies that no triangular structures are formed, potentially
200   limiting the efficiency of information flow across the system. For further details on each indicator
201   and the prompts used to evaluate the scores, refer to Appendix D and Appendix G.2.
202
203   2.3   P OST-E DIT F EATURES
204
205   In addition to recording the relevant information of MAS, our framework is naturally designed to
206   capture the input and output of each agent at each step; this operation enables us to easily do the
207
      pre-edit and the post-edit. In this paper, we experiment with using another LLM to help edit the
      response generated by the agents in the MAS, and this operation is done on-the-fly. The post-edit
208
      operation are inspired by the adapter in the neuron net work where the output of the hidden state
209
      is further passed throught the adapter which is more lightweight and easier to be updated. More
210   similarly, Aligner (Ji et al., 2024a) uses a lighter LLM to elevate the response generated by an LLM
211   and shows the great effectiveness of the weak-to-strong generalization. Our experiments show that
212   without monitoring, a MAS configured with a malicious agent is versatile and easily generates a
213   harmful or helpless response. Conversely, the same MAS that is monitored by our AgentMonitor
214   can generate a more harmless or helpful response. The illustration of the post-edit can be seen in
215   Figure 4. Specifically, the post-edit operation utilizes an LLM to elevate the original response after
      the agents; in our experiment, we use this operation either after one agent in a system or after all

305   3.1             OVERALL R ESULTS FOR P REDICTING TARGET S CORES
306
307   The mean Spearman rank correlation with error bars is shown in Figure 5. The error bars are
308   plotted using a 5-fold cross-validation. We observe a clear pattern: (1) Easier settings, such as
309
      In-Task, In-Arch, and In-Domain, tend to achieve relatively high correlations and low variances,
      which is expected. Among these, the results suggest that HumanEval is the most difficult to pre-
310
      dict. (2) Compared to In-Task and In-Arch, the In-Domain setting–which includes all tasks and
311
      architectures–achieves a higher overall mean score and a relatively lower variance. This indicates
312   that access to information from other tasks or architectures improves predictive performance, and
313   indicators are transferable between different settings. (3) Although some results in more difficult
314   settings lead to lower mean scores, particularly when Arch3 is left out in the Cross-Arch setting
315   or HumanEval is excluded in the Cross-Task setting, the overall average results still reach 0.58,
316   showing the effectiveness of using our AgentMonitor to predict the target score.
317
      Furthermore, the boxplot in Figure 6 shows the distribution of errors in all settings. In general, the
318   median lies close to zero, suggesting that the predicted values are generally close to the observed
319   values. Despite the lower correlations in more difficult settings, we observe that in the Cross-Arch
320   and Cross-Task settings, the median error remains near zero, indicating that for most instances, the
321   predicted values are close to the observed values. Furthermore, although the correlation for Hu-
322   manEval in In-Task setting is lower, the error is not, being smaller overall compared to the other two
323   tasks. This suggests that while predicting the rank is more challenging for this task, the regression
      model can still predict values that are reasonably closer to the observed values.

466   when we substitute the post-editing LLM with a more aligned backbone LLM, we observe a sig-
467   nificant improvement in harmlessness scores, though helpfulness experiences varying degrees of
468   degradation (compare the fourth row with the second and first rows). This suggests that optimizing
469   both dimensions simultaneously is challenging, as noted in Ji et al. (2024a). Additionally, we also
470   conduct an analysis of the position effect of post-edit LLMs, see Appendix C.

      4       R ELATED W ORK
474
475   4.1        LLM BASED AGENTS AND M ULTI -AGENT F RAME W ORK
476
477
      Recent advances in large language models (LLMs), such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), have
      stimulated the development of LLM-based agents. These agents are able to utilize external tools,
478
      such as interpreters (OpenInterpreter, 2023), search engines (Luo et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2024),
479
      web browsers (Nakano et al., 2021; He et al., 2024), or custom-defined tools (Qin et al., 2023;
480   Schick et al., 2024) through function calling. Leveraging the strong instruction-following abili-
481   ties of foundation models, these agents have demonstrated significant progress in various domains.
482   For example, the development of OS-Copilot, which integrates with operating systems (Wu et al.,
483   2024), the creation of XAgent for solving complex tasks (XAgent, 2023), and the introduction of
484   SearchGPT to accelerate search experiences (OpenAI, 2024). In line with these advances, frame-
485   works have emerged for efficiently building LLM agents, such as LangChain (LangChain-AI, 2024),
      AgentGPT (Reworkd, 2024), and AutoGPT (Significant Gravitas, 2024).


                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Beyond single-agent intelligence, recent research indicates that collaboration among multiple
487   agents, each with different expertise, can enhance downstream task performance. Notable suc-
488   cesses include AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023), which facilitates the creation of conversable agents for
489   various pilot applications, such as online decision-making; OpenDevin (Wang et al., 2024), a plat-
490   form for developing powerful and flexible AI agents that interact with the world in ways similar to
491   those of human developers; IOA (Chen et al., 2024), which addresses the challenges of distributed
492   agent deployment by introducing an agent integration protocol, along with a design of an instant
493   messaging architecture. However, constructing reliable MAS with these frameworks often involves
494   trial and error in identifying the optimal configuration. Research has also shown that MAS can be
495
      vulnerable to malicious attacks (Ju et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), such as prompt injections or
      misalignment of the model. To address these challenges, we propose AgentMonitor, a framework
496
      that proactively monitors indicators in MAS and applies on-the-fly corrections. This not only helps
497
      predict downstream task performance, but also mitigates unsafe behaviors.
498
499
500   4.2   LLM P REDICTABLILITY AND S CALING L AWS
501
502   The vast development of LLMs is closely related to the concept of neuron scaling laws (Kaplan
503   et al., 2020; Rae et al., 2021; Henighan et al., 2020). Previous works have attempted to capture the
504   relationships between factors such as training FLOPS and model size, and their impact on validation
505   loss by first training numerous differently configured models and then proposing a power law to fit
506   the coefficients. Once fitted, this power law can be used to extrapolate and predict the loss for a larger
507   model and further simulated to derive the optimal configuration for target size model. This paradigm
508
      has led to several practical and constructive suggestions. For example, Chinchilla law (Hoffmann
      et al., 2022) suggests that while given a computational budget of 10x, the suggested model size
509
      should be 5.5x larger, while training tokens should be 1.8x more. Similarly, Minicpm (Hu et al.,
510
      2024) derive optimal batch size and learning rate configurations from LLM sandbox experiments
511   where they train a multi-set of smaller models, showing that their 2.4B model performs on par with
512   current 7-13B scale models. MM1 (McKinzie et al., 2024) performed a grid search for the optimal
513   learning rate using smaller models and then successfully extrapolated the results to larger scales.
514   BIMIX (Ge et al., 2024) proposed a bivariate law concerning data quantity and mixing proportion,
515   demonstrating that their optimized data mixture outperforms the default mixture.
516
      Inspired by the fruitful results in the construction of LLM, our work aims to investigate the pre-
517   dictability of MAS by proposing a framework that can capture relevant indicators to predict target
518   scores. Another study (Qian et al., 2024) explored collaborative scaling laws by increasing the num-
519   ber of agents in a system, finding that normalized solution quality follows a logistic growth pattern as
520   the number of agents increases. However, given the versatility required in building different MAS,
521   it is challenging to determine a single-variable law for the entire system.
522
      A similar work to ours is Ye et al. (2023), which investigates the predictability of LLM capabilities.
523   Given records of past experiments from different model families, number of parameters, tasks, and
524   in-context examples, they explore whether LLM performance on new configurations can be accu-
525   rately predicted. Our work shares a similar goal; however, to the best of our knowledge, is the first to
526   conduct such a study in the context of building MAS. We hope that this research provides valuable
527   insights and paves the way for the community to build better MAS.
528

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce AgentMonitor, a framework designed to assess the predictability and
533
      enhance the security of MAS. AgentMonitor operates in a non-invasive manner, seamlessly wrap-
534   ping around existing MAS while preserving their original workflows. By capturing the inputs and
535   outputs of each agent, it enables (1) prediction of downstream task performance and (2) on-the-fly
536   corrections. Our experiments demonstrate that AgentMonitor can effectively predict the system’s
537   performance and facilitate the post-editing of agents’ responses, leading to more reliable and ac-
538   ceptable outcomes. As research increasingly focuses on the construction of MAS, we believe that
539   our work contributes valuable insights, and we look forward to AgentMonitor guiding the design of
      more robust and rational MAS.

       F    CASE STUDY ON POST- EDIT
1027
1028
       In this section, we provide a case study of the experiments shown in Sec 3.4, which utilize post-
1029   editing after all agents have completed their generation. Figures 11 and 12 illustrate the effects of
1030   using u8B and 8B as post-edit LLM, respectively (Both of which are rated as harmless). Figures13
1031   and 14 further show the effects of using u8B and 8B as post-edit LLM (With the former being rated
1032   harmful and the latter harmless).
1033
       From Figure 11, we observe that the u8B post-edit can sometimes still generate responses that pose
1034
       potential risks to society, with only minor modifications made after the initial response. In this exam-
1035   ple, u8B mostly mirrors the original response. This suggests that once a model produces a harmful
1036   initial response, it can be challenging for an uncensored model to fully moderate it. However, after
1037   several interactions, the final response generated by the summarizer becomes more moderate and
1038   acceptable. This improvement is attributed to the post-edit by the first agent, which changes a single
1039   word–from ”illegally” to ”legally”–slightly enhancing the acceptability of the response. In contrast,
1040   Figure 12 presents an extreme case where the 8B post-edit exhibits a highly censored manner. We
1041   find that 8B often responds with ”I cannot...” thereby making the response helpful but largely unin-
1042   formative. Another observation is that when the original agent’s response is modified by the phrase
1043
       ”I cannot...”, this effect propagates to the succeeding agent (with the summarizer acting as u8B),
       leading it to also respond with ”I cannot...”.
1044
1045   Another case demonstrating the failure of the u8B post-edit to modify a malicious response is shown
1046   in Figure 13. Although the final response appears moderate, it still contains potentially harmful con-
1047   tent related to the use of stimulants like caffeine, thus are rated harmful. Figure 14 illustrates that the
1048   8B post-edit also displays non-compliance when monitoring the responses of the first two agents.
1049
       Nevertheless, it significantly alters the original answer generated by the summarizer, removing the
       mention of specific drugs or substances, ultimately providing users with harmless yet useful guid-
1050
       ance.
