012                                               A BSTRACT
013
014            Recent advancements indicate that scaling up Multimodal Large Language Models
015            (MLLMs) effectively enhances performance on downstream multimodal tasks.
016            The prevailing MLLM paradigm, e.g., LLaVA, transforms visual features into
017
               text-like tokens using a static vision-language mapper, thereby enabling static
               LLMs to develop the capability to comprehend visual information through visual
018
               instruction tuning. Unfortunately, the static paradigm shares the same parame-
019
               ters to underly multi-task instruction tuning, inevitably introducing the potential
020            task interference or negative transfer, i.e., where an improvement in the perfor-
021            mance of one task reduces the performance of other tasks. In light of this, we
022            introduce HyperLLaVA, which in conjunction with a dynamic visual expert and
023            language expert, respectively adjusts the parameters of the projector and LLM
024            layers conditioned on diverse instruction semantics, thereby minimizing the task
025            interference. These experts are derived from HyperNetworks, which adaptively
026            generates dynamic parameter shifts through visual and language guidance, enabling
027            dynamic vision-language alignment and instruction tuning in two-stage training.
028
               To deeply study the multi-task interference of MLLM, we build the Comprehen-
               sive Multimodal Task benchmark (CMT), a comprehensive benchmark for the
029
               evaluation of multidimensional multimodal tasks. The experiments demonstrate
030
               that the superiority of the dynamic tuning paradigm for multi-task instruction
031            following on CMT and general MLLM benchmarks. Our project is available at
032            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/HyperLLaVA-D58E.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The landscape of Large Language Models (LLMs) Devlin et al. (2018); Radford et al. (2018);
038   Ouyang et al. (2022) has undergone significant evolution, highlighting their exceptional versatility in
039
      managing a wide variety of language-centric applications. To extend the capabilities of LLMs to a
      wider array of modal inputs, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have garnered increasing
040
      attention Radford et al. (2021b); Li et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2023); Achiam et al. (2023); Li et al.
041
      (2023c). MLLMs are crucial for the development of flexible, general-purpose assistants, as everyday
042   interactions encompass information from various modalities in addition to text.
043
044   Contemporary MLLMs (e.g., LLaVA Liu et al. (2023c;a)) typically adhere to a two-step static training
045
      protocol: (i) Vision-Language Alignment: A static projector is trained by leveraging image-text
      pairs to synchronize visual features with the language model’s word embedding space. The projector,
046
      with static parameters, bridges the vision and language modalities by converting visual features into
047
      visual tokens, allowing the LLM to understand visual content. (ii) Multimodal Insturction Tuning.
048   Next, multimodal instruction data are employed to fine-tune the LLM, enabling it to respond to
049   users’ varied requests involving visual content. This step is crucial for enhancing the capabilities
050   and controllability of MLLM for improving different zero-shot multimodal capabilities. Despite the
051   critical importance of the two-step process, the projector’s structure and the LLM tuning strategy
052   remain relatively underexplored in the literature. Quantitative analyses Wang et al. (2019) indicate
053   that a model with static parameters trained across diverse scenarios can introduce task interference or
      negative transfer, where excelling in one task may impede performance on another. Furthermore,

066   an ideal MLLM should effectively comprehend a broader range of multimodal instructions and
067   harness generalizable reasoning capabilities across various multidimensional tasks. Building on the
068   aforementioned insights, our investigation seeks to optimize the two-stage training process in the
069   multi-task tuning scenario, i.e., aiming to simultaneously mitigate task interference and enhance the
070   MLLM’s diverse multimodal comprehension abilities.
071
072   In this paper, we propose       HyperLLaVA (Figure 1(a)), transitioning from “static to dynamic
073   tuning paradigm” to achieve the stated objectives. The dynamic characterization benefits from a
074
      carefully designed expert module, derived from HyperNetwork Ha et al. (2017), to generate the
      dynamic parameters conditioned on instruction-aware semantics. Our bootstrapping philosophy
075
      is to leverage the expert to adaptively generate the strongly correlated MLLM’s parameter shifts,
076
      according to the visual and language input, thereby enabling positive transfer for projector and
077   LLM layers, respectively. By doing so, this dynamic characterization allows us to achieve the
078   best of both worlds by adjusting the MLLM’s parameters while encouraging the model to adapt to
079   each individual multimodal instruction. Notably, in HyperLLaVA, we tailor the HyperNetwork to
080   MLLM, incorporating input guidance-aware parameter generation and a stable learning framework
081   through an adapter. Based on the devised expert module, HyperLLaVA is learned following the two
082   steps: (i) In vision-language alignment, we divide the projector into static layers (original MLPs in
083   LLaVA) and dynamic layers (visual expert), where the parameters of static layers remain fixed and
084   the parameters of dynamic layers are dynamically generated based on visual features. The visual
085
      expert leverages HyperNetwork to assist the static projector in developing a visual-specific projector
      that adaptively models the visual features based on the visual guidance. Thus, the projector can
086
      deliver adaptive visual tokens to the language semantic space. (ii) For multimodal instruction tuning,
087
      we equip the LLM with a language expert, modeling dynamic parameters for LLM blocks. We
088   regard the intermediate output of the LLM as language guidance that guides the language expert to
089   offer an enhanced instruction-specific comprehension of the user’s request. By doing so, the MLLM
090   increases flexibility by generating unique parameters for each specific input, allowing the MLLM
091   to capitalize on similarities between samples across tasks and avoid potential interference among
092   different multimodal instructions.
093
      To thoroughly investigate the issue of multi-task negative interference, we initially developed the
094   Comprehensive Multimodal Task (CMT) benchmark, grounded in different interference dimensions,
095   including multimodal processing, recognition, and comprehension. CMT encompasses 7 diverse
096   multimodal tasks, including Text-Rich Images QA, Spatial Inference, Knowledge OCR, among
097   others. We conducted a systematic evaluation of the proposed CMT. The results suggest that
098   HyperLLaVA’s performance is positively correlated with the number of training task types,
099   while the original LLaVA demonstrated the opposite trend, highlighting the superiority of “dynamic”
100   learning for multi-task instruction tuning. Additionally, we conducted experiments on several existing
101   MLLM datasets, which confirmed the effectiveness and generalizability of HyperLLaVA.

147   2.2    V ISION - LANGUAGE G UIDED E XPERT M ODULE
148
      Original LLaVA’s Liu et al. (2023c) projector and LLM are trained with static parameters. We argue
149
      that the static tuning paradigm may limit the flexible visual token delivery and introduce negative
150   transfer in different downstream multi-modal tasks. Thus, we propose to equip the original’s LLaVA
151   projector and LLM with a visual expert EV and a language expert EL : (i) the visual expert adaptively
152   fits the projector’s output according to the specific visual guidance (e.g, visual features); (ii) the
153   language expert dynamically modeling the posterior blocks of LLM through anterior LLM’s block
154   output. The expert module is derived from HyperNetwork, which is a neural network that generates
155   the parameters for another neural network. Specifically, HyperNetwork treats the parameters of the
156   multi-layer perception (MLP) as a matrix K (n) ∈ RNin ×Nout , where Nin and Nout represent the
157   number of input and output neurons of the nth layer of MLP, respectively. Nin and Nout portray the
158   structure of the MLPs together. The generation of K (n) can be regarded as a matrix factorization:
159                                                                      K (n) = ξ(z (n) ; Θξ ), ∀n = 1, · · · , Nl .                                                                                               (2)
160
161   During the training procedure, ξ(·; Θξ ) is an expert module used to model MLP. z (n) and Θξ are
      randomly initialized, and z (n) represents the learned latent vector for the nth layer of the MLP.

167   HyperNetwork may not satisfactorily dynamic learning for MLLM for two key reasons:
168

169
               model’s parameters. This approach lacks a strong correlation between MLLM’s dynamic
170
               parameters and input multimodal instructions.

172

173
               the input dimension of HyperNetwork. Optimizing such a vast number of parameters is
174
               challenging, and the optimization process is inherently unstable.

206   2.3   V ISUAL E XPERT-A SSISTED P ROJECTOR
207
      In this stage, our objective is to adapt the image tokens to LLM, allowing the LLM to comprehend the
208
      instances in the images. As shown in Figure 2, we divide the projector as static layers and dynamic
209
      layers. Following LLaVA1.5 Liu et al. (2023a), we employ two-layer MLPs as the static layers. To
210   empower the projector’s expression, we develop a visual expert who learns the projector shifts to
211   model the dynamic visual tokens. Specifically, we regard the visual feature fV extracted from the
212   visual encoder as the visual guidance GV , the visual expert will adaptively assist the projector that
213   converts GV to dynamic visual tokens. As commonly known, deep neural networks encode visual
214   features with increasing abstraction, generally, becoming finer as we progress over levels. Given
215   two-layer MLPs, we introduce two selectable configurations for dynamic vision-language alignment:
      dynamic anterior layer and dynamic posterior layer.

257   In this stage, LLM is adjusted to become an MLLM with multi-modal understanding. We use more
258
      complex instructions to achieve a stronger multi-modal understanding. Previous studies have shown
      that features provided by the intermediate layer may suffice to preliminarily understand the given input
259
      samples Xin et al. (2020)and can serve as guidance hints to improve training Romero et al. (2014).
260

261   understanding of the given instruction. Therefore, we regard the output of the intermediate LLM layer

      of language expert tuning: attention-level integration and feedforward-level integration.

288   3.1   CMT B ENCHMARK .
289
290
      To thoroughly investigate the issue of multi-task negative interference and comprehensively bench-
      mark the diverse multimodal instruction following ability, we extensively gather and annotate a wide
291
      variety of multimodal datasets from different fields and scenarios. As illustrated in Figure 3, CMT has
292
      diverse forms of complex instructions and a vast range of instruction-following scenarios, covering 7
293   tasks across 22 scenarios, including Visual QA (VQA), Visual Captioning (VC), Spatial Inference
294   (SI), Detailed Description (DD), Visual Storytelling (VS), Knowledge OCR (KOCR), Text-Rich
295   Images QA (TQA). The tasks are selected that considered five interference dimensions: (i) Single
296   and Multiple Image Processing → Visual Captioning and Visual Storytelling; (ii) Pure Vision
297   and Multimodal Information → Visual QA and Text-Rich Images QA; (iii) Visually Global and
298   Local Details Understanding → Detailed Description and Spatial Inference; (iv) Visual and Textual
299   Recognition in Images→ Spatial Inference and Knowledge OCR; (v) Brief and Detailed Textual
300   Understanding→ Visual Captioning and Detailed Description. All task instances are transformed
301
      into a unified instruction-response form for zero-shot evaluation, including {Task Instruction},
      {Task Instance} and {Response}. In total, CMT includes 505,405 multi-round instruction-
302
      response pairs conversations for training and randomly selected 1,149 instruction-response pairs for
303
      evaluation. Please refer to Appendix 6 for more details of the developed CMT benchmark.

342   Existing Benchmarks. We benchmark HyperLLaVA on a wide range of academic benchmarks,
343   including 5 VQA datasets and 7 Benchmark Toolkits in Table 1. In general, irrespective of
344
      the different benchmarks, HyperLLaVA achieves the best performance on almost all the multimodal
      scenarios across both datasets. Besides, compared to LLaVA, we show that HyperLLaVA achieves
345
      the best performance across 12 out of 12 benchmarks (7B version) and 10 out of 12 benchmarks
346
      (13B version). Such results benefit from the carefully designed dynamic visual and language expert,
347   which empowers the static projector and LLM to facilitate general multimodal tasks.
348
349   CMT Benchmark. To further measure the multimodal understanding capability, we conduct a
350
      comprehensive evaluation of our HyperLLaVA and the recent advanced MLLMs on the proposed CMT
      benchmark, which reveals several key findings: 1) HyperLLaVA consistently outperforms existing
351
      models by a large margin across all categories, which demonstrates stronger generalizability in
352
      following multimodal instructions with different types. 2) Despite existing vision-language models
353   have demonstrated comparable performance in following general multimodal instructions (e.g., Visual
354   QA and Visual Captioning), their competence seems to falter when simultaneously dealing with
355   the complex multimodal instructions (e.g., Spatial Inference and Knowledge OCR). Among these
356   widely varying multimodal tasks, this is perceived as a deficiency in multi-task interference, which
357   may introduce the negative transfer, thus attributing the performance discrepancy. In contrast, the
358   proposed visual and language experts can adapt MLLM’s parameters conditioned for every instruction
359   at two stages, alleviating the potential interference and improving multimodal comprehension across
360   different tasks. 3) The original LLaVA exhibits performance degradation when scaling up the LLM
361
      size, however, our model shows consistent performance improvement for all tasks, indicating the
      suitability and stability for different vision-language instruction understanding.

519
      Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). MLLMs leverage the power of LLMs, mitigating
520   extra computational cost and enhancing the efficacy of multimodal pre-training Zhang et al. (2024), to
521   bridge the gap between textual and multimodal data. Follow-up works of LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b),
522   MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023), InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023b), Qwen-VL-Chat Bai et al. (2023),
523   Flamingo Alayrac et al. (2022b), Otter Li et al. (2023a), mPLUG-Owl (Ye et al., 2023b) propose to
524   fine-tune MLLMs with multimodal instructions. To effectively benchmark the recent progress in
525   MLLMs, concurrent works of LVLM-eHub (Xu et al., 2023) and MME Benchmark (Fu et al., 2023a)
526   are proposed, while they mainly focus on instructions that only involve a single image with limited
527   instruction diversity. However, most of the pieces of literature focus on scaling up the pretraining data,
528
      instruction-following data, visual encoders or language models to facilitate multimodal understanding.
      How to alleviate the multi-task interference of MLLMs remains relatively underexplored. Thus, we
529
      propose HyperLLaVA, addressing the task interference based on the novel dynamic tuning strategy,
530
      yielding an improved understanding of diverse multimodal instructions.

533   5        C ONCLUSION
534   Building upon HyperLLaVA’s innovative dynamic tuning strategy, our work paves the way for
535   groundbreaking advancements in multimodal learning systems. By adaptively tuning both projector
536   and LLM parameters, and integrating dynamical visual and language experts, we not only surpass
537   the performance benchmarks set by LLaVA but also introduce a comprehensive multimodal task
538   benchmark. This approach offers a new horizon for enhancing multimodal task performances through
539   personalized, dynamic adjustments. Future research could further explore the scalability of dynamic
      tuning mechanisms, potentially unlocking new avenues for understanding multimodal instructions.

853   6   CMT B ENCHMARK
854
855   The majority of the 12 benchmarks assessed in Table 1 are primarily centered on a specific
856   task/domain (e.g., Visual Question Answering (VQA)) or straightforward reasoning tasks (MME
857   Benchmark). We contend that these benchmarks may not effectively evaluate the nuanced interplay
858   between different tasks. Therefore, we developed the CMT benchmark, encompasses five interference
859   dimensions among various tasks, serving as a fundamental basis for investigating task interference.
860   Data format. All task instances are transformed into a unified instruction-response form for zero-shot
861   evaluation. Formally, each instance in CMT consists of the following components:
862
863   • Task_Instruction: provides a complete natural language definition of a given task, including
        the input/output format and the task objective.

      • Task_Instance: is a concrete instance of a given task that consists of demonstrative image-text
871     sequential context (e.g., visually-rich textbooks, specific questions about the context).
872
873   • Response: represents the target output in natural language for a given task instruction and task
874     instance. For classification tasks, we convert the class labels as options into the instruction and ask
875     the model to output the option index in natural language as the response.

877   Without any specific emphasis, we use the term ”instruction” to refer to the combination of
878   Task_Instruction and Task_Instance.
879   Criteria for Task Selection. To thoroughly investigate the issue of multi-task negative interference,
880   we first established the Comprehensive Multimodal Task (CMT) benchmark, which is grounded in
881   five key interference dimensions:
882
883   • Interference of single and multiple image processing: Visual Captioning and Visual Storytelling;
884
885   • Interference between images with pure vision and multimodal information: Text-Rich Images
886     QA;
887
      • Interference between understanding global and local details: Detailed Description and Spatial
888
        Inference;
889
890   • Interference between visual and text recognition in images: Spatial Inference and Knowledge
891     OCR;
892
893
      • Interference between brief and detailed textual understanding: Visual Captioning and Detailed
        Description. Building upon the aforementioned criteria, we can effectively and comprehensively
894
        benchmarking of diverse multimodal instruction capabilities across current MLLMs and our
895
        proposed HyperLLaVA.

897
      Task Collection and Categorization. To comprehensively benchmark the diverse instruction fol-
898
      lowing ability, we extensively gathered a wide variety of multimodal datasets from different fields
899
      and scenarios, and performed some processing to obtain the data we wanted, such as we used
900   CogVLM Wang et al. (2023) to generate detailed descriptions for LAION-COCO and CC12M Chang-
901   pinyo et al. (2021b). As illustrated in Figure 3, CMT has three important properties: 1) Demonstrative
902   vision-language context, all instructions contain sequences of (one or more) images and text that are
903   highly correlated and together construct context, such as a storyboard with scripts, a textbook with
904   diagrams. 2) Diverse forms of complex instructions, the instructions range from designing panels
905   for comics, to discovering differences between surveillance images, and to conversational embodied
906   tasks. 3) Vast range of instruction-following scenarios, the benchmark covers multiple practical
907   scenarios, including cartoons, albums, etc.
908   Evaluation Protocols. Thanks to the unified task format of CMT, all tasks can be evaluated in a
909   zero-shot manner. For the open-ended generation tasks, we adopt ROUGE-L for evaluation. For the
910   tasks that require the models to output option indexes, we take Accuracy as the evaluation metric.
911   While well-formated options are provided, we empirically observe that many MLLMs struggle to
912   strictly follow instructions to output the option indexes but generate free-form text. Thus, when
913   models do not exactly output the required options, we match their outputs to one of the given options.
914   Benchmark Analysis. Table 9 details the statistics. The CMT benchmark is divided into two parts:
915   training and evaluation. CMT for training and CMT for evaluation both covers 7 tasks. In total,

949   7.1    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
950
951
      In the training of the HyperLLaVA, we utilize the ADAMW Loshchilov & Hutter (2017) optimizer,
952
      adapting hyperparameters to cater to the specific requirements of each phase. For the feature
953
      alignment stage, parameters are set as B = 32, Lr = 0.001, while for visual instruction tuning stage,
954   we adjust the parameters to B = 16, Lr = 0.00002. The configuration for the ADAMW optimizer
955   incorporates the following settings: β = (0.9, 0.999), ε = 1 × 10−8 , and Wd = 0.0, ensuring a
956   bespoke optimization strategy that effectively addresses the unique demands of each training phase.
957
      Besides, We train our model following the same training process as LLaVA-1.5. The process
958
      includes two stages: (1) feature alignment stage: use 558K subset of the LAION-CC-SBU dataset
959
      to connect a frozen pretrained vision encoder to a frozen LLM; (2) visual instruction tuning stage:
960   use a combination of 150K GPT-generated multimodal instruction-following data and approximately
961   515K VQA instances collected from academic-oriented tasks to guide the model in comprehending
962   multimodal instructions. In addition to leveraging the identical training dataset as LLaVA-1.5, we
963   introduce a supplementary CMT dataset comprising approximately 505K diverse data. This extensive
964   dataset enriches the model’s training regimen, bolstering its instruction-following performance and
965   tackling complex visual tasks with greater finesse.
966   It is noteworthy that while LLaVA-1.5 accounts for the number of images in the input visual instruction
967   task, it does not inherently possess the capability to comprehend intricate multi-image visual tasks.
968   Instead, it confines responses to a single image, thereby forfeiting multi -image contextual information.
969   HyperLLaVA extends this functionality by preserving all ¡image¿ tokens, sequentially substituting
970   ¡image¿ tokens with image features , and employing corresponding masks to avoid loss impact. This
971   augmentation enables the model to effectively process and respond to complex multi- picture visual
      task.

974    Recent advancements in LLMs (OpenAI, 2023a;b) have heralded significant achievements across
975    various domains. Inspired by this success, many MLLMs (Li et al., 2023d; Liu et al., 2023b; Zhu et al.,
976    2023; Alayrac et al., 2022a; Ye et al., 2023b; Gao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a) have been proposed
977    to foster generalist vision-language reasoning. In our experiments, we conducted comparisons with
978    some of the most recent and representative MLLMs in the following.
979
              • LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a) establishes a connection between the visual encoder ViT-
980
                L/14 from CLIP (Radford et al., 2021a) and the language decoder LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
981             2023), utilizing a lightweight, Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) layer. Initially, the system
982             trains this MLP layer using 558K image-text pairs, while keeping both the visual encoder
983             and LLM static. Following this, LLaVA fine-tunes both the MLP layer and LLM using
984             a dataset comprising 665K instructional vision-language pairs. The tested version are
985             ”LLaVA-1.5-7B” and ”LLaVA-1.5-13B”.
986           • MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023) bridges the gap between the visual encoder and text encoder
987             using a fully-connected (FC) layer. Initially, this model trains the FC layer on a dataset
988             comprised of 5M image-text pairs before fine-tuning it on 3.5K instructional vision-language
989             data. Notwithstanding its simplicity, MiniGPT-4 requires the loading of a pre-trained vision
990             encoder from BLIP2, as well as a Vicuna LLM (Chiang et al., 2023). The tested version is
991             “minigpt4-aligned-with-vicuna7b”.
992           • BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023d) employs a dual-stage strategy to seamlessly bridge the modality
993             gap, utilizing a lean Q-Former pre-trained on 129 million image-text pairs. The initial stage
994             kick-starts the learning process of vision-language representation, leveraging a frozen image
995             encoder, the ViT-g/14 from EVA-CLIP (Fang et al., 2023). Subsequently, the second stage
996             harnesses a frozen LLM, the Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), to initiate the vision-to-language
997
                generative learning. This innovative strategy effectively facilitates zero-shot instructed
                image-to-text generation. The tested version is ”blip2-pretrained-vicuna13b”.
998
999           • mPLUG-Owl (Ye et al., 2023b) introduces a visual abstractor, fundamentally close the
1000            Perceiver Resampler in Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022a), as a bridge between the pre-trained
1001
                visual encoder ViT-L/14 and the LLM (LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023)). This model adopts
                a two-stage fine-tuning procedure. In the initial phase, both the visual encoder and the
1002
                visual abstractor undergo comprehensive fine-tuning using a dataset of 204M image-text
1003
                pairs. Subsequently, in the second phase, mPLUG-Owl applies the 158K LLaVA-Instruct
1004            dataset to fine-tune the pre-trained LLM in a parameter-efficient manner through the use of
1005            LoRA (Hu et al., 2021a). The tested version is ”mplug-owl-llama-7b”.
1006
              • Otter (Li et al., 2023a) is a multimodal model that applies in-context instruction tuning based
1007
                on OpenFlamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022a). This model integrates a LLaMA-7B (Touvron
1008            et al., 2023) language encoder and a CLIP ViT-L/14. While the visual and text encoders
1009            remain static, Otter refines an additional 1.3 billion parameters. These parameters are
1010            derived from adaptation modules and are trained using 158K instruction-following data.
1011            The tested version is ”OTTER-Image-LLaMA7B-LA-InContext”.
1012          • InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023a) originates from a pre-trained BLIP-2 model, which consists
1013            of a ViT-g/14 image encoder, a Vicuna LLM, and a Q-Former to act as the bridge between

1017            ”blip2-instruct-vicuna13b”.
1018          • Shikra (Chen et al., 2023) utilizes CLIP ViT-L/14 as the visual encoder and Vicuna as LLM,
1019            with a single fully-connected layer connecting the feature spaces of visual encoder and LLM.

1024

1052   Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning. Our proposed language expert also can serve as a parameter-
1053   efficient fine-tuning function. The structure is similar to the HyperNetwork+Adapter. However,
1054   original hypernetwork-based approaches generally condition their parameters on a learned latent
1055   embedding, implying the model is the same for every example, yielding performance decay. Summing
1056   up, the proposed language expert is an effective and parameter-efficient way to share information
1057   across multiple adapters to enable positive transfer to low-resource and related tasks.
1058   Detailed Performance on MME. We report the detailed performance on the 14 subtasks of the MME

1061   further indicates the effectiveness of our method for diverse multimodal instruction understanding.
1062   Adaptation to other MLLM. To study the generalizability of dynamic tuning to other MLLMs,
1063   we utilized our expert module to train MiniGPT-4. The outcomes of the vision-language tasks, as
1064   presented in Table 15, employing MiniGPT-4, are as follows. Our approach seamlessly integrates

1067   out of 25 instances. In comparison to the original MiniGPT-4, the expert module yields a significant
