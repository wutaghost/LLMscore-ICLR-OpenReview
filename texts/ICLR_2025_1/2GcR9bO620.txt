015                                                A BSTRACT
016
017              Recent advances in AI-generated voices have intensified the challenge of detecting
018
                 deepfake audio, posing further risks for the spread of scams and disinformation.
                 To tackle this issue, we establish a large scale public voice dataset DeepFakeVox-
019
                 HQ, comprising 1.3 million samples, including 270,000 high-quality deepfake
020
                 samples from 14 diverse sources. Despite previously reported high accuracy, ex-
021              isting deepfake voice detectors struggle with our diversely collected dataset, and
022              their detection success rates drop even further under realistic corruptions and ad-
023              versarial attacks. We conduct a holistic investigation into factors that enhance
024              model robustness and show that incorporating a diversified set of voice augmen-
025              tations is beneficial. Moreover, we find that the best detection models often rely
026              on high-frequency features, which are imperceptible to humans and can be easily
027              manipulated by an attacker. To address this, we propose the F-SAT: Frequency-
028              Selective Adversarial Training method focusing on high-frequency components.
029
                 Empirical results demonstrate that our training dataset boosts baseline model per-
                 formance (without robust training) by 33%, and our robust training further im-
030
                 proves accuracy by 7.7% on clean samples and by 29.3% on corrupted and at-
031
                 tacked samples, over the state-of-the-art RawNet3 model.

      To address the above limitations, we first created the largest deepfake audio dataset to date,
      DeepFakeVox-HQ, including 270,000 high-quality deepfake samples from 14 diverse and distinct
080
      origins. We show that simply training on our collected dataset can produce new state-of-the-art
081
      models.
082
083   Moreover, we find that even the state-of-the-art AI-voice detection model (RawNet3) often depend
084   on high-frequency features to make decisions (see Figure 2), which are imperceptible to humans.
085
      On the other hand, the low frequency signals can be heard by humans but are not relied on by the
      model to make predictions. As a result, natural corruptions in high frequency or attackers can easily
086
      manipulate the model by changing the high frequency signals, reducing the detection’s robustness.
087
088   In an initial study, we observed that standard adversarial training on raw waveforms not only fails
089   to bolster robustness but also diminishes performance on unattacked data. To address these short-
090   comings, we propose Frequency-Selective Adversarial Training (F-SAT), which focuses on high-
091
      frequency components. Since our adversarial training is targeted, we can mitigate specific vulnera-
      bilities without touching the true features at lower frequencies, thus enhancing the model’s resilience
092
      to corruptions and attacks while maintaining high accuracy on clean data.
093
094   Visualizations and empirical experiments demonstrate that using only our training dataset, we can
095   produce state-of-the-art models, achieving a 33% improvement on the out-of-distribution portion of
096   our test set, which includes 1,000 deepfake samples from the top five AI voice synthesis companies
097
      and 600 samples from social media. Additionally, by incorporating random audio augmentations,
      our model achieves the highest accuracy across 24 different types of corruptions. Furthermore, after
098
      applying F-SAT, our model further achieves a 30.4% improvement against adversarial attacks in the
099
      frequency domain and an 18.3% improvement against unseen attacks targeting raw waveform data
100   in the time domain.

127   Detection Method: AI deepfake detection models based on deep learning can be grouped into two
128   main categories: those processing raw audio end-to-end and those analyzing spectrum. The first
129
      category includes models like RawNet2, which employs Sinc-Layers (Ravanelli & Bengio, 2018)
      to extract features directly from waveforms, and RawGAT-ST, which utilizes spectral and temporal
130
      sub-graphs (Tak et al., 2021). RawNet3 (Jung et al., 2022), which begins by using parameterized fil-
131
      terbanks (Zeghidour et al., 2018) to extract a time-frequency representation from the raw waveform
132   and then is followed by three backbone blocks with residual connections, a structural approach that
133   sets it apart from ECAPA-TDNN (Desplanques et al., 2020).These models process the audio data in
134   its raw form to capture nuanced details directly impacting model performance.
135

136
      grams for analysis. This process utilizes extracted features such as Mel Frequency Cepstral Coef-
137
      ficients (MFCCs) (Sahidullah & Saha, 2012), Constant Q Cepstral Coefficients (CQCCs) (Todisco
138   et al., 2017), bit-rate (Borzı̀ et al., 2022). The analysis of these features is conducted using tradi-
139   tional machine learning methods like Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) (Todisco et al., 2019) or
140   advanced neural networks such as Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (Bi-LSTM) (Akdeniz &
141   Becerikli, 2021), ResNet (Alzantot et al., 2019), and Transformers (Zhang et al., 2021). These meth-
142   ods enable deeper and more intricate pattern recognition, enhancing the model’s ability to identify
143   and classify deepfake audio accurately.
144   Adversarial Attack: Neural networks are highly vulnerable to nearly imperceptible perturbations,
145   known as adversarial attacks (Szegedy et al., 2013). Although initial studies focused on image mod-
146   els, speech tasks are similarly susceptible. Notably, adversarial attacks generated on spectrograms
147   can deceive 2D CNN models and, when converted back to waveforms, can also effectively fool 1D
148   CNN models (Koerich et al., 2020).
149
      Adversarial training, initially developed for image processing systems (Madry et al., 2017; Mao
150   et al., 2019), has been increasingly adapted to the audio domain (Chen et al., 2023), particularly to
151   enhance the robustness of applications such as Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) and speaker
152   verification systems. Another defense method, smoothing, based on additive noise masking, has
153   demonstrated great improvements in model robustness for these tasks (Olivier et al., 2021; Cohen
154   et al., 2019). Additionally, a defensive strategy that employs diffusion models to counter adversarial
155   audio attacks (Wu et al., 2023) effectively smooths out perturbations and prevents attackers from
156   altering audio signals without significantly compromising signal quality.
157   While these techniques enhance robustness, they hurt the detection on unattacked audio, highlight-
158   ing a trade-off between robustness and accuracy (Zhang et al., 2019; Tsipras et al., 2018). This
159   compromise is particularly critical in scenarios that demand high accuracy and user satisfaction.
160   Furthermore, the generalizability of adversarially trained models to new and unseen attacks remains
161   limited (Rajaratnam et al., 2018), raising questions about their effectiveness in rapidly evolving
      threat environments.

229   Let x be a waveform input audio, and y be its ground-truth category label, To perform classification,
230   neural networks commonly learn to predict the category ŷ = Fθ (x) by optimizing the cross-entropy
231   H(ŷ, y) between the predictions and the ground truth. We use RawNet3 (Jung et al., 2022) that can
232   process waveform audio input. The network parameters θ are estimated by minimizing the expected
233   value of the objective:
234                                       Lc (x, y) = H(Fθ (x), y),

      Time domain Attack: Adversarial attacks on audio can be directly added to the waveform. Let the
236
      additive perturbations be δ. For attacks in the time domain, we directly add δ to the waveform x.
237   Due to the huge amount of freedom of the attack vector δ, the added attack vectors are often high
238   frequency. We formulate this attack as: x′ = x + δ.
239
240
      Frequency Domain Attack: Attacks can also be applied in the frequency domain, accessed through
      a reversible Fourier transformation of the waveform. We transform the waveform x to the frequency
241
      domain X using the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT). The attack modifies X by adding δ,
242
      yielding X′ = X + δ. The Inverse Short-Time Fourier Transform (ISTFT) then reverts X′ back to
243   the time domain, creating the manipulated audio waveform x′ .
244
245   Frequency-Selective Attack: Compared to time-domain attacks, frequency-domain attacks provide
246

      a restricted bandwidth. Building on this, we introduce a Frequency-Selective Attack that targets
247
      specific frequency ranges within the magnitude component of a waveform. We define this attack as

      4.2   R ANDAUGMENT FOR AUDIO
304
305   Realistic Corruptions for Deepfake Audio Generation: As shown in Figure 9, we present a tax-
306   onomy for analyzing the robustness of deepfake audio detection systems that incorporate the most
307   common corruptions and attacks. Corruptions in audio result from unintentional modifications dur-
308   ing recording, processing, or transmission, impacting noise levels and frequency responses. Ad-
309   versarial attacks, in contrast, are intentional manipulations aimed at deceiving detection systems
310   through subtle changes across various attack methodologies.
311   RandAugment: Experiments show that the best detection model, RawNet3, experiences a great
312   drop in accuracy when faced with audio corruptions. Inspired by the image-based RandAug-
313   ment (Cubuk et al., 2020), which improves model robustness, we adapted this method for audio.
314   We selectively apply N transformations from the available options, each assigned a uniform prob-
315   ability. An additional probability p determines whether each transformation is applied at a given
316   instance, to balance the accuracy between original and corrupted samples. The magnitude of each
317
      transformation is controlled within predefined boundaries, with the intensity randomly sampled from
      this range.

      5.5       A BLATION S TUDY AND A NALYSIS
444
445   Choice of attack type for F-SAT: As discussed in Section 4.2, there are three types of adversarial
446   attacks on audio: time domain, frequency domain on magnitude, and frequency domain on phase.
447   All of these can be employed for adversarial training. However, our focus on targeting the magnitude
448   component in attacks is explained in Figure 6a. Through our evaluation, we found that attacks based
449   on magnitude in the frequency domain are particularly effective at degrading model performance.
450   This is due to the fact that changes in magnitude directly affect the amplitude of audio signals, crucial
451   for maintaining core acoustic features. Such changes alter the sound intensity across frequencies,
452   complicating the model’s task of distinguishing key characteristics between real and fake audio.
453
      Conversely, phase attacks have minimal impact on the model’s predictions, as phase alterations
      primarily influence spatial audio perception and do not significantly affect feature detection.
454

520   also susceptible to adversarial attacks. In contrast, low-frequency features, though more robust, do
521   not provide sufficient information on their own to train an effective detector. Therefore, simply using
522   a lowpass filter to eliminate all high-frequency features is not a feasible strategy; instead, a powerful
523   and resilient detector must retain and protect these high-frequency features.
524

526   method. To substantiate this approach, we conducted frequency-selective adversarial training on the
527   high-frequency phase component, which led to a substantial decline in accuracy on unattacked data.
528   This outcome suggests that the model may overfit to artifacts introduced by phase perturbations,
529   thereby diminishing its generalization capacity.

      In our study, we introduce a dataset DeepFakeVoc-HQ that addresses diversity and quality issues
534   in prior datasets, and provide a taxonomy to explore common audio corruptions and attacks. We
535   find that leading AI voice detection models depend on vulnerable high-frequency features. This
536   discovery leads us to develop F-SAT, a targeted adversarial training method that focuses on high-
537   frequency components while while preserving the integrity of low-frequency features. Our approach
538   effectively maintains accuracy on unattacked data and enhances robustness against various attacks.
539   These results pioneer robust training for detecting fake audio for the first time, opening up a new
      direction for identifying such threats.

733   A.3   EXPLANATION OF SOME TYPES OF CORRUPTIONS
734
735   Air Absorption: Air absorption refers to the phenomenon where high-frequency sound waves are
736   more strongly attenuated by air molecules. As sound travels through the air, it loses energy, partic-
737   ularly at higher frequencies, due to air viscosity and thermal conduction.
738   Room Simulator: A room simulator is a digital tool that emulates the acoustics of different rooms
739   or spaces. It includes parameters such as room size, wall materials, and shape, which affect how
740   sound reflects and diffuses.
741
      Peaking: Peaking refers to adjusting a specific range of frequencies around a central frequency. It
742
      is commonly used in parametric equalizers.
743
744   Aliasing: Aliasing occurs when high-frequency components are sampled below the Nyquist rate,
745   causing them to appear as lower frequencies. This can be represented using the Nyquist theorem:
746
                                                 fsample > 2 · fmax ,
747
748   where fsample is the sampling frequency, and fmax is the maximum frequency of the signal. When this
749   condition is violated, the signal is folded back into the lower frequencies, creating aliasing artifacts.
750   Bit-Crush: Bit-crushing reduces the bit depth of an audio signal, causing quantization errors.
751
752
      Tanh Distortion: Tanh distortion is a form of soft clipping achieved using the hyperbolic tangent
      function. The output y is given by:
753
                                               y = tanh(kx),
754
755   where x is the input signal, and k controls the amount of distortion. As k increases, the function
      approximates a hard clipping effect.
