009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            We show that memory-augmented Transformers (Memformers) can implement
012            linear first-order optimization methods such as conjugate gradient descent, momen-
013            tum methods, and more generally, methods that linearly combine past gradients.
014            Building on prior work that demonstrates how Transformers can simulate pre-
015            conditioned gradient descent, we provide theoretical and empirical evidence that
016            Memformers can learn more advanced optimization algorithms. Specifically, we
017
               analyze how memory registers in Memformers store suitable intermediate attention
               values allowing them to implement algorithms such as conjugate gradient. Our
018
               results show that Memformers can efficiently learn these methods by training on
019
               random linear regression tasks, even learning methods that outperform conjugate
020            gradient. This work extends our knowledge about the algorithmic capabilities of
021            Transformers, showing how they can learn complex optimization methods.
022

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   In-context learning (ICL) allows large language models (LLMs) to generate contextually appropriate
027   outputs based solely on examples and queries provided in a prompt, without requiring any parameter
028   adjustments (Brown, 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022).
029
      This remarkable ability has spurred research into understanding how Transformers can implement
      algorithms (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), with recent studies focusing on their capability
030
      to simulate optimization algorithms (Dai et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023a; Garg et al., 2022;
031
      Akyürek et al., 2022). Transformers have been shown to implement gradient-based optimization
032   during their forward pass, such as preconditioned gradient descent for linear regression tasks (Dai
033   et al., 2022; Mahankali et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2024).
034
035
      More recently, studies have demonstrated that Transformers can learn even more advanced optimiza-
      tion methods. For instance, Fu et al. (2023) showed that Transformers exhibit convergence rates
036
      comparable to Iterative Newton’s Method, a higher-order optimization technique that converges expo-
037
      nentially faster than gradient descent for in-context linear regression. Additionally, Vladymyrov et al.
038   (2024) proved that Transformers can, in fact, learn a variant of gradient descent that approximates
039   second-order methods, such as GD++ , achieving convergence rates similar to Newton’s method.
040   These findings lead to the central question of our paper:
041
042       Can Transformers efficiently “learn” more advanced gradient-based optimization methods?
043
044   We aim to address this question by revealing some of the representational power of Transformers as
045
      “algorithm learners,” further motivating the use of machine learning for discovering new optimization
      algorithms. To make our investigation more precise, we focus on learning the class of gradient-based
046
      algorithms obtained by linearly combining past gradients, known as Linear First-Order Methods
047
      (LFOMs) (Goh, 2017), where the (k + 1)st iterate is

054
      By “learning” an algorithm like CGD or LFOM, we mean two key things:
055
056   1. The Memformer, in its forward pass, under certain internal parameter settings, can perform
057   iterations of CGD and/or LFOM. This means that its architecture and parameterization are sufficiently
058
      expressive to execute these optimization methods as part of its computation.
059   2. The Memformer’s learnable parameters can be trained on linear regression tasks. When
060   using these learned parameters, which are shared across all in-context data samples in a batch,
061   the Memformer can execute “CGD-like” and “LFOM-like” iterations during a forward pass.
062   The surprising aspect lies in the Memformer’s ability to achieve competitive—and in some cases
063   even superior—performance compared to CGD, despite using a relatively small number of learned
064
      parameters shared across all test samples drawn independently of the training data.
065   Our key insight for efficiently learning LFOMs is to leverage memory-augmented Transformers,
066   known as Memformers (Wu et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021), which retain intermediate attention values
067   across layers. This memory enables Memformers to store past gradients, facilitating the execution of
068   advanced first-order methods such as conjugate gradient descent and momentum methods. The same
069   mechanism allows Memformers to implement more general LFOMs.
070   While unconditional learning of gradient methods remains out of reach, we build on related work
071   demonstrating that Transformers can learn gradient descent in the context of linear regression
072   tasks (Garg et al., 2022; Akyürek et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023a; Ahn et al., 2024; Zhang
073   et al., 2024). Inspired by these findings, and extending the work of Ahn et al. (2024), we conduct
074   a theoretical analysis of the loss landscape for memory-augmented linear Transformers that omit
075   softmax activation (Schlag et al., 2021; Von Oswald et al., 2023a; Ahn et al., 2024).
076   In the Appendix, we also include our experiments that Memformers can outperform Nesterov
077   Accelerated Gradient (NAG) and momentum GD. In summary, our main contributions are as follows:
078
079   M AIN C ONTRIBUTIONS
080
081      (1) Theoretical justification that Memformers can implement LFOM iterations, including
082
             CGD. We provide a rigorous theoretical framework showing that Memformers, when trained
             on linear regression tasks, can be configured to perform iterations of LFOMs in their forward
083
             pass, encompassing advanced algorithms like CGD. By leveraging their memory mechanisms,
084
             Memformers can store and effectively combine past gradients, enabling them to implement
085          these sophisticated optimization methods within their architecture.
086
087      (2) Empirical evidence of Memformers “learning” optimization algorithms. Through ex-
088
             tensive experiments, we demonstrate that Memformers can learn LFOMs, in a general sense,
             by training on random linear regression tasks. Remarkably, a Memformer utilizing a shared
089
             set of learned parameters is able to process batches of in-context data samples and perform
090
             competitively with, and in some cases even outperform, the CGD (and NAG) algorithm that is
091          individually optimized for and run separately on each data sample in the test batch.
092
             This finding is particularly surprising and significant because CGD tailors its optimization
093
             individually for each data sample, whereas the Memformer applies a general optimization
094          strategy learned from the training data across all samples. The ability of Memformers to
095          generalize optimization strategies across data samples using shared parameters highlights
096          their generalization capabilities, which have not been fully recognized in prior research.
097
         (3) Enhanced performance through multi-headed attention with theoretical insights. We
098
             show empirically that multi-headed attention improves Memformers’ test performance and
099
             offer a heuristic explanation for why increasing attention heads enhances loss performance on
100          test data.
101
102   Our main objective in this paper is to investigate the potential of memory-augmented Transformers
103   to learn advanced optimization algorithms in a general sense. We are not advocating for Trans-
104   formers as replacements for established optimization methods in practical applications. Instead,
105   we aim to shed light on the algorithmic capabilities of Transformers, inspiring further exploration
106   into how these architectures can learn and generalize complex algorithms. We believe our results
107   contribute to a deeper understanding of how augmented Transformers can facilitate optimization,
      which may ultimately lead to the discovery of new and practical gradient-based algorithms.

      1.1   R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Research on Transformers is extremely active, and we cannot hope to fully capture the breadth of the
111   related literature. Below, we summarize the most immediately relevant topics.
112
      In-Context Learning. The ability of Transformer models to perform in-context learning (ICL) has
113
      been extensively studied since its introduction by Brown (2020). Subsequent works have explored how
114   these models adapt to new tasks without requiring parameter updates (Xie et al., 2021; Von Oswald
115   et al., 2023b; Hahn and Goyal, 2023; Liu et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022; Wu et al.,
116   2022). This foundational research has paved the way for studies investigating how Transformers can
117   implement specific algorithms, such as gradient-based methods.
118
      Gradient-Based Methods in Transformers. Garg et al. (2022) analyze the learning of gradient
119
      descent within Transformers, particularly in the context of ICL for linear functions. Empirical studies
120   (Garg et al., 2022; Akyürek et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023a) have shown that Transformers
121   can learn gradient descent after being trained on random linear regression tasks. Expanding on these
122   results, Von Oswald et al. (2023a); Ahn et al. (2024) demonstrate that Transformers can implement
123   preconditioned gradient descent for solving linear regression problems presented in input prompts.
124   Notably, these works—as well as ours—utilize Linear Transformers as discussed in (Schlag et al.,
125   2021; Von Oswald et al., 2023a; Ahn et al., 2023).
126   Higher-Order Optimization Methods in Transformers. Transformers have also been shown to
127   learn higher-order optimization techniques, such as Newton’s method, expanding their capabilities
128   beyond first-order methods (Fu et al., 2023; Giannou et al., 2024; Vladymyrov et al., 2024).
129
      Memory-Augmented Transformers (Memformers). Memformers were introduced by Wu et al.
130
      (2020); Xu et al. (2021). These models retain intermediate attention values across layers through
131
      memory registers, enabling more complex computations and optimization methods to be learned.
132   While significant progress has been made in understanding how Transformers can learn gradient
133   descent, their potential for learning more sophisticated LFOMs remains largely unexplored. Our
134   work addresses this gap by showing how Memformers can efficiently implement a wide range of
135   advanced first-order and quasi-second-order optimization techniques, including CGD and momentum
136   methods, thereby pushing the boundaries of Transformer-based architectures.

252   Memformers can “learn” LFOMs in the specific sense discussed earlier in Section 1. Each layer ℓ of

326   In this section, we present our empirical results for Memformers “learning” conjugate gradient descent
327   (CGD), general linear first-order methods (LFOMs), and general LFOMs with GD++ . The method
328   GD++ is a quasi-Newton method where the inverse Hessian in Newton’s method is approximated by
329   a truncated Neumann series; for more details on GD++ , refer to Section A.10 of Von Oswald et al.
330   (2023a).
331   We consider the in-context loss function (12) for linear regression. The input dimension is set to
332   d = 5, and the number of training observations in the prompt is n = 20. Both the inputs x(i)
333   and the target weight vector w∗ are sampled from Gaussian distributions: x(i) ∼ N (0, Σ) and
334   w∗ ∼ N (0, Σ−1 ), where Σ = U⊤ DU. Here, U is a uniformly random orthogonal matrix, and D
335   is a fixed diagonal matrix with entries diag(1, 1, 1/2, 1/4, 1).
336

337
      matrices A0 , A1 , and A2 (as in (9)) are initialized with independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.)
338   Gaussian entries. Each gradient step is computed using a batch of size 1000, and we resample the
339   batch every 100 steps. We clip the gradient of each matrix to have a maximum norm of 0.01. All
340   plots are averaged over five runs, each with a different randomly sampled U (and thus different Σ).

509         research could explore further architectural innovations to unlock even greater capabilities.

511          LFOMs on quadratic functions, future work should extend this to more general objective func-
512          tions. Doing so may require novel training strategies, and possibly architectural adjustments to
513          handle non-quadratic functions. The role of nonlinear attention and the MLP component of
514          Transformers may also prove to be useful here.

       (iii) Efficiency vs. Generalization: Attention-based methods require more computation than
516
             directly implementing conjugate gradient descent or momentum GD. However, Transformers
517
             excel in learning general parameters, enabling LFOMs to generalize across new data without
518          needing per-instance optimization. Exploring practical use of such “learned optimizers” to
519          either warmstart a solver, or to potentially even bypass it, is a tantalizing research topic.
520
521    (iv) Theoretical Foundations and Convergence Analysis: Strengthening the theoretical basis of
522
            Transformers’ optimization capabilities, including convergence analysis and their alignment
            with classical optimization theory, is another important direction for future research.
523
524     (v) Meta-learning and Transfer Learning: The ability of Transformers to learn and general-
525         ize optimization algorithms offers exciting potential for meta-learning and transfer learning,
526         providing new opportunities in areas where traditional optimization methods fall short.

529   6.1   L IMITATIONS
530
531   We briefly remark on some limitations of our current framework. For instance, while Memformers are
532   quite versatile, our experiments (Figures 1, 2) indicate they do not radically outperform preconditioned
533   GD on general quadratic problems as in (12), where the preconditioner matrix Γℓ (and likewise, Aℓ )
534   for the current layer ℓ is the main contributor to loss performance at each update step ℓ (17). On the
535   other hand, this behavior is likely due to the task being quadratic, and a future study that tackles more
536
      general ICL formulations will likely shed light here.
537   Transformers can implement second-order methods like Newton’s method (Fu et al., 2023; Giannou
538   et al., 2024), which typically outperform LFOMs in convergence speed and accuracy. However, we
539   reiterate that the main focus of our paper is to explore the space of first-order optimization algorithms
      that augmented Transformers can learn, as opposed to looking for “the best” algorithm.

      B.1   N ESTEROV ACCELERATED G RADIENT M ETHOD (NAG)
885
      NAG is a commonly used optimization technique that builds on classical gradient descent by incorpo-
886   rating a momentum term that anticipates the next update. Specifically, the weights are updated using
887   the following update rules:
888
889
890
                                         vk+1 = wk + βk (wk − wk−1 )
891                                       wk+1 = vk+1 − ηk ∇f (vk+1 )
892
893   Here, βk controls the influence of previous updates (momentum), and ηk is the learning rate. In our
894   experiments, we selected ηk = 0.03 and βk = 0.9 after testing various values of these parameters
895   on the given distribution, as in Section 3.3. These values provided the best performance. The
896   momentum term allows NAG to “look ahead” in the optimization trajectory, which often leads to
897   faster convergence than vanilla gradient descent.
898
899   B.2   M OMENTUM G RADIENT D ESCENT (MGD)
900
901
      Momentum Gradient Descent operates similarly to NAG but without the anticipation of future steps.
      The algorithm updates the weights based on a momentum term that accelerates convergence in
902
      directions with consistent gradients. The update rule for MGD is given by:
903
904
905                                        vk+1 = βk vk − ηk ∇f (wk )
906
                                               wk+1 = wk + vk+1
907
908   In our experiments, the learning rate ηk = 0.005 and momentum parameter βk = 0.9 provided the
909   best results on the given distribution, as in Section 3.3. Momentum helps to mitigate oscillations in
910   directions with high curvature, stabilizing the optimization trajectory and leading to faster convergence
911   compared to gradient descent.
