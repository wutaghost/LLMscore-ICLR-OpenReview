009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Replay-based methods provide a promising solution to address catastrophic forget-
012            ting issue in continual learning. They try to retain previous knowledge by using a
013            small amount of data from previous tasks stored in a fix-sized buffer. In this work,
014            we invoke the information bottleneck principles and reveal some fundamental
015            limitations of those methods on their effectiveness in capturing the truly important
016            features from the prior tasks by relying on the buffer data selected according to
017            the model’s performance on known tasks. Since future tasks are not accessible
018
               during model training and buffer construction, the trained model and the buffer
               data tend to be biased towards making accurate predictions on the labels of known
019
               tasks. However, when new task samples are introduced along with labels, the
020
               biased model and the buffer data become less effective in differentiating samples
021            of the old tasks from those of the new ones. Inspired by the way humans learn over
022            time, we propose a novel relearning technique that makes use of additional past
023            data, referred to as the library, to test how much information the model loses after
024            learning the new task. We then realign the model towards those forgotten samples
025            by training on a carefully selected small subset samples from the library for a few
026            epochs with comparable computational cost as existing replay-based models. The
027            experimental results on multiple real-world datasets demonstrate that the proposed
028            relearning process can improve the performance of the state-of-the-art continual
029
               learning methods by a large margin.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033   Continual learning (CL) models are capable of learning from a continuous stream of various tasks
034   over time without forgetting previously acquired knowledge (Wang et al., 2024). Different from
035   the relatively simple task-incremental learning (TIL), the forgetting problem arises as a central
036   challenge for the class-incremental learning (CIL), as it seeks to balance the exploitation of new
037   knowledge while preserving existing information, thereby enabling models to become more versatile
038   and long-lasting learners. Replay buffer-based methods have been developed to address the forgetting
039
      problem. They integrate the concept of a “replay buffer” , which is a designated storage that contains
      a subset of previously learned data. In a replay buffer setting, the model θ learns from both the
040
      current task data Zt and the replay buffer Ct during model training on the t-th task. By combining
041
      the learning of the current task and the retained buffer data, the model is trained to recognize all
042   classes without specifying which task they belong to. The replay buffer contains information of
043   previous tasks 1, .., t − 1 that can help to mitigate catastrophic forgetting by simply including the
044   seen classes in the t-th task training. However, the replay buffer is fundamentally limited given how
045   it is constructed because the model may be biased when selecting the buffer without accessing the
046   future task data. Although some balancing mechanics such as up-sampling can be applied during
047   training to alleviate biases, they are insufficient to help the model regain the knowledge that has
048   already been lost. When the buffer is selected at the end of the t-th task, it may have already forgotten
049   about certain information of previous tasks as this information is less relevant when learning with Zt
050
      and Ct . However, such information may become crucial when generalizing to future new tasks.
051   While catastrophic forgetting has been widely recognized as a major bottleneck of CL, the exact
052   reason causing its occurrence is yet to be discovered. Most previous work attributes the inability
053   to retain the previous task data performance as the forgetting issue. However, they do not explore
      the case of wrong learning. In the case of wrong learning, the old model learns the wrong features

      In this paper, we take the first step of using the information bottleneck principle to explain the issue
078
      of shortcut learning that commonly exists in CL. In principle, a learning process distills the most
079   relevant information from an input variable X that is necessary for predicting an output variable
080   Y , effectively compressing X while retaining its predictive power for Y . The core idea is to find a
081   compact representation of the input data (such as ht ) that maximizes the model’s ability to predict the
082   output, minimizing irrelevant or redundant information. The retained knowledge can be quantified
083   by the mutual information. This effect is seen in the CL setting shown in Figure 1b. In Task 1 of
084   the split-CIFAR-10 dataset in the CL setting, the model learns to classify between only Plane and
085   Auto. This results in the compression of the information about Task 1 data in the hidden layer
086   representation I(X1 ; H) (red plot). This compression takes place because the model can learn to
087

      is trained on combined Task 1 and Task 2 data (Plane, Auto, Bird, and Cat), it can no longer
088
      rely on the sky to classify between Plane and Bird. Thus, the information about the same Task 1
089
      data contained in the hidden layer representation I(X1 ; H) (blue plot) will be higher.

091   This key theoretical insight inspires us to draw analogy from human learning to fundamentally
092   address the shortcut learning issue, advancing the state-of-art in CL. The connection between a
093
      machine’s continual learning and real human learning is often made as continual learning mimics the
      human ability to accumulate and build upon past experiences (see Figure 2), thus making artificial
094
      intelligence systems more adaptable and efficient in dynamic environments. Our innovative library
095
      concept further strengthens this connection by drawing inspiration from how humans leverage a vast
096   reservoir of prior knowledge to enhance learning. Just as humans access and retrieve information
097   from past learning resources to make sense of new experiences, the library in a CL model serves a
098   similar purpose. It acts as an expansive, accessible database from which the model can draw upon
099   past data, significantly enriching the current learning process. This approach is reminiscent of a
100   student revisiting notes from previous lessons or a professional drawing on past experience to solve a
101   new problem. Although revisiting alone helps in solidifying connections within the brain, making
102   it easier to recall information when needed, relearning reinforces the connection by using the most
103   current understandings and allows the swap of more long-term required information. The analogy to
104
      a library-based relearning in a human learning scenario is visualized in Figure 2.
105   It is important to note the fundamental difference between the proposed library and the replay buffer
106   commonly adopted by existing CL models. First, with a larger size, the library forms an accurate and
107   unbiased approximation of the true previous task data distribution. This allows it to provide a much
      richer knowledge source to overcome shortcut learning as verified by our theoretical investigation.

139   2    R ELATED W ORK
140
141   A continual learner continuously updates its parameters to adapt to dynamic environments. However,
142
      during this process, there is a risk that previously acquired knowledge may be overwritten due to
      parameter updates, a phenomenon known as catastrophic forgetting. To address this issue, various
143
      approaches have been proposed. In this work, we categorize and discuss these methods based on
144
      whether they involve task-id prediction, to contextualize our proposed method. Additional related
145   works are discussed in the Appendix.
146
147
      Non-task-id prediction-based CL models. Non-task-id prediction-based methods typically use a
      single classification head and share all parameters across tasks. To retain knowledge from previous
148
      tasks, these methods either regularize the parameters to prevent deviation or use rehearsal examples.
149
      Regularization-based methods estimate the importance of weights with respect to previous tasks.
150   For instance, EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) uses the Fisher information matrix to determine the
151   strength of regularization. Orthogonal to the regularization techniques, rehearsal-based methods
152   use small replay buffer to store a subset of examples from past tasks, periodically revisiting these
153   examples during training. These methods generally focus on either the utilization or selection of replay
154   examples. Particularly, beyond just using labels, they also align the behavior of the current model with
155   the past model on the replay examples using a knowledge distillation loss to enhance performance.
156   For example, Der++ Buzzega et al. (2020) and (Li & Hoiem, 2017) perform knowledge distillation
157   on output logits and feature space, respectively. While those method use randomly sampling, some
158
      methods focus on the selection criteria of replay examples to maximize the performance. The
      sampling criterion could be choosing the most confusing examples (Chaudhry et al., 2021) or samples
159
      with least second-order influence (Sun et al., 2023).
160
161   Task-id prediction-based CL models. In contrast to non-task-id prediction-based methods, task-id
      prediction-based methods employ multiple classification heads and share a portion of parameters


                                                                 3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      across different tasks. The goal is to isolate parameters so that previously learned parameters remain
163   fixed while learning new tasks. Specifically, we utilize the HAT model introduced by (Serra et al.,
164   2018), which employs hard attention to selectively activate parts of the model. However, during
165   testing, this approach relies on task-ids, which are only provided in task-incremental learning (TIL)
166   settings. To generalize such TIL approach to class-incremental learning (CIL), several strategies
167   have been developed to predict task IDs. For example, TPL (Lin et al., 2024) trains a separate gating
168   network using buffer data to predict task id, but its performance depends on the availability of a large
169   buffer. Other approaches operate independently of replay examples. For instance, MoE (Abati et al.,
170   2020) and MOE4CL (Yu et al., 2024) train multiple autoencoders to estimate the relevance between
171
      an input and a task. HATCIL (Kim et al., 2022c) and MORE (Kim et al., 2022b) leverage confidence
      scores from multiple classification heads to identify task-id, relying on the prediction from the most
172
      confident classification head. However, deep neural networks are known to produce overconfident
173
      predictions. In the context of continual learning, shortcut features can lead to overconfident prediction
174   or low reconstruction score, which can undermine the effectiveness of these methods. These methods
175   however only focus on a single task in their training objective making them susceptible to shortcut
176   learning which affects their task-id detection performance. Our library-based relearning approach
177   solves this problem by identifying the samples whose information is lost during the shortcut learning
178   and relearning those samples.
179
      Shortcut learning is a commonly observed behavior in deep networks, yet it is under-explored
180   in the context of continual learning. Our work is among the few attempts to address this issue
181   within continual learning (i.e., OnPro (Wei et al., 2023)). Our approach stems from the information
182   bottleneck perspective, and our techniques differ from the prototype learning strategy adopted by
183   OnPro (Wei et al., 2023).

190
      previous data Zt if we adopt no means to address it. There are two standard strategies to mitigate
191
      catastrophic forgetting. One is using a replay buffer, which is a small size data Ct selected from Zt
192   after training the model θt on Zt . The other approach predicts the task ID from (1, .., t) and then
193   uses the corresponding classifier. An enhanced task ID prediction approach may also use Zt + Ct−1
194   to calibrate the task ID prediction for better generalization. In order to further improve the task ID

196   approach with replay buffers. The feature extractor is continuously trained with Z1 , ..., Zt and only

219   To tackle the first challenge, we resort to using data augmentations during feature learning. Existing
220
      methods (Khosla et al., 2020) have connected contrastive learning with data augmentation to the
      maximization of the lower bound of two mutual information (MI) terms I(X; H) and I(Y ; H). This
221
      approach is intuitive as we want the learned features to contain the most information about both
222
      the input data and target labels of the tasks. We make use of their conclusion that the lower bound
223   of MI I(X; H) can be effectively maximized through contrastive learning using augmentations:
224   I(X; H) ≥ max I(X; X ′ ), where X ′ can be various augmentations of X. However, we make an
225   important observation here that even though we maximize I(X; H) within each task, the loss of
226   information will still happen when we move on to the next task. Additionally, the second challenge
227   also persists because augmentation only happens in-task. We contribute these issues to the shortcut
228   learning behavior of the model. Next, we analyze this shortcut learning phenomenon in the class
229   incremental setting using grouped features.

      Shortcut Learning from an information bottleneck perspective. The loss of information due to
231   shortcut learning can be explained from the perspective of Information Bottleneck principle. Let Yt
232   denote the set of labels corresponding to the inputs Xt for task t in the CIL setting. According to the
233   information bottleneck principle (Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby, 2017), a deep learning model compresses
234   the information about the input Xt and maximizes the information about the label Yt contained in
235   the hidden layer representation Ht . The relationship between these terms can be explained by the
236   chain rule: I(Xt ; Ht ) = I(Xt ; Ht |Yt ) + I(Yt ; Ht ). Thus, although the previous method (Khosla
237   et al., 2020) tries to maximize I(Xt ; Ht ) and I(Yt ; Ht ) at the same time, what happens naturally is
238   that the conditional MI I(Xt ; Ht |Yt ) will be regularized. An alternative formulation describes this

298   4.1   OVERVIEW OF THE R ELEARNING F RAMEWORK
299
      In the proposed framework, the training of each task is divided into three steps: (1) feature extractor
300
      training,(2) classifier head training, and (3) relearning. Figure 3 visualizes the overall framework.
301   While learning task t, the current data Zt is augmented to Zt′ , |Zt′ | > |Zt | to train a feature extractor
302   model fθ using a supervised contrastive loss (Khosla et al., 2020) in the feature space (S TEP 1). When
303   the feature extractor is trained for task t, it is frozen and a separate classifier head for task t is trained
304   using a cross-entropy loss to obtain the classification logits pt (S TEP 2). As the training objective
305   only focuses on a single task, the model only learns the features important to classify current task
306   classes. In this process, the model compresses the information needed to distinguish between the
307   current task and future tasks. Thus, when the future task data is present to the current task classifier
308   head, this current head will output larger prediction values for the future task data. This is how
309
      shortcut learning will hamper the task-id prediction performance. Although the replay-buffer-based
      methods can help the future task model to be calibrated with current task data, our theoretical analysis
310
      indicates that the replay-buffer is not enough because of lack of enough information and the smaller
311
      size of replay-buffer. Furthermore, just increasing replay-buffer size is not feasible as the computation
312   cost of training on the replay-buffer will increase with the size of replay-buffer.
313
314   Based on our theoretical analysis, we know that storing a larger dataset from already seen past
315
      training data is necessary to quiz the model to find which information to relearn. We call this dataset
      the library. It is important to note that the library only serves as a potential knowledge source to
316
      overcome shortcut learning. We propose an active quiz mechanism to test the model on the library.
317
      Specifically, when the model is trained on the new task data, quizzing on the library helps us detect
318   potential shortcut learning. Based on how well the model performs on the quiz, only a small portion
319   of carefully selected samples will be used to perform relearning. The relearning step (STEP 3) selects
320   a fraction of the dataset from the library, called the library buffer which is then used to relearn the
321   classifier heads. The size of this library buffer remains the same as the existing replay-buffer-based
322   approaches, avoiding additional training overhead. This enables the detection of the information lost
323   about past data attempts to restore that information in the model. In the next section, we will describe
      the relearning process in detail.

530   6    C ONCLUSION
531   We perform a theoretical investigation on the shortcut learning issue that commonly exists in modern
532   CL models from the lens of the information bottleneck principle. We address this issue by proposing a
533   novel relearning framework, which relies on a knowledge-rich library that forms an accurate unbiased
534   approximation of the entire data distribution of the previous tasks. The library provides a “testbed” to
535   identify the occurrence of shortcut learning through our uniquely designed active quizzes. Relearning
536   is invoked only if the model fails the test and a small subset of most informative data samples will be
537   selected from the library to perform relearning so that the computational overhead is comparable to
538
      relay-based CL model training. We show that the relearning complements state-of-the art CL model
      and can be used to boost their performance by a large margin.

      A.1    C ONTINUAL L EARNING
764
765   Catastrophic forgetting is one of the major challenges of continual learning (Wang et al., 2024).
766   It refers to the situation where a machine learning model dramatically forgets the knowledge for
767   prior tasks when being trained on new data for a new task. The current proposed solutions target
768   different steps during the training process, including replay-based (Chaudhry et al., 2019; Lopez-Paz
769   & Ranzato, 2017; Chaudhry et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2022; 2023), architecture-based (Jin & Kim,
770   2022; Gurbuz & Dovrolis, 2022; Douillard et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023), representation-based
771   (Pham et al., 2021; Gallardo et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2023), optimization-based
772   (Liu & Liu, 2022; Wang et al., 2022a; 2021), and regularization-based (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Benzing,
773
      2022; Park et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2022; Jung et al., 2020). Replay-based continual learning methods
      store a small amount of previous data to be included in the training process. Architecture-based
774
      continual learning methods modify the neural network structure, such as increasing network size, to
775
      accommodate new information as well as retain old knowledge. Representation-based methods focus
776   on learning a meaningful and general representation through regularization, feature distillation, or
777   generative replay. Optimization and regularization-based methods design loss functions to penalize
778   the neural network weight adjustment that would causes the forgetting of prior knowledge, in order
779   to achieve a balance between learning new knowledge and retraining old knowledge.
780
      A recent work based on influence function (Sun et al., 2023) argues that the buffer selection suffers
781
      from incidental bias. This bias occurs when we have the same or fewer samples in the pool (buffer)
782   as we go towards the subsequent tasks. This suggests a need to have a wide variety of slightly larger
783   data in our collection from which we can choose a smaller buffer which is then used to train a model.
784   This motivates and justifies the use of the library in our work. As the stored library is only an order
785   magnitude larger than the buffer size, the memory requirement is compared to the methods that store
786   auxiliary information, augment the dataset, or train a generative model to replay the previous dataset.
787   Furthermore, the computational cost is the same as that of the past methods because we use the same
788   buffer size as the past methods to train the model.
789
790   A.2    I NFORMATION T HEORY
791
792   The information theory in deep learning was first introduced by (Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby, 2017). They
793   show how the DNNs compress the information about the input and maximize the information about
794   the labels contained in the representation as the training progresses for a larger number of iterations.
795   (Saxe et al., 2018) shows that compression only occurs for a limited number of activation functions
796
      such as tanh and sigmoid activation functions. They also show how information compression can
      still take place for irrelevant datasets. (Kawaguchi et al., 2023) introduce the conditional mutual
797
      information and give improved generalization bound using the conditional mutual information. In
798
      our work, we use mutual information values to explain the effect of shortcut learning in the continual
799   learning setting which worsens the issue of catastrophic forgetting.

803   The relearning approach is also described in algorithm 1. To learn a task t, first the feature extractor
804   fθt parameterized by θ t is trained using the current task data Zt . The current data is augmented
805   and supervised contrastive loss is used in the feature space. In STEP 2, a linear classifier head gθgt
806   parameterized by θ gt for task t is trained using cross-entropy loss. Relearning takes place in STEP 3
807   where the classifier heads from task 1 to t are updated using the library buffer. Library is selected
808   randomly from the current dataset to make a selection unbiased and diverse. Library buffer is selected
809   from library using equation 5. During inference, the task-id is predicted such that the classifier head
      of the task with the highest logit is selected to make the prediction.

858   uses a bias-correction layer along with a validation dataset. DER++(Buzzega et al., 2020) is both a
859   replay and regularization-based method that tries to solve forgetting by preventing the model from
860   deviating too much from the past task logits. HAT (Serra et al., 2018) is a parameter isolation-based
861   method that learns a mask to protect important parameter for each task. HyperNet (Von Oswald
862   et al., 2019) is a parameter isolation-based method that uses task-specific parameters based on task-id
863   and uses entropy to predict task-id. Sup (Wortsman et al., 2020) is also a parameter isolation-based
      approach that learns a mask to isolate parameters for each task. CLOM (Kim et al., 2022a) is also

1050   D     S OCIAL I MPACT AND L IMITATIONS
1051
1052   In this work, we contribute to improving continual learning, which holds the potential to enable more
1053   efficient machine learning tasks including data processing, decision-making, and automation. Our
1054   proposed method is generally applicable to any replay-buffer based continual learning strategy. We
1055   expect similar limitations as other buffer-based methods because the implementation of relearning
1056   strategies may require additional computational resources for data storage and processing, potentially
1057
       limiting the applicability in resource-constrained environments. The additional storage and computa-
       tion required are however minimal as we analyzed in the paper. Concerns regarding data privacy and
1058
       access could restrict the ability to fully utilize these methodologies in sensitive applications.
