009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) is an important area in the AI commu-
012            nity, and interpretability is crucial for building robust and trustworthy AI mod-
013            els. While previous work has explored model-level and instance-level explainable
014            graph learning, there has been limited investigation into explainable graph rep-
015            resentation learning. In this paper, we focus on representation-level explainable
016            graph learning and answer a fundamental question: What specific information
017            about a graph is captured in graph representations? Our approach is inspired by
018            graph kernels, which evaluate graph similarities by counting substructures within
019
               specific graph patterns. First, we present an unsupervised ensemble graph kernel
               method for representation or similarity explanation, which however has limita-
020
               tions such as ignoring node features and being computationally expensive. To
021
               address these limitations, we introduce a deep learning framework for learning
022            and explaining graph representations through graph pattern analysis. We start by
023            sampling graph substructures of various patterns. Then, we learn the representa-
024            tions of these patterns and combine them using a weighted sum, where the weights
025            indicate the importance of each graph patternÔÇÖs contribution. Note that our method
026            can be both unsupervised and supervised and is a one-shot explanation, not speci-
027            fied to single samples or predictions. We also theoretically analyze the robustness
028            and generalization ability of our models. Importantly, the generalization analysis
029            shows that incorporating multiple graph patterns lowers the generalization error
030
               bound. In our experiments, we show how to learn and explain graph represen-
               tations for real-world data using pattern analysis. Additionally, we compare our
031
               method against multiple baselines in both supervised and unsupervised learning
032
               tasks to demonstrate its superiority in terms of accuracy.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   The field of explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) (Dos╠îilovic╠ü et al., 2018; Adadi & Berrada, 2018;
039
      Angelov et al., 2021; Hassija et al., 2024) is gaining significant attention in both AI and science
      communities. Interpretability is crucial for creating robust and trustworthy AI models, especially
040
      in critical domains like transportation, healthcare, law, and finance. Graph learning is an important
041
      area of AI that particularly focuses on graph-structured data widely exist in social science, biology,
042   chemistry, etc. Explainable graph learning (XGL) (Kosan et al., 2023) can be generally classified
043   into two categories: model-level methods and instance-level methods.
044
045
      Model-level methods of XGL provide transparency by analyzing the model behavior. Examples in-
      clude XGNN (Yuan et al., 2020), GLG-Explainer (Azzolin et al., 2022), and GCFExplainer (Huang
046
      et al., 2023). Instance-level methods of XGL offer explanations tailored to specific predictions, fo-
047
      cusing on why particular instances are classified in a certain manner. For instance, GNNExplainer
048   (Ying et al., 2019) identifies a compact subgraph structure crucial for a GNNÔÇÖs prediction. PGEx-
049   plainer (Luo et al., 2020) trains a graph generator to incorporate global information and parameterize
050   the explanation generation process. AutoGR (Wang et al., 2021) introduces an explainable AutoML
051   approach for graph representation learning. MotifExplainer (Yu & Gao, 2022) identifies critical
052   motifs (small subgraphs) in a graph. UNR-Explainer (Kang et al., 2024) identifies the top-k most
053   important nodes in a graph to determine the most significant subgraph as the counterfactual expla-
      nation. More about XGL can be found in the Appendix C.1.

054
      However, these works mainly focus on enhancing the transparency of GNN models or identifying the
055   most important substructures that contribute to predictions. The exploration of representation-level
056   explainable graph learning (XGL) is limited. We propose explainable graph representation learning
057   and ask a fundamental question: What specific information about a graph is captured in graph
058   representations? Formally, if we represent a graph G as a d-dimensional vector g, our goal is to
059   understand what specific information about the graph G is embedded in the representation g. This
060   problem is important and has practical applications. Some graph patterns are highly practical and
061   crucial in various real-world tasks, and we want this information to be captured in representations.
062   For instance, in molecular chemistry, bonds between atoms or functional groups often form cycles
063
      (rings), which indicate a moleculeÔÇÖs properties and can be used to generate molecular fingerprints
      (Morgan, 1965; Alon et al., 2008; Rahman et al., 2009; OÔÇÖBoyle & Sayle, 2016). Similarly, cliques
064
      characterize protein complexes in Protein-Protein Interaction networks and help identify community
065
      structures in social networks (Girvan & Newman, 2002; Jiang et al., 2010; Fox et al., 2020).
066
067   Although some previous works such as (Kosan et al., 2023) aimed to find the most critical subgraph
068   S by solving optimization problems based on perturbation-based reasoning, either factual or coun-
069
      terfactual, this kind of approach assumes that the most important subgraph S mainly contributes to
      the representation g, neglecting other aspects of the graph, which doesnÔÇÖt align well with our goal of
070
      thoroughly understanding graph representations. Analyzing all subgraphs of a graph G is imprac-
071
      tical due to their vast number. To address the challenge, we propose to group the subgraphs into
072   different graph patterns, like paths, trees, cycles, cliques, etc, and then analyze the contribution of
073   each graph pattern to the graph representation g.
074
075
      Our idea of pattern analysis is inspired by graph kernels, which compare substructures of specific
      graph patterns to evaluate the similarity between two graphs (Kriege et al., 2020). For example, ran-
076
      dom walk kernels (Borgwardt et al., 2005; Ga╠łrtner et al., 2003) use path patterns, sub-tree kernels
077
      (Da San Martino et al., 2012; Smola & Vishwanathan, 2002) examine tree patterns, and graphlet ker-
078   nels (Prz╠îulj, 2007; Shervashidze et al., 2009) focus on graphlet patterns. The graph kernel involves
079   learning a pattern counting representation vector h, which counts the occurrences of substructures
080   of a specific pattern within the graph G. While the pattern counting vector h is an explainable rep-
081   resentation, it has some limitations, such as the high dimensionality and ignorance of node features.
082
      There also exist some representation methods based on subgraphs and substructures, such as Sub-
083
      graph Neural Networks (SubGNN) (Kriege & Mutzel, 2012), Substructure Assembling Network
084   (SAN) (Zhao et al., 2018), Substructure Aware Graph Neural Networks (SAGNN) (Zeng et al.,
085   2023a), and Mutual Information (MI) Induced Substructure-aware GRL (Wang et al., 2020). How-
086   ever, these methods mainly focus on increasing expressiveness and do not provide explainability for
087   representation learning. We will discuss the details in the Appendix C.2.
088
      In this work, we propose a novel framework to learn and explain graph representations via graph
089
      pattern analysis. We start by sampling graph substructures of various patterns. Then, we learn the
090   representations of these patterns and combine them adaptively, where the weights indicate the im-
091   portance of each graph patternÔÇÖs contribution. We also provide theoretical analyses of our methods,
092   including robustness and generalization. Additionally, we compare our method against multiple
093   baselines in both supervised and unsupervised learning tasks to demonstrate its effectiveness and
094   superiority. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
095
096          ÔÇó Unlike previous model-level and instance-level XGL, we introduce a new problem ÔÇö
097            representation-level explainable graph learning. This problem focuses on understanding
098            what specific information about a graph is embedded within its representations in unsuper-
099            vised learning.
100
101          ÔÇó We propose two strategies to learn and explain graph representations, including a graph
102            ensemble kernel method and a pattern analysis GNN method. The latter involves using
103            GNNs to learn the representations of each pattern and evaluate its contribution to the en-
104            semble graph representation.
105
106          ÔÇó We provide robust analyses and generalization analysis for our methods theoretically. Par-
107            ticularly, our generalization analysis shows adding graph patterns lowers the generalization
               error bound.

      3.3           L IMITATIONS OF PATTERN C OUNTING V ECTOR
234
235   The pattern counting vector h from Definition 3.1 is easy to understand and its importance can be
236   evaluated using the weight parameter ╬╗ÔłŚ from (6). However, it cannot directly explain the repre-
237   sentation of graph G due to the following limitations, which are also the limitations of the proposed
238   PXGL-EGK.
239
                    ÔÇó Ignoring Node Features: h captures the topology of G but ignores node features X. As
240
                      shown by previous GNN works, node features are crucial for learning graph representa-
241
                      tions.
242
                    ÔÇó High Dimensionality: The pattern set P = {P1 , P2 , . . . , Pi , . . .} can be vast, making h
243
                      high-dimensional and impractical for many tasks.
244
245                 ÔÇó High Computational Complexity: Counting patterns Pi in G is time-consuming due to
246
                      the large number of patterns in P. The function ¤Ľ(G; P) needs to be run for each new
                      graph. In addition, in PXGL-EGK, the computation of the M kernel matrices of size |G| ├Ś
247
                      |G| is very expensive especially when |G| is large.
248
249
                    ÔÇó Lacking Implicit Information and Strong Expressiveness: h is fixed and not learnable.
                      GNN (Kipf & Welling, 2016) shows that message passing can learn implicit information
250
                      and provide better representations, which should be considered if possible.

394   5.3   T IME AND S PACE C OMPLEXITY
395
396   Given a dataset with N graphs (each has n nodes and e edges), we select M different patterns and
397   sample Q subgraphs of each pattern. First, our PXGL-EGK requires computing M kernel matrices,
                                                                                       PM
398   of which the space complexity is O(M N 2 ) and the time complexity is O(N 2 m=1 ¤łi ), where
399   ¤łi denotes the time complexity of the m-th graph kernel. For instance, the time complexities of
400
      the graphlet kernel, shortest path kernel, and Weisfeiler-Lehman Subtree kernel are O(nk ), O(n4 ),
      and O(hn + he) respectively, where k and h are some kernel-specific hyperparameters. When N is
401
      large, the method has high time and space complexities. Regarding PXGL-GNN, suppose each rep-
402
      resentation learning function Fm is an L-layer GCN, of which the width is linear with d. For both
403   supervised and unsupervised learning, suppose the batch size and the number of iterations in the
404   optimization are B and T respectively. Then, in supervised learning, the space complexity and time
405
                                                                                            PM
      complexity are O(BM Q(e + nd) + M Ld2 + Cd) and O(T BM QL(ed + nd2 ) + N Q m=1 ¤Ĺm )
406   respectively, where ¤Ĺm denotes the time complexity of generating a sample of the m-th pattern. For
407   instance, when the m-th pattern is graphlets with size k Ôłł {3, 4, 5}, we have ¤Ĺm ÔëĄ nukÔłĺ1 (Sher-
408   vashidze et al., 2009), where u denotes the maximum node degree of the graph. In unsupervised
409   learning, the space complexity and time complexity are O(BM Q(e + nd) + M Ld2 + Cd + B 2 ) and
                                                 PM
410   O(T BM QL(ed + nd2 ) + T B 2 + N Q m=1 ¤Ĺm ) respectively. PXGL-GNN is scalable to large
411   graph datasets because the complexities are linear with BM Q and B 2 and ¤Ĺm are controllable.

      C.1   E XPLAINABLE G RAPH L EARNING (XGL)
811
812   Explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) is a rapidly growing area in the AI community (Dos╠îilovic╠ü
813   et al., 2018; Adadi & Berrada, 2018; Angelov et al., 2021; Hassija et al., 2024). Explainable graph
814   learning (XGL) (Kosan et al., 2023) can be roughly classified into two categories: model-level
815   methods and instance-level methods.
816
817   Model-level Model-level or global explanations aim to understand the overall behavior of a model
818   by identifying patterns in its predictions. For example, XGNN(Yuan et al., 2020) trains a graph
819   generator to create graph patterns that maximize a certain prediction, providing high-level insights
820   into GNN behavior. GLG-Explainer(Azzolin et al., 2022) combines local explanations into a logi-
821
      cal formula over graphical concepts, offering human-interpretable global explanations aligned with
      ground-truth or domain knowledge. GCFExplainer(Huang et al., 2023) uses global counterfactual
822
      reasoning to find representative counterfactual graphs, providing a summary of global explanations
823
      through vertex-reinforced random walks on an edit map of graphs.
824
825
      Instance-level Instance-level methods offer explanations tailored to specific predictions, focusing
826
      on why particular instances are classified in a certain manner. For instance, GNNExplainer (Ying
827   et al., 2019) identifies a compact subgraph structure and a small subset of node features crucial for
828   a GNNÔÇÖs prediction. PGExplainer (Luo et al., 2020) trains a graph generator to incorporate global
829   information and uses a deep neural network (DNN) to parameterize the explanation generation pro-
830   cess. SubgraphX (Yuan et al., 2021) efficiently explores different subgraphs using Monte Carlo tree
831   search to explain predictions. RG-Explainer (Shan et al., 2021) constructs a connected explanatory
832   subgraph by sequentially adding nodes, consistent with the message passing scheme. MixupEx-
833   plainer (Zhang et al., 2023a) introduces a general form of Graph Information Bottleneck (GIB) to
834   address distribution shifting issues in post-hoc graph explanation. AutoGR (Wang et al., 2021) in-
835

      C.2   G RAPH R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
842
843   Graph representation learning is crucial for transforming complex graphs into vectors, particularly
844   for tasks like classification. The methods for graph representation learning are mainly classified into
845   two categories: supervised and unsupervised learning.
846
847   Supervised Representation Learning Most GNNs can be used in supervised graph represen-
848   tation learning tasks by aggregating all the node embeddings into a graph representation using a
849   readout function (Hamilton, 2020; Chami et al., 2022). Besides traditional GNNs like GCN (Kipf
850   & Welling, 2016), GIN (Xu et al., 2018), and GAT (Velic╠îkovic╠ü et al., 2017), recent works include:
851   Template-based Fused Gromov-Wasserstein (FGW) (Vincent-Cuaz et al., 2022) computes a vec-
852
      tor of FGW distances to learnable graph templates, acting as an alternative to global pooling lay-
      ers. Path Isomorphism Network (PIN) (Truong & Chin, 2024) introduces a graph isomorphism test
853
      and a topological message-passing scheme operating on path complexes. Graph U-Net (Amouzad
854
      et al., 2024) proposes GIUNet for graph classification, combining node features and graph struc-
855   ture information using a pqPooling layer. Unified Graph Transformer Networks (UGT) (Lee et al.,
856   2024) integrate local and global structural information into fixed-length vector representations us-
857   ing self-attention. CIN++ (Giusti et al., 2023) enhances topological message passing to account for
858   higher-order and long-range interactions, achieving state-of-the-art results. Graph Joint-Embedding
859   Predictive Architectures (Graph-JEPA) (Skenderi et al., 2023) use masked modeling to learn em-
860   beddings for subgraphs and predict their coordinates on the unit hyperbola in the 2D plane.
861
862   Unsupervised Representation Learning Unsupervised methods aim to learn graph representa-
863   tions without labeled data. Notable methodologies include: InfoGraph (Sun et al., 2019) emphasizes
      mutual information between graph-level and node-level representations. Graph Contrastive Learn-


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      ing techniques (You et al., 2020; Suresh et al., 2021; You et al., 2021) enhance graph representations
865   through diverse augmentation strategies. AutoGCL (Yin et al., 2022) introduces learnable graph
866   view generators. GraphACL (Luo et al., 2023) adopts a novel self-supervised approach. InfoGCL
867   (Xu et al., 2021) and SFA (Zhang et al., 2023b) focus on information transfer and feature augmen-
868   tation in contrastive learning. Techniques like GCS (Wei et al., 2023), NCLA (Shen et al., 2023),
869   S3 -CL (Ding et al., 2023), and ImGCL (Zeng et al., 2023b) refine graph augmentation and learn-
870   ing methods. GRADATE (Duan et al., 2023) integrates subgraph contrast into multi-scale learning
871   networks.
872
873   GNNs using Subgraphs and Substructures Our pattern analysis method samples subgraphs
874   from different graph patterns to conduct explainable graph representation learning. The key novelty
875   and contribution of our paper is that graph pattern analysis provides explainability for representa-
876   tions. We discuss other GNN methods based on subgraphs and substructures here: Subgraph Neural
877   Networks (SubGNN) (Kriege & Mutzel, 2012) learn disentangled subgraph representations using
878
      a novel subgraph routing mechanism, but they sample subgraphs randomly, lacking explainability.
      Substructure Aware Graph Neural Networks (SAGNN) (Zeng et al., 2023a) use cut subgraphs and
879
      return probability to capture structural information but focus on expressiveness rather than explain-
880
      ability. Mutual Information (MI) Induced Substructure-aware GRL (Wang et al., 2020) maximizes
881   MI between original and learned representations at both node and graph levels but does not pro-
882   vide explainable representation learning. Substructure Assembling Network (SAN) (Zhao et al.,
883   2018) hierarchically assembles graph components using an RNN variant but lacks explainability in
884   representation learning.
885
      Several works focus on analyzing the expressiveness of methods by their ability to count substruc-
886
      tures, but they do not provide explainable representation learning. For example: (Chen et al., 2020)
887   analyze the expressiveness of MPNNs (Gilmer et al., 2017) and 2nd-order Invariant Graph Networks
888   (2-IGNs) (Maron et al., 2019) based on their ability to count specific subgraphs, highlighting tasks
889   that are challenging for classical GNN architectures but not focusing on explainability. (Frasca et al.,
890   2022) compare the expressiveness of SubGNN (Kriege & Mutzel, 2012) and 2-IGNs (Maron et al.,
891   2019) using symmetry analysis, establishing a link between Subgraph GNNs and Invariant Graph
892   Networks.

894   C.3   G RAPH K ERNELS
895
896   Graph kernels evaluate the similarity between two graphs. Over the past decades, numerous graph
897   kernels have been proposed (Siglidis et al., 2020). We classify them into two categories: pattern
898   counting kernels and non-pattern counting kernels.
899
900   Pattern Counting Kernels Pattern counting kernels compare specific substructures within graphs
901   to evaluate similarity (Kriege et al., 2020). For examples, Random walk kernels (Borgwardt et al.,
902   2005; Ga╠łrtner et al., 2003) measure graph similarity by counting common random walks between
903   graphs. Shortest-path kernels(Borgwardt & Kriegel, 2005) compare graphs using the shortest dis-
904
      tance matrix generated by the Floyd-Warshall algorithm, based on edge values and node labels.
      Sub-tree kernels (Da San Martino et al., 2012; Smola & Vishwanathan, 2002) decompose graphs
905
      into ordered Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) and use tree kernels extended to DAGs. Graphlet
906
      kernels (Prz╠îulj, 2007) count small connected non-isomorphic subgraphs (graphlets) within graphs
907   and compare their distributions. Weisfeiler-Lehman subtree kernels (Kriege & Mutzel, 2012) use
908   small subgraphs, like graphlets, to compare graphs, allowing flexibility to compare vertex and edge
909   attributes with arbitrary kernel functions.
910
911
      Non-pattern Counting Kernels Non-pattern counting kernels evaluate graph similarity without
912   relying on specific substructure counts. For examples, Neighborhood hash kernel (Hido & Kashima,
913   2009) use binary arrays to represent node labels and logical operations on connected node labels.
914   This kernel has linear time complexity. GraphHopper kernel (Feragen et al., 2013) compare shortest
915   paths between node pairs using kernels on nodes encountered while hopping along shortest paths.
916   Graph hash kernel (Shi et al., 2009) use hashing for efficient kernel computation, suitable for data
917   streams and sparse feature spaces, with deviation bounds from the exact kernel matrix. Multiscale
      Laplacian Graph (MLG) kernel (Kondor & Pan, 2016) account for structure at different scales using


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Feature Space Laplacian Graph (FLG) kernels, applied recursively to subgraphs. They introduce a
919   randomized projection procedure similar to the Nystrom method for RKHS operators.
