009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Large language models (LLMs) excel in complex tasks but often struggle with
013
               inconsistencies in problem framing, a critical skill for real-world scenarios. This
               paper introduces SPARK, a novel evaluation framework grounded in the Hierar-
014
               chical Three-Space Theory, to assess LLMs’ ability to identify missing informa-
015
               tion and challenge flawed problem setups. We propose a general framework to
016            create benchmarks by introducing inconsistencies and misleading cues in diverse
017            question-answering datasets, covering mathematics, science, and reading compre-
018            hension. To assist with robust measuring of critical thinking, we employ two key
019            metrics: problem-solving capability rate and challenge rate. Our experiments with
020            state-of-the-art LLMs reveal their limitations in critical thinking, particularly in
021            recognizing inconsistencies. We also explore mitigation strategies, such as modi-
022            fied prompting and targeted fine-tuning. Furthermore, we conduct comprehensive
023            experiments to investigate how model and problem properties influence critical
024
               thinking capabilities in LLMs.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As large language models (LLMs) become increasingly integrated into decision-making processes,
031   ensuring they possess robust critical thinking skills is of paramount importance. While significant
032   attention has been given to LLMs’ ability to generate responses and solve problems, the research
033   community has also recognized the importance of understanding the limitations and potential risks
034   associated with these models (Weidinger et al., 2022; Kaddour et al., 2023). A crucial question
035   arises:
036            Can LLMs critically assess the very foundation of a problem—its initial framing—
037            and identify inherent inconsistencies?
038   Failure to do so could lead to flawed reasoning, inaccurate conclusions, and ultimately, unreliable
039   performance, especially in complex, real-world scenarios.
040
      Recent research has explored various facets of critical thinking in AI, including handling incomplete
041
      or ambiguous requests (Asai & Choi, 2021; Kamath et al., 2020; Kuhn et al., 2022), discerning truth
042   from falsehood (Xu et al., 2023; Chen & Shu, 2023), and reconciling contradictory information (Xie
043   et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). However, the ability to recognize inconsistencies in problem framing
044   remains under-explored. Current evaluation methods, while providing valuable insights into model
045   performance on well-defined tasks, often fail to capture the challenges posed by such inconsisten-
046   cies. This limitation highlights a significant gap in our understanding of LLMs’ capabilities.
047
      This study contributes to the ongoing discussion of LLM capabilities by introducing a novel frame-
048   work for assessing this specific aspect of critical thinking in problem-solving. We contribute a gen-
049   eral methodology for creating benchmarks to assess this crucial skill, addressing a significant gap in
050   current evaluation methods. Our work is grounded in the Three-Space Theory of Problem Solving
051   (Burns & Vollemeyer, 2000), which describes problem-solving as a process of interacting searches
052   within three distinct but interconnected spaces: the Problem Framing Space (the general understand-
053   ing of the task), the Strategy Space (possible solution approaches), and the Implementation Space
      (specific applications of those strategies).

106   Problem-Solving in Cognitive Science The Hierarchical Three-Space Theory of problem-
107   solving, which underpins our SPARK framework, is grounded in classic cognitive science theories
      (Newell, 1972; Stein et al., 1984) and addresses challenges of ill-structured problems (Rittel &


                                                              2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Webber, 1973; Simon, 1973). Its dynamic Problem Framing Space aligns with metacognitive pro-
109   cesses (Flavell, 1979) and complex problem-solving research (Dörner, 1986; Funke, 2010; Greiff
110   et al., 2014), representing interactions between problem framing, strategy development, and imple-
111   mentation. The theory integrates critical thinking skills (Elder & Paul, 2007; Facione, 1990; Dwyer
112   et al., 2014) and resonates with current complex problem-solving (CPS) frameworks (Quesada*
113   et al., 2005; Grable, 2006). SPARK extends these foundations, offering complementary perspec-
114   tives for evaluating LLMs. It provides a structured approach to assess critical thinking in artificial
115   agents, introducing quantifiable metrics like correctness, problem-solving capability and challenge
116   rates. SPARK’s examination of interactions between Problem Framing, Strategy, and Implemen-
117
      tation spaces offers a novel lens for understanding complex problem-solving processes. The LLM
      problem-solving strategy is shaped by prompting techniques. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al.,
118
      2022) breaks down problems into intermediate reasoning steps. Tree-of-Thought (Yao et al., 2024)
119
      extends CoT by exploring multiple branches of reasoning through a tree structure. Graph-of-Though
120   (Besta et al., 2024) extends CoT by structuring the reasoning process as a graph. Algorithm-of-
121   Thought (Sel et al., 2023) provides well-defined rules to guide the LLMs to reason logically and
122   effectively. By focusing on LLMs’ robustness to misinformation and the influence of model archi-
123   tecture on problem-solving capabilities, SPARK addresses contemporary challenges in AI.
124
      Critical Thinking in AI Recent literature explores critical thinking in AI through various lenses,
125
      including LLM noncompliance (Asai & Choi, 2021; Kamath et al., 2020; Brahman et al., 2024),
126   misinformation susceptibility (Xu et al., 2023; Chen & Shu, 2023), knowledge conflicts (Xie et al.,
127   2023; Zhou et al., 2023), input perturbations (Jia & Liang, 2017; Zhao et al., 2021), and sycophancy
128   (Perez et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023). These studies examine various facets of critical thinking
129   in LLMs, including their ability to recognize limitations, handle misinformation, resolve contradic-
130   tions, and resist biases. An emerging trend focuses on evaluating LLMs’ ability to assess and correct
131   reasoning processes, as exemplified by benchmarks like MR-BEN (Zeng et al., 2024), PRM800K
132   (Lightman et al., 2023), and MR-MATH (Xia et al., 2024), and others that evaluate higher-order
133   cognitive skills by examining the reasoning process. Recent work has further explored LLMs’ ca-
134
      pacity for self-correction (Tyen et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023) and provided metrics for scoring
      step-by-step reasoning (Golovneva et al., 2023).
135
136   Our work distinguishes itself by focusing on LLMs’ capacity to critique problem formulations across
137   domains and actively identify flaws in problem setups, a fundamental aspect of critical thinking often
138   overlooked. Using the Three-Space Theory, we provide a unified framework to evaluate this abil-
139   ity in multiple-choice, mathematical, and reading comprehension tasks, offering a comprehensive,
140
      cross-domain analysis of this critical thinking skill. Compared with existing benchmarks evaluating
      LLM on ambiguous or unanswerable questions (Brahman et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2023; Min et al.,
141
      2020), we create our dataset by modifying the options or context of well-defined questions and pro-
142
      vide a more fine-grained analysis of LLM responses, investigating the factors that influence their
143   critical thinking capabilities.

      3.1   A DAPTING H IERARCHICAL T HREE -S PACE T HEORY FOR LLM S AND E STABLISHING
            SPARK H YPOTHESES FOR C RITICAL T HINKING
149
150   We adapt the Hierarchical Three-Space Theory (visualized in Fig. 1) to the context of language
151   model processing, reframing the three spaces as:1
152   Problem Framing Space (Model Space): Represents the LLM’s initial understanding and assump-
153   tions about the given task/question, derived from the prompt and the model’s pre-trained knowledge.
154   Strategy Space (Hypothesis Space): Encompasses potential reasoning paths or approaches to ad-
155   dress the task, manifesting in the model’s generation of intermediate thoughts or steps, such as those
156   observed in chain-of-thought reasoning. The exploration of this space is influenced by the model’s
157   training and the specific prompting technique used.
158
      Implementation Space (Experiment Space): Represents the actual output generation process, in-
      cluding token-by-token text generation where the model applies its selected strategy to produce a
159
      response. This space is directly observable through the model’s output.
160
          1
161         We have renamed the spaces from the original Three-Space Theory (shown in parentheses) to better reflect
      their application to LLMs and avoid terminological confusion (e.g., ‘hypothesis space’ or ‘model’ in ML).


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Grounded in the Three-Space Theory, we analyze LLM critical thinking as an ability to facilitate
163   feedback from the (Strategy and Implementation Spaces) to enable revisions in the Problem Fram-
164   ing Space. Building upon this adapted theory and the critical thinking definition, we propose the
165   SPARK framework to evaluate the five key hypotheses to evaluate the critical thinking in LLMs:
166
      Strategy Space Interaction (SSI) Hypothesis: The way LLMs solve problems (their Strategy Space)
167
      influences their ability to update their Problem Framing Space. To evaluate this hypothesis, we
168
      compare different solving strategies (Strategy Space), in particular original prompting vs chain-of-
169   thought prompting, and evaluate the effect on the Problem-Solving Space (Section 4.3.
170   Problem Space Sensitivity (PSS) Hypothesis: LLMs can detect inconsistencies or missing informa-
171   tion in their Problem Framing Space, but this ability varies based on problem complexity and model
172   architecture. To study the model’s ability to detect insconsistencies or missing information of the
173   problem statement, we first design problem setup to incorporate such cases. Then we evaluate the
174   model’s general ability to challenge the incorrect problem formulation (Section 4.1. We then study
175   whether the degree of challenging depends on the model’s solving capability for the given problem
176   (Section 4.2) or the problem’s complexity, where we simulate by increasing the number of missing
177
      constraints (Section 4.4).
      Across-Domain Abstraction (ADA) Hypothesis: LLMs’ critical thinking abilities are partly domain-
178
      general, but effectiveness varies across problem types. To find the domain-general critical thinking
179
      ability of an LLM, we search over a wide range of diverse domain datasets to find the clusters of
180   datasets that share similar critical-thinking patterns between datasets (Section 4.6).
181   Robustness to Misleading Information (RMI) Hypothesis: LLMs’ Problem Framing Space can be
182   influenced by misleading or noisy information. To test the robustness of the model to misleading
183   information, we design experiments with conflicting information as a form of gaslighting hints in
184   the prompt (Section 4.5).
185   Knowledge and Behavior Conditioning (KBC) Hypothesis: LLM knowledge and behavior that gov-
186   erns the dynamic interplay among all three spaces can be shaped or conditioned through fine-tuning
187   and in-context learning. To investigate the interplay among all spaces, we study how different fine
188
      tuning strategies and datasets condition the model behavior (Section 4.7 and also how different in-
      context learning examples can affect the model critical-thinking ability differently (Section 4.8).
189
190   This framework with proposed experiments allow us to systematically investigate critical thinking in
191   LLMs, providing insights into their capabilities and limitations in complex problem-solving scenar-
192   ios nad how large language models (LLMs) navigate within the spaces of the Three-Space Theory
193   and interact across them.

195   3.2   B ENCHMARK C REATION OVERVIEW, R EPRODUCTION , AND E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
196
197
      Our work provides a framework to evaluate critical thinking of a large language model by modifying
      existing, correctly annotated datasets of interest.
198
199   Datasets. In our study, we employ multiple existing datasets covering a range of topics and skills: 8
200   multiple-choice datasets (QA): Hellaswag (commonsense NLI), TAL (math), OpenBook QA (text
201   comprehension with commonsense reasoning), ARC Challenge (science), GPQA (domain-specific
202   science), LSAT (law reading comprehension), MMLU-Math(math subset of general knowledge),
203
      TruthfulQA (human falsehood), 3 free-form generation datasets (FG): GSM8K (math), Quail
      (reading comprehension), and HotPotQA (multi-hop reasoning). For each dataset, we sample 300
204
      test queries for evaluation. We refer the reader to Appendix A for further details on the construction
205
      of these datasets.
206   Dataset Modification. We create two new versions of these datasets to test LLMs’ ability to detect
207   inconsistencies or missing information in problem setups:
208
      • (Hidden Correct Answer) For 8QA datasets, we remove the correct answer choice from the
209
        multiple answer choices. Here, we study whether the model is able to update its Problem Framing
210     assumptions that the correct answer choice might actually not be provided within the problem
211     statement. Thus, requiring the model to change its own initial assumptions about the multiple-
212     choice problems.
213   • (Missing Information) For 3FG datasets, we remove the necessary condition from the problem
214     statement so that the answer cannot be inferred from the provided context, thus, requiring the
215     LLM to update its Problem Framing Space assumptions that the model cannot arrive at the final
        answer due to missing information. The detailed question modifications are explained in A.1.2.

216
      These modifications allow us to evaluate the model’s ability to recognize inconsistencies and chal-
217   lenge insufficient problem setups. Crucially, we assess the model’s capacity to self-recognize these
218   flaws without any additional guidance. These datasets span diverse problem types—including math-
219   ematics, reading comprehension, domain-specific science, and story completion—each designed to
220   evaluate specific problem-solving skills. We prioritize reasoning tasks as they align with our defi-
221   nition of critical thinking, while providing observable intermediate steps that enable us to evaluate
222   inconsistencies in LLMs’ inference processes. To further evaluate robustness to misleading infor-
223   mation, we augment the 8QA datasets by creating three versions with different misleading hints:
224   • (Gaslight Correct): By the end of each problem statement, we add a hint claiming that the correct
225     answer (e.g., 9) is incorrect (e.g., Hint: 9 is incorrect).
226   • (Gaslight Wrong): By the end of each problem statement, we add a hint claiming that the wrong
227     answer (e.g., 8) is correct (e.g., Hint: 8 is correct).
228   • (Gaslight Both): By the end of each problem statement, we add a hint claiming that the wrong
229
        answer is correct and the correct answer is incorrect (e.g., Hint: 8 is correct and 9 is incorrect).
230   Models. After building the evaluation datasets, we aim to evaluate LLMs across a range of training
231   parameter sizes and diverse capabilities. Therefore, we include the following models: Llama-3.1-
232   8/70B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3, and GPT4o (Achiam et al., 2023). We
233
      configure each model with a temperature of 0 and a maximum token limit of 1024 for inference. For
      more model, inference, and training details, we refer the reader to Appendix B.
234
235   Evaluation Metrics. In all experiments, we measure two key metrics for critical thinking evaluation:
236   • Problem-Solving Rate: Measures whether the LLM’s incorporates the correct knowledge about
237     the question We leverage binary correctness label on clear generative tasks corc and modified
238     questions corm , where the correctness represents whether the response demonstrates the correct
239     knowledge. To construct clear tasks, we remove options for the multiple-choice problem and use
240     the original questions for free-form generation problems. Problem-solving capability is measured
241     by corc ∪ corm , as correct solutions in either scenario indicate the model’s ability to solve the
242     task.
243
      • Critical-Thinking Rate: Measures the LLM’s ability to identify flaws in the problem setup. We
        first identify well-defined questions that the LLM does not challenge the problem setup. Let N1
244
        denote the number of unchallenged clear questions, and N2 denote the number of their corre-
245
        sponding modified versions that are challenged. The ratio N N1 measures the LLM’s capability to
                                                                      2
246
        identify problem inconsistencies. The detailed explanation is in Appendix C.
247
248   We employ off-the-shelf LLMs to measure these two scores for efficient evaluation. Particularly, we
249
      use Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct to measure the correctness of the answer with respect to the ground truth
      answer and GPT-4o to measure the challenge rate of the models. Due to high efficacy, we choose
250
      these models as the judges, reaching 100% accuracy in measuring correctness and 92% accuracy in
251
      measuring challenges on our manually curated held-out datasets, respectively. We provide relevant
252   template judge prompts in Appendix D.

      5      D ISCUSSION AND I MPLICATIONS
502
      Key Findings. Our experiments reveal that while state-of-the-art LLMs demonstrate some ca-
503
      pacity for critical thinking, their ability to consistently recognize and challenge inconsistencies in
504   problem framing remains limited, as evidenced by the generally low challenge rates. These find-
505   ings offer a nuanced understanding of the SPARK hypotheses. The PSS hypothesis is supported
506   by the observation that larger models and those with instruction-following training exhibit higher
507   challenge rates, but the overall low rates highlight the need for further research. The SSI hypothesis
508   is supported by the mixed effects of chain-of-thought prompting, suggesting a complex interplay
509   between strategy and problem understanding. The RMI hypothesis is confirmed by the observation
510   that gaslighting increases challenge rates but reduces correctness, underscoring LLMs’ vulnerability
511   to manipulation.
512
513   Implications for LLM Evaluation and Development. These findings have implications for LLM
514
      development and evaluation. Our research underscores the need to incorporate critical thinking as a
      key evaluation criterion, using frameworks like SPARK to systematically assess these capabilities.
515
      For LLM developers, our findings highlight the need to explicitly incorporate critical thinking skills
516
      into model training and design, including enhancing robustness to misleading information, promot-
517   ing deeper understanding, improving inconsistency detection, and optimizing prompting strategies.
518
519   Limitations. Our results span a diverse range of benchmark datasets, yet this selection is not
520   exhaustive. Researchers can apply our evaluation methodology to their own datasets of interest to
521   assess an LLM’s critical thinking abilities. While our current evaluations focus on the final response
522   output generated by LLMs, future work could delve deeper by analyzing model activations. Recent
523   advancements in LLM reasoning have led to improved capabilities, as demonstrated by the gpt-o1
524   model. Due to its recent release, we have not had the opportunity to evaluate this model in depth.
525   However, preliminary results suggest that even this advanced model may also face challenges in
526   critical thinking tasks.
527
528   6      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
529
530   This paper presents a novel framework for evaluating critical thinking in LLMs, grounded in the
531   Three-Space Theory. Our findings reveal limitations in LLMs’ ability to challenge problem setups
532   and highlight the influence of various factors (e.g., solving capability, problem complexity, mis-
533   leading information, fine-tuning, and in-context learning) on their critical thinking capabilities. The
534   proposed framework is readily adaptable across diverse problem types, providing a key step towards
535   evaluating and enhancing critical thinking in LLMs.
536   Future research could extend this framework to more complex, real-world-oriented tasks like di-
537   alogue generation and code design. Additionally, our observations reveal that various prompting
538   techniques including gaslight, gaslight with warning, and CoT influence the model performance,
539   and we noted a trade-off between response correctness and critical thinking capability. Future work
      could investigate how to optimize this trade-off.

      A.1   DATASETS FOR E VALUATING LLM S
759
760          • Free Generation
761            1. GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) includes multi-step, grade-school-level arithmetic prob-
762            lems designed to test LLMs’ mathematical reasoning abilities. Each problem contains
763            multiple necessary conditions, enabling us to quantitatively modify questions by selectively
764
               hiding a certain number of these conditions.
765            2. HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) is a multi-hop reasoning dataset that challenges models
766
               to combine information from multiple documents. It provides several context documents,
               with only a few containing relevant information. LLMs must first identify these related
767
               documents before inferring the answer. The dataset includes titles of the related documents,
768
               facilitating quantitative modification by selectively omitting certain documents.
769
               Both GSM8K and HotpotQA require models to infer answers by leveraging multiple con-
770
               ditions in the context and following several reasoning steps. The multi-hop reasoning paths
771            exhibited in these datasets effectively display LLMs’ problem-solving strategies. Conse-
772            quently, we can investigate how the LLM updates its Problem Framing Space assumption
773            according to the feedback from the Implementation Space.
774
             • Multiple-Choice
775
               1. QuAIL (Rogers et al., 2020) is a reading comprehension dataset that includes a variety
776
               of question types to evaluate a model’s ability to evaluate models’ abilities to understand
777            and reason about complex texts
778
               2. TAL (matheval.ai, 2023) contains mathematical competition questions across primary,
779            junior high, and high school levels. Each question requires multiple intermediate steps to
780            solve, thus reflecting logical reasoning and critical thinking capability.
781            3. TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2021) is a dataset designed to evaluate an LLM ability to avoid
782            generating false or misleading responses. It adversarially crafted problems that exploit
783            common human misconceptions, potentially leading to incorrect answers. The dataset en-
784            compasses a diverse range of topics, including health, law, finance, and politics. By chal-
785            lenging models with questions that humans might answer incorrectly, TruthfulQA evaluates
786            the LLM capacity to avoid generating false and misleading information.
787            4. OpenBookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018) includes elementary-level science questions,
788            which require the LLM to retrieve of factual information and leverage external ”open-book”
789            knowledge to infer the answer. This dataset evaluates the LLM capability to integrate the
790            retrieved information and logical inference.
791            5. MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020) includes a wide variety of science questions from
792            high school to professional difficulty levels. The dataset is designed to evaluate the un-
793
               derstanding and reasoning capability of LLM across different domains of knowledge, thus
               displaying the depth and width of LLM understanding.
794
795
               6. GPQA (Rein et al., 2023) includes highly challenging, domain-specific questions across
               scientific fields such as biology, physics, and chemistry. The extremely difficult questions
796
               are used to evaluate the LLM performance on complex scientific inquiries. By using ex-
797
               ceptionally difficult questions that are not tailored to any single discipline, GPQA evaluates
798            the LLM’s versatility and adaptability.
799
               7. AI2 Reasoning Challenge(ARC) (Clark et al., 2018) contains grade-school level science
800            exam problems with two components: Easy Set and Challenge Set, where the questions
801            from Challenge Set cannot be answered using simple fact retrieval or superficial reasoning.
802            ARC evaluates the LLM’s ability to understand scientific knowledge and integrate multiple
803            information.
804            8. LSAT (Zhong et al., 2021) includes standardized questions primarily used for law school
805            admissions focusing on logical reasoning and reading comprehension skills. These com-
806            plex tasks, set within law-related contexts, are designed to evaluate an LLM’s analytical
807            reasoning and cognitive abilities.
808            9. HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019) contains multiple-choice questions that challenge lan-
809            guage models to select the most plausible continuation of a given scenario. This prediction
               task evaluates the LLM capability of commonsense reasoning, context understanding, and

                logical inference. HellaSwag assesses their ability to grasp implicit information, apply
811             real-world knowledge, and make sensible deductions.
812
                For the multiple-choice dataset, the LLM needs to integrate diverse information and nav-
813
                igate multiple inference steps to arrive at a result. The crafted options in these datasets
814             provide an effective means to evaluate an LLM’s critical thinking abilities. This format
815             operates under the initial assumption in the Problem Framing Space that the correct answer
816             is among the provided options. While removing the ground-truth option, we may observe
817             the LLM generate the correct answer during its reasoning process, yet still select an incor-
818             rect option from those provided. Alternatively, it might recognize that all given choices are
819             unsuitable, but nevertheless feel compelled to choose one. These scenarios clearly demon-
820             strate how an LLM can be constrained by its initial assumptions, revealing a reluctance or
821             inability to update its framework when faced with conflicting evidence.

       This project investigates how LLMs challenge problem setups while identifying inconsistencies or
       insufficient information in the given context. However, an LLM’s tendency to challenge problems
1002
       may stem from factors unrelated to ambiguity or inconsistency in the problem statement. For in-
1003
       stance, an LLM could be fine-tuned to challenge all problems indiscriminately, which would not
1004   reflect genuine critical thinking capabilities. To control for such model inherent biases, we leverage
1005   the rate at which models challenge well-defined problems. Based on this approach, we propose a
1006   probabilistic framework to model challenge behavior and develop a metric for evaluating critical
1007   thinking capabilities.
1008
       We model an LLM’s challenge behavior as a boolean variable C, which depends on two independent
1009
       binary random variables: data inconsistency D and model-inherent challenge tendency M . Here,
1010   M represents causes of challenge behavior unrelated to question inconsistency. D = 1 indicates
1011   the presence of inconsistency in the question, while D = 0 indicates a well-defined question. Sim-
1012   ilarly, M = 1 indicates the presence of model-inherent properties that trigger challenge behavior,
1013   P (C = 1|M = 1) = 1. Additionally for the well-defined questions, if the model inherent challenge
1014   condition is not triggered, LLMs never challenge the problem P (C = 1|D = 0, M = 0) = 0.
1015
       Consider for each dataset, we have a N pair of well-defined questions and modified questions,
1016   P (D = 0) = P (D = 1) = 21 . Our experimental analysis first examines LLMs’ challenge behavior
1017   on well-defined questions. Since these questions contain no inconsistencies, any challenges must
1018   stem from the model’s inherent tendency M . We assume this inherent tendency is independent
1019   of data inconsistency. To isolate the effect of actual inconsistency detection, we first identify well-
1020   defined questions that the LLM does not challenge. Let N1 denote the number of unchallenged clear
1021   questions, and N2 denote the number of their corresponding modified versions that are challenged.

1412

1420     involved, which are influenced by the mass of the planet and the Sun. The mass of the planet
1421     affects its gravitational interaction with the Sun, which in turn influences its orbital period. Plan-
1422     etary color and a planet’s core temperature are not directly relevant to the orbital mechanics that
1423     determine the length of a planetary year.

1463
         Answer:
1464
1465
         Response: To determine the cost of each top that Ann bought, we need to break down her total
1466
         expenditure and account for the known costs of the shorts and shoes.
1467     First, let’s calculate the total cost of the shorts:
1468     5 times $7 = $35
1469     Next, let’s calculate the total cost of the shoes:2 shoes times $10 = $20
1470     Now, let’s add the costs of the shorts and shoes together:$35 + $20 = $55
1471     Ann’s total expenditure was $75. To find out how much she spent on the tops, we subtract the
1472     cost of the shorts and shoes from her total expenditure:$75 - $55 = $20
1473     Let x be the cost of each top. If she bought n tops, then the total cost of the tops is:
1474     n times x = $20
1475
         Since we don’t know the number of tops n she bought, we can only express the cost of each top
         in terms of n:

         x = 20/n
