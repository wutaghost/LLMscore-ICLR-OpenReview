009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Generative models that can produce realistic images have improved significantly
012            in recent years. The quality of the generated content has increased drastically, so
013            sometimes it is very difficult to distinguish between the real images and the gen-
014            erated ones. Such an improvement comes at a price of ethical concerns about the
015            usage of the generative models: the users of generative models can improperly
016            claim ownership of the generated content protected by a license. In this paper,
017            we propose an approach to embed watermarks into the generated content to allow
018            future detection of the generated content and identification of the user who gen-
019            erated it. The watermark is embedded during the inference of the model, so the
020
               proposed approach does not require the retraining of the latter. We prove that wa-
               termarks embedded are guaranteed to be robust against additive perturbations of
021
               a bounded magnitude. We apply our method to watermark diffusion models and
022
               show that it matches state-of-the-art watermarking schemes in terms of robustness
023            to different types of synthetic watermark removal attacks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Recent advances in generative models have brought the performance of image synthesis tasks to
030
      a whole new level. For example, the quality of the images generated by diffusion models [DMs,
      Croitoru et al. (2023); Rombach et al. (2022); Esser et al. (2024)] is now sometimes comparable to
031
      the one of the human-generated pictures or photographs. Compared to generative adversarial net-
032
      works [GANs, Goodfellow et al. (2014); Brock et al. (2019)], diffusion models allow the generation
033   of high-resolution, naturally looking pictures and incorporate much more stable training, leading
034   to more diverse generation. More than that, the image generation process with diffusion models is
035   more stable, controllable, and explainable. They are easy to use and are widely deployed as tools for
036   data generation, image editing [Kawar et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023)], music generation [Schneider
037   et al. (2024)], text-to-image synthesis [Saharia et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023); Ruiz et al. (2023)]
038   and in other multimodal settings.
039   Unfortunately, there are several ethical and legal issues that may arise from the usage of diffusion
040   models. On the one hand, since diffusion models can be used to generate fake content, for example,
041   deepfakes [Zhao et al. (2021); Narayan et al. (2023)], it is crucial to develop automatic tools to
042   verify that a particular digital asset is artificially generated. On the other hand, a dishonest user
043   of the model protected by a copyright license can query it, receive the result of generation, and
044   later claim exclusive copyright. In this work, we focus on the detection of the content generated by a
045   particular model and the identification of the end-user who queried the model to generate a particular
046   content. We develop a technique to embed the digital watermark into the generated content during
047
      the inference of the generative model, so it does not require retraining or fine-tuning the generative
      model. The approach allows not only to verify that the content was generated by a source model but
048
      also to identify the user who sent a corresponding query to the generative model. We prove that the
049
      watermark embedded is robust against additive perturbations of the content of a bounded magnitude.
050

               ative content of continuous nature. Our method embeds the watermark during the process

      2.1    D IFFUSION M ODEL
087
088   Inspired by non-equilibrium statistical physics, [Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015)] introduced the dif-
089   fusion model to fit complex probability distributions.[Ho et al. (2020)] introduced a new class of
090   models called Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) by establishing a novel connec-
091   tion between the diffusion model and the denoising scoring matching. Later, the Latent Diffusion
092   Model (LDM) [Rombach et al. (2022)] was developed to improve efficiency and reduce computa-
093   tional complexity, with the diffusion process happening within a latent space Z. During training the
094   LDM uses an encoder E to map an input image x to the latent space: z = E(x). For the reverse
095   operation a decoder D is employed, so that x = D(z). During inference, the LDM starts with a
096
      noise vector z ∼ N (0, I) in the latent space and iteratively denoises it. The decoder then maps the
      final latent representation back to the image space.

099   2.2    WATERMARKING OF D IGITAL C ONTENT
100
      Watermarking has been recently adopted to protect the intellectual property of neural networks [Wu
101
      et al. (2020); Pautov et al. (2024)] and generated content [Kirchenbauer et al. (2023); Zhao et al.
102   (2024); Fu et al. (2024)]. In a nutshell, watermarking of generated content is done by injection of
103   digital information within the generated image allowing the subsequent extraction. Existing meth-
104   ods of digital content watermarking can be divided into two categories: content-level watermarking
105   and model-level watermarking. The methods of content-level watermarking operate in some repre-
106   sentation of content, for example, in the frequency domain of the image signal [ó Ruanaidh et al.
107   (1996); Cox et al. (1996)]. When the image is manipulated in the frequency domain, the watermark
      embedding process can be adapted to produce watermarks that are robust to geometrical image


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      transformations, such as rotations and translations [Wen et al. (2024)]. Model-level watermarking
109   approaches are designed to embed information during the generation process. In end-to-end meth-
110   ods, the models to embed and extract watermark are learned jointly [Zhu et al. (2018); Hayes &
111   Danezis (2017)]. In [Yu et al. (2021)], it was proposed to teach the watermark encoder on the train-
112   ing data of the generative model; such an approach yields a watermarking scheme that is conditioned
113   on the generative model and its training dataset. This method was later adapted to latent diffusion
114   models [Fernandez et al. (2023)] and unconditional diffusion models [Zhao et al. (2023)]. In con-
115   trast, there are methods that do not require additional model training. These methods are designed
116   to alter the output distribution of the generative model to embed previously learned watermark into
117
      the model or the content itself [Kirchenbauer et al. (2023); Wen et al. (2024)].
118
119   2.3   ROBUSTNESS TO WATERMARK R EMOVAL ATTACKS
120
121   Watermarking attacks are aimed at removing the watermark embedded into the model’s weights
122
      or generated content. In the prior works on removing the watermarks from generated images [Li
      et al. (2019); Cao et al. (2019)], the attack problem is formulated in terms of the image-to-image
123
      translation task, and methods to remove watermarks via an auxiliary generative adversarial network
124
      are presented. Other approaches [Hertz et al. (2019); Liang et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2023)] perform
125   watermark removal in two steps: firstly, the visual watermark is localized within an image; secondly,
126   it is removed via a multi-task learning framework.
127
128
      In practice, watermarking scheme has to be robust to destructive and constructive attacks, or syn-
      thetic transformations of the data. Destructive transformations, such as brightness and contrast ad-
129
      justment, geometric transformations, such as rotations and translations, compression methods, and
130
      additive noise are aimed at watermark removal by applying a transformation. In contrast, construc-
131   tive attacks treat watermarks as noise and are aimed at the restoration of original content [Zhang
132   et al. (2024)]. It is usually done by applying purification techniques, such as Gaussian blur [Hosam
133   (2019)] or image inpainting [Liu et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2017)].
134
      Signal Processing Attacks focus on noise addition, compression, and filtering. Robust watermark-
135
      ing schemes based on frequency domain transformations and randomizing offered higher resilience
136
      against these types of attacks Taran et al. (2019).

140
141   In this section, we formulate the problem statement and the research objectives. Note that we focus
142   on the watermarking of images generated by diffusion models, but the formulation below is valid
143   for watermarking of any generated content, for example, audio, video, or text.

145   3.1   I MAGE WATERMARKING
146
147   In our approach, we focus on detection and attribution of the generated image simultaneously: while
148   detection is aimed to verify whether a particular image is generated by a given model, attribution is
149   aimed at determining the user who generated the image.
150   Suppose that we are given the generative model f deployed in the black-box setting, i.e., as a service:
151   in the generation phase a user ui ∈ [u1 , . . . , um ] sends a query to the model and receives a generated
152   image x ∈ Rd . If x is a watermarked image, the owner of model f should be able to identify that x
153   is generated by user ui by querying the model f . In our method, the image is watermarked during
154   the generation phase, not during the post-processing. We formulate the process of watermarking
155   and attribution in the following way:

               embedded in it.

209
210   When the user ui receives the watermarked image x, it can post-process it to obtain the other image,
211   x′ , which does retain the sufficient part of the watermark w(ui ). The transition from x to x′ may be

      We evaluate the robustness of the watermarks embedded by our method against the following water-

334

335

336
337   Brightness adjustment of an image x was performed by adding a constant value to each pixel:
338   xbrightness = x + b, where b was sampled from the uniform distribution U[−20, 20].
339   Contrast shift was done in two ways: positive and negative. The positive contrast shift implies the
340   multiplication of each pixel of an image by a constant positive factor: xcontrast = cx, where c was
341   sampled from the uniform distribution, c ∼ U[0.5, 2].
342
      In contrary, when the contrast shift is performed with the negative value of c (namely, c ∼
343   U[−2, −0.5]), such a transform turns an image into a negative. Later, we treat these transforms
344   separately and denote them as “Contrast +” and “Contrast −”, depending on the sign of c.
345
346

      where g ∼ U[0.5, 2].

349   age [Riba et al. (2020)] with the following parameters: asaturation = 2.0, ahue = 0.2 and

      5.4   L IMITATIONS
424
425
      Note that the proposed approach has several limitations. First of all, since the watermarking is
426   performed during the model’s inference, it affects both the inference time and, in some cases, the

428   watermarked counterparts. See Fig. 5 in Appendix for details. Note that these artifacts, although
429   visible, barely spoil the images’ content. Secondly, the proposed watermarking method does not
430   provide robustness against cropping, rotation, and translation attacks. However, the robustness to
431   rotation and translation can be achieved by inserting the watermarks in the frequency domain of the
      image.
