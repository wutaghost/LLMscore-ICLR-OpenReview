009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Model ensemble adversarial attack has become a powerful method for generating
012            transferable adversarial examples that can target even unknown models, but its
013            theoretical foundation remains underexplored. To address this gap, we provide
014            early theoretical insights that serve as a roadmap for advancing model ensemble
015            adversarial attack. We first define transferability error to measure the error in
016            adversarial transferability, alongside concepts of diversity and empirical model
017            ensemble Rademacher complexity. We then decompose the transferability error
018            into vulnerability, diversity, and a constant, which rigidly explains the origin of
019            transferability error in model ensemble attack: the vulnerability of an adversarial
020
               example to ensemble components, and the diversity of ensemble components.
               Furthermore, we apply the latest mathematical tools in information theory to bound
021
               the transferability error using complexity and generalization terms, contributing to
022
               three practical guidelines for reducing transferability error: (1) incorporating more
023            surrogate models, (2) increasing their diversity, and (3) reducing their complexity
024            in cases of overfitting. Finally, extensive experiments with 54 models validate our
025            theoretical framework, representing a significant step forward in understanding
026            transferable model ensemble adversarial attacks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Neural networks are highly vulnerable to adversarial examples (Szegedy et al., 2013; Goodfellow
033
      et al., 2014)—perturbations that closely resemble the original data but can severely compromise
      safety-critical applications (Zhang & Li, 2019; Kong et al., 2020; Bortsova et al., 2021). Even more
034
      concerning is the phenomenon of adversarial transferability (Papernot et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2017):
035
      adversarial examples crafted to deceive one model often succeed in attacking others. This property
036   enables attacks without requiring any knowledge of the target model, significantly complicating
037   efforts to ensure the robustness of neural networks (Dong et al., 2019; Silva & Najafirad, 2020).
038
039
      To enhance adversarial transferability, researchers have proposed a range of algorithms that fall into
      three main categories: input transformation (Xie et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021), gradient-based
040
      optimization (Gao et al., 2020; Xiong et al., 2022), and model ensemble attacks (Li et al., 2020;
041
      Chen et al., 2024b). Among these, model ensemble attacks have proven especially powerful, as they
042   leverage multiple models to simultaneously generate adversarial examples that exploit the strengths
043   of each individual model (Dong et al., 2018). Moreover, these attacks can be combined with input
044   transformation and gradient-based optimization methods to further improve their effectiveness (Tang
045   et al., 2024). However, despite the success of such attacks, their theoretical foundation remains
046   poorly understood. This prompts an important question: Can we establish a theoretical framework
047   for transferable model ensemble adversarial attacks to shape the evolution of future algorithms?
048   To conduct a preliminary exploration of this profound question, we propose three novel definitions
049   as a prerequisite of our theoretical framework. Firstly, we define transferability error as the gap in
050   expected loss between an adversarial example and the one with the highest loss within a feasible
051   region of the input space. It captures the ability of an adversarial example to generalize across
052   unseen models, representing its transferability. Secondly, we introduce prediction variance across the
053   ensemble classifiers. It address an open problem in model ensemble attack about how to to quantify
      diversity and assists in selecting ensemble components. Finally, we also introduce the empirical

097   2.1     T RANSFERABLE A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
098
099
      Researchers have developed various algorithms to enhance adversial transferability. Most of them
      fall into three categories: input transformation, gradient-based optimization, and model ensemble
100
      attack. Input transformation techniques apply data augmentation strategies to prevent overfitting to
101
      the surrogate model. For instance, random resizing and padding (Xie et al., 2019), downscaling (Lin
102   et al., 2019), and mixing (Wang et al., 2021). Gradient-based optimization optimizes the generation
103   of adversarial examples to achieve better transferability. Some popular ideas include applying
104   momentum (Dong et al., 2018), Nesterov accelerated gradient (Lin et al., 2019), scheduled step
105   size (Gao et al., 2020) and gradient variance reduction (Xiong et al., 2022). Model ensemble attack
106   combine outputs from surrogate models to create an ensemble loss, increasing the likelihood to
107   deceive various models simultaneously. It can be applied collectively with both input transformation
      and gradient-based optimization algorithms (Tang et al., 2024). Some popular ensemble paradigms


                                                                 2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      include loss-based ensemble (Dong et al., 2018), prediction-based (Liu et al., 2017), logit-based
109   ensemble (Dong et al., 2018), and longitudinal strategy (Li et al., 2020). Moreover, advanced
110   ensemble algorithms have been created to ensure better adversarial transferability (Li et al., 2023; Wu
111   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b). An extended and detailed summary of related work is in Appendix C.
112
      Within the extensive body of research on model ensemble attacks, two notable observations stand
113
      out. First, increasing the number of models in an ensemble improves adversarial transferability (Liu
114
      et al., 2017; Dong et al., 2018; Lin et al., 2019; Gubri et al., 2022b). Second, using more diverse
115   surrogate models with varying architectures and back-propagated gradients (Tang et al., 2024) further
116   enhances transferability. However, to our best knowledge, these intriguing phenomena have yet to be
117   fully understood from a theoretical perspective. In this paper, we are the first to provide a theoretical
118   explanation for them, offering insights that can guide future algorithm design.

      2.2   T HEORETICAL U NDERSTANDING OF A DVERSARIAL T RANSFERABILITY
121
122
      In contrast to the wealth of empirical and intuitive studies, research on the theoretical understanding
123   of adversarial transferability remains limited. Recent efforts have primarily focused on aspects such
124   as data (Tramèr et al., 2017), surrogate model (Wang & Farnia, 2023), optimization (Yang et al.,
125   2021; Zhang et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024b) and target model (Zhao et al., 2023). Tramèr et al.
126   (2017) investigates the space of transferable adversarial examples and establishes conditions on
127   the data distribution that suggest transferability for some basic models. In terms of the surrogate
128   model generalization, Wang & Farnia (2023) builds the generalization gap to show that a surrogate
129   model with a smaller generalization error leads to more transferable adversarial examples. From an
130   optimization perspective, Yang et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2024a) establish upper and lower bounds
131
      on adversarial transferability, linking it to model smoothness and gradient similarity, while Chen
      et al. (2024b) provides theoretical evidence connecting transferability to loss landscape flatness and
132
      closeness to local optima. Regarding the target model, Zhao et al. (2023) theoretically reveals that
133
      reducing the discrepancy between the surrogate and target models can limit adversarial transferability.
134
135   Despite these theoretical advances, to the best of our knowledge, transferable model ensemble
136   adversarial attacks remain unexplored. To address this gap, we take a pioneering step by presenting
137
      the first theoretical analysis of such attacks. Our work not only offers theoretical insights into these
      attacks but also incorporates recent advancements in learning theory, laying the groundwork for
138
      future theoretical investigations into adversarial transferability.

348   The trade-off between complexity and diversity. Reducing model complexity may conflict with
349   increasing diversity. We discuss this issue from two angles. On one hand, when generating adversarial

351   but diversity may also be limited due to the simpler structure of the ensemble attackers. On the other
352

356   From generalization error to transferability error. The mathematical form of Eq. (12) is in
357   line with the generalization error bound (Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002). However, we note that a key
358
      distinction between transferability error and generalization error lies in the independence assumption.
      Conventional generalization error analysis relies on an assumption: each data point from the dataset
359
      is independently sampled (Zou & Liu, 2023; Hu et al., 2023). By contrast, the surrogate models for
360
      ensemble attack are usually trained on the datasets with similar tasks, e.g., image classification. In this
361   case, we cannot assume these surrogate models behave independently for a solid theoretical analysis,
362   which increases the difficulty of proof in our paper. To build the gap between generalization error
363   and transferability error, our proof introduces the latest techniques in information theory (Esposito &
364   Mondelli, 2024). And Refer to Appendix D.6 for a detailed discussion.

366   4.3   G ENERALIZATION AND A DVERSARIAL T RANSFERABILITY
367
368   We offer new insights into a foundational and popular analogy in the literature: The transferability
369   of an adversarial example is an analogue to the generalizability of the model (Dong et al., 2018;
370   Lin et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021; Wang & He, 2021; Xiong et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024b).
371   Interestingly, our theory also sheds light on this insight in several ways.
372   First, the mathematical formulations in Lemma 1 is similar to generalization error (Vapnik, 1998;
373   Bousquet & Elisseeff, 2002). Also, Lemma 2 is similar to the bound of the original Rademacher
374   complexity (Golowich et al., 2018). More importantly, recall that in the conventional framework
375   of learning theory: (1) increasing the size of training set typically leads to a better generalization
376   of the model (Bousquet & Elisseeff, 2002); (2) improving the diversity among ensemble classifiers
377   makes it more advantageous for better generalization (Ortega et al., 2022); and (3) reducing the
      model complexity (Cherkassky, 2002) may mitigate overfitting and benefit the generalization ability.

      5               E XPERIMENTS
406
407   We conduct our experiments on four datasets, including the MNIST (LeCun, 1998), Fashion-
408   MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), and ImageNet-1K (Russakovsky
409
      et al., 2015) datasets. We use the first three datasets to empirically validate our theoretical contribution,
      and leave the experiments on ImageNet to build a powerful ensemble adversarial attack in practice.
410
411   We build six deep neural networks for image classification, including three MLPs with one to three
412   hidden layers followed by a linear classification layer, and three convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
413   with one to three convolutional layers followed by a linear classification layer. To ensure diversity
414   among the models, we apply three different types of transformations during training. Additionally,
415
      we set the weight decay under the L2 norm to 10−4 , 10−3 , 10−2 , respectively. This results in a total
      of 6 × 3 × 3 = 54 models. To establish a gold standard for adversarial transferability evaluation,
416
      we additionally train a ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) from scratch on three datasets (MNIST, Fashion-
417
      MNIST, and CIFAR-10), respectively. We will leverage the models at hand to attack this ResNet-18
418   for a reliable evaluation. For models trained on MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, we set the number of
419   epochs as 10. For models trained on CIFAR-10, we set the number of epochs as 30. We use the
420   Adam optimizer with setting the learning rate as 10−3 . We set the batch size as 64.

492   5.2   E VALUATION ON THE E NSEMBLE F RAMEWORK
493
494   We further validate the effectiveness of the vulnerability-diversity decomposition within the ensemble
495   framework. Specifically, instead of focusing solely on the training dynamics, we progressively
496
      increase the number of models in the ensemble attack to evaluate the decomposition’s impact. We
      begin by incorporating MLPs with different architectures and regularization terms, followed by CNNs.
497
      In total, up to 18 models are included in a single attack. We depicted the results in fig. 5.
498
499   We can consistently observe that increasing the number of ensemble models improves the attack
500   success rate, i.e., reduces the transferability error. On the MNIST and Fashion-MNIST datasets,
501   both vulnerability and diversity also increase as the number of models grows. Although the diversity
502
      sometimes shows a decreasing trend on the CIFAR-10 dataset, its magnitude is approximately 100
      times smaller than vulnerability, thus having a minimal impact on the attack success rate.

505   6     C ONCLUSION
506
507   In this paper, we address the underdeveloped theoretical foundation of transferable model ensemble
508   adversarial attacks. We introduce three key definitions: transferability error, prediction variance, and
509
      empirical model ensemble Rademacher complexity. Through the vulnerability-diversity decompo-
      sition of transferability error, we identify a crucial trade-off between vulnerability and diversity in
510
      ensemble components, presenting the challenge of optimizing both simultaneously. To overcome
511
      this, we introduce recent mathematical tools and derive an upper bound on transferability error,
512   offering practical guidelines for improving adversarial transferability. Our extensive experiments
513   validate these insights, marking a significant advancement in the understanding and development of
514   transferable model ensemble adversarial attacks.

       C.1   T RANSFERABLE A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
1519
1520   Input transformation. Input transformation-based attacks have shown great effectiveness in
1521   improving transferability and can be combined with gradient-based attacks. Most input transformation
1522   techniques rely on the fundamental idea of applying data augmentation strategies to prevent overfitting
1523   to the surrogate model (Gu et al., 2024). Such methods adopt various input transformations to further
1524   improve the transferability of adversarial examples (Wang et al., 2023b;a). For instance, random
1525   resizing and padding (Xie et al., 2019), downscaling (Lin et al., 2019), mixing (Wang et al., 2021),
1526
       automated data augmentation (Yan et al., 2023), block shuffle and rotation (Wang et al., 2024), and
       dynamical transformation (Zhu et al., 2024).
1527
1528   Gradient-based optimization. The central concept of these methods is to develop optimization
1529   techniques in the generation of adversarial examples to achieve better transferability. Dong et al.
1530   (2018); Lin et al. (2019); Wang & He (2021) draw an analogy between generating adversarial
1531   examples and the model training process. Therefore, conventional optimization methods that improve
1532
       model generalization can also benefit adversarial transferability. In gradient-based optimization
       methods, adversarial perturbations are directly optimized based on one or more surrogate models
1533
       during inference. Some popular ideas include applying momentum (Dong et al., 2018), Nesterov
1534
       accelerated gradient (Lin et al., 2019), scheduled step size (Gao et al., 2020) and gradient variance
1535   reduction (Wang & He, 2021; Xiong et al., 2022). There are also other elegantly designed techniques
1536   in recent years (Gubri et al., 2022b; Wang et al., 2022; Xiaosen et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Wu
1537   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b), such as collecting weights (Gubri et al., 2022b), modifying gradient
1538   calculation (Xiaosen et al., 2023) and applying integrated gradients (Ma et al., 2023).
1539
       Model ensemble attack. Motivated by the use of model ensembles in machine learning, researchers
1540
       have developed diverse ensemble attack strategies to obtain transferable adversarial examples (Gu
1541   et al., 2024). It is a powerful attack that employs an ensemble of models to simultaneously generate
1542   adversarial samples. It can not only integrate with advanced gradient-based optimization methods,
1543   but also harness the unique strengths of each individual model (Tang et al., 2024). Some popular
1544   ensemble paradigms include loss-based ensemble (Dong et al., 2018), prediction-based (Liu et al.,
1545   2017), logit-based ensemble (Dong et al., 2018), and longitudinal strategy (Li et al., 2020). There
1546   is also some deep analysis to compare these ensemble paradigms (Zhang et al., 2024b). Moreover,
1547   advanced ensemble algorithms have been created to ensure better adversarial transferability (Zou
1548   et al., 2020; Gubri et al., 2022a; Xiong et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024;
1549
       Chen et al., 2024b).

1553
       Statistical learning theory forms the theoretical backbone of modern machine learning by providing
       rigorous frameworks for understanding model generalization (Vapnik, 1999). It introduces founda-
1554
       tional concepts such as Rademacher complexity (Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002), VC dimension (Vapnik
1555
       & Chervonenkis, 1971), structural risk minimization (Vapnik, 1998) . It has also been instrumental in
1556   the development of Support Vector Machines (Cortes & Vapnik, 1995) and kernel methods (Shawe-
1557   Taylor & Cristianini, 2004), which remain pivotal in supervised learning tasks. Recent advances
1558   extend statistical learning theory to deep learning, addressing challenges of high-dimensional data
1559   and model complexity (Bartlett et al., 2021). These contributions have significantly enhanced the
1560   capability to design robust learning algorithms that generalize well across diverse applications (Du
1561   & Swamy, 2013). In addition, there are also some other novel theoretical frameworks, such as
1562   information-theoretic analysis (Xu & Raginsky, 2017), PAC-Bayes bounds (Parrado-Hernández et al.,

1607   D.2.1    OTHER D EFINITIONS
1608
       In Yang et al. (2021), gradient diversity is defined using the cosine similarity of gradients between
1609   different models, and instance-level transferability is introduced, along with a bound for transferability.
1610   This work cleverly uses Taylor expansion to establish a theoretical connection between the success
1611   probability of attacking a single sample and the gradients of the models. In Kariyappa & Qureshi
1612   (2019), inspired by the concept of adversarial subspace (Tramèr et al., 2017), diversity is defined
1613   based on the cosine similarity of gradients across different models. The authors aim to encourage
1614   models to become more diverse, thereby achieving “no overlap in the adversarial subspaces,” and
1615   provide intuitive insights to readers. Both papers define gradient diversity and explain its impact.
1616   In contrast, our definition of diversity stems from the unified theoretical framework proposed in this
1617   paper. Specifically:
1618
1619           • We draw inspiration from statistical learning theory (Shalev-Shwartz et al., 2010; Bartlett &
                 Mendelson, 2002) on generalization, defining transferability error accordingly.


                                                          30
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1620
               • Additionally, we are motivated by ensemble learning (Abe et al., 2023; Wood et al., 2023),
1621             where we define diversity as the variation in outputs among different ensemble models.
1622
1623           • Intuitively, when different models exhibit significant differences in their outputs for the
1624
                 same sample, their gradient differences during training are likely substantial as well. This
                 suggests a potential connection between our output-based definition of diversity and the
1625
                 gradient-based definitions in (Shalev-Shwartz et al., 2010; Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002),
1626
                 which is worth exploring in future research.
1627
1628   Overall, our perspective differs from that of Shalev-Shwartz et al. (2010); Bartlett & Mendelson
1629   (2002). However, despite the differences in definitions, both our work and Shalev-Shwartz et al.
1630   (2010); Bartlett & Mendelson (2002) provide valuable explanations for phenomena in the field of
1631   adversarial transferability. Our work introduces a novel theoretical toolset to the field, offering
1632   researchers an alternative lens through which to understand adversarial transferability. We aim to
1633   inspire more theoretical insights and foster further advancements in this domain.

1635   D.2.2    C ONFLICTING O PINIONS
1636
       We observe a significant and intriguing disagreement within the academic community concerning the
1637
       role of “diversity” in transferable model ensemble attacks:
1638
1639           • Some studies advocate for enhancing model diversity to produce more transferable ad-
1640             versarial examples. For instance, Li et al. (2020) applies feature-level perturbations to
1641             an existing model to potentially create a huge set of diverse “Ghost Networks”. Li et al.
1642             (2023) emphasizes the importance of diversity in surrogate models and promotes attacking a
1643             Bayesian model to achieve desirable transferability. Tang et al. (2024) supports the notion of
1644             improved diversity, suggesting the generation of adversarial examples independently from
1645             individual models.
1646           • In contrast, other researchers adopt a diversity-reduction strategy to enhance adversarial
1647             transferability. For example, Xiong et al. (2022) focuses on minimizing gradient variance
1648             among ensemble models to improve transferability. Meanwhile, Chen et al. (2023) intro-
1649             duces a disparity-reduced filter designed to decrease gradient variances among surrogate
1650             models in ensemble attacks.
1651
1652   Although all these studies reference “diversity,” their perspectives appear to diverge. In this paper,
1653   we advocate for increasing the diversity of surrogate models. However, we also recognize that
1654
       diversity-reduction approaches have their merits.
1655   Consider the vulnerability-diversity decomposition of transferability error presented in Theorem 1. It
1656   suggests the presence of a vulnerability-diversity trade-off in transferable model ensemble attacks.
1657   In other words, we may need to prioritize either vulnerability or diversity to effectively reduce
1658   transferability error. Diversity-reduction approaches aim to stabilize the training process, thereby
1659   increasing the “bias.” In contrast, diversity-promoting methods directly enhance “diversity.” This
1660
       analysis, framed within our unified theoretical framework, provides insight into the differing opinions
       regarding adversarial transferability in the academic community.

1668   Lemma 5 (Yang et al. (2021)). Let f, g : X → Y be classifiers, δ, ρ, ϵ ∈ (0, 1) be constants, and

1672   for a given random instance x and A(·) is ρ-conservative (TV distance between the adversarial

       Lemma 5 states an intriguing conclusion: if two models exhibit low risk on the original data
1675   distribution and the distributional discrepancy between adversarial examples and the original data
1676   is small, the predictions of the two models on the same input will be close. In other words, for two
1677   well-performing models, if an attack strategy successfully targets one model, it is highly likely to
1678   succeed on the other. Lemma 5 thus describes the success rate of transferring an attack from one
1679   model to another. In contrast, Theorem 1 demonstrates that if the ensemble models exhibit significant
1680   output differences on the same input, the resulting diverse ensemble is more effective at generating
1681   adversarial examples with reduced transferability.
1682   To better clarify, let A denote the ensemble models generating adversarial examples and B the model
1683   being attacked. Comparing Lemma 5 and our work leads to the following reasoning:
1684
1685          • Assumptions: Suppose A and B both fit the original data distribution well (i.e., the risk of A
1686            and B is bounded by ϵ, as in Lemma 5).
1687          • Vulnerability-diversity trade-off: As shown in our work, increasing ensemble diversity while
1688            keeping vulnerability constant reduces the transferability error of adversarial examples
1689            generated by the ensemble.
1690          • Distributional gap: Many models in parameter space, such as A and B, are vulnerable to
1691            these adversarial examples. However, fitting both the original data distribution and the
1692            adversarial example distribution simultaneously becomes challenging, leading to a large
1693            distributional discrepancy.
1694          • Impact on Lemma 5: This discrepancy enlarges ρ in Lemma 5, thereby loosening its
1695            “conservative condition” and weakening its theoretical guarantee of successful transferability.
1696            Consequently, adversarial transferability decreases, which could be interpreted as a potential
1697            contradiction.
1698
1699
       We argue that no actual contradiction exists between Lemma 5 and our work. Instead, they provide
       complementary analyses:
1700
1701          • Upper bound interpretation: Lemma 5 provides an upper bound rather than an equality or
1702            lower bound. While an increase in ρ loosens this upper bound, it does not necessarily imply
1703            that the left-hand side (i.e., transferability success) will increase. The significance of an
1704            upper bound lies in the fact that a tighter right-hand side suggests the potential for a smaller
1705            left-hand side. However, a looser upper bound does not necessarily imply that the left-hand
1706            side will increase. Therefore, while increasing ensemble diversity may loosen the upper
1707            bound in Lemma 5, it does not contradict the fundamental interpretation of it.
1708          • Complementary perspectives: While Lemma 5 analyzes the trade-off between ϵ (model fit
1709            to the original data) and ρ (distributional discrepancy), our work focuses on the trade-off
1710            between vulnerability and ensemble diversity. Together, they provide a comprehensive
1711            understanding of the factors influencing adversarial transferability.
1712
       We now further elucidate the relationship between our results and Lemma 5:
1713
1714          • Reducing Transferability Error: To minimize transferability error (as in our work), the
1715            adversarial transferability described by Lemma 5 may have stronger theoretical guarantees,
1716            requiring its upper bound to be tighter.
1717          • Trade-off Between ϵ and ρ: To tighten the bound in Lemma 5, either ϵ or ρ must decrease.
1718            However, the two exhibit a trade-off:
1719
                  – If ϵ decreases, A and B fit the original data distribution better. However, beyond a
1720
                     certain point, the adversarial examples generated by A diverge significantly from the
1721                 original data distribution, increasing ρ.
1722

1723                 data distribution. However, beyond a certain point, A exhibits similar losses on both
1724                 distributions, resulting in a higher ϵ.
1725
1726   Therefore, Lemma 5 indicates the potential trade-off between ϵ and ρ in adversarial transferability,
1727   while our Theorem 1 emphasizes the trade-off between vulnerability and diversity. By integrating
       the perspectives from both Lemma 5 and our findings, these results illuminate different facets of

       adversarial transferability, offering complementary theoretical insights. This combined understanding
1729   deepens our knowledge of the factors influencing adversarial transferability and lays a solid foundation
1730   for future research in the field.

2150   D.6    C OMPARE WITH G ENERALIZATION E RROR B OUND
2151
       We note that a key distinction between transferability error and generalization error lies in the
2152
       independence assumption. Conventional generalization error analysis relies on an assumption: each
2153
       data point from the dataset is independently sampled (Zou & Liu, 2023; Hu et al., 2023). By
2154   contrast, the surrogate models f1 , · · · , fN for ensemble attack are usually trained on the datasets
2155   with similar tasks, e.g., image classification. In this case, such models tend to correctly classify easy
2156   examples while misclassify difficult examples (Bengio et al., 2009). Consequently, such correlation
2157   indicates dependency (Lancaster, 1963), suggesting that we cannot assume these surrogate models
2158   behave independently for a solid theoretical analysis. Additionally, there are alternative methods for
2159   analyzing concentration inequality in generalization error analysis that do not rely on the independence
       assumption (Kontorovich & Ramanan, 2008; Mohri & Rostamizadeh, 2008; Lei et al., 2019; Zhang


                                                          40
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2160
       et al., 2019a). However, such data-dependent analysis is either too loose (Lampert et al., 2018)
2161   (because it includes an additional additive factor that grows with the number of samples (Esposito &
2162   Mondelli, 2024)) or requires specific independence structure of data (Zhang & Amini, 2024) that may
2163   not align well with model ensemble attacks. Therefore, we uses the latest techniques of information
2164   theory (Esposito & Mondelli, 2024) about concentration inequality regarding dependency. To our
2165   best knowledge, it is the first mathematical tool in concentration inequality that fits our needs.

2167   D.7    T HE A NALOGY BETWEEN G ENERALIZATION AND A DVERSARIAL T RANSFERABILITY
2168
2169   Besides providing inspiration for model ensemble attacks, the theoretical evidence in this paper
2170   also offers new insights into another fascinating idea. Within the extensive body of research on
2171   transferable adversarial attack algorithms accumulated over the years (Gu et al., 2024), we revisit a
2172   foundational analogy that is universally applicable in the adversarial transferability literature: The
2173   transferability of an adversarial example is an analogue to the generalizability of the model (Dong
2174   et al., 2018). In other words, the ideas that enhance model generalization in deep learning may
2175
       also improve adversarial transferability (Lin et al., 2019). Over the past few years, this analogy has
       significantly inspired the development of numerous effective algorithms, which directly reference it
2176
       in their papers (Lin et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021; Wang & He, 2021; Xiong et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
2177
       2024b). And some recent papers are also inspired by it (Chen et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Wang
2178   et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024). Thus, validating this influential analogy is indispensable for defining
2179   the future landscape of research in adversarial transferability. Interestingly, our paper sheds light on
2180   this insight in several ways.
2181
       First, the mathematical formulations in Lemma 1 is similar to generalization error (Vapnik, 1998;
2182
       Bousquet & Elisseeff, 2002) , which also derives an objective as a difference between the population
2183
       risk and the empirical risk. Such similarity between transferability error and generalization error
2184   suggests the possible validity of the analogy. Also, Lemma 2 is similar to the bound of the original
2185   Rademacher complexity (Golowich et al., 2018), which also suggests that obtaining a larger training
2186   set as well as a less complex model contribute a tighter bound of Rademacher complexity. Such
2187   similarities between transferability error and generalization error suggests the possible validity of the
2188   analogy. More importantly, if the analogy is correct, then recall that in the conventional framework
2189   of learning theory: (1) increasing the size of training set typically leads to a better generalization
2190   of the model (Bousquet & Elisseeff, 2002); (2) improving the diversity among ensemble classifiers
2191   makes it more advantageous for better generalization (Ortega et al., 2022); and (3) reducing the
2192
       model complexity (Cherkassky, 2002) benefits the generalization ability. It is natural to ask: In model
       ensemble attack, do (1) incorporating more surrogate models, (2) making them more diverse, and (3)
2193
       reducing their model complexity theoretically result in better adversarial transferability?
2194
2195   In Section 4, our theoretical framework provides consistently affirmative responses to the above
2196   question as well as the analogy. Considering a higher perspective, the theory is also instructive in two
2197   ways. On the one hand, from the perspective of a theoretical researcher, the extensive and advanced
2198
       generalization theory may yield enlightening insights in the field of adversarial transferability. On
       the other hand, from an practitioner’s point of view, ideas from deep learning algorithms can also be
2199
       leveraged to develop more effective transferable attack algorithms.

2202   D.8    V ULNERABILITY- DIVERSITY T RADE - OFF C URVE
2203
2204
       The relationship between vulnerability and diversity, as discussed in Section 5, merits deeper explo-
       ration. Drawing on the parallels between the vulnerability-diversity trade-off and the bias-variance
2205
       trade-off (Geman et al., 1992), we find that insights from the latter may prove valuable for under-
2206
       standing the former, and warrant further investigation.
2207
2208   The classical bias-variance trade-off suggests that as model complexity increases, bias decreases
2209   while variance rises, resulting in a U-shaped test error curve. However, recent studies have revealed
2210
       additional phenomena and provided deeper analysis (Neal et al., 2018; Neal, 2019; Derumigny &
       Schmidt-Hieber, 2023), such as the double descent (Belkin et al., 2019; Nakkiran et al., 2021).
2211
2212   Our experiments indicate that diversity does not follow the same pattern as variance in classical bias-
2213   variance trade-off. Nonetheless, there are indications within the bias-variance trade-off literature that
       suggest similar behavior might occur. For instance, Yang et al. (2020) proposes that variance exhibits


                                                          41
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2214
       a bell-shaped curve, initially increasing and then decreasing as network width grows. Additionally,
2215   Lin & Dobriban (2021) offers a meticulous understanding of variance through detailed decomposition,
2216   highlighting the influence of factors such as initialization, label noise, and training data. Overall,
2217   the trend of variance in model ensemble attack remains a valuable area for future research. We may
2218   borrow insights from machine learning literature to get a better understanding of this.

2220   D.9                I NSIGHT FOR M ODEL E NSEMBLE D EFENSE
2221
2222   While our paper primarily focuses on analyzing model ensemble attacks, our theoretical findings can
2223   also provide valuable insights for model ensemble defenses:
2224   From a theoretical perspective. The vulnerability-diversity decomposition introduced for model
2225   ensemble attacks can likewise be extended to model ensemble defenses. Mathematically, this results
2226   in a decomposition similar to conclusions in ensemble learning (see Proposition 3 in Wood et al.
2227   (2023) and Theorem 1 in Ortega et al. (2022)), which shows that within the adversarial perturbation
2228   region,
2229                         Expected loss ≤ Empirical ensemble loss − Diversity.
2230   Thus, to improve model robustness (reduce the expected loss within the perturbation region), the core
2231   strategy involves minimizing the ensemble defender’s loss or increasing diversity.
2232
       However, there is also an inherent trade-off between these two objectives: when the ensemble loss
2233   is sufficiently small, the model may overfit to the adversarial region, potentially reducing diversity;
2234   conversely, when diversity is maximized, the model may underfit the adversarial region, potentially
2235   increasing the ensemble loss. Therefore, from this perspective, our work provides meaningful insights
2236   for adversarial defense that warrant further analysis.
2237
       From an algorithmic perspective. We can consider recently proposed diversity metrics, such as
2238
       Vendi score (Friedman & Dieng, 2022) and EigenScore (Chen et al., 2024a). Following the method-
2239   ology outlined in Deng & Mu (2023), diversity can be incorporated into the defense optimization
2240   objective to strike a balance between diversity and ensemble loss. By finding an appropriate trade-off
2241   between these two factors, the effectiveness of ensemble defense may be enhanced.
