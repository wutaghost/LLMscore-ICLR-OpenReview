009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               In Federated Learning (FL), the distributed nature and heterogeneity of client data
012
               present both opportunities and challenges. While collaboration among clients can
013            significantly enhance the learning process, not all collaborations are beneficial;
014            some may even be detrimental. In this study, we introduce a novel algorithm
015            that assigns adaptive aggregation weights to clients participating in FL training,
016            identifying those with data distributions most conducive to a specific learning
017            objective. We demonstrate that our aggregation method converges no worse than
018            the method that aggregates only the updates received from clients with the same
019            data distribution. Furthermore, empirical evaluations consistently reveal that col-
020            laborations guided by our algorithm outperform traditional FL approaches. This
021
               underscores the critical role of judicious client selection and lays the foundation
               for more streamlined and effective FL implementations in the coming years.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Federated Learning (FL) introduces an innovative paradigm redefining traditional machine learning
028   workflow. Instead of centrally pooling sensitive client data, FL allows for model training on decen-
029   tralized data sources stored directly on client devices (Konečný et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2021; Li
030   et al., 2020a; Beznosikov et al., 2021). In this approach, rather than training Machine Learning (ML)
031   models in a centralized manner, a shared model is distributed to all clients. Each client then performs
032   local training, and model updates are exchanged between clients and the FL orchestrator (often
033   referred to as the master server) (McMahan et al., 2017; Shokri & Shmatikov, 2015; Karimireddy
034   et al., 2020).
035   Personalized Federated Learning (PFL). The concept of PFL (Collins et al., 2021; Hanzely et al.,
036   2020; Sadiev et al., 2022; Almansoori et al., 2024; Borodich et al., 2021; Sadiev et al., 2022) has
037   been gaining traction. In this framework, each client, often referred to as an agent, takes part in
038   developing their own personalized model variant. This tailored training approach leverages local
039   data distributions, aiming to design models that cater to the distinct attributes of each client’s dataset
040
      (Fallah et al., 2020). In contrast, standard Parallel SGD (Zinkevich et al., 2010) often leads
      to models that generalize across all clients rather than personalize to the specific data distributions
041
      and unique characteristics of individual clients, potentially resulting in suboptimal performance on
042
      personalized tasks. However, a prominent challenge arises in this decentralized training landscape
043   due to the data’s non-IID (independent and identically distributed) nature across various clients.
044   Data distributions that differ considerably can have a pronounced impact on the convergence and
045   generalization capabilities of the trained models. While certain client-specific data distributions
046   might strengthen model performance, others could prove detrimental, introducing biases or potential
047   adversarial patterns. Additionally, within the personalized federated learning paradigm, the emphasis
048   on crafting individualized models could inadvertently heighten these data disparities (Kairouz et al.,
049   2021). Consequently, this may lead to models that deliver subpar or, in some cases, incorrect results
050   when applied to wider or diverse datasets (Kulkarni et al., 2020).
051   Collaboration as a service. In this paper, we introduce a modified protocol for FL that deviates from
052   a strictly personalized approach. Rather than focusing solely on refining individualized models, our
053   approach seeks to harness the advantages of distinct data distributions, curb the detrimental effects
      of outlier clients, and promote collaborative learning. Through this innovative training mechanism,

      our algorithm discerns which clients are optimal collaborators to ensure faster convergence and
055   potentially better generalization.

070   time, several clinics can have similar data distributions.

072   1.2   C ONTRIBUTION
073
074
      Our main contributions are listed below.
075   • New method: MeritFed. We proposed a new method called Merit-based Federated Averaging
076     for Diverse Datasets (MeritFed) that aims to solve (1). The key idea is to use the stochastic
077     gradients received from the clients to adjust the weights of averaging through the inexact solving of
078     the auxiliary problem of minimizing a validation loss as a function of aggregation weights.
079   • Provable convergence under mild assumptions. We prove that MeritFed converges not worse
080
        than SGD that averages only the stochastic gradients received from clients having the same data
        distribution (these clients are not known apriori) for smooth non-convex and Polyak-Lojasiewicz
081
        functions under standard bounded variance assumption.
082
      • Utilizing all possible benefits. We numerically show that MeritFed can even benefit from
083     collaboration with clients having different data distributions when these distributions are close to
084     the target one. That is, MeritFed automatically detects beneficial clients at any stage of training.
085     Moreover, we illustrate the Byzantine robustness of the proposed method even when Byzantine
086     workers form a majority.

107   (2022). Ghosh et al. (2020) develop an algorithm that updates clusters’s centers using the gradients
      of those clients that have the smallest loss functions at the considered cluster’s center. It is worth

125   during the preprocessing stage, and the training within each cluster uses static aggregation weights.
126   Non-uniform averaging. There are also works studying the convergence of distributed SGD-type
127   methods that use non-uniform (but fixed) weights of averaging. Ding & Wang (2022) propose
128   a method to detect collaboration partners and adaptively learn "several" models for numerous
129   heterogeneous clients. Directed graph edge weights are used to calculate group partitioning. Since the
130   calculation of optimal weights in their approach is based on similarity measures between clients’ data,
131   it is unclear how to compute them in practice without sacrificing the users’s data privacy. Even et al.
132   (2022) develop and analyze another approach for personalized aggregation, where each client filters
133   gradients and aggregates them using fixed weights. The optimal weights also require estimating the
134
      distance between distributions (or communicating empirical means among all clients and estimating
      effective dimensions). Both works do not consider weights evolving in time, which is one of the key
135
      features of our method.

138   problem formulation. In particular, Wu & Wang (2021) propose the method called FedAdp that uses
139   cosine similarity between gradients and the Gompertz function for updating aggregation weights.

183   In fact, there are two major difficulties in estimating hypergradient. The first one is that the optimal

185

186

440   In all cases, the weights are almost the same for all workers during the few initial steps (even if
441   workers have quite different distributions like for the last nine clients). This phenomenon can be
442   explained as follows: if we have two different convex functions with different optima (e.g., two
443   quadratic functions), then for a far enough starting point, the gradients of those functions will point
444   roughly in the same direction. Therefore, during a few initial steps, both gradients are useful and the
445   method gives noticeable weights to both. However, once the method comes closer to the optima, the
446   gradients become noticeably different, and after a certain stage, the gradient of the second function
447
      no longer points closely towards the optimum of the first function. Therefore, starting from this
      stage, MeritFed assigns a smaller weight to the gradient of the second function. Going back
448
      to Figures 4-7, we see a similar behavior: for α = 0.5, the advantages of collaboration with clients
449
      2-11 disappear after a certain stage since the method reaches the region where two distributions
450   become noticeably different. In contrast, when α = 0.99, those workers have a very close distribution
451   to the target worker, and therefore, their stochastic gradients remain useful during the whole learning
452   process. FedAdp is biased to the target client and assigns almost identical weights to either clients
453   with similar or dissimilar distributions, which results in an accuracy decrease at the end of the training,
454   in contrast to MeritFed, which tracks and maintains less weights to non-beneficial clients. TAWT
455   is much more biased to the target client, which makes it almost identical to SGD Ideal.

514   5    C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this paper, we introduced a novel algorithm called Merit-based Federated Learning (MeritFed)
517   to address the challenges posed by the heterogeneous data distributions in federated learning (FL) via
518   the adaptive selection of the aggregation weights through solving an auxiliary minimization problem
519
      at each iteration. We demonstrated that MeritFed can effectively harness the advantages of distinct
      data distributions, control the detrimental effects of outlier clients, and promote collaborative learning.
520
      Our approach assigns adaptive aggregation weights to clients participating in FL training, allowing
521
      for faster convergence and potentially better generalization. MeritFed stands in contrast to TAWT,
522   which depends on computationally intensive hypergradient estimations, and FedAdp, which utilizes
523   cosine similarity for weight calculation. In addition, we incorporate zero-order mirror descent (MD)
524   to enhance privacy. The key contributions of this paper include the development of MeritFed,
525   provable convergence under mild assumptions, and the ability to utilize benefits from collaborating
526   with clients having different but similar data distributions.
527
      However, our work has some limitations. Firstly, (in theory) MeritFed relies on the fact that the
528   objective from the problem in Line 7 gives a good enough approximation of the expected risk f ,
529   which in some situations may require the availability of additional data on the target client to solve
530   the problem (though in all of our experiments, it was not the case and MeritFed worked well
531   even without additional data). Collecting and maintaining extra data may not always be practical or
532   efficient. Secondly, the experiments used a limited number of clients and a dataset of moderate size.
533   Extending MeritFed to large-scale FL with a substantial number of clients and massive datasets
534   may pose scalability challenges. Addressing these limitations is part of our plan for future work.
535   Furthermore, MeritFed serves as a foundation for numerous extensions and enhancements. Future
536   research can explore topics such as acceleration techniques, adaptive or scaled optimization methods
537   (e.g., variants akin to Adam) on the server side, communication compression strategies, and the
538   efficient implementation of similar collaborative learning approaches for all clients simultaneously.
539   These directions will contribute to the continued development of federated learning methods, making
      them more efficient, robust, and applicable to a wide range of practical scenarios.

      A.1   R ELATION TO T RANSFER L EARNING
867
868   While our approach resembles transfer learning (West et al., 2007), where a model trained on one
869   dataset is then enhanced/fine-tuned on another related dataset, MeritFed differs significantly in both
870   motivation and framework. Unlike transfer learning, which involves adapting a pre-trained model
871   to new data, MeritFed enhances the training process itself. Transfer learning can be theoretically
872   viewed as training with “better” initialization, while MeritFed decides on the fly what dataset to
873
      use and to what extent.
874   That is, MeritFed performs adaptive aggregation and benefits from clients having data with the
875   same distribution. It promotes collaborative learning, which is particularly applicable in cross-silo
876   federated learning (scenarios such as medical imaging).
877   Furthermore, in situations where datasets are unrelated, traditional transfer learning may not yield
878   performance improvements. In contrast, MeritFed performs not worse than SGD Ideal under
879   such conditions. Additionally, MeritFed provides robustness against Byzantine attacks, further
880   distinguishing it from conventional transfer learning methods.
881
      Exploring whether MeritFed can outperform transfer learning techniques in specific applications
882
      remains a valuable direction for future research but outside the scope of our work.
883
884
      A.2   P ERSONALIZED FL BY G RAPH - BASED AGGREGATION
885
886   Another related direction in FL more accurately addresses client clustering by constructing a clients’
887   relation graph. Chen et al. (2022b) does a graph-based model aggregation (k-hop) based on an
888   adaptively learned Graph Convolution Net (GCN). Zhang et al. (2024) also uses GCN to perform
889   graph-guided aggregation but focuses on recommendations. Both works lack theoretical analysis and
890   require solving a subproblem (similar to BLO) of learning GCN at each iteration. This subproblem
891   has a higher computation cost than MeritFed has for adaptive aggregation.

1123   C.3                              ROBUSTNESS AGAINST B YZANTINE ATTACKS
1124
1125   MeritFed is robust to Byzantine attacks since our proof of Theorem 3.4 does not make any
1126   assumptions on the vectors received from the workers having different data distribution than the target
1127   client. This means that any worker i ̸∈ G can send arbitrary vectors at each iteration, and MeritFed
1128   will still be able to converge. Moreover, MeritFed can tolerate Byzantine attacks even if Byzantine
1129   workers form a majority, e.g., the method converges even if all clients are Byzantine except for the
1130
       target one.
1131   To test the Byzantine robustness of our method on the mean estimation problem, we chose the total
1132   number of peers equal to 55 with the 50 clients being malicious. Malicious clients know the target
1133   distribution of the first 5 client and use it for performing IPM (with parameter εIPM = 0.1) (Xie et al.,
       2019) and ALIE (with parameter zALIE = 100) (Baruch et al., 2019) attacks. We also consider the Bit
