                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               In recent years, automated medical report generation (MRG) has gained signif-
012            icant research value for its potential to reduce workload and prevent diagnostic
013            errors. However, generating accurate radiology reports remains challenging due
014            to the prevalence of normal regions in X-ray images and normal descriptions in
015            medical reports. Despite various efforts to address these issues, the definitions
016            of visual bias and textual bias remain unclear and there is still a lack of com-
017            prehensive analysis of how these biases affect model behavior. In this work, we
018            rigorously define and conduct an in-depth examination of visual and textual bi-
019            ases inherent in MRG datasets. Our analysis emphasizes that global patterns, such
020
               as normal regions and findings, contribute to visual and textual bias. Further, we
               discuss how these biases make MRG models especially prone to frequency bias,
021
               where models tend to prioritize low-frequency signals that capture global pat-
022
               terns, while neglecting high-frequency signals. To debiase the frequency bias, we
023            propose the high-frequency amplification layer (HAL), aimed at enhancing the
024            model’s perceptiveness to fine-grained details. Our extensive experiments show
025            that by amplifying high-frequency signals, HAL reduces both visual and textual
026            biases, leading to improved performance in MRG tasks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The automation of diagnosis and treatment using medical images has received growing attention
032   in both academia and industry (Wolleb et al., 2022; Manzari et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2022). In
033
      particular, medical report generation (MRG) is one of the most promising tasks as it can alleviate
      the heavy burden of radiologists and reduce diagnostic errors. MRG aims to automatically generate
034
      a free-text description given a medical image (e.g., chest X-ray), describing the detailed findings on
035
      both normal and abnormal regions.
036
037   Generating diagnostically accurate and domain-specific radiology reports is challenging due to the
038   presence of severe visual and textual biases. From the perspective of data, most medical images are
039
      dominated by normal regions, making it difficult to capture distinct features (see Figure 1a). Simi-
      larly, medical reports primarily describe normal findings, complicating the explanation of abnormal
040
      findings (see Figure 1b). Recently, several methods have been proposed to address visual and textual
041
      biases (You et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021a; Tanida et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021a;
042   Huang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). However, the definitions of visual bias and textual bias have not
043   been clearly established and there remains a lack of comprehensive analytical understanding of how
044   these biases affect model behavior.
045
      Our work focuses on rigorously defining and identifying the fundamental challenges in MRG, an-
046
      alyzing how visual and textual biases hinder model performance. Further, from the perspective of
047
      the model, we relate these biases to frequency bias, where the model tends to capture low-frequency
048   signals, while neglecting high-frequency signals. In this context, we associate normal features with
049   low-frequency signals and abnormal features with high-frequency signals. In MRG, where trans-
050   formers are widely used, this issue is exacerbated by inherent visual and textual biases. To address
051   this fundamental challenge, we introduce a simple method called high-frequency amplification,
052   which amplifies high-frequency signals to better capture abnormal features. We demonstrate that
053   this simple approach effectively debiases frequency bias through extensive experiments, including
      pseudo-spectrogram analysis, cross-attention analysis, and representation analysis. We evaluate our


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      model on two benchmarks, MIMIC-CXR (Johnson et al., 2019) and IU X-ray (Demner-Fushman
055   et al., 2016). The contributions of our study can be summarized as follows:
056
057          • We precisely define visual bias and textual bias, which are crucial but underexplored chal-
058            lenges in MRG. Through comprehensive analysis, we empirically confirm the presence of
059            each bias and show how they exacerbate frequency bias. We emphasize that debiasing and
060            mitigating frequency bias is a fundamental challenge that must be addressed in MRG tasks.
061          • We introduce a simple yet effective method named high-frequency amplification, specif-
062            ically designed to mitigate the dominance of normal features in medical images and re-
063            ports. By amplifying high-frequency signals, which correspond to abnormal features, our
064            approach enables models to effectively capture both global and local patterns.
065          • We validate the effectiveness of our approach through extensive experiments, including
066            pseudo-spectrogram analysis, cross-attention analysis, and representation analysis. We
067            demonstrate our simple approach achieves performance superior or comparable to state-
068            of-the-art models across both natural language generation and clinical efficacy metrics.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
071
072
      Most existing MRG methods follow standard image captioning approaches due to the similarities
073
      between the two tasks. Despite remarkable success in image captioning models, MRG still faces
074   significant challenges due to severe visual and textual biases inherent in medical images and reports.
075
076   Medical images, often captured from consistent angles (e.g., frontal), tend to have similar appear-
077
      ances but contain subtle, localized abnormal regions. To better identify these abnormal regions,
      some studies enhanced the alignment between abnormal regions and corresponding disease tags
078
      (You et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021a), generating disease-grounded visual features. Liu et al. (2021b)
079
      introduced a differentiated attention mechanism that subtracts common features from the input im-
080   age, enabling the model to better focus on abnormal regions. Tanida et al. (2023) utilized a scene
081   graph dataset to detect anatomical regions and describe corresponding abnormal regions, enhancing
082   the explainability of the model. All of these prior studies aimed to overcome the limitations of med-
083   ical images that are visually biased due to localized abnormal regions. However, none of them have
084   thoroughly analyzed or empirically shown the existence of visual bias.
085
      Medical reports are relatively lengthy, comprising multiple sentences that describe both normal and
086   abnormal findings. Early approaches attempted to generate long reports by integrating relational
087   memory into transformers or incorporating memory matrices. (Chen et al., 2020; 2022). However,
088   these methods often struggled to accurately describe abnormal findings, as they prioritized generat-
089   ing extended narratives over capturing specific abnormalities. To improve the precision of abnormal
090   findings, more recent works have incorporated prior knowledge into MRG models using medical
091   knowledge graphs (Zhang et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021a; Huang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). All
092   of these studies aimed to address so-called textual bias, which has been inconsistently defined—
093   sometimes based on text length and at other times on the articulation of abnormal findings. That is,
094
      none of the previous works have provided a clear definition of textual bias.
095   In this paper, we establish precise definitions for visual bias and textual bias and rigorously confirm
096   the presence of each bias. We believe that this attempt will promote more focused and productive
097   discussions in future MRG research.

      4.1   T HREE I MBALANCES AND T WO B IASES IN MRG DATASET
143
144   Figure 1 illustrates three key imbalances in MRG datasets. First, X-ray images are mostly composed
145   of normal regions, with only a small portion representing abnormal areas. This visual imbalance
146   makes model performance heavily dependent on the normal regions, resulting in visual bias. Second,
147   medical reports are asymmetrically written, with far more sentences describing normal findings than
148   abnormal ones. This textual imbalance causes model performance to rely on the normal findings,
149   leading to textual bias. Finally, the distribution of disease labels is highly skewed; certain diseases
150
      are common (e.g., cardiomegaly), while others are relatively rare (e.g., pneumothorax). Such a label
      imbalance can further deteriorate model performance, but we do not explicitly address it given that
151
      mitigating visual and textual biases will inherently resolve this issue. Formal definitions of visual
152
      bias and textual bias are provided below.
153
154
      Definition 4.1 (Visual Bias). Let fZ|X denote an image classifier trained to predict a disease label
      Z given an X-ray image X. Given that the classification accuracy is highly sensitive to the size of the
155
      abnormal region |X (z) |, the model exhibits a bias towards classifying images as normal. This bias
156
      arises because normal regions typically represent global patterns, while abnormal ones are local.
157
      Definition 4.2 (Textual Bias). Let fZ|Ŷ denote a text classifier trained to predict a disease label Z
158
159   from a generated report Ŷ ∼ GY |X (ŷt |Ŷ1:t−1 , X) where GY |X is a report generator. Given that
160   the classification accuracy is highly sensitive to the number of abnormal findings |Y (z) |, the model
161   exhibits a bias towards classifying the generated report as normal. This bias arises because normal
      findings typically represent global patterns, while abnormal ones are local.

      4.2                 E XISTENCE OF V ISUAL AND T EXTUAL B IASES
177
178   To demonstrate the existence of visual and textual biases, we analyzed IoU and classification accu-
179   racy (e.g., precision and F1) in relation to the size of abnormal regions and the number of abnormal
180   findings. The image encoder and text decoder, followed by fZ|X and fZ|Ŷ , were examined inde-
181   pendently to assess the impact of visual and textual biases, respectively. The IoU (Intersection-over-
182   Union) score quantifies the overlap between ground truth bounding boxes and predicted attention
183   regions, as identified by the Grad-CAM heatmap (Selvaraju et al., 2017; Li et al., 2021; Xiao et al.,
184   2023).1 This metric allows us to evaluate how well the model captures representations relevant to
185   MRG tasks. Classification accuracy measures the performance of the image and text classifiers,
186
      fZ|X and fZ|Ŷ , with values ranging from 0 to 1. A higher score indicates that the image encoder or
187   text decoder has been effectively aligned with MRG tasks.
188   Figure 2 presents evidence of visual bias. Specifically, Figure 2a shows that as the size of the bound-
189   ing box (i.e., abnormal region) increases, both IoU and classification accuracy improve. This sug-
190   gests that as the proportion of normal regions in the X-ray increases, the image classifier fZ|X is
191   more likely to misclassify, indicating that the image encoder is influenced by visual bias. This find-
192   ing is further supported by Figure 2b. The left plot shows the number of samples misclassified as
193   negative,2 suggesting that smaller bounding boxes (i.e., larger normal regions) tend to trigger mis-
194
      classification. The right plot shows a positive correlation between IoU and F1 scores, implying that
      reduced attention to abnormal regions increases the likelihood of misclassification. This highlights
195
      that the abnormal region size contributes to visual bias.
196
197   Figure 3 presents evidence of textual bias. The left plot in Figure 3a compares classification accuracy
198   before and after training GY |X , with and without the inclusion of negative samples. The results indi-
199   cate that the text classifier, fZ|Ŷ , is more prone to misclassification when negative samples dominate
200   the training data, where the number of abnormal findings is relatively low. The right plot further re-
201
      inforces this observation: classification accuracy improves as the number of diseases increases. This
      demonstrates that the number of abnormal findings significantly affects classification accuracy, em-
202
      phasizing that the text decoder suffers from textual bias.3 Figure 3b confirms the presence of textual
203
      bias. The left plot shows the number of samples misclassified as negative samples, suggesting that
204   fewer abnormal findings are more likely to trigger misclassification. The right plot shows a linear
205   correlation between classification accuracy with and without negative samples, with a slope greater
206   than 0.5.45 This indicates that excluding negative samples improves classification accuracy, further
207   highlighting that the number of abnormal findings is a significant factor contributing to textual bias.

      4.3   F REQUENCY B IAS IN T RANSFORMER A RCHITECTURE
217
218   As discussed in previous sections, global patterns, such as normal regions and findings, contribute
219   to visual and textual biases. This bias towards global patterns has been extensively studied from the
220   model’s perspective, commonly known as frequency bias or spectral bias. Frequency bias refers to
221   the phenomenon where models tend to prioritize low-frequency signals that capture global patterns
222   across multiple samples, while neglecting high-frequency signals that represent local patterns unique
223   to each sample (Schwarz et al., 2021; Tian et al., 2023).
224   Transformers (Vaswani, 2017) are particularly vulnerable to frequency bias, as the self-attention
225   module functions as a low-pass filter, inherently paying more attention to low-frequencies than high-
226   frequencies (Wang et al., 2022; Park & Kim, 2022; Piao et al., 2024). This globality-seeking behavior
227   of the self-attention module has also been discussed in relation to Principal Component Analysis
228   (PCA) (Zhou et al., 2023; Teo & Nguyen, 2024). Given this, MRG models, where transformers are
229   dominantly used, are especially susceptible to frequency bias, because as described in §4.1 and §4.2,
230
      the training data itself is inherently biased towards global patterns. Therefore, mitigating frequency
      bias is an important and obvious challenge in MRG tasks. The following sections introduce our
231
      simple yet powerful approach to addressing this issue.

      5.2   H IGH -F REQUENCY A MPLIFICATION L AYER
271
272   As described in §4.3, the attention module introduces an inductive bias, so-called the frequency bias,
273   having transformer-based models less focused on local patterns. To address this, we introduce a high-
274   frequency amplification layer (HAL), wherein Fourier transform, high-pass filtering, and inverse
275   Fourier transform are applied subsequently. This layer enhances the model’s ability to capture fine-
276   grained details, thereby mitigating its bias towards global patterns.
277

294

295   capture normal regions and findings across (X, Y )-pairs, while local patterns represent abnormal
296   ones unique to each pair. High-pass filtering (HPF) is applied to emphasize these local patterns
297   by removing low-frequency components, thus enabling the model to focus on fine-grained details

301   T − 1, 1 ≤ d ≤ |d|}, with mc,d = 1 for high-frequency components and mc,d = 0 otherwise.

315   of (Chen et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023), we used the dataset only when both frontal and lateral view
316   images were available for each report, resulting in 2,069 images for the training set, 296 for the
317   validation set, and 590 for the test set.

417
       suggesting an indistinguishable activation pattern. In contrast, there is a rich and non-uniform acti-

418
       ture fine-grained details by amplifying high-frequency signals, which might otherwise be ignored.

472   feature discrimination by diseases. In contrast, the embeddings after HAL form well-separated clus-

474   HAL, where amplified high-frequency signals highlight the local patterns unique to each sample,
475   but erase the global patterns shared across samples, which contribute to entangled representations.
476   For multi-disease cases (see Figure 7b), we cannot conduct cluster analysis as T-SNE embeddings

      7.3   P ERFORMANCE C OMPARISON
492
493   Through the previous sections has it been shown that HAL is an effective tool for addressing fre-
494   quency bias, especially crucial in MRG tasks where visual and textual biases are already prevalent.
495   Nevertheless, one might argue that HAL, due to its simplicity, may not be competitive against exist-
496   ing methods for MRG. To address this concern, this section presents a comparative evaluation on two
497   MRG benchmark datasets: MIMIC-CXR and IU-Xray. Tables 1 and 2 summarize the performance
498   of MRG models using both NLG and CE metrics, illustrating that our model performs competi-
499   tively against other baselines. Specifically, our model outperforms baseline models on NLG metrics,
500
      demonstrating its ability to generate high-quality reports containing featured medical terminology
      found in real-world clinical texts. Furthermore, when comparing the top-3 ranks for each metric, our
501
      model ranks consistently high across almost all metrics. This balanced achievement across diverse
502
      metrics suggests that HAL not only enhances the overall quality of generated reports but also pro-
503   vides robustness in capturing key clinical concepts, making it a reliable tool for MRG tasks. Note
504   that the performance of baselines was taken directly from the results reported in the original papers.9
505
506
507   8     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
508
509   It is important to note that our current results were obtained without extensive hyperparameter op-
510   timization. We believe that a systematic exploration of hyperparameters could further enhance the
511
      model’s performance and stability, providing stronger evidence of HAL’s effectiveness. The primary
      goal of HAL is to reduce the impact of global patterns by amplifying high-frequency signals. How-
512
      ever, this approach may be less effective if the training data is either insufficient or contains too much
513
      randomness, making it difficult for dominant global patterns to emerge. In such cases, HAL might
514   even introduce a bias towards local patterns instead. Accordingly, future research should explore
515   methods to balance global and local patterns, especially when training data is limited or noisy. In
516   addition, while this study empirically links visual and textual biases with frequency bias, additional
517   theoretical grounding is needed to strengthen those empirical findings. We hope that future research
518   will further explore this area. Meanwhile, further improvements could also be explored in the pre-
519   training phase. We anticipate that pre-training the encoder or decoder models on chest X-ray data
520   would yield greater performance.

      9     C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this work, we demonstrated the existence of visual and textual biases in the MRG dataset (§4.1)
525
      and discussed how these biases make MRG models especially prone to frequency bias, with a ten-
526   dency to prioritize low-frequency components. To counter this vulnerability, we introduced the high-
527   frequency amplification layer (HAL) (§5.2), designed to mitigate the model’s predisposition towards
528   such biases. Our results showed that HAL significantly enhances various aspects, including neuron
529   activation, cross-attention map, and representation quality, as detailed in ablation studies (§7.2). De-
530   spite its simplicity, HAL exhibited outstanding performance in comparative evaluations (§7.3). All
531   these findings strongly support our arguments that: (1) debiasing is a fundamental issue for improv-
532   ing MRG tasks, and (2) mitigating frequency bias is crucial for enabling models to capture both
533   global and local patterns of medical images more effectively. We believe this work will pave the
534
      way for more robust MRG models that are better equipped to handle the complexities of real-world
      medical data, ultimately contributing to advanced medical imaging tasks.

      Grad-CAM in ViTs In this paragraph, we briefly explain how we calculated the IoU score based
771   on Grad-CAM. Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) is a method for generating visual explanations
772   to identify which parts of the input image have the most influence on a given class prediction.
773   It was originally proposed for CNN architecture and is now applicable to ViTs as well. Similar
774   to CNN-based models, which extract feature maps from the last convolutional layer, Grad-CAM
775   for ViTs extracts feature maps from the norm1 layer of the final block. These feature maps have
                                 2
776   the shape of f ∈ RN ×(P ×C) , where N , P , and C represent the number of patches, the patch
777   size, and the number of channels, respectively. The feature maps are then projected back into the
778   original image space by reversing the patchification process, allowing for spatial interpretation. In
779   this experiment, we extracted activation maps based on the ground truth disease label. To generate
780   predicted bounding boxes, we retained only the regions of the activation maps that exceed 75% of the
781
      maximum activation value. We then utilized OpenCV’s findContours function to detect the contours
      of these regions, followed by the minAreaRect function to generate the minimum area bounding
782
      rectangles that enclose each contour (see Figure 8).

887   A.3            V I T OUTPERFORMS R ES N ET IN M EDICAL I MAGING TASKS
888
      Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have been widely used in various computer vision tasks.
889   After the advent of Vision Transformers (ViTs), ViTs have shown its potential as a competitive
890   alternative to CNNs. Since CNNs and ViTs each exhibit distinct advantages and limitations, it is
891   general to choose the appropriate backbone model based on the downstream tasks. For instance,
892   CNNs possess a high inductive bias, while ViTs effectively capture long-range dependencies. Al-
893   though CNN-based models might seem suitable for the localized nature of abnormalities in medical
894   images, numerous studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of ViTs in automated medical image
895   diagnosis, ranging from medical image segmentation (Karimi et al., 2021), medical image classifi-
896   cation (Matsoukas et al., 2021), and medical image reconstruction (Zhang et al., 2021), especially
897
      when pre-trained on ImageNet. Since diagnosis often requires consideration of distant organs and
      tissues, the long-range dependencies captured by ViTs are particularly advantageous in the medical
898
      domain. In medical images, abnormal regions may span large or subtle regions. Unlike CNNs, which
899
      focus on local features in the lower layers and global features in the higher layers, ViTs capture
900   both local and global features at every layer, preserving fine-grained details and contextual relation-
901   ships (Raghu et al., 2021). Additionally, sparse attention mechanisms in ViTs are known to enhance
902   robustness against noise (Zhou et al., 2022). ViTs perform particularly well when pre-trained on
903   large-scale datasets or via self-supervision.
904
      This finding is further validated through our simple experiment. Specifically, we compared the clas-
905
      sification performance of ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) and ViT-S (Dosovitskiy, 2020), both pre-
906   trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned on the Vindr-CXR dataset for 50 epochs. The results demon-
907   strated that ViT-S outperformed ResNet-50, despite the latter having a slightly higher number of
908   parameters (See Table 3).
