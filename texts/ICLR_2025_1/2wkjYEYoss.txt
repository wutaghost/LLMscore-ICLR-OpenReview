009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Image assessment aims to evaluate the quality and aesthetics of images and has
012            been applied across various scenarios, such as natural and AIGC scenes. Exist-
013            ing methods mostly address these sub-tasks or scenes individually. While some
014            works attempt to develop unified image assessment models, they have struggled to
015            achieve satisfactory performance or cover a broad spectrum of assessment scenar-
016            ios. In this paper, we present Gamma, a Generic imAge assessMent model using
017            Mixture of Assessment Experts, which can effectively assess images from diverse
018
               scenes through mixed-dataset training. Achieving unified training in image as-
               sessment presents significant challenges due to annotation biases across different
019
               datasets. To address this issue, we first propose a Mixture of Assessment Ex-
020
               perts (MoAE) module, which employs shared and adaptive experts to dynamically
021            learn common and specific knowledge for different datasets, respectively. In addi-
022            tion, we introduce a Scene-based Differential Prompt (SDP) strategy, which uses
023            scene-specific prompts to provide prior knowledge and guidance during the learn-
024            ing process, further boosting adaptation for various scenes. Our Gamma model is
025            trained and evaluated on 12 datasets spanning 6 image assessment scenarios. Ex-
026            tensive experiments show that our unified Gamma outperforms other state-of-the-
027            art mixed-training methods by significant margins while covering more scenes.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Image assessment is a long-standing research topic in the field of image processing, primarily com-
033
      prising two tasks: Image Quality Assessment (IQA) and Image Aesthetic Assessment (IAA). These
      tasks require models to automatically evaluate the visual quality and aesthetic appeal of images,
034
      respectively. They have broad applications in various real-world scenarios, such as guiding image
035
      dehazing (Zhao et al., 2021), selecting high-quality images in a data engine (Rombach et al., 2022),
036   serving as tools in an agentic system (Yang et al., 2024), or acting as reward models when aligning
037   image generative models with human feedback (Liang et al., 2024).
038
039
      Due to differences in image content and application scenarios, image assessment has spawned many
      sub-tasks, such as Natural-IQA for natural images, Face-IQA for facial images, AIGC-IQA for
040
      generated images, and IAA. Accordingly, numerous methods (Ke et al., 2021; He et al., 2022; Su
041
      et al., 2023b) have been proposed to address these specific tasks. However, these models often
042   struggle to apply directly to other scenes or typically require task-specific fine-tuning on a given
043   dataset. As illustrated in Figure 1, it is challenging for DEIQT (Qin et al., 2023) to transfer to other
044   datasets without fine-tuning. This limitation prevents image assessment models from being widely
045   applicable, e.g., IQA models are needed to assess facial, artistic, and natural images in the AIGC
046   scene. Hence, there is an urgent need for a model that can effectively handle a variety of scenarios.
047
      To mitigate this issue, some approaches attempt to combine many datasets from various assessment
048   tasks to train a general image assessment model. For instance, UNIQUE (Zhang et al., 2021) and
049   LIQE (Zhang et al., 2023) utilize multiple authentic and synthetic natural IQA datasets for mixed
050   training, but they focus only on Natural-IQA. Q-Align (Wu et al., 2023) uses a large language model
051   with billions of parameters to unify IQA and IAA tasks, but it has a low inference speed and focuses
052   solely on natural images. Additionally, PromptIQA (Chen et al., 2024b) employs image-score pairs
053   as prompts for quality predictions, but it is inflexible as it requires multiple images as references
      during inference. These methods, however, fail to achieve competitive performance compared to

073
      develop a foundational image assessment model capable of evaluating images from various scenes.
074
075   To this end, we present Gamma, a Generic imAge assessMent model using Mixture of Assessment
076   experts, to achieve unified image assessment across multiple datasets effectively. We found that the
077
      primary challenge in mixed-dataset training is the mean opinion score (MOS) bias between different
      datasets, e.g., images with similar MOS may exhibit different perceptual qualities across various
078
      datasets. As shown in Figure 2, samples from KonIQ (Hosu et al., 2020), LIVEC (Ghadiyaram
079
      & Bovik, 2015), UWIQA (Yang et al., 2021a), and SPAQ (Fang et al., 2020) show obvious dif-
080   ferences in perceptual quality despite being labeled with similar MOS. To address this challenge,
081   we introduce a novel Mixture of Assessment Experts (MoAE) module in our Gamma model.
082   MoAE consists of two types of experts: shared experts and adaptive experts. The shared experts
083   are employed throughout to learn dataset-shared knowledge, while the adaptive experts are dynami-
084   cally activated to varying degrees to learn dataset-specific knowledge. Additionally, an image-based
085   router modulates the contributions of each adaptive expert. This strategy allows the model to capture
086   common features while flexibly adjusting representative features for different datasets. Compared
087   with general Mixture of Experts (MoE) (Shazeer et al., 2017) and Lora-based MoE (Liu et al., 2024),
088
      we equip the MoAE module only in the rear blocks instead of all blocks, making it more efficient.
      Secondly, we propose a Scene-based Differential Prompt (SDP), which uses different prompts
089
      for different datasets according to their scenes. This strategy provides scene-specific knowledge for
090
      representation learning of different datasets, guiding the mixed-dataset training process.
091
092   Our Gamma model is uniformly trained on a mixture of 12 datasets from 6 image assessment scenar-
093   ios spanning IQA and IAA tasks. We then evaluate it on 12 datasets, demonstrating that it not only
094
      outperforms state-of-the-art (SOTA) mixed-training methods by notable margins, but also covers
      more scenarios. In some benchmarks, Gamma even surpasses some SOTA task-specific methods.
095
      Additionally, if we fine-tune our MoAE-equipped Gamma on specific datasets, it can achieve SOTA
096
      performance on 12 datasets. Moreover, the unified pre-trained Gamma can be utilized as a foun-
097   dational model to significantly enhance other image assessment tasks, e.g., medical image quality
098   assessment, and can achieve SOTA performance after task-specific training. Our contributions can
099   be summarized as follows:
100
101                  • We present Gamma, a powerful and generic image assessment model, capable of accu-
102                    rately assessing images from various scenarios through mixed training.
103
                     • We propose a novel Mixture of Assessment Experts (MoAE) module to extract represen-
104
                       tative features adaptively and a Scene-based Differential Prompt (SDP) strategy to provide
105
                       guidance for representation learning, thereby achieving effective mixed-dataset training.
106
107                  • Extensive experiments show that Gamma achieves SOTA performance on 12 datasets
                       across 6 image assessment scenes in both mixed training and task-specific training settings.

      2.1   I MAGE A SSESSMENT
111
112   Image Assessment mainly includes two subtasks: Image Quality Assessment (IQA) and Image Aes-
113   thetic Assessment (IAA). The IQA task focuses on the distortion level of the image, while IAA
114   aims to evaluate the aesthetic appeal of the image. In the deep learning era, these two tasks have
115   achieved significant breakthroughs. For the IQA task, researchers develop various advanced tech-
116   niques to improve performance, including multi-level feature aggregation (Li et al., 2018; Chen
117   et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2018; Mittal et al., 2012b; Ying et al., 2020), adaptive con-
118
      volution (Su et al., 2020), transformer methods (Ke et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2023), vision-language
      models (VLMs) (Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) and large language models (LLM) (You
119
      et al., 2023). Moreover, besides the natural image assessment, these are various IQA methods for
120
      other scenes, such as face IQA (Ou et al., 2021; Su et al., 2023b; Jo et al., 2023), AIGC IQA (Yuan
121   et al., 2023), underwater IQA (Yang et al., 2021b; Guo et al., 2023; Yang & Sowmya, 2015; Liu
122   et al., 2023). For the IAA task, numerous methods have also been proposed to improve the model
123   performance, including loss function (Talebi & Milanfar, 2018), novel transformer architecture (Tu
124   et al., 2022), multi-level features (Hosu et al., 2019), theme information (He et al., 2022; Li et al.,
125   2023b) and multimodal pre-training (Ke et al., 2023).
126
127   2.2   M IXED T RAINING FOR I MAGE A SSESSMENT
128
129   As a fundamental image processing task, image assessment has achieved remarkable success and
130   has been applied to various scenarios. Recently, some works have attempted to develop unified
131
      methods that can be used in multiple IQA settings. To achieve this goal, one approach is to con-
      duct mixed training across multiple IQA datasets. UNIQUE (Zhang et al., 2021) sampled pairs
132
      of images from IQA datasets and computes the probability that the first image of each pair is of
133
      higher quality. StairIQA (Sun et al., 2023) designed separate IQA regression heads for each dataset.
134   PromptIQA (Chen et al., 2024b) utilized a short sequence of Image-Score Pairs as prompts for qual-
135   ity predictions. Q-Align (Wu et al., 2023) used large language model (LLM) to unify IQA and
136   IAA tasks. However, most existing works fail to achieve competitive performance with task-specific
137   methods and do not cover various scenes. This paper combines various datasets from both tasks and
138   designs innovative modules to effectively learn a unified and generic image assessment perception.
139
140   2.3   M IXTURE OF E XPERTS
141
142   Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) divides specific parts of the parameters into several subsets, each of
143   which is called an expert. It sets up a router that assigns experts to different inputs. Recently,
144
      the MoE structure has achieved remarkable success in large language models (LLM). For instance,
      DeepSeekMoE (Dai et al., 2024) proposes a novel MoE architecture that uses shared and routed
145
      experts to extract common and dynamic knowledge simultaneously. Beyond the natural language
146
      processing tasks, the idea of MoE has also been applied to vision models (Dai et al., 2021; Riquelme
147   et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023) and multimodal transformers (Wang et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2023).
148   In Gamma, we utilize MoE to effectively learn specific and general features of multi-dataset.

205   3.3      M IXTURE OF A SSESSMENT E XPERTS
206
207   To develop a unified and generic image assessment model, we aim to combine multiple image as-
208   sessment datasets for joint training. Unfortunately, the mean opinion score (MOS) introduces sig-
209
      nificant biases across different datasets, which hinders joint training. To address this challenge, we
      propose the MoAE module, where several experts are employed to learn the diverse biases of differ-
210
      ent datasets. As shown in Figure 3 (b), the proposed MoAE module includes a shared assessment
211
      expert (E shared ) to learn common knowledge of image assessment and several adaptive assessment
212
      experts (from E1adaptive to Enadaptive ) to dynamically learn dataset-specific knowledge.
213
214   The Shared Assessment Expert. The shared assessment expert E shared inherits the image assess-
215   ment capabilities of the original CLIP model by reusing its weights. This expert remains frozen
      during training to ensure that the learned world knowledge is retained. Thus, the model can capture

      3.4   S CENE - BASED DIFFERENTIAL PROMPT
251
252   To facilitate scene-guided learning, we introduce a Scene-based Differential Prompt (SDP) to help
253   the model acquire scene-specific knowledge from different datasets. We utilize 12 datasets spanning
254   6 image assessment scenarios, including synthetic distortion nature IQA, authentic distortion nature
255   IQA, face IQA, AIGC IQA, underwater IQA, and IAA, for mixed training (details are recorded in
256   Appendix A.2). We categorize these datasets into five groups based on their scenes: natural quality,
257   AI-generated quality, underwater quality, face quality, and natural aesthetics. Specifically, for the
258   face quality assessment dataset, we use prompts such as face bad-quality, face poor-quality, face
259
      fair-quality, face good-quality, face perfect-quality appended to the word image. For more details
      on these prompts, please refer to Appendix A.4.
260
261   This strategy effectively differentiates the feature space of images from various scenes and enhances
262   scene-specific knowledge, thereby mitigating the MOS bias across different datasets. Experimental
263   results show that this method significantly improves the model’s performance (Table 1).

305   4.2     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
306
      Following the settings in (Su et al., 2020; Ke et al., 2021), we randomly divide each dataset into 80%
307
      for training and 20% for testing. The training dataset is a mixture of the training sets of each dataset
308   and we test Gamma on each test data separately. This process is repeated 10 times, and the median
309   of the 10 scores is reported as the final score. We use the pre-trained weight of UniQA (Zhou et al.,
310   2024), which uses CLIP-B/16 as multimodal encoder. We freeze the CLIP visual and text encoders,
311   training only the MoAE module and adapter. For the unified training, we train the model for 10
312   epochs with a batch size of 8. The initial learning rate is set to 2e-5. We normalize the MOS/DMOS
313   scale to [0, 1] for all datastes. We utilize Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) and MSE loss to optimize
314   the model. For the task-specific training, we use different training settings according to the task and
315   size of datasets. More training details are provided in the appendix.

529   5     C ONCLUSION
530
531   This paper introduces Gamma, a generic image assessment model that can be applied to various
532   image scenarios. To achieve this, we utilize the mixed training of different datasets to obtain the
533   assessment abilities of different scenarios. We propose a Mixture of Assessment Expert (MoAE)
534   module and a Scene-based Differential Prompt (SDP) strategy to effectively cope with the MOS
535
      bias in different datasets. MoAE utilizes shared experts and adaptive experts to extract common and
      representative features adaptively. SDP strategy employs different prompts for different datasets
536
      to provide guidance for feature learning. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method can
537
      achieve SOTA performance on various datasets simultaneously, showing the strong generalization
538   and general image assessment capabilities.
