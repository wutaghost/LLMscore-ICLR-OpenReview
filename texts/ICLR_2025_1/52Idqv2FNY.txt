011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The field of natural language processing (NLP) historically evaluated language
014            models using benchmarks with automated metrics. However, the recent advent of
015
               highly capable chat language models (LMs) has caused a tectonic shift from NLP
               benchmarks to human evaluations. The relationship between these two evaluation
016
               processes is unclear and underexplored for chat LMs. Broadly, to what extent are
017
               human evaluations and NLP benchmarks correlated with one another? How well
018            can computationally inexpensive and automated benchmarks predict expensive and
019            time-intensive human evaluations? Which benchmarks provide predictive signals
020            for human preference for LMs? What role, if any, should benchmarks play in the
021            era of chat LMs? To answer these questions, we conducted a large-scale study
022            of the relationships between human evaluations and benchmarks. We show that
023            benchmarks are broadly highly correlated with human evaluations, and we identify
024            which benchmarks exhibit strong correlations with human evaluations and which
025            do not. Having established that reliable correlations exist, we fit models to predict
026
               a language model’s human evaluation scores from its academic evaluation scores
               and provide evidence that such predictive models can generalize across LM scales.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   For decades, the field of natural language processing (NLP) has relied on academic benchmarks
032   and automated metrics (e.g., Accuracy, Brier Score (Brier, 1950), BLEU Papineni et al. (2002)) to
033   evaluate the performance of language models (LMs). These NLP benchmarks provide a standardized
034   and efficient way to measure model capabilities such as machine translation, text summarization, and
035   question answering (Wang et al., 2018; 2019; Srivastava et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
036   2023a). However, the recent emergence of highly capable chat LMs such as GPT (Ouyang et al.,
037   2022; Achiam et al., 2023), Llama (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Dubey et al., 2024), Gemini (Team et al.,
038
      2023; Reid et al., 2024) and Claude (Anthropic, 2023) has prompted a re-evaluation of how we assess
      LMs, with a growing emphasis on assessing LMs based on their ability to interact with and assist
039
      human users in real-world scenarios (Zheng et al., 2023; Reuel et al., 2024).
040
041   This shift towards human evaluations raises important questions about the relationship between NLP
042   benchmarks and human evaluations of chat LMs. Additionally, human evaluations are not without
043   challenges; they can be expensive, time-intensive and noisy, in contrast with computationally cheaper,
044
      faster and precise benchmarks. In this paper, we aim to explore the relationship between human
      evaluations and NLP benchmarks in pursuit of understanding what role, if any, benchmarks should
045
      play in the era of chat LMs. As shown in Fig. 1, we seek to answer two key research questions:
046
047         1. To what extent are human evaluations and NLP benchmarks correlated with one another?
048
            2. How well can NLP benchmarks predict human evaluations?
049
050   To answer these questions, we conducted a large-scale study comparing human evaluations and
051   NLP benchmarks using four Llama 2 Chat language models (LMs) (Touvron et al., 2023b). For
052   human evaluations, we constructed a large-scale dataset of single-turn and multi-turn prompts from a
053   diverse taxonomy (Fig. 2) and collect high quality pairwise preference data of the four Chat Llama 2
      models against GPT 3.5 (Ouyang et al., 2022) from paid human annotators. For NLP benchmarks,

083   2    R ELATED W ORK
084
085   The evaluation of language models has a rich and constantly evolving history. Human evaluations
086   have long been considered the gold standard (Gatt & Krahmer, 2018; Van Der Lee et al., 2019;
087   Celikyilmaz et al., 2020; Roller et al., 2020; van der Lee et al., 2021), despite serious objections
088   raised regarding the collection, analysis, and interpretation of human evaluation scores (Novikova
089   et al., 2018; Howcroft et al., 2020; Bowman & Dahl, 2021; Karpinska et al., 2021; Clark et al., 2021;
090   Smith et al., 2022; Gehrmann et al., 2023; Finch et al., 2023). Many classic NLP benchmark metrics,
091   such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), NIST (Doddington, 2002), ROUGE (Lin, 2004), and METEOR
092   (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), were introduced on the premise that they correlate with human judgments.
093
      However, subsequent studies revealed that the relationship between automated metrics and human
      evaluations is often complex and not straightforward (Liu et al., 2016; Novikova et al., 2017; Reiter,
094
      2018; Karpinska et al., 2021). Another prominent class of evaluation methods are based on machine
095
      learning models, e.g., word mover distance (Kusner et al., 2015) and BERT-Score (Zhang et al., 2019)
096   that have since evolved into using chat LMs themselves as evaluators (Wang et al., 2023b; Zheng
097   et al., 2024; Chiang & yi Lee, 2023; Chan et al., 2023; Bavaresco et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024), albeit
098   with limitations, e.g., (Dorner et al., 2024; Szymanski et al., 2024; Thakur et al., 2024).
099
      The earliest investigations into the general relationship between NLP benchmark scores and human
100
      evaluations date back to Bangalore et al. (2000), Belz & Reiter (2006), and Liu et al. (2016). In the
101
      context of natural language generation, Clinciu et al. (2021) found that embedding-based automated
102   metrics (e.g., BERT-Score (Zhang et al., 2019) and BLEURT Sellam et al. (2020)) correlate more
103   strongly with human judgments compared to word-overlap metrics (e.g., ROUGE (Lin, 2004) and
104   BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002)). In the domain of natural language inference, Schuff et al. (2021)
105   found that automated metrics do not appear to correlate with human judgment scores. However,
106   the majority of these works predate the current era of chat LMs, which exhibit significantly more
107   advanced capabilities compared to their predecessors. This new era motivates our work to investigate
      the relationship between NLP benchmarks and human evaluations when evaluating chat LMs.

143   3    M ETHODS : M ODELS , H UMAN E VALUATIONS AND NLP B ENCHMARKS
144
145   We briefly outline our methodology here; for additional information, please see Appendix A.
146
147   Models Our paper leverages the Llama 2 model family, consisting of four Chat LMs with 7, 13, 34,
148   and 70 billion parameters pre-trained on 2 trillion tokens and finetuned using supervised finetuning
149   (Sanh et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2022; Longpre et al., 2023) and reinforcement learning from human
150   feedback (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020). We chose the Llama 2
151   models because at the time we collected our data, the Llama 2 family contained leading open-access
152   chat-finetuned models spanning multiple scales with minimal variations in architecture, ensuring
153   consistency in our analyses and a robust foundation for our investigations.
154
155   Human Evaluations: Single Turn & Multi-Turn In this work, our aim was specifically to
156   identify which NLP benchmark scores are predictive of human preferences on open-ended prompts
157   representative of real-world chat model usage. We chose this approach to maximize the ecological
158
      validity and generalizability of the findings to real-world use cases. For a concrete example, we
      may want our chat language models (LMs) to excel at providing bespoke career advice; which NLP
159
      benchmarks provide useful signals for whether models are improving at such tasks?
160
161   To answer such questions, we created a taxonomy of single-turn and multi-turn interactions (Fig. 2)
      between chat LMs and humans. For single-turn interactions, we generated a diverse set of prompts


                                                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      spanning common areas of interest: Factual Questions, Procedural Questions, Language Assistance,
163   Writing & Content Creation, Dialogue, Code, Reasoning, Recommendations / Brainstorming and
164   Safety, with nested categories and subcategories. For multi-turn prompts, non-annotator humans were
165   asked to have conversations (3 to 15 turns long) with all models on similar topics of interest: Factual
166   Questions, Procedural Questions, Language Assistance, Writing & Content Creation, Summarization
167   & Editing, General Dialogue, Reasoning and Recommendations / Brainstorming. This taxonomy was
168   chosen to broadly cover common use-cases of Chat LMs. Example prompts include: “What is the
169   tallest mountain in the world?” (Factual Question); “How do I make minestrone soup?” (Procedural
170   Question); “Please make this sentence more friendly: I need you to stop parking in my space”
171
      (Language Assistance); “Write me a poem about getting to the weekend after a long day at work”
      (Writing & Content Creation). See Appendix A.2 for more information.
172
173   We then paid human annotators to evaluate each of the four Chat Llama 2 models against ChatGPT
174   3.5 (Ouyang et al., 2022) (gpt-3.5-0301) on a dataset of single-turn and multi-turn prompts (Fig
175   2). We chose gpt-3.5-0301 because, at the time this data was collected, gpt-3.5-0301 was a good
176   balance of three desirable properties for our study: performant, cheap, and stable. For each pair of
177
      conversations (one conversation with Chat Llama responses and the other with ChatGPT responses),
      at least three unique human annotators independently indicated which conversation was preferred
178
      using a Likert scale (Likert, 1932) from 1 to 7, where 1 denotes the Chat Llama model was strongly
179
      preferred and 7 denotes gpt-3.5-0301 was strongly preferred. Across the 11291 single-turn samples
180   and 2081 multi-turn samples, we had at least 3 unique human annotators per pairwise comparison,
181   with 2104 unique human annotators overall. For our analyses, we averaged the annotators’ scores for
182   each pairwise comparison to give us an average human evaluation score per datum.
183
184   Natural Language Processing (NLP) Benchmarks We evaluated the four Chat Llama 2 models
185   on large-scale and commonly-used NLP benchmarks: AGI Eval (Zhong et al., 2023), AI2 Reasoning
186   Challenge (ARC; both Easy and Hard) (Clark et al., 2018), BIG Bench Hard (Srivastava et al., 2022;
187   Suzgun et al., 2022) BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019), CommonSenseQA (Talmor et al., 2019), COPA
188   (Roemmele et al., 2011), DROP (Dua et al., 2019), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), HellaSwag (Zellers
189
      et al., 2019), HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021), InverseScaling (McKenzie et al., 2022a;b; 2023), MBPP
      (Austin et al., 2021), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), Natural Questions (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019),
190
      OpenbookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020), QuAC (Choi et al., 2018), RACE
191
      (Lai et al., 2017), SIQA (Sap et al., 2019), SQUAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016), TLDR (Völske et al.,
192   2017), TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021) and XSum (Narayan et al.,
193   2018). Some of these benchmarks (e.g., MMLU) contain subsets (e.g., Jurisprudence) that we do not
194   aggregate over. These tasks cover commonsense reasoning, world knowledge, reading comprehension,
195   coding and more. We used standard evaluation processes for all academic benchmarks including
196   prompt formatting, metrics, 0-shot/few-shot, etc. This structured approach facilitates an exhaustive
197   examination of model performances across varied metrics. For more information, see Appendix A.1.
198
199   Scores for Subsequent Analyses For each dataset and evaluation process (either human or NLP),
200   we average each model’s scores across all samples, yielding two matrices of scores:
201                                XNLP ∈ R160×4              XHuman ∈ R55×4
202   Here, 4 is the number of models, 160 is the number of NLP benchmarks per model and 55 is the
203   number of human evaluation area-category-subcategory scores per model. We subsequently study the
204   correlations between XNLP and XHuman , then test how well XNLP can predict XHuman .

      4   C ORRELATING H UMAN E VALUATIONS WITH NLP B ENCHMARKS
207
208
      We began by computing correlations between human evaluations and NLP benchmarks, computing
209
      three standard correlations over the 4 average scores per model — Pearson (Galton, 1877), Spearman
210   (Spearman, 1904) and Kendall (Kendall, 1938) — giving us three correlation matrices of shape
211   160 × 55 between every pair of NLP benchmark and human evaluation area-category-subcategory
212   (Fig. 3). Pearson correlation measures the linear relationship between two continuous variables,
213   whereas Spearman and Kendall correlations assess the monotonic relationship between two variables;
214   Spearman correlation is based on the rank order of the data points, whereas Kendall correlation is
215   determined by the number of concordant and discordant pairs. By using different correlation metrics,
      we aim to robustly characterize the relationships between human and NLP benchmarks.

         4.3                                         W HAT COMMUNITIES EXIST BETWEEN HUMAN EVALUATIONS AND NLP BENCHMARKS ?
363
      To detect what communities exist between human evaluations and NLP benchmarks, we computed the
364
      singular value decomposition of the pairwise Pearson correlation matrix between human evaluations
365
      and NLP benchmarks (Fig. 6 top). The maximum rank the correlation matrix can have is 4 because
366   the correlations are computed over the 4 Chat Llama 2 models, but we found that the correlation
367   matrix has only 3 non-zero singular values (App. Fig. 12). Decomposing the correlation matrix
368   into its 3 rank-one components σ1 u1 v1T + σ2 u2 v2T + σ3 u3 v3T revealed three levels of increasing
369   fine-grained structure in the correlations (App. Fig. 13). We then visualized the human evaluations
370   and NLP benchmarks in the (dimension-scaled) plane defined by the first two rank-one components
371   of the Pearson correlation matrix (Fig. 6 bottom).
372   The bulk of human evaluations and NLP benchmarks live in one community; however, there are also
373   several smaller interesting communities. Starting on the left of Fig. 6 and moving clockwise, at the
374   top left is a loose community of Dialogue.Code, Dialogue.Language Assistant, several Kth Sentence
375   tasks, Openbook Question Answering (OBQA), AGI.LSAT, AGI.Lawyer Qualification Test, which
376   generally measure model capabilities at identifying and using key information within the context.
377   On the top right, Inverse Scaling.NEQA Classification is alone; this benchmark measures whether
      models are tripped up by negated questions, which most humans try not to do and likely explains why

465             5                             P REDICTING H UMAN E VALUATIONS FROM NLP B ENCHMARKS
466
467         Having established the existence of correlations between human evaluations and NLP benchmarks,
468         we next investigated the feasibility of predicting human evaluations from NLP benchmarks. Our goal
469         is to build predictive models that accurately predict a language model’s average human evaluation
470         scores per evaluation areas and categories using the model’s average scores on NLP benchmarks
471         and tasks. However, we faced a significant challenge due to the overparameterized nature of our
472         data: for each target human evaluation area or category, there are approximately 150 covariates (NLP
473         benchmarks and tasks) but only 4 samples (Chat Llama 2 models).
474
475
476           Predictive Modeling: Overparameterized Linear Regressions To predict human evaluations
477
              from NLP benchmarks, we used overparameterized linear regression. In general, overparameterized
              linear regression is known to be capable of generalizing (App. Sec. A.3), although whether linear
478
              models would generalize in this setting was an empirical question. For each human evaluation area
479
              and category, we fit a linear model to predict a language model’s average human evaluation score
480           from its average scores on all NLP benchmarks and tasks. To assess the predictive accuracy of these
481           overparameterized models, we employed leave-one-out cross validation: we fit four separate linear
482           models, each time fitting on three of the chat LMS’ scores and holding out the fourth to test the
483           performance of the linear model. This approach allows us to estimate the models’ performance
484           on unseen data, albeit with limitations due to the small sample size. Before fitting the models, we
485           normalized all human evaluation scores to lie in [0, 1] rather than [−7, −1] (recalling that higher
              scores indicate the human evaluator prefers the Chat Llama 2 model compared to GPT-3.5).

      Results Across the various human evaluation areas and categories, we found that the linear models’
487   predicted average human evaluation scores generally align well with the actual average human
488   evaluation scores, as evidenced by most points falling close to the identity line in the predicted score
489   vs. actual score plane (Fig. 7). This suggests that, despite the overparameterization, the linear models
490   can capture meaningful relationships between NLP benchmarks and human evaluations. However, we
491   caution against over-interpreting these results, as the small sample size and the assumption of linearity
492   may limit the generalizability of these findings to other language models or evaluation settings.
493   To gain insight into which NLP benchmarks are most informative for predicting human evaluation
494   scores, we examine the learned weights of the linear models (Fig. 18). NLP benchmarks with
495   consistently high absolute weights across different human evaluation areas and categories are likely to
496   be more predictive of human judgments. However, due to the overparameterized nature of the models,
497   we refrain from drawing strong conclusions about the relative importance of individual benchmarks
498   and instead focus on the overall predictive performance. These results suggest that scaling up the
499   number of chat LMs and human evaluation data could unlock highly predictive models of slow, noisy
500   and expensive but valuable human evaluations using fast, precise and cheaper NLP benchmarks.
501
502   6    D ISCUSSION
503
504   In this paper, we explored the relationship between human evaluations and NLP benchmarks of
505   chat-finetuned language models (chat LMs). Our work is motivated by the recent shift towards human
506   evaluations as the primary means of assessing chat LM performance, and the need to understand the
507   role that NLP benchmarks can play in this new era.
508   Through a large-scale study of the Chat Llama 2 model family on a diverse set of human and
509   NLP evaluations, we demonstrated that NLP benchmarks are generally well-correlated with human
510   judgments of chat LM quality. However, our analysis also reveals some notable exceptions to this
511   overall trend. In particular, we find that adversarial and safety-focused evaluations, as well as language
512   assistance and open question answering tasks, exhibit weaker or negative correlations respectively
513   with NLP benchmarks. We also explored predicting human evaluation scores from NLP evaluation
514   scores using overparameterized linear regression models. Our results suggest that NLP benchmarks
515   can indeed be used to predict aggregate human preferences, although we caution that the limited
516
      sample size and the assumptions of our models may limit the generalizability of these findings. Our
      results suggest that NLP benchmarks can serve as fast and cheap proxies of slower and expensive
517
      human evaluations in assessing chat LMs.
518
519   Additionally, our work highlights the need for further research into NLP evaluations that can effec-
520   tively capture important aspects of LM behavior, such as safety, robustness to adversarial inputs, and
521   performance on complex, open-ended tasks. It is possible that new NLP benchmarks can provide
522
      signals on these topics, e.g., (Wang et al., 2023a). Of particular interest is developing human-
      interpretable and scaling-predictable evaluation processes, e.g., (Schaeffer et al., 2024a; Ruan et al.,
523
      2024; Schaeffer et al., 2024c). Developing and refining such evaluation methods (Madaan et al.,
524
      2024), as well as detecting whether evaluations scores faithfully capture models’ true performance
525   (Oren et al., 2023; Schaeffer, 2023; Roberts et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Duan
526   et al., 2024) will be crucial for ensuring that LMs are safe, reliable, and beneficial as they become
527   increasingly integrated into real-world use cases.
528
      In conclusion, our study provides insights into the relationship between human evaluations and NLP
529
      benchmarks of chat language models. By leveraging the complementary strengths of both human and
530
      NLP benchmarks, we can build a more complete understanding of LM capabilities and behaviors,
531   ultimately enabling the development of models more capable, trustworthy, and beneficial to society.
