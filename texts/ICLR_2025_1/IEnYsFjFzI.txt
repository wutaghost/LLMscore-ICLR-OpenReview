009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               We propose a novel quantum online algorithm for learning Markov Decision Pro-
012            cesses (MDPs) with continuous state space in the average reward model. Our
013            algorithm is based on the line of work on classical online UCCRL algorithms
014            by Ortner and Ryabko (NeurIPS’12). To the best of our knowledge, our work
015            is the first to consider MDPs with continuous state space in the fault-tolerant
016            quantum setting. In the case where the state space is one-dimensional and the
017            MDP’s rewards and transition probabilities are assumed to be Lipschitz, we
018            show that, via quantum-accessible environments, our quantum algorithm obtains a
019            Õ(T 1/2 ) regret, improving upon the Õ(T 2/3 ) bound of Lakshmanan, Ortner, and
020            Ryabko (PMLR’15), where T is the number of iterations of the     algorithm. With-
021            out the Lipschitz assumption, a regret bound of Õ T 1/(1+α) is obtained when
                                                                √
022            0 < α < 1 and when α ≥ 1, the regret is Õ( T ). For a general d-dimensional √
023            state space, the regret is bounded by Õ(T 1/(1+dα )) when dα < 1 and Õ( T )
024            when dα ≥ 1. Our quantum algorithm uses quantum extended value iteration as
025            a subroutine, which is our second main contribution, and may be of independent
026            interest. We show that quantum extended value iteration achieves a subquadratic
027
               speedup in the size of the discretized state space S and a quadratic speedup in
               the size of the action space A, as compared to its classical counterpart. As our
028
               third contribution, we study the limiting behaviour of the sequence of value func-
029
               tions generated by quantum extended value iteration. We show that the sequence
030            converges to the optimal average reward ρ∗ up to ϵ additive error, for some small
031            ϵ > 0.

      1   I NTRODUCTION

      Markov decision processes. Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) [1] serve as a foundational
037
      framework for modeling decision-making in a wide array of dynamic and uncertain environments.
038
      Developed within the realm of stochastic control and mathematical optimization, MDPs provide a
039   systematic and rigorous approach to understanding and solving sequential decision problems. An
040   MDP models the interaction between an agent and the reinforcement learning environment. In-
041   formally, it consists of a set X of states, a set A of actions, a transition model P describing the
042   probability of moving from one state to another after taking an action and a reward function r.
043   At any time step, the agent in a particular state x(t) ∈ X chooses an action a(t) ∈ A, obtains
044   a reward r(x(t) , a(t) ), and moves to a new state x(t+1) according to some probability distribution
045   p(x(t+1) |x(t) , a(t) ). The goal in an MDP is to find a policy π — a mapping from states to actions —
046   that maximizes the cumulative reward ρ over time.
047
048   Reinforcement learning. Reinforcement learning (RL) [2] is a type of machine learning that uses
049   MDPs as the underlying framework. In RL, an agent learns an optimal policy by interacting with an
050   environment, receiving feedback through rewards, and using this experience to improve its decision-
051   making. Popular RL algorithms such as Q-learning, policy gradient, value iteration, and policy
052   iteration methods [3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9] have been widely studied. By effectively balancing between
053   exploration and exploitation, these algorithms enhance their performance in dynamic and uncertain
      settings, thereby learning the optimal policy.

      Online algorithms. Online algorithms model the interaction between an agent/learner with the
055   environment/nature. Such algorithms are usually associated with learning or decision making [10,
056   11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17]. Unlike offline algorithms where the agent has full access to the training
057   data as a whole, the setting of online algorithms is such that the agent receives part of the training
058   data in a (possibly adversarial) sequential manner. Based on incomplete knowledge of the entire
059   training data, the agent is required to make a decision, after which feedback from the environment is
060   provided in the form of a gain according to a pre-defined reward function. This process is repeated
061   for T number of time steps. The maximum gain incurred when making the best fixed decision in
062   hindsight is known as the offline gain. Moreover, the difference between the offline gain and the
063
      gain incurred when making some other sequence of decisions is called the regret. In the context of
      learning (communicating1 ) MDPs under the average reward model, the regret is given by T ρ∗ (M )−

114   Quantum computing In classical computing, the basic unit of information is a bit, which can
115   take values 0 or 1. In quantum computing, the basic unit is known as a quantum bit, or qubit. It is
116
      a two-level quantum system with states |0⟩ and |1⟩. Unlike a classical bit that has only two states,
                                                                 P1
117   a qubit is a superpositions of |0⟩ and |1⟩, i.e. |v⟩ = i=0 vi |i⟩, where vi ∈ C is the amplitude
                            Pd−1
118   of |i⟩ and satisfies i=0 |vi |2 = 1. The states |0⟩ , |1⟩ forms the (orthogonal) computational basis
119   of the two-dimensional Hilbert space. This extends to any d-dimensional system, where d > 2.
120   Quantum states from different Hilbert spaces can be combined using tensor product. For simplicity
121
      of notation, we use |u⟩ |v⟩ to denote the tensor product |u⟩ ⊗ |v⟩. Operations in quantum computing
      are unitary, i.e. a linear transformation U that satisfies U U † = U † U = I, where U † is the conjugate
122
      transpose of U .
123
124   The information in a quantum state cannot be “read” directly. In order to observe a quantum state
125   |v⟩, we perform a quantum measurement on it. The measurement results in a classical state i with
126   probability |vi |2 , and the measured quantum state collapses to |i⟩. Quantum access to input data
127
      is encoded in a unitary operator known as the quantum oracle. Quantum oracles allow data to be
      accessed in superposition, thereby allowing operations to be performed “simultaneously” on states,
128
      which is the core of quantum speedups.

131   Computational model. We refer to the running time of a quantum computation as the number of
132   basic gates performed, excluding the gates that are used inside the oracles. We assume a quantum
133   arithmetic model, which allows us to ignore issues arising from the fixed-point representation of
134   real numbers. In this model, all basic arithmetic operations take constant time. We also assume a
135
      quantum circuit model, where an application of an elementary gate is equivalent to performing an
      elementary operation. The query complexity of a quantum algorithm with some input length is the
136
      maximum number of queries the algorithm makes on any input.

138   Our quantum algorithm shall commonly build KP-trees [24, 25] of vectors. In short, a KP-tree is
139   a classical binary-tree-like data structure, with leaves storing the value of every entry of a vector
140   and each internal node stores the sum of absolute values (or sum of absolute values squared) of its
141
      children. The root of the tree stores the ℓ1 - (or ℓ2 -) norm of the whole vector. For a vector u ∈ RS ,
      the KP-tree for u is denoted as KPu . The KP-trees are accessible in superposition by a quantum
142
      computer via quantum random access memory (QRAM). A single query to any entry of u can be
143
      done in constant time. More specifically, this allows the quantum computer to query the oracles Ou
144   that performs the mapping Ou : |s⟩|0̄⟩ 7→ |s⟩|u(s)⟩ ∀s ∈ S in time O(poly log(S)). Moreover, (all
145   or part of) the entries of u can be classically updated by writing new values into the KP-tree in at
146   most Õ(S)time.

      6   D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
487
488
      We study the problem of online learning MDPs with continuous state space. In this setting, only
489
      the state and action spaces are known to the algorithm. Other parameters of the MDPs such as the
490
      reward function and transition probabilities are unknown.
491
492   We give a quantum algorithm, that in each episode, chooses an optimistic MDP and its correspond-
493   ing (nearly) optimal policy. This is done using a quantum subroutine, called quantum extended value
494
      iteration. The chosen policy is then executed until some action in some state-actions pair has been
      visited as often in the episode as before the episode. The observed rewards are √  accumulated and
495
      the regret is analyzed. Our results show that the quantum algorithm achieves a Õ( T ) regret when
496
      the state space is one-dimensional and assuming that the MDPs’ rewardds and transition probabil-
497
      ities are Lipschitz. This improves upon the regret bound obtained√by [18]. Without the Lipschitz
498
      assumption, the regret is Õ(T 1/(1+α) ) when 0 < α < 1 and Õ( T ) when α ≥ 1. This bound
499   also implies that MDPs with continuous state space can be learned with the same (order in T ) regret
500   as those with discrete state space when α ≥ 1. For the case where the√state space is d-dimensional
501   (d ≥ 1), the regret is bounded by Õ(T 1/(1+dα) ) when dα < 1 and Õ( T ) when dα ≥ 1.
502
503   We point out that a similar work to ours has been done by Ref. [21]. Unlike our quantum algorithm
504
      that learns general MDPs, the quantum algorithm proposed in [21] learns specific MDPs, i.e. tabular
      and value target MDPs. Furthermore, episodes in the algorithm of [21] have fixed length. This allows
505
      their algorithm to achieve a logarithmic regret in T , the number of episodes. This is in contrast to
506
      our algorithm, whose length of episodes grows indefinitely with T .
507
508   The quantum extended value iteration subroutine is a combination of techniques such as quantum
509   mean estimation, quantum norm estimation         and quantum
                                                   1.5 √           minimum finding with approximate uni-
                                                  S      A     1
510   tary. It has a per-iteration runtime of O       ϵ    log δ , achieving a speedup that is subquadratic in
511   the size of the discretized state space S and quadratic in the size of the action space A, as compared
512   to its classical counterpart. By studying the limiting behaviour of the sequence of value functions
513   {ũ(i) } generated by an approximate analogue of standard value iteration, we show that quantum
514   extended value iteration converges up to additive error ϵ and the greedy policy with respect to the
515   value function is ϵ-optimal. Furthermore, the sequence {ũ(i) } when compared to that generated by
516   standard value iteration {u(i) }, satisfies u(i) − iϵe ≤ ũ(i) ≤ u(i) for some ϵ > 0 and any i ≥ 1. We
517   hope that our quantum extended value iteration algorithm and its analysis would be of independent
518   interest to readers.
519   We highlight some future directions following our work. In this work, we follow the approach
520   of [22, 18, 57] to discretize the state space using ϵ-nets. It would be interesting to learn if other
521   discretization methods could lead to better regret bounds of the algorithm. Besides, the lower bound
522   on the regret still remains an open problem since the work of [18, 22]. Other future directions
523   include extending the problem setting to continuous action space.

      A    R ELATED WORK
811
812
      In the discounted reward model, Bas-Serrano et al. [77] proposed a logistic Q-learning algorithm,
813
      which is closely related to the relative entropy policy search algorithm [78]. Using a convex loss
814
      function for policy evaluation, the algorithm outputs a sequence of policies whose average quality
815   approaches that of the optimal policy. Subsequently, Neu and Olkhovskaya [20] incorporated the
816   algorithm of [77] into their online algorithm to learn MDPs with linear function approximation in
817   the setting where the reward function is allowed to change adversarily between episodes, obtaining
           √
818   a Õ( T ) regret, where T denotes the number of episodes.
819
      In the average reward model, Meyn [79] studied policy iteration in general (continuous) state spaces.
820
      Their algorithm was shown to output a sequence of policies that satisfy a strong stability condition
821
      and finds an optimal average cost policy under further conditions. Besides that, Ref. [58], under
822   MDPs with continuous state and action spaces, presented an approximate relative value iteration
823   algorithm that outputs a sequence of piecewise-linear convex relative value functions, which has
824   a monotonically non-decreasing lower bound on the average reward. Another work that considers
825   MDPs with continuous state and action spaces is Ref. [57], which gave a discretization-based ap-
826   proximation method for MDPs with continuous spaces, accompanied by a detailed error analysis.
827   They also developed synchronous and asynchronous Q-learning algorithms for continuous spaces
828   via discretization. In the online learning framework, Auer, Jaksch, and Ortner [19] gave√ an algo-
829   rithm to learn MDPs with discrete state and action spaces. Their algorithm achieves a O( T ) regret,
830   where T is the number of time steps. Their work was extended to the continuous setting by Ortner
                                                                                                      2d+1
831   and Ryabko [22], who gave a Õ(T 3/4 ) regret bound for 1-dimensional state space and Õ T 2d+2
832   when the state space is d-dimensional. The follow-up work of Lakshmanan, Ortner, and Ryabko [18]
                                                                          2+d
833   improved upon these results, giving a regret of Õ(T 2/3 ) and Õ T 3+d in 1 and d-dimensional state
834   space, respectively.
835
      In the quantum setting, Wiedemann et al. [73] gave a full implementation and simulation of a policy
836   iteration algorithm that is based on amplitude amplification. Besides numerically showing that the
837   policy output by their algorithm is close to optimal, they conjectured that a quadratic speedup in the
838   size of the set of all possible policies as compared to classical Monte Carlo estimation methods is
839   achievable. Wang et al. [74] gave two quantum algorithms that approximate an optimal policy, the
840   optimal value function, and the optimal Q-function using quantum mean estimation and quantum
841   maximum finding. They showed a quadratic improvement over the best possible classical sample
842   complexities with respect to the approximation error, the effective time horizon, and the size of the
843   action space. On the other hand, two quantum policy gradient algorithms were developed by Jerbi et
844
      al. [75] to estimate the optimal policy using quantum numerical and analytical gradient estimation
      respectively, gaining a quadratic reduction in sample complexity over their classical analogues when
845
      the trained policies satisfy certain conditions. Based on the classical least-squares policy iteration
846
      algorithm [8], Cherrat et al. [80] gave a general framework for quantum reinforcement learning via
847   policy iteration using block-encoding techniques [81, 82]. They showed that the value functions out-
848   put by the algorithms in their framework are close to optimal. Finally, the first line of study on explo-
849   ration in online quantum reinforcement learning was done by Zhong et al. [21] who showed √       a worst-
850   case regret guarantee that scales logarithmically in the number of episodes, beating the Ω( T ) re-
851   gret lower bound in classical reinforcement learning. Subsequently, Ganguly et al. [83] gave an
852   upper-confidence-bound-based quantum algorithm that achieves an exponential improvement in re-
853   gret and quadratic improvement in the sample complexity as compared to the classical counterparts.
854   The aforementioned works consider the discounted reward model and MDPs with discrete state and
855   action spaces. Other related work in the near-term regime includes [84, 85, 86, 87, 88].
