                                                  A BSTRACT
 1            We consider the task of learning to recover clean signals given only access to
 2            noisy data. Recent work in computer vision has addressed this problem in the
 3            context of images using denoising autoencoders (DAEs). However, to date DAEs
 4            for learning from noisy data have not been explored in the context of time-series
 5            data. DAEs for denoising images often rely on assumptions unlikely to hold in the
 6            context of time-series, e.g., multiple noisy samples of the same example. Here,
 7            we adapt DAEs to cleaning time-series data with noisy samples only. To recover
 8            the clean target signal when only given access to noisy target data, we leverage a
 9            noise-free auxiliary time-series signal that is related to the target signal. In addi-
10            tion to leveraging the relationship between the target signal and auxiliary signal,
11            we iteratively filter and learn from clean samples using an approach based on co-
12            teaching. Applied to the task of recovering carbohydrate values for blood glucose
13            management, our approach reduces noise (MSE) in patient-reported carbohydrates
14            from 72g 2 (95% CI: 54,93) to 18g 2 (13,25), outperforming the best baseline (MSE
15            = 33g 2 (27,43)). We demonstrate strong time-series denoising performance, ex-
16            tending the applicability of DAEs to a previously under-explored setting.


17   1   I NTRODUCTION
18   Background. Denoising autoencoders (DAEs) (Vincent et al., 2008) have been used to accurately
19   denoise various signals, including medical images (Gondara, 2016), ECG signals (Xiong et al.,
20   2016), and power system measurements (Lin et al., 2019). With respect to time-series data, DAEs
21   have been used for forecasting (Romeu et al., 2015), classification (Zheng et al., 2022) and impu-
22   tation (Zhang & Yin, 2019), but generally require access to clean samples at training and do not
23   provide de-noised outputs. In many real-world settings, clean samples are unavailable at training.
24   Work in computer vision has addressed this problem through extensions that either require paired
25   samples (Lehtinen et al., 2018) or rely on patch-based analysis (Krull et al., 2018; Laine et al., 2019;
26   Xie et al., 2020; Batson & Royer, 2019). Similar approaches do not extend to time-series data,
27   where paired samples rarely exist and patch-based techniques do not apply. Beyond approaches that
28   rely on paired samples or patch-based analyses, researchers have recently proposed techniques that
29   utilize knowledge of the noise distribution to recover the clean signal. These approaches either use
30   the properties of the distribution to recover the clean signal after training on noisy data (Kim & Ye,
31   2021; Moran et al., 2019), or rely on the noise having low expectation and variance compared to the
32   signal, in which case a model trained on noisy data can approximate one trained on clean data (Xu
33   et al., 2020). While these approaches may be considered in a time-series setting (and are treated as
34   baselines here), their applicability is limited as noise in time-series settings is rarely weak or known.
35   Our Contribution. In light of this gap, we adapt denoising autoencoders for time-series data. Our
36   approach, ‘Noise+ 2Noise’, learns to map a noisy target signal to a clean signal given only noisy
37   samples and an auxiliary clean signal. Inspired by work in image denoising (Lehtinen et al., 2018;
38   Xu et al., 2020), we add additional noise to the noisy target signal during training and attempt to
39   recover the original noisy signal. Provided that the noise has low expectation and variance, a network
40   trained in this manner can learn to recover the true signal because the noise will minimally impact
41   the expected value of the output of the network (Xu et al., 2020). The auxiliary signal is input
42   along with the target signal into a denoising autoencoder, which allows our network to leverage the
43   relationship between the auxiliary and target signals. To address the fact that the signal to noise
44   ratio might not be weak, we adapt a co-teaching approach to train two DAEs (Jiang et al., 2018;

45   Han et al., 2018). We use this approach to identify the cleaner samples; the most likely low-noise
46   samples are identified as the low-loss samples of the other model and used for backpropagation.
47   This co-teaching approach has never been applied to de-noising in a time-series or any other setting.
48   It has also never been utilized in a continuous output setting. By adapting this co-teaching approach
49   to DAEs, we provide a solution to denoising in this novel (time-series) setting.
50   Real-world Inspiration. Disparate levels of noise across variables are common in data streams.
51   Measurement reliability can vary across sensors, from essentially noiseless to highly corrupted.
52   Throughout this work we take inspiration from a real-world problem affecting millions in the US:
53   blood glucose management. Individuals with diabetes monitor several variables over time, including
54   their blood glucose, insulin administrations, and carbohydrate intake. Blood glucose when measured
55   by a continuous glucose monitor (CGM) has little noise, while the number of grams of carbohydrates
56   in a meal are patient-reported, and, as a result, are often inaccurate. Recognizing the variation in the
57   level of noise across signals in a data stream, we propose an approach that utilizes the more reliable
58   variables (e.g., blood glucose) to update noisy variables like carbohydrates. Our approach aims to
59   retrospectively correct measurements of patient-reported signals, like carbohydrates in the context
60   of diabetes, which could lead to improved treatment decisions: patients can learn when they are
61   over- or under-reporting and adjust in the future, ultimately improving health outcomes.


62   2   R ELATED W ORK

63   In denoising autoencoder training, a model input is corrupted and a network is tasked with recovering
64   the original input. In this way, the network cannot learn the identity, unlike in basic autoencoder
65   training (Vincent et al., 2008). Recent work in machine learning has focused on using DAEs to
66   recover clean signals from only noisy signals. The vast majority of this work lies in image analysis
67   and builds off of “noise2noise” (Lehtinen et al., 2018), an approach that uses multiple noisy instances
68   of the same image to train a model to learn to denoise the image. Briefly, the approach relies on the
69   fact that if the noise is mean zero, using a secondary noisy instance (besides the input image) as
70   a target will produce a network that learns the clean image, in expectation, when enough training
71   data is available. When paired samples are unavailable, others have proposed approaches based
72   on exploiting patches sampled from the image (Krull et al., 2018; Laine et al., 2019; Xie et al.,
73   2020; Batson & Royer, 2019), but these are not applicable to time-series data. Other approaches
74   eschew relying on inter-variable relationships but rely heavily on a known noise function (Moran
75   et al., 2019; Kim & Ye, 2021) or a low expectation and variance noise function (Xu et al., 2020).
76   Our approach builds off of Xu et al. (2020), learning to reconstruct a noisy signal from only noisy
77   samples of that signal, but in contrast to Moran et al. (2019) or Kim & Ye (2021), we do not make
78   strong assumptions about the noise distribution. To get around these assumptions, we leverage an
79   auxiliary signal and iteratively identify the cleaner samples within the dataset. Combined with the
80   auxiliary signal these samples are used to train a DAE that treats these samples as ground truth.
81   Our approach to denoising a noisy target signal by iteratively identifying clean samples is, in part,
82   related to work in noisy-label learning. A common approach to learning from noisy labels involves
83   identifying and reweighting samples with clean labels during training. Samples are filtered based
84   on gradient values (Ren et al., 2020), Jacobian ranking(Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020) or some latent
85   state (Lee et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020). Co-teaching (Han et al., 2018), which builds off of mentor
86   net (Jiang et al., 2018), is performed by training two networks in parallel. Each network is back-
87   propgated using only samples within the current mini-batch for which the loss of the other network
88   is lowest. Intuitively, samples with incorrect labels are likely to have higher loss and therefore be
89   removed. Using two networks in parallel provides robustness to outliers and initially misclassified
90   samples, which single-network boosting-style approaches are sensitive to. These approaches have
91   been primarily explored in a supervised setting. In contrast, we consider an unsupervised setting in
92   which labels are unavailable and, instead, the input signals themselves are corrupted. To the best of
93   our knowledge, such a co-teaching approach has not been explored in the context of denoising.

110   4    M ETHODS

111   Overview. Our method, ‘Noise+ 2Noise’ (N+ 2N), is summarized in Figure 1. We filter out noisy
112   samples during training, refining the model parameters on selected samples of y estimated to have
113   the least noise. These filtered samples are used to learn to denoise the signal. To identify the
114   samples within a batch to use during training, y and b are passed to a DAE that outputs ỹ, or a
115   de-noised y value. Assuming the DAE accurately reconstructs x, the loss L(ỹ, y) represents the
116   expected error between y and x. Given a batch {yi }ni=1 , we identify the subset of samples with
117   values of L(ỹ, y) below the R(t)th percentile (where R(t) is an increasing function of iteration t).
118   These ‘low-noise’ samples, yj ∀j ∈ J, are then augmented with additional noise mj ∼ M and,
119   along with corresponding b vectors, are input to the DAE, which outputs a reconstruction of yj :
120   ŷj . We then backpropagate using the squared error between ŷj and yj . In this way, the DAE is
121   trained on the samples estimated to have the least noise while utilizing the noisy signal as input
122   without the susceptibility to learning the identity function that comes with standard auto-encoder
123   training. Co-teaching is utilized in that two DAEs are used at each step (DAE 1 and DAE 2 ), and
124   during training, the samples identified as low-loss by DAE 1 are augmented and passed to DAE 2
125   for backpropagation, and vice-versa. This adds the benefit of ensembling and curtails sensitivity to
126   outliers or incorrectly selected samples. We note that the auxiliary signal b is input to the model
127   during both the sample selection and backpropagation steps. This allows our approach to function
128   even when the variable of interest y is too noisy or low-dimension to be de-noised alone.

130   Denoising Autoencoder. In our setup, additional noise (m) is added to y to produce z, a ‘doubly’
131   noisy measurement of x. z and b are input to a network (henceforth denoted DAE) that outputs
132   ŷ = DAE(z, b), and the network is trained to reconstruct y: loss is measured between y and ŷ.
133   As shown by Xu et al. (2020), when the expectation and variance of the noise distribution N are
134   negligible compared to those of the signal, the model parameters that minimize the loss between ŷ
135   and y are very close to the optimal parameters of a model trained on clean data. Thus, provided the
136   signal to noise level is high enough, we can pass y to DAE and expect a reduction in noise, with an
137   output much closer to x, at inference time.
138   Co-teaching DAEs. We do not expect the noise to be weak in general, but we do assume that
139   some of the samples will be lower noise than others. We identify and train using these samples
140   via an adapted co-teaching approach (Han et al., 2018). We utilize two DAEs, and for each, we
141   backpropagate using only the samples for which the denoised y values from the other DAE are near
142   the original y values. If the denoised y values approach x, we are then selecting samples for which
143   the estimated noise n is lowest.
144   Claim. When using co-teaching to train two DAEs (DAE 1 and DAE 2 ) in parallel, ỹ 1 =
145   DAE 1 (y, b) and ỹ 2 = DAE 2 (y, b) approach x.
146   Justification. Based on the main result ofXu et al. (2020), these values approximately equal x when
147   a DAE is trained on a dataset where the expectation and variance of the signal are much greater than
148   those of the noise. We have assumed that such a sub-sample exists in our dataset, and we hypothesize
149   that co-teaching will select such a sub-sample. Note that because the noise n is independent of both
150   y and b, any signal learned by DAE 1 must be of the form DAE 1 (y, b) = g(x) + q, for some
151   function g, where q is independent of all variables. The DAE might begin to learn a biased function
152   of x; for example, if the noise distribution N is not mean-zero, a DAE might learn the function
153   g ′ (x) = x + N̄ . We curtail this behavior by initializing the DAEs to output the identity function
154   (y). Because the model is near the identity function g(x) = y early in training, if it began to learn
155   a biased function such as g ′ , it would begin to output values g ′ (x) ≈ g ′ (y) = y + N̄ . These values
156   would be further from the identity than if the model learned a non-biased function of x, which would
157   in turn guide the model away from learning g ′ . By similar logic, the model would be encouraged to
158   learn a non-biased function of x, the simplest of which is g(x) = x. This is only a conjecture, but
159   in practice, we have found that this approach works well even when there is fairly substantial bias
160   in the noise. We note that although the two DAEs are initialized to output the identity, their weights
161   are otherwise random so they are not likely to converge to the same minima. We further prevent
162   convergence by utilizing the co-teaching+ variant (see below).
163   Sample selection. We select the samples with the lowest estimated noise to backpropagate with.
164   If ỹ 1 and ỹ 2 approach each network’s estimated value of x, then DAE 1 ’s estimate of n, the noise
165   between x and y, is approximately y − ỹ 1 (and similar for DAE 2 ). For loss function L, we use
166   L(ỹ 1 , y) and L(ỹ 2 , y) to select samples. Given a batch {yi }ni=1 , at iteration t, we identify the
167   subset of samples with values of L(ỹi1 , yi ) below the R(t)th percentile as J 1 = {j : L(yj , ỹj1 ) <
168   PR(t) (L({yj }, {ỹj1 }))}, and similarly define J 2 for DAE 2 . As in Han et al. (2018), we begin by
169   training on the full sample. Over the course of training, as the DAEs are expected to become more
170   accurate, we gradually reduce the sample. This prevents the memorization of noisy samples that can
171   occur later in training. Hyper-parameter τ ∈ (0, 1) represents the maximum proportion of samples
172   removed and Ek represents the iteration at which we stop increasing the proportion of samples
173   removed. A linear decrease in sample size as a function of iteration t is implemented by using the
174   lowest-loss R(t) = (1 − M aximum( EtK τ, τ )) · 100% of samples for backpropagation.
175   Training. Each DAE is trained on the samples for which the other network estimates that the noise
176   is lowest: samples selected by DAE 1 (yj ∀j ∈ J 1 ) are augmented with additional noise mj ∼ M
177   to generate zj values. zj , along with corresponding b vectors, are input to DAE 2 , which outputs
178   a reconstruction of yj : ŷj2 . We then backpropagate using the squared error between ŷj2 and yj .
179   Similarly, we only use samples yj ∀j ∈ J 2 , augmented with mj ∼ M , to backpropagate DAE 1 .
180   By selecting samples based on L(ỹ, y) rather than based on L(ŷ, y), we are able to select a sample
181   independent of secondary noise value m. Selecting samples dependent on m would be confounding
182   because samples might then be selected based on how low the value of m is at the current iteration,
183   rather than the value of n, which is hidden. Back-propagation is performed on an input that does not

194   which L(ỹ 1 , y) is lowest are used for the backpropagation of DAE 2 and vice versa.

195   5     R EAL - WORLD P ROBLEM S ETUP : B LOOD G LUCOSE M ANAGEMENT

196   To explore the benefit of our proposed approach, we consider a real-world problem setup based
197   on blood glucose management that inspired the setting described in Section 3. Nearly two million
198   people in the US have type I diabetes and require insulin to maintain healthy glucose levels due to
199   a pancreatic deficiency in insulin production. Because of this, they must deliver boluses of insulin
200   through an injection or an insulin pump prior to eating to counteract the rise in blood sugar that re-
201   sults from the ingestion of meals. Bolus amounts are calculated based on patient-reported estimates
202   of carbohydrates. Carbohydrates and bolus insulin generally cause blood glucose values to increase
203   or decrease after a delay of 30 minutes to an hour. In our setup, carbohydrates correspond to x
204   values, glucose levels are b.
205   Blood glucose forecasting and control have been extensively studied in the past (Silvia Oviedo,
206   2016; Fox et al., 2020). Accurate models for blood glucose dynamics are critical to the devel-
207   opment of algorithms for managing blood glucose in individuals with diabetes both in terms of
208   patient-selected treatment options and automated solutions. Work in this field is popular largely be-
209   cause the ubiquity of devices for measuring blood glucose and administering insulin make obtaining
210   fairly clean measurements for those values straightforward. However, carbohydrates consumed are
211   patient-reported and as a result are often inaccurate. This in turn leads to inaccurate doses of insulin
212   and poor blood glucose management. Besides misestimation, there are other sources of inconsis-
213   tency between recorded carbohydrate values and their effects on blood glucose. Variability in meal
214   types is generally poorly captured, which is problematic because the effect of carbs on blood glucose
215   can be moderated by how quickly a meal was consumed, or the amount of protein, fat and other nu-
216   trients eaten. Additionally, the timing of a meal may not be recorded accurately. These factors alone
217   make utilizing carbohydrate information difficult, even when carbs are accurately recorded. In an
218   unsupervised setting, denoising approaches could learn representations of carbohydrate values that
219   incorporate these other sources of variability. These representations could be more relevant to blood
220   glucose management than the exact number of grams consumed. This could improve performance
221   of forecast and control algorithms.

222   6     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP

223   We implement our approach in the context of learning to correct noisy patient-reported carbohydrate
224   measurements. We compare performance to several baselines across real and simulated datasets.

225   6.1   DATASETS

226   We utilize two T1D-based datasets. The simulated dataset provides access to ground truth to which
227   we can directly compare our method’s denoised outputs. The real dataset provides a more challeng-
228   ing setting for quantifying the efficacy of our approach, but corresponds to real-world scenarios.
229   Both datasets are publicly available and have been previously explored in the context of forecasting
230   and control (Man et al., 2014; Xie, 2018; Marling & Bunescu, 2018; 2020). Both datasets consist
231   of blood glucose, bolus (fast-acting) insulin, basal (slow-acting) insulin, and carbohydrate values.
232   All variables were scaled to be between zero and one. For both datasets, time-series trajectories for
233   each patient were split into windows of 2 hour length (T = 24 5-minute time points). We ignore

234   windows where a carbohydrate occurs in anywhere but the first position, using only windows with
235   no carbohydrates or carbohydrates at the beginning of the window during training. This means we
236   also ignore windows with more than one carbohydrate present. In a real-world setting these values
237   could be updated recursively, but we simplify our setting here.
238   Simulated. Our primary analyses are performed on data generated with a commonly-used T1D
239   simulator. We use the UVA-Padova simulator (Man et al., 2014) via a publicly available implemen-
240   tation (Xie, 2018). For ten simulated individuals (the “adult” patients modeled in the simulator), we
241   generated approximately 150 days worth of data each, in 30 day roll-outs of the simulator. Days
242   where a patient either had more than 25 timepoints of glucose at the minimum value of 40, or more
243   than 35 timepoints over 450 were thrown out for being non-realistic. The meal schedule used to
244   generate simulated data was based on the Harrison-Benedict equation (Harris & Benedict, 1919) as
245   implemented in (Fox et al., 2020). In our simulation, for all datasets generated, we used the default
246   basal-bolus controller from the existing implementation of the simulator to administer insulin, but
247   we delayed five sixths (randomly selected) of the bolus administrations up to 3.5 hours, with delay
248   time randomly sampled from a uniform distribution. The delay allows for disentanglement between
249   carbohydrate and bolus effects. The carbohydrate values serve as x values, while the CGM values
250   output by the simulator serve as b values. Noise was added to carbs during data generation.
251   We use noise proportional to the true carb value, as studies on the accuracy of carbe counting re-
252   port errors relative to the total carbs consumed (Brazeau et al., 2012). Also based on Brazeau et al.
253   (2012), we use a noise distribution with a negative bias, as the carbs were found to be more-often
254   under-reported than not. We therefore set y = (1 + N (−.25, .5))x. We then cap y below and above
255   at 1 and 200 to keep values realistic. We consider additional noise distributions as sensitivity anal-
256   yses. Bolus values were calculated based on the noisy carbohydrate values. Additionally, 20% of
257   carbohydrates are not reported, to make the dataset more realistic, as missingness is commonplace.
258   Real. This dataset includes both the OHIOT1DM 2018 and 2020 datasets, developed for the
259   Knowledge Discovery in Healthcare Data Blood Glucose Level Predication Challenge (Marling &
260   Bunescu, 2018; 2020). The data pertain to 12 individuals, each with approximately 10,000 5-minute
261   samples for training and 2,500 for testing. 12% of glucose values are missing, but we do not include
262   windows with missing glucose values. We do not include windows with more than one carbohydrate
263   measurement in our analysis. We sum carbohydrates to the first timepoint if they are less than 15
264   minutes apart to maximize the amount of usable data. We include only individuals with at least 100
265   training carbohydrate measurements, as fewer than this are not sufficient for learning a model.


266   6.2   BASELINES AND U PPER B OUND

267   For all non-coteaching methods, we train two DAEs in parallel and report results on their averaged
268   output for a fair comparison. We also note that all models receive the same auxiliary variables (blood
269   glucose/ insulin) as input in an identical fashion.
270   • CAE: An upper performance bound. This model is an autoencoder trained with clean data, which
271   we would expect to perform better than any method without access to clean data.
272   • NAC: Our first baseline. A DAE that treats the noisy data as clean which has been shown to
273   perform well in low noise settings (Xu et al., 2020).
274   • NR2N: Our second baseline is noisier2noise (Moran et al., 2019), which uses the known noise
275   distribution to recover the clean signal. NR2N trains similarly to NAC, but at evaluation time a
276   transform is used to recover the clean values (briefly, if the distribution of N is known and we set
277   M = N , the model should learn to recover half of the noise so the value used at evaluation is 2ŷ−z).
278   • SUP: Our motivating setting can be re-framed as a supervised learning problem: predict y (or x)
279   values using b values as input. Depending on the noise distribution, it is possible that a model trained
280   on noisy y values could learn to predict the correct x, using similar logic to that found in Lehtinen
281   et al. (2018). We therefore use this supervised setting as a naive baseline. We simply input b to the
282   same network used in the DAE setting and calculate loss as (ŷ − y)2 during training, but here the
283   model has no information regarding y or z. As in the DAE setting, at test time we evaluate (ỹ − x)2 .
284   • SUPCT: We apply co-teaching to the supervised setting (SUP), to ensure that performance gains
285   observed are due to the combination of DAEs and co-teaching, and not co-teaching alone. Here, the
286   model is tuned and trained identically to N+ 2N, except the model does not receive y or z values.

287   6.3   I MPLEMENTATION & T RAINING D ETAILS

288   Each DAE is implemented as a 2-layer bidirectional LSTM with 100 hidden units. The final hidden
289   state is passed to a FC layer with a single output. The output of the model is added to the input
290   value corresponding to y, so that the network is tasked with learning the error term rather than a
291   complete reconstruction. This initializes the network’s output to be the identity (provided the output
292   is initially low magnitude), which is useful for addressing biased data. Because we only aim to
293   correct a single carbohydrate (y) value but use a time-based model, we set one dimension of the
294   model input to be y for all timepoints. Blood glucose values b, bolus and basal insulin are input
295   to the model as time-series. We also carry over bolus insulin values (which occur sparsely) to the
296   end of the input window, to increase their impact on gradient calculations. We threshold the output
297   of each DAE at 0, because carbohydrates (our x and y) values cannot be negative. For each co-
298   teaching method and NR2N, we tune on a single subject’s validation data (adult#001) and use the
299   found parameters for all further analyses. Tuning is described in Appendix A. At evaluation we
300   report the result of the average correction learned by both networks when y values are given as input
301   (i.e., where ỹi = DAEi (y, b), we report L(x, (ỹ 1 + ỹ 2 )/2)).
302   We split each dataset into training, validation and test sets used for evaluation purposes. For the
303   simulated dataset, we use 80 days for training, 20 for validation, and 50 for testing. For the real
304   dataset, we split the training data into 80% train and 20% validation. The held-out test data were
305   used for evaluation only. We implement and train our models in Pytorch 1.9.1 with CUDA version
306   10.2, using Ubuntu 16.04.7, a GeForce RTX 2080, an Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014), and a
307   batch size of 500. We use a learning rate of 0.01 and a weight decay of 10−7 . We train for at least
308   500 iterations, and then until validation performance does not improve for 50 iterations, selecting
309   the model for which validation performance was best. For both datasets, we train and test a model on
310   each individual and report across-individual averages. Such individual-specific models/evaluations
311   are common in blood glucose control and forecasting (Silvia Oviedo, 2016), since dynamics vary
312   greatly across individuals and individual-specific training data are typically available
313   We perform co-teaching on samples containing non-zero y values only. However, when training all
314   models (including baselines) we also pass zero-valued y samples (and their corresponding b values)
315   through both DAEs and take loss equal to ŷ 2 for these samples. We do this because there are many
316   more samples with zero-valued carbohydrates than there are with positive values, and this allows
317   the models to learn from this larger corpus. We report results on only positive-valued ys, because
318   denoising is only applied to such values.
319   For sample selection during co-teaching, we use mean squared percentage error (100%·((ŷ−y)/y)2 )
320   to avoid eliminating all high-valued y samples, as they are likely to have higher noise values. As a
321   noise function during training, we use z = (1 + N (0, .5))Bern(.5)y, i.e. we add random noise to
322   half of the samples so that the model can learn to utilize noisy z information, and zero-out the other
323   half so that the model has to learn to distinguish zero from non-zero y values based on b alone.

324   6.4   E VALUATION

325   Simulated Data Metrics. When clean carbohydrate values are available, we use MSE between the
326   cleaned carbs and true carbohydrate values as our metric to report the remaining noise (mean((x −
327   ỹ)2 ), where ỹ = (ỹ 1 + ỹ 2 )/2). The lower this value, the more noise has been removed. Although
328   we assume that these data are not available at training time, we use them for evaluation. Since it can
329   be difficult to interpret the meaning of a difference in MSE, we also consider a clinically motivated
330   evaluation metric: time in range. Time in range is a measure of blood glucose management and
331   varies with the accuracy of the carbohydrate measurements. The more accurate the carbohydrate
332   estimates the more time an individual will spend ‘in range.’ Here, we run a simulation of the subject
333   of interest with the default basal-bolus controller using bolus values calculated from the updated
334   carbohydrate values, and report the proportion of time in the simulation that each individual spent
335   with blood glucose values between 70 and 180, or the euglycemic/healthy range. This metric serves
336   to indicate the real-world impact each approach might have. For both metrics, 95% confidence
337   intervals are calculated for each subject using 1,000 bootstrap re-samples, and the average 2.5 and
338   97.5 percentiles across subjects are reported.
339   Sensitivity analyses. To evaluate our model under different noise assumptions, we repeat our analy-
340   sis with multiple noise generation methods (x → y), without altering hyperparameters or our y → z

341   noise function. We use various Gaussian and uniform distributions reported in Appendix B, which
342   include zero and negative mean multiplicative and additive noise functions. We do not aim at a com-
343   prehensive evaluation of all possible noise types, but rather we aim to include various distributions
344   that are likely similar to those that might arise in our motivating domain.
345   Real Data analysis. Without access to clean carbohydrate values at test time for the real dataset
346   (unlike the simulated dataset), we evaluate the performance of our denoising approach based on
347   a proxy. We take advantage of the fact that poorly estimated carbohydrates result in inaccurate
348   bolus calculations, which result in poor blood glucose management. We expect that inaccurate
349   carbohydrates estimates (large [x − y] values) result in the poor blood glucose management. For a
350   model that has come close to estimating x correctly, we would observe a correlation between [ỹ − y]
351   values and blood glucose control in the time period following a meal. We assess this with Correction-
352   Risk-Correlation, henceforth referred to as CRC, defined as the Spearman correlation between the
353   squared carbohydrate correction value ((ỹ − y)2 ) and the average Magni Risk (Magni et al., 2007)
354   of blood glucose in the second hour following the carbohydrate. We use the Spearman correlation to
355   account for non-linearities in the risk and correction value distributions. Magni risk is a measure of
356   how far from a safe value blood glucose is; higher risk values correspond to blood glucose values that
357   are either dangerously high or dangerously low. We use the second hour following the carbohydrate
358   because the effects of the carbohydrate consumption and insulin bolus have not fully taken effect in
359   the first hour. We calculate this correlation across all carbohydrates observed in all individuals. For
360   validation purposes, we also calculate this metric for the simulated dataset.

400   8   C ONCLUSIONS

401   We propose a new approach to denoising, ‘Noise+ 2Noise’, that does not assume access to clean
402   samples and applies to time-series data. Our approach leverages an auxiliary time-series that is re-
403   lated to the target signal to help identify target samples with less noise. Our approach is the first to
404   adapt co-teaching to de-noising. In the context of carbohydrate recovery for blood glucose manage-
405   ment, compared to existing approaches, ‘Noise+ 2Noise’ leads to better signal reconstruction that is
406   both statistically significant and clinically significant. While promising, our approach is not without
407   limitations. Our primary analyses are on simulated data where ground truth labels are available, but
408   in real datasets common evaluation metrics (e.g., MSE) do not apply and we must rely on proxies. As
409   presented, our approach is designed for retrospective carbohydrate correction; more work is neces-
410   sary to investigate its applicability to closer-to-real-time correction. While our approach shows clear
411   significance for the individuals available, evaluation on a much larger population would be necessary
412   prior to widespread adoption. Finally, while we have empirically shown that co-teaching appears to
413   select a low-noise sample, we have not proven any statistical guarantees. Despite these limitations,
414   we have demonstrated that is is feasible to correct a noisy variable without access to clean samples,
415   expanding the utility of ideas the image analysis and noisy label learning to time-series reconstruc-
416   tion. Applied to domains in which datastreams are composed of both individual-reported data and
417   data measured from relioable sensors (e.g., mHealth), or datastreams composed of series with dis-
418   parate noise levels (e.g., external vs internal temperature, multiple audio recordings), our approach
419   could aid in improving the reliability of uncertain time-series data.
